The bridal bouquet is such a fixed feature of modern weddings that it's easy to assume it has always existed in roughly its current form. It hasn't. The flowers carried at weddings have changed dramatically across history, and the changes track closely with shifting cultural attitudes toward marriage, womanhood, social class, and the relationship between nature and civilization.

## Ancient Rome: the herb bouquet

Roman brides carried herbs — particularly **garlic, herbs, and wheat** — bound together and worn in garland form. This was not aesthetic but apotropaic: the bundle was intended to ward off evil spirits, protect fertility, and symbolise the agricultural prosperity of the new household. The emphasis was entirely protective and symbolic rather than decorative.

**Myrtle** (*Myrtus communis*) was the sacred plant of Venus, the goddess of love, and was included in Roman wedding ceremonies as an offering to the goddess and a symbol of love and fertility. This association was powerful enough to survive the transition from Roman paganism to Christian Europe, and myrtle has remained associated with weddings ever since.

In Victorian England, Queen Victoria famously carried a bouquet that included myrtle from a plant said to have been grown from a cutting sent by her grandmother, Princess Augusta. A tradition developed among the British royal family of incorporating myrtle from the same original plant in every royal bride's bouquet — a tradition that has continued to the present day. Princess Diana, Catherine Middleton, and Meghan Markle all carried myrtle from this plant.

## Medieval Europe: garlands and crowns

In medieval Europe, brides typically wore **flower crowns** (chaplets) rather than carrying bouquets. The flower crown marked the transition from maidenhood to wifehood and connected the bride symbolically with the cycles of nature and fertility.

The flowers used were those available seasonally and locally — primarily wildflowers and garden herbs. May weddings (before the church began discouraging spring weddings during Lent and Easter observances) used hawthorn blossom, violets, and primroses. Summer weddings used roses and lavender. The emphasis was on garlands, crowns, and woven decorations rather than the held bouquet form.

Strongly scented herbs — **rosemary, thyme, sage** — were incorporated in medieval wedding ceremonies not only for their symbolism (rosemary for remembrance; thyme for courage) but for a thoroughly practical reason: in an era when personal hygiene and public sanitation were limited, a bundle of aromatic herbs held at face level served as a literal air freshener.

## The Victorian era: when the bouquet became fashionable

The modern bridal bouquet as we recognise it — a formal arrangement of flowers held in the hands — emerged as a fashionable object in the 19th century Victorian era. Several factors converged:

**Queen Victoria's influence**: Victoria's wedding in 1840 was exhaustively documented and her choices became immediately fashionable. She carried a cascading arrangement of white flowers (including myrtle and orange blossom), and her white wedding dress created a new fashion standard. Before Victoria, brides wore their finest clothing in any colour — the all-white wedding dress was not traditional before 1840.

**The language of flowers**: Victorian floriography gave every flower a specific meaning, and brides could compose a bouquet that communicated a specific message to a knowledgeable audience. **White roses** meant purity and silence; **orange blossom** (a favourite Victorian wedding flower) symbolised purity and fertility and gave fragrance; **lily of the valley** signified a return to happiness and humility.

**The hothouse economy**: Advances in greenhouse technology during the Industrial Revolution meant that wealthy Victorians could access flowers from across the world regardless of season. This made exotic, rare, and out-of-season flowers available to those who could afford them — and the bridal bouquet became a display of social status as much as personal meaning.

**The cascade shape** became fashionable in the Victorian era — long, trailing bouquets that draped over the arm rather than the compact tied bunch form. This theatrical shape was well-suited to the elaborate Victorian wedding gown with its long train.

## The 20th century: democratisation and changing fashions

The 20th century brought dramatic changes in both wedding culture and the flowers available for it. The development of global air freight in the 1960s and 70s revolutionised the cut flower market, making flowers from Kenya, Colombia, the Netherlands, and Israel available year-round worldwide at relatively accessible prices.

The **long-stemmed red rose** was the dominant wedding flower of much of the mid-20th century — particularly in formal, traditional weddings — though florists of the era also worked extensively with **carnations** (now less fashionable but still beautiful), **chrysanthemums**, **freesia**, and **gladiolus**.

The 1980s saw an explosion of elaborate arrangements — large, round "ball" bouquets, heavily wired formal designs — that reflected the decade's general aesthetic of excess and theatricality.

## The contemporary garden-style bouquet

The current dominant aesthetic in wedding floristry — established gradually through the 2000s and solidified in the 2010s — is what florists call the **"garden-style" or "loose" bouquet**: a hand-tied, loosely gathered arrangement that looks as though the flowers were just gathered from a beautiful garden, artfully but apparently casually assembled.

The defining flowers of this aesthetic are **peonies, ranunculus, garden roses** (particularly David Austin roses with their full, cup-shaped blooms), **sweet peas, astrantia**, and generous quantities of interesting foliage — eucalyptus, ruscus, olive, or ferns. The colour palette tends toward soft, dusty, slightly desaturated tones: blush, ivory, dusty rose, mauve, sage green.

This aesthetic reflects several broader cultural shifts: a preference for things that look hand-made and organic over the formal and manufactured; an increasing awareness of the floral calendar and a valuing of seasonal, locally grown flowers; and a reaction against the perceived artificiality of heavily wired, architectural arrangements.

**Dried flower bouquets** have become a significant recent trend — partly driven by sustainability concerns (no waste after the wedding), partly by the visual quality of dried flowers that complements contemporary rustic and bohemian wedding aesthetics.

What remains constant across all these changes — from Roman herb bundle to Victorian cascade to contemporary garden bunch — is the flower's role as a carrier of intention and meaning at one of life's most significant transitions. The specific flowers change; the impulse to mark the moment with something beautiful, living, and fragrant does not.
