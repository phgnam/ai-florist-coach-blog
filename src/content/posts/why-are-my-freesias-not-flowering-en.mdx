## Quick Answer

Freesias usually fail to flower because the **corms are too small** (under 6 cm circumference), **planted too deep**, **grown too warm** (above 18 °C), **fed too much nitrogen**, or **not given the long cool growing period** they need (10–14 °C for 12–14 weeks before flowering). Lift, sort by size, replant only fat corms 5 cm deep in cool conditions, and feed potassium-only — flowers reliably follow.

## Why are freesias so picky about temperature?

Freesias are South African corms from the winter-rainfall Cape region, so they evolved to grow during cool wet winters and bloom in spring before going dormant for the dry summer. Replicating that cycle is the single biggest factor in whether they flower:

- **Above 18 °C** at night during bud development — they go blind (no flowers, just leaves)
- **Below 4 °C** during the cool growing phase — growth stalls and flowers abort
- **Sweet spot**: 10–14 °C nights, 15–18 °C days, for 12–14 weeks from sprouting to flower

A cool greenhouse, an unheated conservatory, or an outdoor pot in a frost-free coastal climate gives the right conditions. A heated indoor windowsill almost never does.

## How can I tell if my corms are big enough to flower?

Freesia corms have a **minimum flowering size** below which they will only produce leaves no matter how well you treat them.

- **Under 5 cm circumference** (about 1.5 cm diameter) — leaves only, no flowers expected
- **5–6 cm circumference** — possible flower spike, often weak
- **6–7 cm circumference** — reliable flowering size
- **Over 7 cm circumference** — multiple spikes per corm

When you lift corms in summer, sort them. Plant the fat ones in pots for indoor display. Plant the tiny daughter cormels separately in a "growing on" tray to bulk up for 1–2 years before they flower.

## Why does planting depth matter so much for freesias?

Freesias dislike being buried deep. Unlike tulips or daffodils that go 10–15 cm down, freesias prefer:

- **5 cm of soil over the top of the corm**
- **Pointy end up** (this seems obvious but the corm shape is subtle)
- **Free-draining gritty mix** — they hate sitting in wet soil

Too deep and the emerging shoot exhausts itself reaching daylight, leaves are small, and flowering aborts. Too shallow and the corm dries out and may rock loose in wind.

## How long do freesias need from planting to flowering?

The full timeline:

- **Plant corms** in late summer for spring flowering, or late winter for autumn flowering
- **Sprouting** within 2–3 weeks
- **Leaf growth phase** 6–8 weeks
- **Bud emergence** 2 weeks
- **Flower spike development** 2 weeks
- **Total**: 12–14 weeks from planting to flower

Skip any cool weeks (e.g. by keeping them too warm) and the flower stage cancels. The plant returns excess energy to the corm and goes dormant earlier than expected.

## What feeding mistakes prevent freesia flowering?

The wrong feed produces a luxurious mound of leaves with zero flower stems.

- **High-nitrogen feed (lawn or general garden fertiliser)** — produces leafy growth at the expense of flowers
- **Too much feeding overall** — freesias are poor-soil natives, not heavy feeders
- **No potassium specifically** — buds rely on potassium for development

The right approach:

- Bone meal or low-nitrogen base when planting (a small pinch per pot)
- Switch to a **high-potash tomato feed** (5-2-7 type) once flower buds emerge
- Stop feeding entirely once flowers fade

If you have over-fed leaves with no buds, lift and replant in poorer compost the next season — a partial recovery is possible but the season is lost.

## Why might bought freesias bloom one year but not again?

Many gardeners find their first-year freesias spectacular and the second-year display thin. Several reasons combine:

- **Energy drain** — the first season's flowers used all the corm's stored sugars
- **Inadequate lift-and-dry** — corms left in wet soil over summer rotted
- **Insufficient growing season** — leaves removed too early so corms didn't refill
- **Daughter cormels cluster** — original corm shrinks while small ones siblings around it appear

The fix:

- **Allow leaves to die back fully** — yellow brown is fine, do not cut while still green
- **Lift corms in mid-summer** before autumn rains
- **Dry indoors at room temperature for 2–3 weeks**, then store in dry sand at 8–12 °C until autumn replanting
- **Replant only the largest corms** in fresh compost; grow small ones on for next year

## Can freesias be forced to bloom indoors like hyacinths?

Sort of, but they want cooler conditions than hyacinths so they need a different approach.

- **A cool, bright unheated room** (not a warm centrally heated room)
- **Plant 6 corms in a 15 cm pot** in late September
- **Keep at 7–10 °C** for 4 weeks (a porch, garage, or cold frame works)
- **Move to a slightly warmer cool spot** (12–15 °C, bright window) for 8 weeks until buds emerge
- **Keep the same cool position** through flowering — bringing them into a warm room collapses the spike fast

Stake the stems early — freesias have weak stems and lean once they bud.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why do my freesia leaves grow but no flowers form?

The most common cause is corm size below the flowering threshold (under 6 cm circumference) or excess nitrogen during the leaf phase. Lift, sort, and replant the fat corms only in poorer compost.

### Why do freesia stems flop over?

Tall freesia stems naturally arch and the elegant droop is part of their charm. If they collapse fully, the cause is usually low light during bud development, too-warm growing conditions, or not enough potassium. Stake young stems with a thin cane and a soft tie to keep them upright for cutting.

### Should freesias be lifted every year?

In wet temperate climates yes — summer rain rots dormant corms. In dry Mediterranean climates they can stay in the ground for 2–3 years before rejuvenating. Always lift if soil drainage is poor or if rainfall is high in summer.

### Are scented freesias different?

Yes — older heirloom varieties like 'Aurora', 'Athene', and the species *Freesia alba* are heavily scented. Modern "Tetra" and "Easy Pot" cultivars often have larger flowers but weaker scent. If perfume matters, look specifically for "scented" or "fragrant" cultivars.

### Can I grow freesias outdoors in the UK?

In coastal Cornwall, Pembrokeshire, and parts of southwest Ireland — yes, in well-drained soil. Inland or northern UK they rarely survive winter outdoors. Treat as cool greenhouse plants or as autumn-lifted summer-flowering corms instead.

### Do freesias need to be staked?

Yes if grown for cutting or display. The thin arching stems lean as buds open and a small twiggy support holds them upright without looking obvious. Set the support when leaves are 15 cm high, before buds emerge.
