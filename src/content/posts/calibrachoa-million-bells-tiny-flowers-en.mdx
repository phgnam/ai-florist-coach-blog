## Quick Answer

Calibrachoa (Million Bells) baskets typically look perfect in June and disappointing by mid-July because the compost is exhausted and the plants have started to go bald inside, with all flowers pushed out to the trailing tips. The fix is a coordinated rescue: a hard cut-back of about a third, a thorough flush of the compost, daily watering with a half-strength balanced liquid feed, and a switch from "bloom booster" feeds to a feed containing iron. Recovery takes 10–14 days; the basket returns to full flower for August and September.

## What is calibrachoa and why does it look so good early summer?

*Calibrachoa* spp. (sold as Million Bells, Superbells, MiniFamous, Aloha) are short-lived perennial trailing flowers in the same family as petunias (Solanaceae). The flowers look like miniature petunias — typically 2–3 cm across instead of 7–10 cm — but produced in vastly greater numbers, in a much wider colour range. Modern hybrids include doubles, frilled, two-tone "Kabloom" types, and trailing forms ideal for hanging baskets and patio pots.

Their early-summer brilliance comes from three things:

1. **Vegetative propagation.** Modern calibrachoa cultivars are F1 hybrids propagated from cuttings, so every plant flowers reliably and uniformly.
2. **Continuous flowering on new growth.** Each shoot produces a flower at every node, so a fast-growing plant flowers heavily.
3. **Greenhouse pampering.** Plug plants and finished baskets are raised under ideal conditions — perfect compost, controlled water, weekly feed — so they leave the nursery at peak performance.

The trouble starts when those ideal conditions stop. After 6–8 weeks at home, the basket is in a different microclimate, often with neglected watering and exhausted compost. Performance drops fast.

## Why do the flowers shrink and go sparse in midsummer?

The "tiny flower / fewer flower" failure pattern in calibrachoa is almost always a combination of related problems.

**Compost exhaustion.** Calibrachoa is one of the heaviest feeders in the bedding-plant world. A full hanging basket of mature calibrachoa exhausts a multipurpose compost's nutrient charge in 4–6 weeks. The plant survives without feed but flowers are dramatically reduced.

**Iron lockout.** Calibrachoa is even more iron-sensitive than petunias. As compost pH drifts up with hard tap water, iron becomes unavailable. Yellow leaves with green veins on the newest growth confirm the diagnosis.

**Bald centre.** Calibrachoa's growth pattern naturally pushes flowers outward to the trailing tips. The interior of the basket goes leafless and flowerless within 6 weeks unless trimmed.

**Heat stress.** Above 30 °C with low humidity, flower production drops while leaves continue to grow. The basket looks lush but unflowered.

**Phosphate excess.** "Bloom booster" feeds are heavy in phosphate, which exacerbates iron lockout. Use them on calibrachoa and you make the problem worse.

**Salt build-up.** Repeated feeding without flushing accumulates salts in the compost. Symptoms include leaf-edge scorch, slow growth, and reduced flowering.

## How exactly do I rescue a tired calibrachoa basket?

A staged rescue works in nearly every case. Allow 10–14 days for full recovery.

### Day 1: Cut back

Trim every trailing stem back by about one third — even more if the basket has gone particularly bald. This sounds drastic but is essential. New shoots will branch from each cut and rebuild the centre of the basket within 2 weeks.

Tools: clean sharp scissors. Cut just above a leaf node where possible.

### Day 1: Flush

Take the basket down. Stand it in a tub or shower and water heavily until clear water runs out the bottom, then wait an hour and repeat. This flushes accumulated salts and resets compost chemistry.

### Day 1: Feed

While the basket is still in the tub, mix a half-strength balanced liquid feed (e.g. 10-10-10 or "all-purpose") and apply. Re-hang the basket in its usual spot.

### Days 2–10: Daily light feeding

Switch from weekly heavy feed to daily watering with a quarter-strength balanced liquid feed. Calibrachoa responds dramatically to little-and-often feeding once flowering recovery starts. If using a basket-specific feed, it will already include iron — confirm.

### Day 14: Assess

By day 14 you should see new flowering shoots from every cut tip and a return to full bloom. If iron deficiency is still showing (yellow leaves, green veins on new growth), apply chelated iron (Fe-EDDHA) once.

## Should I deadhead calibrachoa, or is it self-cleaning?

Calibrachoa is genuinely self-cleaning — old flowers wither and drop without leaving brown remnants on the plant. This is one of its big advantages over old-school petunias.

Practical implications:

- **No daily deadheading needed.** Save your time for the watering and feeding routine.
- **Spent stems still need trimming.** Self-cleaning applies to flowers, not whole stems. Stems that have flowered to their tip slow down; trim them back to encourage new branches.
- **Wilted whole flower heads are not normal.** If clusters of flowers wilt at once, suspect water stress, fungal disease, or root problems — not lack of deadheading.

## Which calibrachoa cultivars are easiest to keep flowering all summer?

Cultivar choice matters as much as care.

| Cultivar | Habit | Ease | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 'Million Bells' (original) | Trailing | Moderate | The classic; reliable but heavy feeder. |
| 'Superbells' series | Trailing | High | Bred for heat tolerance and flower size. |
| 'MiniFamous' / 'MiniFamous Neo' | Trailing | High | Compact branching habit; less midsummer balding. |
| 'Aloha' series | Mounding | Very high | Self-branching, fewer cutbacks needed. |
| 'Kabloom' | Mounding | Moderate | First seed-grown calibrachoa; less vigour but cheaper. |
| 'Cabaret' series | Trailing | High | Bred for early flowering. |
| 'Calitastic' | Trailing | Moderate | Wide colour palette; standard care. |

**'Aloha' or 'Superbells' are the easiest** if you want something that survives benign neglect. They self-branch (so balding is less of a problem) and tolerate heat better than most.

## How do I water calibrachoa correctly through summer?

Watering is the second-biggest factor after feeding.

**The rules:**

1. **Water daily in summer.** Hanging baskets in full sun dry out within 18 hours; check the compost every morning.
2. **Water until water runs out the bottom.** Surface-only watering encourages shallow roots that fail under heat stress.
3. **Avoid waterlogging.** Calibrachoa hates sitting in saturated compost. Ensure drainage and never use saucers under hanging baskets.
4. **Switch to rainwater where possible.** Hard tap water elevates pH and triggers iron lockout.
5. **Water in the morning, not evening.** Damp foliage overnight encourages botrytis.
6. **Group baskets to slow drying.** Isolated single baskets dry fastest.
7. **Rotate every few days.** This keeps the flowering even on all sides.

## Are calibrachoa flowers safe for cats and dogs?

Calibrachoa is generally regarded as low-toxicity and is not on the ASPCA toxic plants list. Mild gastrointestinal upset is possible if pets eat large amounts, but no serious poisoning is documented. They are safer than many other summer container flowers (including some lilies and daffodils).

That said, the iron sulphate and chelated iron treatments often used for calibrachoa *can* be harmful if a pet drinks the dilute liquid feed. Apply at the compost surface, mix into the compost not the patio surface, and store the bottle out of reach.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why are my calibrachoa leaves yellow with green veins?**
Iron deficiency. Calibrachoa is more iron-sensitive than even petunias. Apply chelated iron, switch to rainwater, top-dress with ericaceous compost, and avoid bloom-booster feeds. New leaves will emerge green within 7–10 days.

**Can I overwinter calibrachoa indoors and use it again next year?**
Possibly — they are technically perennials in USDA zones 9–11 — but most gardeners find the effort isn't worth it. Take cuttings in late summer instead; they root easily in 2–3 weeks and give you fresh young plants for next spring.

**My calibrachoa stopped flowering completely. Is it dead?**
Probably not. Cut back hard (by half), flush the compost, restart light daily feeding, and wait 2 weeks. If the stems are still green and pliable, recovery is almost certain. Brown brittle stems suggest serious root failure — replace.

**Should I pinch out the flowers when first planting calibrachoa baskets?**
Yes — pinching the first flush of flowers in the first week after planting redirects energy to root and shoot growth. This delays peak flowering by 2 weeks but produces a far fuller basket that flowers heavily for the entire summer.

**Why are tiny black bugs visible on the new growth?**
Aphids. Calibrachoa is mildly attractive to aphids, especially in dry warm conditions. Spray off with a strong jet of water, repeat every 3 days for a week. If persistent, apply organic insecticidal soap. A healthy plant tolerates light aphid pressure without permanent damage.
