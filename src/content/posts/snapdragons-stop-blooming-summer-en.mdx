## Quick Answer

Snapdragons stop blooming in summer because they are **cool-season annuals** that go heat-dormant once daytime temperatures stay above **27°C** for more than a week or two. They also stop if you let the **first flush set seed**, if they are **pot-bound** in undersized containers, or if they run dry repeatedly. The fix is to **cut plants back by half**, water deeply, mulch the roots, and wait. Most snapdragons re-bloom in late summer or autumn once nights cool below 18°C.

## Why are snapdragons "cool-season" plants in the first place?

Snapdragons (*Antirrhinum majus*) evolved on rocky Mediterranean hillsides where bloom timing is locked to **cool nights and bright but moderate days**. The plant's flowering hormones (gibberellins, plus the flowering switch genes that control the inflorescence) work best in **night temperatures of 5–18°C and daytime temperatures of 12–24°C**.

Once daytime temperatures sit consistently above **27°C** and night temperatures rarely drop below **20°C**, three things happen at the cellular level:

1. **Pollen viability collapses.** Most snapdragon cultivars become functionally sterile above 28°C, so even open flowers do not set seed and the plant senses a "wasted bloom" feedback loop.
2. **Bud initiation slows or stops.** New flower spikes form much more slowly above 27°C; some cultivars stop initiating new spikes entirely until temperatures fall again.
3. **Carbohydrate reserves shift.** Energy gets diverted from flowers to leaf maintenance and into the root crown, where the plant essentially waits out the heat.

This is why a snapdragon that flowered beautifully in May suddenly looks "stuck" in July: it has not died, it has gone into reproductive standby until conditions improve.

In hot-summer climates the plant is usually grown as a **winter / spring annual** (autumn planting → late winter or spring bloom) and discarded as summer hits. In cool-summer climates, it can flower right through August and into autumn.

## How can you tell heat dormancy from a real problem?

Heat dormancy and a sick plant look superficially similar but have different cures. Read the plant before reaching for any treatment.

**Probably heat dormancy:**

- Daytime temperatures have averaged 27°C+ for at least a week.
- Foliage is still green and reasonably full.
- The plant has already given a strong first flush of bloom.
- New side shoots are forming low on the plant but no buds appear at the tips.

**Probably a real problem:**

- Lower leaves yellowing and dropping (water issue or root rot).
- Foliage faded grey-green with fine webbing on the underside (spider mites, common in heat).
- Distorted, sticky new growth (aphids).
- Brown spots with yellow halos on leaves (fungal leaf spot, common after wet warm spells).
- Plant collapsing entirely with stems brown at the base (stem rot).

If the plant looks broadly healthy and just refuses to flower, treat it as heat-dormant. If foliage is failing on top of the bloom failure, treat the foliage problem first.

## What should you do during the hot stretch?

This is the most common moment for snapdragons to die from owner intervention rather than from heat. The instinct is to feed and water heavily; in fact the plant wants the opposite.

**1. Cut the plant back hard.** Once the first flush is done and the spikes look spent, **cut the whole plant back by 30–50%**, removing all faded flower spikes and any stems above the lowest healthy side shoots. This signals the plant to push fresh growth from the base instead of trying to fix exhausted upper stems.

**2. Stop the seed cycle.** Snapdragons that are allowed to **set seed** stop producing new buds — they have done their reproductive job for the season. Even a single seed pod left on a plant suppresses bloom. Deadhead religiously, and remove any pods that have already formed.

**3. Water deeply, less often.** Switch from frequent shallow watering to **deep weekly soaks** (or twice-weekly in containers). Deep water encourages roots to follow moisture down; shallow water keeps roots near a hot, drying surface.

**4. Mulch the soil surface.** A 5 cm layer of bark, straw, or composted leaf mould drops root-zone temperature by **3–6°C**, which is enough to keep gibberellin pathways functioning in marginal heat.

**5. Cut back on feeding.** High-nitrogen feeding during heat dormancy pushes leafy growth that the plant cannot support. **Skip feeds during the hot weeks**, and resume a light flowering feed only when nights cool below 18°C.

**6. Provide afternoon shade.** Move pots into morning sun + afternoon shade. In ground beds, a temporary shade cloth (30–40% shade) over the worst weeks of summer makes a measurable difference.

## Are some snapdragon varieties more heat-tolerant than others?

Yes, and this is often the cheapest fix for next season. Modern breeding has produced cultivars that hold up much better in heat than older garden classics.

**Better in heat:**

- **'Rocket' series** — tall cut-flower types bred for warm-summer regions; often flower into 30°C territory.
- **'Sonnet' series** — mid-height bedding cultivars selected for heat tolerance.
- **'Liberty Classic' series** — early-flowering, holds up in mid-summer in mild-summer regions.
- **'Madame Butterfly' series** — open-faced doubles; surprisingly tough in shoulder-season heat.

**Less tolerant of heat:**

- Many older 'Tahiti' and 'Floral Showers' dwarf series — bred for cool-season bedding and stop flowering early in warm summers.
- Most "rock garden" or alpine-type miniatures — built for cool, bright Mediterranean spring.

If you live in a climate where summers regularly hit 30°C, plant heat-tolerant series and time them to peak in **late spring** (so the first flush coincides with the warming weather) and **autumn** (after cool nights return).

## Will they flower again in autumn?

In most climates, yes — and the autumn flush is often **better than the spring one** if you treated the plant well during the heat.

The trigger is **night temperatures dropping below 18°C consistently**. Once that happens:

1. New flower spikes start forming within **2–3 weeks**.
2. The plant uses the carbohydrate reserves it built up while resting, so spikes are often sturdier.
3. Cooler weather extends each bloom's life by 30–50% compared to spring blooms.

To get the best autumn flush:

- Resume a **balanced flowering feed** at half strength once nights cool.
- Continue deep watering; autumn is often dry in temperate regions.
- Stake any tall regrowth — autumn winds knock down mid-height varieties more than spring ones.
- Continue deadheading right up to the first frost.

In zones where winter stays above about −5°C, perennial-leaning cultivars can survive into a second year and re-bloom the following spring earlier than seed-grown plants. In colder zones, treat them as annuals and start fresh from seed or plugs.

## How do potted and bedded snapdragons differ in summer?

The same plant in a pot and in the ground respond differently to heat, and the diagnosis can be different.

**Pots heat up faster.** A black plastic 25 cm pot in full sun can hit **40°C+ at the rootball** when air is only 30°C. That is enough to kill flower buds before they open. Mitigations: light-coloured pots, pot-in-pot insulation, mulched surface, and afternoon shade.

**Pots dry out faster.** A pot in summer often needs daily watering. Inconsistent watering during heat is one of the top causes of pot-grown snapdragons stalling.

**Pots run out of room.** A snapdragon that filled a 20 cm pot in spring is probably root-bound by July. Either pot up by **one size in early summer** or accept that the plant will stop flowering when the roots run out of room.

**Beds buffer temperature.** A snapdragon in 30 cm of moist mulched garden soil is far more tolerant of a heat spike than the same cultivar in a pot, because the root mass dampens temperature swings.

If your bedded snapdragons are still flowering in mid-summer but pot-grown ones have stalled, the problem is almost certainly **pot temperature, pot moisture, or pot size** — not the cultivar.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are snapdragons annuals or perennials?

Botanically, snapdragons are **short-lived perennials** in their native Mediterranean range, but in most gardens they are grown as **cool-season annuals** because they rarely survive a hot summer or a cold wet winter. In USDA zones 7–10 with mild summers, individual plants can persist for 2–3 years.

### How hot is too hot for snapdragons?

Bloom slows above about **24°C** and usually stops once daytime temperatures stay above **27°C** for a sustained week. A few modern heat-tolerant series (Rocket, Sonnet) push the ceiling closer to 30°C, but no snapdragon flowers reliably above about 32°C.

### Do snapdragons need to be cut back to keep blooming?

Yes. Snapdragons flower on **fresh upright spikes** that arise from the base or from upper leaf nodes after older spikes are removed. Cut spent spikes back to the lowest healthy side shoot. Without this cutback, the plant directs energy to seed pods and new flowering slows or stops.

### Why are my snapdragon leaves bleached and silvery in summer?

This is usually **spider mite damage**, which spikes in hot, dry conditions. Look at the underside of leaves with a magnifier; mites and fine webbing are clearly visible. Treat with a strong jet of water for several days in a row, then with insecticidal soap if needed. Mites can shut down flowering on their own, separate from heat.

### Should I pull snapdragons out in mid-summer and replant in autumn?

In hot-summer climates this is often the right call. Pulling out exhausted spring-flush plants in June, planting heat-loving annuals (zinnia, cosmos, marigold) for July–August, and then replanting fresh snapdragon plugs in early autumn for a strong autumn-into-winter flush gives more total bloom than nursing summer-stressed plants through the worst weeks.

### Can snapdragons survive winter?

In USDA zones 7–10 (and similar mild-winter climates) snapdragons often **overwinter** and re-flower the following spring earlier than spring-sown plants. In colder zones, autumn-planted snapdragons usually survive a mild winter under a thick straw mulch but die in hard freezes; treat them as annuals and resow from seed each spring.
