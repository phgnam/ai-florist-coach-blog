## Quick Answer

The single most common reason a healthy-looking peony plant refuses to flower is that the **crown is buried too deep** — peony eyes (buds) need to sit no more than 2–5 cm below the soil surface, or they sulk for years. The next most common reasons are an **immature plant** (peonies often skip their first one to three seasons), **not enough sun** (a peony needs 6+ hours of direct sun), and **competition** from tree roots. Most non-blooming peonies fix themselves once you correct the planting depth and wait a season.

## Is your peony just too young to bloom yet?

A peony grown from a one-year bare-root division usually skips flowering in its **first year**, produces one or two flowers in its **second year**, and only really gets into stride in its **third or fourth year**. Garden centres often sell peonies in 2-litre pots that are essentially first-year divisions — so the customer plants it, sees no flowers, and assumes something is wrong. Nothing is wrong. The plant is putting all its energy into roots.

If your peony is in its first or second season after planting and is producing healthy foliage, the simplest answer is: **wait one more year**.

## Did you plant the crown too deep?

This is the single most-corrected mistake in home peony beds. The crown of a peony — the woody mass with pink buds called "eyes" — must be planted with the eyes **2–5 cm below the soil surface, no deeper**. If the eyes sit 10 cm down, the plant grows perfectly happy leaves and roots but never gets the temperature signal it needs to set flower buds.

Symptoms of a too-deep peony:

- Healthy, vigorous foliage every year
- No flower buds at all, or buds that "blast" (form and then dry up)
- The plant has been in the ground for 3+ years and still does not flower

The fix is to lift the plant in autumn (October to early November in the northern hemisphere) and replant with the eyes 2–5 cm below the surface. The plant will sulk for one season, then return to normal flowering.

## Is your peony getting enough sun?

A peony needs **at least six hours of direct sunlight** per day to set flower buds reliably. A few cultivars (mostly the Itoh hybrids) tolerate part shade, but they still produce fewer flowers in shade than in full sun.

Common sun problems:

- A peony planted in front of a hedge that has grown taller over the years
- A new tree casting afternoon shade onto an established bed
- A peony moved to "tidy up" the border into a corner that gets only morning sun

If your peony has gone from flowering to non-flowering and the only thing that has changed is a nearby tree or shrub getting bigger, that is almost certainly the cause. Move the peony in autumn.

## Are nearby tree roots stealing water and nutrients?

Peonies dislike root competition. A peony planted within 2–3 metres of a mature tree (especially fast-growing trees like willow, sycamore, or birch) will lose the moisture and nutrient battle. The peony stays alive but never has the resources to flower.

The solution is the same: lift in autumn and move the peony to an open spot at least 2 metres from any mature tree.

## Did a late frost damage the buds?

If your peony **had** buds and they turned black or dropped without opening, the most likely culprit is a late frost. Peony buds are vulnerable when they reach pea-size (around 5–10 mm). A frost at that stage will brown the buds, and they will dry up rather than open.

This is a one-season problem — the plant will flower normally next year. To reduce risk in subsequent years, throw a horticultural fleece over the plant on any night where the forecast drops below 0°C in late spring.

## Are you cutting back the foliage too early in autumn?

Peonies build next year's flower buds during summer, using energy stored from this year's leaves. If you cut the foliage back in August or early September because it looks tatty, you cut off the plant's energy supply before next year's buds finish forming.

The rule: leave peony foliage alone until it turns yellow or brown on its own — usually mid to late autumn. Then cut it back to ground level.

## What about ants on the buds — are they the problem?

No. Ants on peony buds are eating the sweet sap that the buds produce naturally. They do **not** cause non-blooming, and you do not need to remove them. The "peonies need ants to open" myth is also false — peonies open perfectly well without ants. Just shake the stems gently before bringing flowers indoors.

## FAQs

### How many years does it take for a new peony to flower?

A bare-root division usually flowers lightly in year two and properly in year three or four. Garden-centre potted peonies are often first-year divisions, so they can be effectively one season behind what the label promises.

### Can I dig up and move a non-flowering peony?

Yes — autumn is the right time. Lift the whole crown, divide if necessary, and replant with eyes only 2–5 cm below the soil. Expect one transplant year of no flowers, then normal flowering resumes.

### Should I fertilise to make my peony flower?

Only modestly. A handful of low-nitrogen, high-potassium fertiliser (such as 5-10-10 or rose fertiliser) in early spring helps. Too much nitrogen produces lush leaves and no flowers — the same outcome as not feeding at all.

### My peony has buds but they go brown and dry up. What is happening?

This is "bud blast," usually caused by a late frost or by a sudden hot, dry spell while the buds are forming. It is a one-season problem. The plant will flower normally next year if conditions are right.

### Do peonies flower better if I cut the foliage back in summer?

No — the opposite. Foliage feeds next year's buds. Leave the leaves alone until they yellow naturally in autumn, then cut back to ground level.

### Can a peony stop flowering after years of doing fine?

Yes — usually because something has changed: a tree shading the spot more than before, soil that has compacted around the crown raising the effective depth, or a long drought during bud-setting. Identify what changed and correct it.
