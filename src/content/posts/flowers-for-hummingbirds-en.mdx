## Quick Answer

Hummingbirds prefer **tubular flowers** in **red, orange, and bright pink** with **high-sugar nectar**. The most reliable garden plants are salvia (especially _Salvia coccinea_ and _Salvia greggii_), bee balm (_Monarda_), trumpet vine, cardinal flower, fuchsia, columbine, and penstemon. Plant in clusters of 5+ of the same species so a hummingbird's brief visit is rewarded with multiple flowers, not one.

## Why are hummingbird flowers almost always red and tubular?

Co-evolution. Hummingbird flowers and hummingbirds shaped each other over roughly 30 million years.

**Tubular shape.** A hummingbird's bill is long, slender, and slightly curved. The bird's tongue extends another 1.5–2× past the bill tip. Flowers shaped like a deep narrow tube exclude bees and butterflies (whose mouthparts are too short or too thick to reach the nectar) and reserve the nectar for the bird that fits the lock.

**Red colour.** Hummingbirds see well into the red end of the spectrum. Bees do not — they see ultraviolet, blue, and green, but red appears black to them. A red flower is therefore highly visible to hummingbirds and almost invisible to bees. The flower advertises selectively to its preferred pollinator.

**No fragrance.** Hummingbirds have a poor sense of smell. Flowers that have evolved to attract them often have very little scent — they spend zero metabolic energy on something the bird can't detect. (Bee-pollinated flowers, by contrast, are heavily scented.)

**High sugar concentration.** Hummingbird-pollinated flowers typically produce **20–25% sucrose nectar** — about twice as concentrated as bee-pollinated flowers. Hummingbirds need the calorie density: their metabolism burns through their body weight in nectar every day.

**Production through the day.** Hummingbird flowers refill their nectar reservoirs over a few hours so that a bird can visit the same flower several times in a day. This pattern reinforces "this garden is a reliable food source" and brings birds back.

## What are the best garden flowers for hummingbirds?

Reliable hummingbird plants for a temperate-climate garden:

**Salvia (sage)** — the single most reliable hummingbird genus.
- *Salvia coccinea* (tropical sage) — annual or short-lived perennial in zone 8+. Continuous flowering. Bright red.
- *Salvia greggii* (autumn sage) — woody perennial in zone 7+. Long-flowering, drought-tolerant. Available in red, coral, hot pink, and white.
- *Salvia guaranitica* ('Black & Blue') — tall, blue-flowered, hummingbirds love it despite the unusual colour.
- *Salvia microphylla* and *Salvia × jamensis* — long flowering, mid-sized shrubs.

**Bee balm (Monarda)** — *M. didyma* especially. Tall (90–120cm), summer-flowering, intensely red. A single mature clump pulls in several hummingbirds simultaneously.

**Trumpet vine (Campsis radicans)** — vigorous (sometimes too vigorous) climbing vine, large orange-red trumpets, blooms all summer. Plant where root spread won't be a problem.

**Cardinal flower (Lobelia cardinalis)** — North American native, deep red, prefers wet feet. Spectacular in pond margins and rain gardens.

**Fuchsia** — the pendant flowers are essentially hummingbird-shaped. *Fuchsia magellanica* is hardy in zone 7+; tender fuchsias work in pots. Excellent for shaded garden areas.

**Columbine (Aquilegia)** — spring flower, ideal for early-arriving migrant hummingbirds. *A. canadensis* is the native red species; the elegant nodding spurs are essentially purpose-built for hummingbirds.

**Penstemon** — open tubular flowers in red, coral, pink, and purple. Drought-tolerant. *P. barbatus* and *P. eatonii* are particularly hummingbird-friendly.

**Honeysuckle (Lonicera sempervirens)** — coral honeysuckle, native to the Americas, tubular orange-red flowers in early summer. Twining vine; needs a trellis or fence.

**Bottlebrush (Callistemon)** — for warm-temperate gardens (zone 9+). Almost exclusively hummingbird-pollinated in cultivation.

**Hibiscus and tropical flowers** in warm-climate gardens — see our [tropical flowers guide](/posts/tropical-flowers-guide-en/) for the wider tropical-pollinator picture.

## How should I plant hummingbird flowers for the best result?

Three layout principles that produce dramatically better results than scattering hummingbird flowers individually:

**Plant in clusters of 5+ of the same species.** A hummingbird burns roughly 7–10 calories per gram of body weight per minute while flying. A flower that produces a tiny amount of nectar isolated from any other rewarding flower is, calorically, a net loss for the bird. Five plants of the same species in a 1–2 metre cluster is energy-positive — the bird visits the cluster, drinks from 4–6 flowers in 30 seconds, and the visit pays for itself.

**Stagger flowering windows for season-long supply.** Hummingbirds in temperate North America can be in residence from late April to early October — six months. A garden with one wave of bee balm in July goes silent in August. Plant for a continuous succession: columbine in May, cardinal flower in June, bee balm in July, salvia in August–October.

**Place flowers near vertical structure.** Hummingbirds prefer to feed near a perch where they can rest and watch for rivals (they are aggressively territorial). A salvia bed at the back of an open lawn gets fewer visits than the same bed planted next to a tall shrub or a pergola.

A mature hummingbird-friendly garden can host 5–15 individual birds across the season. Once one bird discovers a reliable food source, others follow.

## Do hummingbird feeders work as well as flowers?

Sugar-water feeders work, but they are a supplement to flowers, not a replacement.

**What feeders do well:** provide a reliable backup during dry periods when flowers underproduce, and give a fixed observation point so you can watch the birds.

**What feeders do badly:** **provide complete nutrition.** Hummingbirds get protein from tiny insects they catch from leaves and from spiderwebs. A garden of feeders with no flowers and no insect-supporting plants is a malnutrition garden — adult birds may visit, but very few young birds will be raised in it.

**The standard sugar-water recipe:** **1 part white sugar to 4 parts water**, no dye, no honey, no brown sugar, no artificial sweeteners. Boil briefly to dissolve, cool, and refill clean feeders every 3–5 days (every 1–2 days in hot weather — sugar water ferments fast and fermented sugar water is dangerous to the birds).

The optimal hummingbird garden is roughly **80% flowers / 20% feeder support**, not the other way round.

## What flowers do NOT attract hummingbirds, even when they look like they should?

A handful of plants are commonly assumed to be hummingbird flowers but underperform:

- **Most modern hybrid roses.** Roses produce almost no nectar at all and rarely have the tubular shape hummingbirds prefer. Bees and beetles are the rose pollinators, not birds.
- **Daisies, sunflowers, and other composite flowers** (Asteraceae). The visible "flower" is actually hundreds of tiny disc florets clustered together — the wrong shape for a hummingbird's bill.
- **Most lavender and most rosemary.** Bee plants. Hummingbirds will sometimes visit but they are not the target audience.
- **Hostas.** Some hummingbirds will visit pale lavender hosta flowers, but the calorie return is too low to be a reliable draw.
- **Petunias** in standard supermarket varieties. Many modern petunias have been bred for compact form and bedding-plant uniformity; the species-level petunias and a few heritage cultivars are still hummingbird-friendly, but the average garden-centre tray rarely is.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do hummingbirds only visit red flowers?**
No. Red is the strongest signal, but hummingbirds visit orange, hot pink, magenta, and even some blue and purple flowers (especially *Salvia guaranitica* 'Black & Blue'). The flower must still be tubular and produce nectar. Hummingbirds learn to associate any tubular nectar-rich flower with food regardless of colour.

**Will planting flowers attract hummingbirds to my garden?**
Yes, in any region where hummingbirds are native. North America has 15+ species (mostly western and tropical, with the ruby-throated hummingbird the only common eastern resident). Mexico, Central America, and South America host roughly 360 species. Europe, Africa, Asia, and Australia have **no hummingbirds**; Old World ecological equivalents are sunbirds (Africa, Asia) and honeyeaters (Australia), which are attracted by similar flowers.

**When do hummingbirds arrive in the garden each year?**
In temperate North America, ruby-throated hummingbirds arrive between mid-April and early May depending on latitude. Western species arrive earlier in the southern parts of their range. Tropical species (Anna's, Allen's) are year-round residents along the US west coast.

**Are hummingbird feeders safe for the birds?**
Yes when properly maintained. Use plain white sugar (4:1 water:sugar by volume), never honey or brown sugar. Clean feeders every 3–5 days, more often in hot weather. Avoid red food dye — it serves no purpose and there is suggestive evidence it may be harmful to the birds at long-term consumption.

**Will hummingbird flowers attract bees too?**
Tubular hummingbird flowers exclude most bees by design — bees can't reach the nectar. However, the same garden bed can pull in butterflies (especially monarch and swallowtail species) which use a similar but slightly shorter nectar tube. Most well-designed hummingbird gardens are also good butterfly gardens.

**Can hummingbirds find flowers planted in a small balcony or container garden?**
Yes — hummingbirds are remarkably good at finding small clusters of suitable flowers. A pot of 3 fuchsia plants and a pot of 5 *Salvia greggii* on a city balcony in the right region will reliably attract migrating hummingbirds. The key is the flower choice, not the garden size.
