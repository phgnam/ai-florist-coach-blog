## Quick Answer

When petunia leaves turn yellow but the veins stay bright green, you are almost certainly seeing iron chlorosis — iron is in the compost but the plant cannot absorb it. This happens in containers when the compost pH drifts above 6.5, when phosphate is too high, when watering with hard tap water, or when waterlogging stresses the roots. To fix it, repot or top-dress with fresh ericaceous compost, water with a chelated iron product (Sequestrene or Fe-EDDHA), switch to rainwater, and check drainage. Improvement is visible within 7–10 days.

## What does iron deficiency look like in petunias?

Petunias (especially modern hybrids like 'Surfinia', 'Wave', and 'Supertunia') are notorious for showing iron deficiency in containers. The diagnostic pattern is very specific:

**Interveinal chlorosis.** The leaves turn pale yellow or even almost white, but the veins themselves stay bright green. Holding a leaf up to the light, you see a green skeleton of veins running through a yellow leaf blade.

**Newest leaves first.** Iron is an immobile nutrient — once incorporated into older leaves, it cannot be remobilised by the plant. Deficiency therefore shows on the youngest, top growth first. If your bottom leaves are yellow and the top leaves are green, the cause is something else (often nitrogen or magnesium).

**Stunted new growth.** New shoots are pale, small, and grow slowly. In severe cases the growing tips abort entirely.

**Reduced flowering.** Iron is essential for chlorophyll synthesis, so deficient plants photosynthesise poorly. Flower production drops to a trickle even though buds may form.

**No leaf spots or burn.** Unlike fungal disease, iron chlorosis is a clean, even yellowing. If you see brown patches, leaf-edge scorch, or spots, look for additional problems.

## Why is iron deficiency so common in container petunias?

Iron is rarely actually deficient in compost — what's deficient is the plant's *ability to absorb the iron that's there*. Several factors that pile up specifically in containers cause "iron lockout".

**High pH (alkaline compost).** Iron is most available to roots at pH 5.5–6.5. Above pH 6.5, iron forms insoluble compounds the roots cannot absorb. Many multipurpose composts start at pH 6.5–7.0 and drift higher as carbonates from tap water accumulate.

**Hard tap water.** Tap water in chalk and limestone regions contains high concentrations of calcium carbonate. Each watering deposits more calcium in the compost, raising pH and locking out iron.

**Phosphate excess.** "Bloom booster" feeds heavy in phosphate (the middle number on a fertiliser label) react with iron in the soil to form insoluble iron phosphate. Counterintuitively, generous feeding with high-P fertiliser can *cause* iron deficiency.

**Cold, waterlogged roots.** Roots absorb iron actively (it requires energy). Cold, oxygen-starved roots in waterlogged compost struggle to absorb iron even when soluble.

**Containers worsen everything.** A container has a small soil volume, so any chemistry imbalance amplifies fast. Open ground rarely shows iron deficiency in petunias even with hard water, because the soil volume buffers the pH.

## How do I fix iron deficiency once it has started?

A four-step rescue plan works in nearly all cases.

### Step 1: Check the obvious causes

Test compost pH with a cheap soil pH probe. Anything above 6.8 confirms the diagnosis. Check drainage by lifting the pot — heavy waterlogged compost is part of the problem.

### Step 2: Apply chelated iron

A chelated iron supplement is the fastest fix. Two options:

- **Sequestrene (Fe-EDTA):** Cheap and effective at pH below 6.5. Loses effectiveness in alkaline conditions.
- **Fe-EDDHA:** More expensive but works across a wide pH range, including alkaline conditions. The premium choice for hard-water gardens.

Mix at the rate on the label and water in. Greening of new leaves is visible within 7–10 days.

### Step 3: Acidify the compost

If pH is the underlying cause:

- **Top-dress with ericaceous compost.** Remove the top 2 cm of old compost and replace with fresh ericaceous compost. Repeat every 4–6 weeks during the growing season.
- **Water with a sulphate-based feed.** Liquid feeds containing ammonium sulphate or iron sulphate gradually acidify compost.
- **Switch to rainwater.** Rainwater is naturally slightly acidic and contains very little calcium. The single biggest long-term fix in hard-water regions.
- **Use vinegar in extreme cases.** 1 tablespoon white vinegar per 4 litres of water, applied once. Measure pH afterwards — do not overdo it.

### Step 4: Adjust feeding

Switch from a "bloom booster" to a balanced or high-nitrogen liquid feed. Modern petunia feeds are formulated to avoid the iron-lockout problem; look for "petunia feed" or "tomato feed without iron".

## How do I prevent iron deficiency from starting in the first place?

Prevention is far easier than treatment. A handful of practices in spring container preparation eliminates the problem.

1. **Use ericaceous compost or mix 30% ericaceous into your multipurpose.** Lower starting pH means more headroom before iron lockout begins.
2. **Switch to rainwater for petunias.** Even a small water butt saves enough rainwater for daily petunia watering through summer.
3. **Choose a slow-release fertiliser with iron.** Look for products marketed as "for petunias" or "for hanging baskets" — these include chelated iron in the formula.
4. **Avoid bone meal and "bloom booster" feeds.** Both elevate phosphate and trigger iron lockout.
5. **Ensure free drainage.** Use pots with adequate drainage holes and elevate them on pot feet so excess water escapes.
6. **Avoid overhead watering on hot days.** Salt residues accumulate fast when foliage is repeatedly wetted.
7. **Refresh compost annually.** Even with perfect care, container compost chemistry drifts over a season; start fresh next year.

## Which petunia varieties are most susceptible to iron deficiency?

Susceptibility varies dramatically between cultivar groups.

| Variety | Iron sensitivity | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Trailing Surfinia / Wave / Supertunia | Very high | Vegetatively propagated cascading types are the most prone. |
| Grandiflora (large flowered) | High | Frilly large flowers; fast growth makes deficiency visible early. |
| Multiflora | Moderate | Smaller flowers, more flowers; bedding plants. |
| Floribunda | Moderate | Mid-size; somewhat tougher than grandiflora. |
| Milliflora ('Picobella' etc.) | Low | Tiny tough flowers; least susceptible. |
| Calibrachoa (Million Bells) | Very high | Often shows iron deficiency before petunias do. Same fixes work. |

The cascading "Surfinia" and "Wave" types — by far the most popular today — are also the most demanding. If you struggle with iron deficiency every summer, switching to a Multiflora or Milliflora type often resolves the problem entirely.

## Are petunias safe for households with cats and dogs?

Petunias are generally regarded as low-toxicity for cats and dogs and are not on the ASPCA toxic plants list. Some pets may develop mild gastrointestinal upset if they eat large quantities, but petunias are not considered seriously poisonous. They are also not on the daffodil/lily/hyacinth list of bulb-related toxicities — there are no bulb compounds at all.

That said, the iron sulphate or chelated iron products you might use to fix deficiency *can* be harmful if a pet drinks the liquid feed solution. Apply iron treatments at the base of the plant, mix into the compost not the patio surface, and store the bottle out of reach.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My petunia leaves are yellow but the bottom leaves first, not the top. Is that still iron deficiency?**
No — that is more likely nitrogen deficiency, magnesium deficiency, or natural ageing. Nitrogen and magnesium are mobile; the plant pulls them out of older leaves to support new growth, so deficiency shows from the bottom up. Iron is immobile and shows on top growth first.

**I added Epsom salts but the petunias still look yellow. Why?**
Epsom salts (magnesium sulphate) corrects magnesium deficiency, not iron deficiency. The two look similar but appear on different leaf positions. Magnesium deficiency tends to be on older leaves with green veins, brown edges, sometimes purple tints. Iron deficiency is on new leaves. Diagnose first; supplement second.

**Can I use ordinary garden iron sulphate (lawn moss killer) on my petunias?**
Yes — but very carefully. Iron sulphate at lawn rates will burn petunias. Dilute to about 1 g per litre of water and apply once. Better: use a product labelled for ornamentals or buy chelated iron, which is gentler.

**How long does it take for chelated iron to green up the leaves?**
The newest unfurling leaves emerge green within 7–10 days. Existing yellowed leaves do *not* re-green — iron is locked into them and cannot be remobilised. Tolerate the existing yellow leaves for now; new growth will mask them within a fortnight.

**Should I throw out old hanging-basket compost or can I reuse it next year?**
Don't reuse for petunias. Old compost is exhausted of nutrients, has often risen in pH, and may carry pathogen inoculum. Tip onto the compost heap or use as soil-improver in the open vegetable garden where pH and chemistry drift do not matter as much.
