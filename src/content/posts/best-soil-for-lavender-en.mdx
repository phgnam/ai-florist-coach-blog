Lavender thrives in **alkaline (pH 6.5–8.0), fast-draining, low-fertility soil** that mimics its Mediterranean origins. Rich, moisture-retentive garden soil is lavender's biggest killer — root rot from wet feet causes more plant losses than cold, pests, or disease combined. Get the soil right and lavender practically takes care of itself.

## Why does lavender need such poor soil?

Lavender evolved on the rocky limestone hillsides of the Mediterranean — thin, gravelly soil baked by sun, with minimal organic matter and excellent drainage. The plant's silvery, aromatic foliage is an adaptation to drought, heat, and nutrient scarcity.

When you plant lavender in rich, well-amended garden soil (the kind that makes tomatoes and roses happy), two things go wrong:

1. **Excess moisture** around roots promotes fungal root rot (*Phytophthora*, *Pythium*) — the number one cause of lavender death
2. **High nitrogen** drives soft, floppy green growth at the expense of the compact, bushy habit and essential oil production that makes lavender beautiful and fragrant

The counterintuitive truth: **starving lavender makes it better**. Less water, less fertiliser, and leaner soil produce denser plants, more intense fragrance, and longer life.

## What is the ideal soil mix for lavender in the ground?

If your native soil is heavy clay or rich loam, amend the planting area:

1. **Dig a hole twice the root ball width** and mix the excavated soil 50/50 with coarse sand or pea gravel
2. **Add a handful of horticultural lime** if your soil pH is below 6.5 (lavender prefers neutral to alkaline)
3. **Raise the planting bed** 15–20 cm above the surrounding soil level to ensure water drains away from roots
4. **Do not add compost** to the planting hole — this is counterintuitive for most gardeners, but lavender doesn't want rich soil
5. **Mulch with gravel or stone**, not bark or organic mulch. Organic mulch holds moisture against the stem crown and promotes rot

For naturally sandy or gravelly soils, you probably don't need to amend at all — just check pH and add lime if it's acidic.

## What potting mix works best for container lavender?

Container growing is excellent for lavender because you control the soil completely. A proven recipe:

- **40% standard potting mix** (provides structure)
- **30% perlite or coarse sand** (ensures fast drainage)
- **20% small gravel** (weight stability + drainage)
- **10% horticultural lime or crusite** (raises pH)

**Critical**: Use a **terracotta pot** rather than plastic. Terracotta is porous and allows the soil to dry faster between waterings. Plastic retains moisture — exactly what lavender doesn't want.

Pot size: **30–40 cm** diameter minimum. Lavender has a deep root system relative to its size and needs room. Use a pot with at least one large drainage hole (ideally several).

## How do you test and adjust soil pH for lavender?

Lavender grows best at **pH 6.5–8.0**. Most garden soils in temperate regions are naturally slightly acidic (pH 5.5–6.5), so some adjustment is often needed.

**Testing**: Use an inexpensive soil pH meter or litmus test kit from any garden centre. Take readings from several spots in the planting area.

**Raising pH (making soil more alkaline)**:
- **Garden lime** (calcium carbonate): 200–400g per square metre, worked into the top 15 cm. Effects take 2–3 months.
- **Dolomite lime**: Similar to garden lime but also adds magnesium. Good for magnesium-deficient soils.
- **Wood ash**: A lighter touch — sprinkle a thin layer around plants annually. Slightly raises pH and adds potassium.

**If your soil is already alkaline** (pH 7.0–8.0): Perfect — plant directly without amendment.

## What common soil mistakes kill lavender?

**Over-watering**: Lavender needs water to establish (first 2–3 months), then prefers dry conditions. Established plants need watering only during extended drought. Feel the soil 5 cm down — if it's damp, don't water.

**Organic mulch**: Bark, straw, and leaf mulch hold moisture against the stem crown, creating ideal conditions for crown rot. Use **gravel mulch** instead.

**Over-fertilising**: Lavender needs almost no fertiliser. A light top-dressing of bone meal or a handful of slow-release fertiliser in spring is plenty. Never use high-nitrogen feeds.

**Planting too deep**: Set the crown (where stems meet roots) at or slightly above soil level. Burying it invites rot.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can lavender grow in clay soil?**
Not without significant amendment. Heavy clay retains water for too long. If you have clay soil, build a raised bed 20+ cm high filled with sandy mix, or grow lavender in containers instead.

**Does lavender like coffee grounds?**
No. Coffee grounds are acidic (pH 4.5–5.5) and nitrogen-rich — the opposite of what lavender wants. Use them on acid-loving plants like blueberries or azaleas instead.

**How often should you replace soil in lavender pots?**
Repot every 2–3 years with fresh, fast-draining mix. Potting soil breaks down over time, becoming denser and less free-draining. Spring (March–April) is the best time to repot.
