## Quick Answer

White powdery spots on begonia leaves are almost always powdery mildew, a fungal disease that thrives in still humid air. Confirm it by gently rubbing — true powdery mildew wipes off as a fine white dust, leaving a faint stain. Stop the spread by removing the worst leaves, spraying the rest with a 1:9 milk-water mix every 5 days, increasing airflow and light, and reducing leaf-on-leaf contact. Most plants recover within 3 weeks.

## How do you confirm it's powdery mildew and not something else?

Several conditions cause white markings on begonia leaves. Powdery mildew is the most common, but it has lookalikes that need different treatment. The diagnostic test is simple: rub a clean fingertip across one of the white patches.

| What you see | What it is | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Wipes off as fine dust, leaves faint stain | Powdery mildew | Milk-water spray + airflow |
| Doesn't wipe off, raised white spots | Mealybugs | Manual removal + soapy spray |
| Doesn't wipe off, white "salt-like" crust | Mineral salt deposit | Flush soil, switch water source |
| Silvery-white markings on leaf surface | Natural variegation | None needed |
| White fuzzy mould at soil line | Saprophytic mould (harmless) | Improve drainage |

If a fingertip rub leaves a faint dust on the finger and the leaf surface clears slightly, it is powdery mildew. If the white doesn't move, examine more closely under good light — mealybugs look like tiny cotton balls; mineral salts look crystalline.

Powdery mildew on begonia is most often caused by *Erysiphe orontii* or *Oidium begoniae*. Both species infect leaves through stomata, spread by airborne spores, and thrive at humidity above 60 % with still air and moderate temperatures (15–25 °C). The fungus rarely kills the plant outright but distorts new growth, causes leaves to drop, and ruins flowering displays if left unchecked.

## What's the most effective spray for powdery mildew on begonias?

For light to moderate infections, the well-tested home remedy is a 1:9 milk-water spray. Cow's milk (any fat content; full-fat works as well as skimmed) contains lactoferrin and other proteins that activate in sunlight to produce free radicals that kill fungal hyphae on the leaf surface.

The recipe and procedure:

1. **Mix** 1 part milk with 9 parts water (e.g. 100 ml milk to 900 ml water).
2. **Add a drop of dish soap** as a wetting agent — about 0.5 ml per litre.
3. **Decant into a fine-mist spray bottle.**
4. **Spray in early morning** on a dry day, both sides of every leaf, until just running.
5. **Allow to dry in sunlight** — the activation happens during drying.
6. **Repeat every 5 days for 3 weeks** during the worst of the infection.
7. **Continue weekly preventive spray** after visible mildew is gone, especially in humid weather.

The milk spray works for two reasons. First, the lactic acid creates a slightly hostile pH on the leaf surface. Second, the milk proteins activated by sunlight produce reactive oxygen species lethal to fungal cells. The combined effect is a 70–90 % reduction in mildew on susceptible plants compared with no treatment, and roughly equivalent to commercial sulphur-based fungicides without the toxicity concerns.

For commercial options if the milk spray is insufficient:

- **Potassium bicarbonate** (sold as "MilStop" or similar): 5 g per litre water, spray weekly.
- **Sulphur-based fungicides**: effective but not safe to use above 25 °C as they damage the plant.
- **Bicarbonate of soda** (1 teaspoon per litre + a drop of soap): works similarly to potassium bicarbonate but is harder on the leaf surface.

Avoid: copper-based fungicides (cause leaf burn on begonias), high-strength sulphur (damages leaves above 25 °C), or any fungicide labelled for vegetables only without "begonia" or "ornamentals" on the label.

## Why does powdery mildew keep coming back on begonias?

Even after a successful treatment round, mildew often returns within 3–4 weeks. The reason is almost always the underlying conditions, not insufficient spraying. Three factors determine whether powdery mildew can re-establish:

**1. Stagnant humid air.** Powdery mildew germinates at 60 %+ humidity with no air movement. Begonias indoors often sit in pockets of still air — a corner shelf, a dense plant cluster, a bathroom. Airflow is the single most effective preventive.

**2. Leaf-to-leaf contact.** Spores spread when wet or damp leaves touch. A plant with leaves crowding into neighbours has many more transfer points than an isolated plant. For prized specimens, separate by 20+ cm from other plants.

**3. Inconsistent watering.** Begonia roots that swing between dry and wet stress the plant; stressed plants are easier targets for mildew. Steady, even watering — letting the top 2 cm dry between waterings — produces a more resistant plant.

Indoor plants:

- **Move to a bright, well-ventilated spot.** Near (not above) a vent, a window that opens, or a fan-cooled room.
- **Use a small fan** on low for 4–6 hours a day to keep air moving.
- **Reduce humidity** from 70 %+ to 40–55 % if you have a hygrometer.
- **Space plants apart** — 30 cm minimum between begonias and any other plant.

Outdoor plants:

- **Plant in part sun** with morning sun and afternoon shade — full shade is mildew's preferred habitat.
- **Avoid overhead watering**; water at the base.
- **Thin overcrowded plantings.**
- **Mulch the soil surface** to reduce humidity rebound from wet ground.

## Which begonia types are most vulnerable, and which resist?

Resistance varies dramatically by begonia group. Some types tolerate moderate mildew pressure with no treatment; others succumb to mild humidity within weeks.

**High susceptibility:**

- **Wax begonias** (*B. semperflorens*) — particularly susceptible.
- **Cane-stemmed (angel-wing) begonias** — large leaf surface area, prone in humid air.
- **Tuberous begonias** — develop mildew in cool damp summers.

**Moderate susceptibility:**

- **Rex begonias** — variable, depending on cultivar and conditions.
- **Begonia 'Dragon Wing'** — moderately resistant compared with traditional wax begonias.

**Lower susceptibility:**

- **Rhizomatous begonias** (eyelash, beefsteak) — more resistant due to thicker waxy cuticle.
- **Begonia 'BabyWing'** and modern F1 hybrids — bred specifically for mildew resistance.

If you have repeated mildew problems despite good treatment, switching to a more resistant cultivar in the same pot solves the recurring issue. 'BabyWing' and 'Dragon Wing' both perform well in conditions where traditional wax begonias collapse.

For tuberous begonias specifically, growing in part sun with airflow rather than full shade reduces mildew pressure considerably without affecting bloom.

## Should you remove and discard mildewed leaves?

Yes, especially for the worst-affected leaves. Removing affected tissue reduces the spore load and gives the rest of the plant a head start on recovery. The cut goes back to the base of the leaf petiole, not partway up the stem.

The procedure:

1. **Wear disposable gloves** to avoid spreading spores on hands.
2. **Cut affected leaves** with sharp snips at the base of the petiole.
3. **Bag the leaves immediately** — do not let them touch other plants or surfaces.
4. **Dispose in the bin**, not the compost. Powdery mildew spores survive composting.
5. **Wipe snip blades with isopropyl alcohol** before working on the next plant.

How much to remove:

- **Mild infection** (<10 % of leaves affected): remove the worst 5–10 leaves only.
- **Moderate infection** (10–30 % affected): remove all visibly affected leaves; the plant should still have 50 %+ healthy foliage.
- **Severe infection** (>50 % affected): cut the plant back hard to 10 cm of bare stem; treat with milk spray on the new growth as it emerges.

Plants cut back hard often emerge stronger than before, with denser, more compact growth and renewed mildew resistance. The trade-off is a flowering pause of 4–6 weeks while new growth establishes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use baking soda instead of milk?**
Yes, with care. 1 teaspoon baking soda + 1 litre water + a drop of soap is a standard alternative. It can be slightly harsher on leaves than milk; spot-test on one leaf and check after 24 hours.

**Will mildew kill my begonia?**
Rarely. Mildew weakens the plant, distorts new growth, and reduces flowering, but most begonias recover with treatment. Only severe untreated infections over many months kill the plant.

**Is powdery mildew on a begonia contagious to other plants?**
Yes, but only to plants susceptible to powdery mildew species — other begonias, roses, zinnias, cucurbits. It will not jump to a fern or palm.

**Can I prevent mildew with a humidity tray?**
A water-filled tray increases humidity, which actually favours mildew. Stop using humidity trays around begonias if mildew is a recurring issue.

**Should I move an outdoor mildewed begonia indoors?**
No. Indoor air is usually still and warm — the worst possible conditions. Treat in place outdoors with airflow and the milk spray.
