## Quick Answer

The best flowers for an east-facing balcony are those that prefer 3–5 hours of gentle morning sun followed by bright afternoon shade. Reliable picks include fuchsia, begonia (semperflorens and tuberous), impatiens, lobelia, viola, primula, hardy geranium (cranesbill), heuchera, and astilbe. Avoid sun-lovers like petunia, lavender, and most pelargoniums — they stretch and bloom poorly without afternoon sun.

## How much light does an east-facing balcony actually get?

An east-facing balcony in the Northern Hemisphere typically receives direct sun from sunrise until late morning or early afternoon, then bright but indirect light for the rest of the day. In summer, that translates to roughly 4–6 hours of usable direct sun; in winter, often less than 2 hours. The light is also gentler in temperature than west-facing afternoon sun, because the air has not yet warmed up.

This pattern matters more than the total hour count. Flowers that need full sun (defined as 6+ hours of direct light, often with afternoon heat) will not thrive. Flowers that need part shade (3–6 hours of direct light, ideally cooler hours) will. Most flower lists are written for "full sun" or "part shade" without distinguishing morning sun from afternoon sun, which is why some "part shade" recommendations still fail on an east balcony.

A simple field test: stand on the balcony at noon in July and again at 3 pm. If your hand casts a sharp shadow at noon but only a soft, blurred shadow at 3 pm, you have a classic east-facing situation. The flowers below are picked for that exact light curve.

## Which flowers love morning sun and afternoon shade?

These five plants are reliable east-facing performers because they want exactly the morning-on, afternoon-off light pattern.

**Fuchsia (hardy and tender hybrids).** Fuchsias bloom from June to first frost on an east balcony and dislike the afternoon scorch of west-facing sites. Choose trailing types for hanging baskets and upright bush types for pots. Water consistently — fuchsias drop buds when soil dries between waterings.

**Begonia (semperflorens and tuberous).** Wax begonias (semperflorens) flower non-stop in part shade and shrug off both rain and gentle sun. Tuberous begonias produce larger, almost rose-like flowers and prefer slightly brighter morning light. Both are excellent in window boxes and pots.

**Impatiens.** Once described as "the workhorse of part shade," modern downy-mildew-resistant cultivars (Beacon, Imara) bloom from late spring to autumn on an east balcony with weekly feeding.

**Lobelia.** Trailing varieties (Lobelia erinus 'Sapphire', 'Crystal Palace') cascade out of a pot in sky-blue or violet, perfect to soften the front edge of an east-balcony display.

**Viola and pansy (cool-season).** From autumn to late spring, violas and pansies are the workhorses of an east balcony, when summer-flowering plants are dormant. Switch them out for impatiens or begonias once nights stay above 12 °C.

## What perennials are reliable on an east-facing balcony?

Perennials cost more upfront but return year after year. These five thrive in pots with morning-only sun.

**Hardy geranium (cranesbill).** True *Geranium* species like 'Rozanne' and *G. macrorrhizum* flower for months in part shade and tolerate root competition in shared pots. Do not confuse with pelargoniums (often called "geraniums"), which need full sun.

**Heuchera.** Coral bells are grown as much for their burgundy, lime, or silver leaves as for their wiry flower spikes. Foliage looks best in morning sun and burns in afternoon sun, making them ideal east-facing perennials.

**Astilbe.** Soft, feathery plumes in pink, white, or red bloom for several weeks in early summer. Astilbes need consistent moisture; they hate drying out, which is the main reason they fail in pots — water deeply and mulch the surface.

**Hosta.** Grown for foliage but most cultivars produce lavender or white flower spikes in midsummer. East-facing balconies suit blue-green hostas particularly well; afternoon sun bleaches the wax that creates the blue colour.

**Hellebore (Lenten rose).** Winter-into-spring flowers in white, pink, deep purple, and green appear when nothing else is blooming. Hellebores tolerate root crowding in a 30 cm pot for several years.

## What annuals give the most colour for the longest season?

For a single-season splash, pair these annuals with the perennials above. They flower from late spring to first frost on an east balcony with weekly liquid feeding.

| Annual | Habit | Bloom window | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wax begonia | Mound, 25–30 cm | Late May to Oct | Self-cleaning; no deadheading needed |
| Tuberous begonia | Upright or trailing | June to Oct | Lift tubers in autumn for next year |
| Trailing fuchsia | Cascade, 30–60 cm | June to Oct | Pinch out in spring for bushiness |
| Trailing lobelia | Cascade, 20 cm | May to Sep | Trim hard mid-summer to refresh |
| Impatiens | Mound, 20–30 cm | June to Oct | Choose downy-mildew-resistant cultivars |
| Heliotrope | Upright, 30–40 cm | June to Oct | Vanilla scent, lovely near a sitting area |
| Bacopa | Cascade, 20 cm | May to Oct | White, blue, or pink mat of small flowers |

## What should you avoid on an east-facing balcony?

These plants regularly disappoint because they need afternoon sun to flower well.

- **Petunia and calibrachoa** — bloom thinly without 6+ hours of direct sun.
- **Pelargonium ("zonal geranium")** — stretches, flowers sparsely, prone to mildew in lower light.
- **Lavender** — needs full sun and dry roots; gets leggy and blooms poorly in part shade.
- **Sun-loving herbs** like rosemary, thyme, and oregano — they survive but produce less flavour and few flowers.
- **Sunflower, zinnia, cosmos** — these full-sun annuals look good for a few weeks then collapse without sun. The [cosmos no flowers](/posts/cosmos-no-flowers-en/) post explains the same diagnosis applies in low-light pots.
- **Hibiscus and bougainvillea** — tropical sun-lovers that simply will not bloom in 4–5 hours of morning light.

If you must have one of these, place its pot at the very edge of the balcony where it can catch the most direct morning light, and accept a thinner display than the same plant would give on a south or west balcony.

## How do you set up the watering for an east balcony?

East-facing balconies dry less aggressively than west-facing ones because the hottest hours are shaded. That changes watering habits in two useful ways.

**Less daily water than west-facing.** A medium pot on a west balcony might need water twice a day in July; on an east balcony, once a day is usually enough, sometimes once every two days for larger containers.

**More risk of overwatering.** Because the soil cools faster in afternoon shade, evaporation slows and roots can sit in damp compost for longer. Add 20 % perlite or grit to the compost when planting, and always let the top 2 cm dry between waterings except in true heatwaves.

A simple finger-test rule beats any timer. Push your finger 2–3 cm into the compost. If it feels cool and damp, wait. If it feels dry and crumbly, water until the pot drains. East-balcony plants almost always do better on this rhythm than on a fixed daily schedule.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I grow tomatoes or strawberries on an east-facing balcony?**
Strawberries crop well in 4–5 hours of morning sun. Tomatoes are borderline — small cherry varieties can produce fruit, but the harvest will be lighter than on a south or west balcony.

**Will succulents flower on an east balcony?**
Some will (sedum, sempervivum) but most popular flowering succulents (echeveria in bloom, kalanchoe) need more sun than morning-only provides. They survive on an east balcony but flower less.

**Is east-facing or west-facing better for balcony flowers?**
Different. East-facing favours soft-petalled, cool-loving flowers; west-facing favours sun-lovers and Mediterranean species. There is no overall winner — match plants to aspect.

**Should I move flowers to a sunnier spot on weekends?**
No, plants do not adjust well to sudden light changes. Pick species that match your balcony's actual light pattern instead.

**How do I extend the season on an east balcony?**
Plant winter-flowering hellebores and violas, switch to spring bulbs in autumn, then change over to summer annuals once nights stay above 12 °C. The [flowers in season monthly](/posts/flowers-in-season-monthly-en/) guide has a year-round rotation that adapts cleanly to east-facing pots.
