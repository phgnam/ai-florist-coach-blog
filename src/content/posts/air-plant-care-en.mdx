## Quick Answer

Air plants (Tillandsia) need **weekly soaking in room-temperature water for 20–30 minutes**, **bright indirect light**, and **good airflow** to dry between waterings. They don't live on air alone — that's a marketing myth. Each plant flowers **once in its life**, then slowly dies over 1–2 years while producing 2–6 "pups" (offsets) that grow into the next generation. Keep them away from direct sun, never let them sit in standing water, and feed monthly with a half-strength orchid fertiliser during the growing season.

## What is an air plant, really?

Tillandsia is a genus of about 650 species in the bromeliad family, native to the Americas — from the southern United States through Mexico, Central America, and most of South America. In the wild they grow as **epiphytes**: plants that perch on other plants (mostly trees) without taking nutrients from them.

Crucially, they take water and nutrients **through their leaves, not their roots**. The little roots they do produce are anchors, not feeding organs.

This means:

- They don't need soil
- They do need regular wetting
- They don't need (or want) to be planted in pots like normal houseplants
- Their roots can be glued, wired, or wedged into a holder without harming them

## How often do I water an air plant?

The standard schedule is one of:

- **Weekly soak**: submerge in room-temperature water for 20–30 minutes once a week
- **Heavy mist**: spray thoroughly 2–3 times per week in addition to a monthly soak
- **Climate-adjusted**: more frequent in dry summer rooms, less frequent in damp winter conditions

**Soaking is more reliable than misting** for most homes. After soaking, shake the plant gently to remove water from between the leaves and lay it upside down on a towel for an hour or two to drain. Then return it to its display.

Critical: **do not let water sit trapped between the leaves for more than a few hours**. Trapped water is the single most common killer of air plants. The plant rots from the centre outward and there is no recovery once it starts.

The clearest sign of underwatering is **leaves curling tightly inward and becoming crispy at the tips**. The clearest sign of overwatering or trapped water is **the central leaves pulling out easily** when tugged — the centre has rotted.

## How much light does an air plant need?

Bright **indirect** light. The right placements:

- A few feet back from a south or west window
- Right at an east window
- A few feet from a north window in a bright room

Wrong placements:

- Direct midday sun (scorches the leaves to brown crisps within hours on a hot day)
- Deep interior of a windowless room (slow decline over months)
- Inside a closed glass terrarium (no airflow, traps humidity, rots the plant)

Most "globe terrarium" displays you see in shops are essentially death sentences for the air plant inside. The plant looks great for 2–3 months and then mysteriously dies. The cause is almost always trapped humidity and zero airflow.

## Why does my air plant seem to be dying after it flowered?

This is the most common point of confusion. Tillandsia flowers **once in their life**. The flower may last anywhere from a few days to several months depending on species, with colours from neon pink and red to soft purple and blue. Many species also turn the centre leaves bright pink or red just before flowering.

After flowering, the parent plant **slowly declines and dies** over 1–2 years. This is normal. It is not your fault.

What the plant does **before** dying is the genius part: it produces **2–6 small offset plants** (called "pups") at its base. These are clones of the parent. As the parent dies back, the pups grow until each is roughly half the size of the original parent. At that point you can gently twist them off (or leave them attached to form a clump) and the cycle continues.

So a single air plant typically lives **2–4 years from a young plant to flowering**, then **another 1–2 years declining while pups grow**, while the pups themselves live another 2–4 years before flowering. A starter Tillandsia can become an indefinite line of descendants over a decade.

## Should I fertilise an air plant?

Yes, lightly. Air plants get most of their nutrients in the wild from rain washing nutrients out of bark and dust. Indoor tap water is comparatively sterile, so monthly feeding helps.

The protocol:

- **Half-strength orchid or bromeliad fertiliser** dissolved in the soak water
- **Once a month** during spring and summer
- **Every 2 months** in autumn and winter
- **Skip feeding** within 2 weeks of flowering or when the plant produces a flower spike

Avoid regular tomato or houseplant fertiliser — both are too high in nitrogen for air plants and cause leaf burn at the tips.

## What water should I use?

In order of preference:

1. **Rainwater** — best
2. **Filtered or de-chlorinated tap water** — fine for most areas
3. **Tap water from a hard-water region** — usable but builds up calcium deposits over time, manifesting as white crusts on the leaves
4. **Distilled water alone** — works if alternated with fertiliser-feeding water (pure distilled water provides zero nutrients)

If your tap water leaves a white crust on a kettle, your Tillandsia will accumulate the same crust on its leaves over months. A monthly rainwater soak helps reset this.

## Why is my air plant turning brown?

Three common causes, easy to distinguish:

**Tips going brown and crisp**: chronic underwatering. Increase soak frequency to twice per week for a month and the new growth should be normal.

**Centre going brown and mushy**: rot from trapped water. Usually unrescuable. Discard.

**Whole plant going dull silvery-brown after flowering**: normal post-flowering decline. Look for pups at the base.

## What temperature is best?

Most common Tillandsia species (T. ionantha, T. xerographica, T. caput-medusae) are happy at typical room temperatures of 15–28°C. They tolerate brief dips to 5°C and brief peaks to 35°C. Frost kills them.

The **softer-leaved tropical species** (T. cyanea, T. lindenii) prefer warmer and more humid conditions. The **silver, scaly xeric species** (T. xerographica, T. tectorum) tolerate dry air much better but need brighter light.

## FAQs

### Can I glue my air plant onto a piece of driftwood?

Yes. Use a non-toxic glue like aquarium-grade silicone or hot-melt glue (cooled briefly between application and contact). Glue the basal stub or the woody base, never the leaves themselves. The plant will continue growing and eventually root onto the wood.

### Why do some air plants look fluffy and silver while others look smooth and green?

Silver, fluffy ones (xeric Tillandsia like T. xerographica, T. tectorum) come from dry, bright environments. Their fuzzy "trichomes" trap moisture from morning fog. They tolerate drier conditions and brighter light. Smooth, green ones (mesic Tillandsia like T. cyanea) come from cloud forests. They need more frequent water and prefer indirect light.

### Are air plants safe around cats and dogs?

Yes, Tillandsia are not toxic to common pets. They are physically interesting to cats, who may bat them around as toys.

### How long does an air plant flower last?

Anywhere from a few days (T. ionantha) to several months (T. cyanea, with its pink "fan" bract that lasts for ages even after the small purple flowers themselves fade). The colour change in the bract often outlasts the actual flowers by weeks.

### Can I leave an air plant outside in summer?

In frost-free climates, yes — under a tree or pergola, in bright dappled light. Outdoor Tillandsia often flower more reliably than indoor ones because of better airflow and natural rainwater. Bring them in before the first frost.

### How do I separate pups from the parent?

Wait until each pup is at least one-third the size of the parent. Then grasp the pup gently at the base and twist while pulling sideways. It should pop off cleanly. If it resists, leave it another month — pulling too early damages both pup and parent.
