## Quick Answer

The bad smell is a **bacterial film** growing on the cut stem ends and feeding on dissolved sugars (both natural plant sap and the flower-food sachet). The bacteria multiply fastest when **leaves are submerged**, when the **stem ends are not re-cut**, and when **water is changed less than every 2 days**. The fix is straightforward: **strip leaves below the waterline, re-cut every 2 days, change water every 2 days, and wash the vase with mild bleach** between bouquets.

## What is actually growing in the cloudy water?

The cloudy, milky look in vase water is a colony of bacteria — mostly common environmental species like *Pseudomonas* and *Erwinia*. They are not particularly dangerous to humans, but they are catastrophic for cut flowers because they:

- Form a sticky biofilm on the cut stem end that physically blocks water uptake
- Release enzymes that break down the cells around the cut
- Produce smelly metabolic byproducts (the unmistakable "vase rot" smell, often compared to swamp water or rotting cabbage)

A flower in heavily contaminated water can stop drinking entirely within 24 hours, even though there is plenty of liquid in the vase. The flower wilts not from lack of water in the room, but from a blocked plumbing system.

## Why does the smell appear after exactly 2–3 days?

Bacterial population growth in a typical vase follows a J-curve. You start with a low count (assuming the vase was clean), the bacteria double roughly every 30–60 minutes for the first day, then they hit the carrying capacity of the available sugar.

Roughly:

- **Day 0–1**: water still clear, bacteria invisible to the naked eye
- **Day 2**: cloudiness becomes obvious, faint smell beginning
- **Day 3**: water visibly murky, distinctive sour smell
- **Day 4+**: stem ends slimy, leaves rotting, accelerating decline

This is why florists tell you to change water "every 2 days" — that interval drops the bacterial population back to a manageable level before it hits the smelly phase.

## Why is the smell worse with some flowers than others?

Three flower categories produce dramatically smellier vase water:

**Stocks (Matthiola incana)** are the worst offender. Their hollow stems trap water, decay rapidly, and produce a notoriously eye-watering stench. Re-cut stocks every day if you want them to last a week.

**Daffodils and other narcissi** secrete a milky sap that feeds bacteria heavily. Daffodils need to be conditioned in their own bucket for 12 hours before mixing, and the water you mix them into still goes off faster than usual.

**Chrysanthemums** in long-stem form are surprisingly stinky around the leaf nodes. The cure is brutal stripping of every leaf below the waterline.

In contrast, **roses, tulips, lilies, and gerbera** produce relatively low-bacteria water and last well with normal 2-day water changes.

## What does flower food actually do?

Commercial flower-food sachets contain three things in roughly equal proportions:

1. **Sugar** (sucrose or fructose) — feeds the flower
2. **Acidifier** (usually citric acid) — drops water pH to 3.5–4.0, which most bacteria don't like
3. **Biocide** (low-dose chlorine, silver, or 8-HQS) — kills bacteria directly

The biocide is the part that controls smell. Without it, the sugar in the sachet feeds bacteria as much as it feeds flowers, and you get a stinkier vase faster than with no food at all.

The DIY substitute: **a teaspoon of sugar plus a teaspoon of plain household bleach (sodium hypochlorite) per litre of water**. The bleach is the biocide; the sugar is the food. It works but is harsh; commercial flower food is gentler.

## How do I prevent the smell from starting in the first place?

The conditioning protocol that florists use:

1. **Wash the vase with hot water and a drop of bleach** before every new bouquet. Rinse thoroughly. Dried biofilm in a "clean-looking" vase reseeds the next bouquet within hours.
2. **Strip every leaf below the waterline.** Submerged leaves rot fastest of any tissue. This single step probably accounts for half of the smell-prevention benefit.
3. **Re-cut every stem at a 45° angle under running water.** The angle exposes more vascular tissue; the running water washes off air bubbles and biofilm.
4. **Use the flower food sachet at the correct dose.** Half-strength is worse than nothing — it provides sugar without enough biocide to control bacteria.
5. **Change water and re-cut every 2 days.** Rinse the vase between changes.
6. **Keep flowers cool and away from fruit.** Ethylene from ripening fruit accelerates senescence; warm rooms accelerate bacterial growth.

A bouquet conditioned this way should last 7–10 days with no smell.

## What if my vase is already smelly — can I rescue it?

Yes, if you act on day 2 or 3:

1. Pour out the water
2. Take the bouquet out and rinse the bottoms of the stems under running water for 30 seconds
3. Re-cut every stem 1–2 cm above the previous cut
4. Wash the vase with hot water and a drop of bleach, rinse
5. Refill with fresh water and a new sachet of flower food
6. Replace the bouquet

If the smell has been building for 5+ days and the water is brown not just cloudy, individual stems are usually too far gone — try to save just the freshest-looking ones.

## FAQs

### Will adding a copper coin really keep vase water clean?

Copper is a mild biocide, but the dose from a coin in a litre of water is too low to make much difference compared with proper flower food. It is a folk remedy that does no harm, but it is not a substitute for water changes and stem re-cuts.

### What about lemonade, vodka, or aspirin in the vase?

Lemonade provides acidifier (citric) plus sugar but no biocide — net negative. Vodka in tiny doses (5 ml per litre) acts as a mild biocide but the alcohol stresses the flower. Aspirin acidifies water slightly but doesn't kill bacteria. None of these match a proper flower-food sachet.

### Why does water in some vases stay clear longer than others?

Vase shape matters. Narrow-neck vases that grip the stems and keep leaves above the water line have less surface area for bacterial growth than wide-mouth vases. Glass and ceramic both work; metal can taint water; uncoated wood usually leaches tannins.

### Is it the flower food itself that smells?

No, fresh flower food has almost no smell. If your bouquet smells on day 1, it was already contaminated when it arrived (sometimes the case with supermarket bouquets that have been stored cool but not changed). Re-cut and re-water immediately.

### Can I just use a tiny drop of dishwashing liquid instead of flower food?

No. Detergents lower the surface tension of water and damage the cuticle of the cut stem. They smell deceptively clean but kill flowers fast.

### My vase still smells after washing. How do I fully clean it?

Soak the vase overnight in a 1:10 bleach-water solution, then rinse thoroughly and dry. Persistent biofilm in the bottom of narrow vases sometimes needs a bottle brush.
