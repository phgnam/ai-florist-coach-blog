## Quick Answer

The hibiscus is a tropical and subtropical genus of around 200 species, with *Hibiscus rosa-sinensis* (the "Chinese hibiscus") and *Hibiscus syriacus* (the rose of Sharon) most common across Asia. The red five-petalled *Bunga Raya* is Malaysia's national flower, designated in 1960. Hibiscus is widely used in tea, traditional medicine, and ceremonial offerings across Southeast Asia.

## What is the hibiscus and which species are common in Asia?

"Hibiscus" is the everyday name for around 200 species in the *Hibiscus* genus of the Malvaceae (mallow) family. The flower is unmistakable: five large overlapping petals around a long central stamen column, often in saturated red, pink, yellow, or white. The bloom typically opens for one to two days only, then drops — but a healthy plant produces hundreds of flowers across a season.

The four species florists and gardeners across Asia encounter most often:

- ***Hibiscus rosa-sinensis*** (Chinese hibiscus, Bunga Raya, *gumamela*) — the standard tropical garden hibiscus, the Malaysian national flower, and the species most commonly grown ornamentally across Southeast Asia.
- ***Hibiscus syriacus*** (rose of Sharon, *mugunghwa*) — temperate-climate woody shrub, the national flower of South Korea.
- ***Hibiscus sabdariffa*** (roselle, *atiso*, *bissap*) — the species whose dried calyx is brewed as the deep-red sour hibiscus tea.
- ***Hibiscus mutabilis*** (Confederate rose, cotton rose) — the colour-changing species whose flowers shift from white in the morning to deep pink by evening; long-cultivated in southern China.

For an adjacent treatment of other tropical-region flowers, see the [tropical flowers guide](/posts/tropical-flowers-guide-en/).

## Why is the hibiscus Malaysia's national flower?

Malaysia's national flower is the **Bunga Raya** ("great flower" or "celebration flower" in Malay), a red five-petalled cultivar of *Hibiscus rosa-sinensis*. It was designated by Tunku Abdul Rahman, Malaysia's first prime minister, on 28 July 1960 — three years after independence — and the choice was officially explained as follows:

- **Five petals** represent the five *Rukun Negara* (national principles): belief in God, loyalty to king and country, supremacy of the Constitution, the rule of law, and good behaviour and morality.
- **Red** represents the courage that brought the country to independence.
- **Year-round bloom** in Malaysia's tropical climate represents the durability and vitality of the new nation.

The flower is on the back of every Malaysian Ringgit coin and appears on government insignia, postage stamps, and the national airline's livery. For comparison, see how the [chrysanthemum became Japan's imperial flower](/posts/chrysanthemum-asia-en/) and how the [lotus became Vietnam's national flower](/posts/lotus-flower-asia-en/) for parallel stories from elsewhere in Asia.

## What does the hibiscus symbolise across other Asian cultures?

The flower carries different specific meanings across Asia:

**South Korea.** *Mugunghwa* (*Hibiscus syriacus*) is the national flower; the name translates as "eternal flower" or "flower that never falls". The South Korean national anthem itself is sometimes called *Aegukga* — but the flower's name appears in the chorus: *"mugunghwa samcheolli hwaryeo gangsan"* — "land of mugunghwa, three thousand *li* of beautiful rivers and mountains". The Korean idea is endurance, not Malaysia's celebration.

**China.** The *fu rong* (*Hibiscus mutabilis*) is the city flower of Chengdu, Sichuan, where it has been cultivated since the Five Dynasties period (10th century). Its colour-changing flower symbolises modesty and the variability of fortune.

**India.** The red hibiscus is sacred to the goddess Kali — the deity of time, destruction, and renewal — and is offered daily at her temples. The flower is also commonly woven into hair ornaments in South India.

**Philippines.** The *gumamela* is one of the most common garden flowers and is used in folk medicine; in children's culture, the petals and stamens are crushed in water to make bubble solution.

**Hawaii** (cultural extension into the Pacific). The hibiscus has been the state flower since 1923, with the yellow native species *Hibiscus brackenridgei* designated specifically in 1988. The behind-which-ear flower-wearing convention (single = available, married = unavailable) is the most-tourist-quoted cultural usage.

## How do you grow and care for hibiscus?

For the tropical *Hibiscus rosa-sinensis* (the Malaysian/Philippine garden hibiscus):

- **Sun.** Full sun, 6+ hours per day. Plants flower poorly in shade.
- **Water.** Consistently moist but never waterlogged. Tropical hibiscus drinks hard during peak bloom and may need daily watering in pot culture.
- **Temperature.** Above 13 °C; sensitive to frost. In temperate climates, treat as a conservatory or container plant brought indoors over winter.
- **Feeding.** A balanced flower-bias fertiliser (lower nitrogen, higher potassium and phosphorus) every 2–3 weeks during the growing season. High-nitrogen feeds produce leaves at the cost of flowers.
- **Pruning.** Cut back by a third at the start of the growing season to encourage branching; deadhead spent flowers to keep blooming.

For the temperate *Hibiscus syriacus* (rose of Sharon), care is closer to that of any deciduous flowering shrub — full sun, decent drainage, late-summer flowering on new wood, and an annual hard prune in early spring.

## Can you eat hibiscus flowers?

Yes — and the flower is one of the most widely consumed flowers on earth, as a tea more often than as a petal:

- ***Hibiscus sabdariffa*** (roselle) — the dried red calyx (the cup-shaped structure under the petals, not the petals themselves) is the source of *karkadeh* (Egypt), *bissap* (West Africa), *agua de Jamaica* (Mexico), *atiso* (Vietnam), and the standard "hibiscus tea" of Western health-food shops.
- ***Hibiscus rosa-sinensis*** petals — eaten fresh in some Polynesian and Indian salads; relatively bland and mucilaginous in texture.
- **In medicine.** Hibiscus extract has been studied in randomised trials as a mild antihypertensive (lowers systolic blood pressure by approximately 5–8 mmHg in the published meta-analyses); it's a real effect, not placebo, but smaller than first-line medication.

Flowers from a florist or supermarket may have been sprayed; for culinary use, source organic blooms or grow your own. For a wider treatment of edible flowers, see the [edible flowers guide](/posts/edible-flowers-guide-en/).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is the hibiscus Malaysia's national flower?**
The red Bunga Raya hibiscus was designated Malaysia's national flower on 28 July 1960 by Tunku Abdul Rahman. The five petals represent the five Rukun Negara (national principles); the red represents courage; and the flower's year-round bloom in Malaysia's tropical climate represents the durability of the new nation.

**Is the hibiscus a tropical flower?**
Mostly yes. *Hibiscus rosa-sinensis* — the standard ornamental hibiscus across Southeast Asia — is strictly tropical and frost-sensitive. *Hibiscus syriacus* (the Korean *mugunghwa*) is the cold-hardy temperate-climate exception and grows well across most of Europe, North America, and northern China.

**What does the hibiscus symbolise?**
Meaning depends on the country: celebration and national identity (Malaysia); endurance and "the flower that never falls" (Korea); modesty and changing fortune (Chengdu, China); the goddess Kali (India); and welcome (Hawaii and the Philippines). Across all of them, the flower is associated with vitality and femininity.

**Can you drink hibiscus tea every day?**
Most studies use 2–3 cups per day for 4–6 weeks in their hypertension protocols. There is no evidence of harm at that dose, but hibiscus tea can interact with hydrochlorothiazide and chloroquine, and lowers blood pressure cumulatively, so it should be moderated by anyone on antihypertensives or with already-low blood pressure.

**How long does a hibiscus flower last on the plant?**
A single hibiscus flower opens for **one to two days** before dropping. A healthy mature *Hibiscus rosa-sinensis* shrub can produce dozens of flowers a week through its growing season, so the short individual lifespan is rarely visible to the casual observer.
