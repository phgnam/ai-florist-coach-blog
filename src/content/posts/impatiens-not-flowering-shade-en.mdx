## Quick Answer

Impatiens stop flowering in shade for four common reasons: **deep shade with less than 2 hours of dappled light**, **inconsistent watering** (especially drying out in pots), **too much nitrogen** producing leafy growth at the expense of buds, or **downy mildew** infection that has wiped out the *Impatiens walleriana* population in your area. The fix is usually to **lift to a brighter dappled-shade spot, water deeply twice a week, switch to a low-nitrogen flowering feed**, and — if mildew is suspected — to replace *I. walleriana* with **New Guinea impatiens** (*I. hawkeri*) or *I. balfourii*, which are mildew-resistant.

## Are you growing the right impatiens for your shade?

There are three commonly sold "impatiens" and they have very different shade tolerances. Diagnosing the right plant first saves you weeks of unnecessary tinkering.

**Common impatiens / busy lizzie (*Impatiens walleriana*).** The classic shade-bedding annual. Tolerates the deepest shade of the three but needs at least **2 hours of dappled light per day** for flowering. Devastated by downy mildew (*Plasmopara obducens*) since around 2011 in many regions; this is the single biggest reason a once-reliable border has stopped flowering.

**New Guinea impatiens (*Impatiens hawkeri*).** Larger, glossier leaves, often bronze or variegated. **Tolerates more sun** than common impatiens (4–6 hours direct morning sun is fine) but **flowers less in deep shade**. Highly resistant to downy mildew. Often the right swap if your shade has gradually become deeper as trees matured.

**Balsam / garden balsam (*Impatiens balsamina*).** Older cottage-garden type with cuplike flowers along upright stems. Prefers **partial sun to light shade**, not deep shade, and usually flowers reliably.

If your "impatiens" suddenly stopped flowering after years of reliable bloom, you almost certainly have *I. walleriana* hit by downy mildew. Switching to New Guinea or balsam types is far more reliable than trying to nurse the affected variety back.

For broader context on which plants actually do well in real garden shade, see [the best flowers for shade gardens](/posts/flowers-for-shade-gardens-en/).

## How much shade can impatiens really handle?

"Shade" in catalogues and "shade" in real gardens are often very different things. Impatiens need a specific kind of shade to flower well.

**Ideal:** **dappled shade** — moving patches of light through deciduous trees, or **bright shade** under a north-facing wall with open sky overhead. Roughly **2–4 hours** of indirect bright light through the day.

**Workable:** **morning sun + afternoon shade** for *I. walleriana*; **morning sun + light afternoon shade** for New Guinea types. Light coming in low from one side counts as much as direct light from above.

**Marginal:** **deep shade under evergreens or a covered porch** with very little open sky. Plants survive but produce mostly foliage. New Guinea impatiens and balsam will not flower at all here; *I. walleriana* may produce a few sparse flowers.

**Definitely too dark:** **closed canopy under conifers**, **shade alongside a north-facing wall blocked by buildings**, or **windowless courtyard corners**. Impatiens here grow tall, leggy, pale, and bloom-free.

A useful rule of thumb: **if your phone in auto-mode triggers HDR or low-light night mode at noon**, the spot is probably too dark for impatiens to flower well. Move them to a position where you can read a paperback book comfortably without artificial light at midday.

## Are you watering enough — and consistently?

Impatiens are originally **moist-tropical-forest plants**. They flower most when soil moisture is steady; even short droughts cause **bud blast** (developing buds drop without opening) for the next 1–2 weeks.

**Soil moisture targets:**

- **In ground beds:** keep the top 5 cm of soil consistently moist but not soggy. Mulched beds typically need watering twice a week in dry weather.
- **In containers:** **daily watering** in summer, sometimes twice a day in hot weather. Containers under tree canopies are often shaded but also under the rain shadow of foliage, so they receive far less rain than you'd expect.
- **Hanging baskets:** the most demanding situation; water until water runs from the drainage holes, every day in summer.

**Common watering mistakes:**

- **Light frequent sprinkling.** Wets the leaves but never reaches the rootball. Switch to deep less-frequent watering.
- **Letting tree-canopy beds dry between rains.** Big trees take most of the moisture; mulch heavily and supplement-water.
- **Hanging baskets in dry shade.** Worst-of-both-worlds: shaded enough to flower poorly, dry enough to abort buds. Move to brighter spots and water more.

If your impatiens look fine in the morning but wilt by noon, the rootball has dried out and is repelling water on rewatering. **Soak the pot in a tray of water for 20 minutes**, then drain. This often restores the plant within 24 hours and bloom resumes within a week.

## Are you feeding too much, or the wrong thing?

This is the second-most-common cause of leafy-but-bloomless impatiens. Container plants in particular get over-fed because gardeners equate "more fertiliser" with "more flowers".

**The nitrogen problem.** A high-nitrogen feed (the first number on a fertiliser label, e.g. "20-5-5") **pushes leafy growth and suppresses flowering**. Lawn fertiliser drift, fresh manure, and most "general-purpose" fertilisers fall into this trap.

**The fix:** switch to a **low-nitrogen, higher-phosphorus and potassium flowering feed** (something like 5-10-10 or a "tomato" feed). Apply at **half strength every 2 weeks** during the growing season, less in cool weather.

**The compost issue.** Pre-fed multipurpose compost contains slow-release nitrogen sufficient for the first 4–6 weeks. After that, the plant has burned through it and either gets re-fed (with the right feed) or starves. **Container impatiens that "stopped flowering at midsummer" have usually run out of food**.

**Signs of over-feeding:**

- Big lush dark-green leaves with few buds.
- Soft floppy growth that wilts easily despite watering.
- Salt deposits crusting on the soil surface or pot rim.

**Signs of under-feeding:**

- Pale or yellowing older leaves.
- Slow growth with normal flower count but small flowers.
- Plant looks "tired" by mid-summer despite good watering.

If symptoms point to over-feeding, **flush the pot** with twice its volume of plain water to wash out excess salts, then resume with a low-nitrogen feed at half strength.

## Could it be downy mildew or another disease?

This is the question that has reshaped impatiens growing in the last decade. **Downy mildew of impatiens** (*Plasmopara obducens*) is a water-mould disease that appeared as a serious problem around 2011 and has since wiped out *I. walleriana* in many regions where it was previously the default shade annual.

**Symptoms:**

- **Leaves develop a fluffy white coating on the underside** (look closely with a magnifier; it is unmistakable).
- Leaves yellow, curl, and drop.
- Stems collapse to a leafless skeleton in 1–2 weeks.
- Flowering stops or never begins.

**What you can do:**

- **Remove infected plants immediately**, including all dropped leaves. Bag and bin — do not compost.
- Do **not replant *I. walleriana* in the same spot for 3+ years**; spores persist in soil.
- Switch to **New Guinea impatiens** (*I. hawkeri*) or *I. balfourii*, both of which are highly resistant.
- Improve airflow by spacing plants further apart and avoiding overhead watering.

**Other diseases to rule out:**

- **Botrytis (grey mould):** fuzzy grey patches on flowers in cool damp conditions. Improve airflow.
- **Bacterial wilt:** entire plants collapse suddenly with no visible leaf damage. Remove and discard.
- **Root rot:** lower leaves yellow, base of stem soft. Lift plant, check roots; if mushy, the plant is usually lost.

If your impatiens have stopped flowering and you see white fuzz on the underside of the leaves, the diagnosis is downy mildew until proven otherwise — and the answer is to switch species, not to fight the disease.

## What about pots, hanging baskets, and indoor pots?

Pot impatiens have their own quirks on top of the general issues above.

**Pot size.** Impatiens are surprisingly heavy feeders for a small plant. **A single plant in less than a 17 cm pot will run out of root space by midsummer**, no matter how much you feed. Plant 1 per 20 cm pot, 3 per 30 cm pot, or 5 per 35 cm hanging basket.

**Drainage.** Impatiens hate waterlogged feet. Holes in the bottom of the pot are non-negotiable; saucers underneath should be emptied 30 minutes after watering.

**Indoor impatiens.** *I. walleriana* and New Guinea types both make decent **temporary indoor flowering plants** in cool bright conservatories or sunporches. They struggle in heated dim sitting rooms because of poor light and dry air, and rarely flower long indoors. For consistently shade-tolerant indoor flowering plants, see [low-light indoor flowers](/posts/low-light-indoor-flowers-en/).

**Pinching back leggy plants.** If a plant has stretched and bloomed sparsely, **cut back by one-third in mid-summer**. The plant will sulk for 2–3 weeks then push fresh bushy growth and resume flowering through the rest of the season. This single intervention often doubles total bloom for the year.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are my impatiens leaves yellowing and flowers dropping?

Three common causes: **downy mildew** (look for white fuzz on leaf undersides), **over-watering with poor drainage** (lower leaves yellow first, soil stays wet), or **cold nights below 10°C** (whole plant stalls and drops buds). Diagnose by checking the leaf undersides first, then the rootball.

### Will impatiens flower in full sun?

Common impatiens (*I. walleriana*) usually flag and flower poorly in full sun, especially in warm climates. **New Guinea impatiens (*I. hawkeri*)** tolerate **4–6 hours direct sun** and often flower better with morning sun than in deep shade. Match the species to the light you actually have.

### How can I tell downy mildew from normal yellowing?

Look at the underside of yellowing leaves. **Downy mildew produces a fluffy white coating** that looks like fine flour or thin fungal growth; normal age yellowing or watering stress does not. Mildew also spreads from plant to plant within days, while watering stress affects only the worst-positioned plants in the bed.

### Can I save impatiens that have stopped flowering?

Often yes. The fastest reset is: (1) **cut back by one-third**, (2) **flush the pot or soil with plain water** to clear excess salts, (3) resume **half-strength low-nitrogen flowering feed** every 2 weeks, (4) ensure consistent watering. Most plants resume flowering within 2–3 weeks. If symptoms suggest disease, remove and replant with a different species instead.

### Do impatiens need deadheading?

No — impatiens are **self-cleaning**, dropping spent flowers cleanly. Deadheading is unnecessary for tidiness. A **mid-summer pinch-back** to encourage bushiness is more useful than picking individual flowers.

### What is the best fertiliser schedule for shade-grown impatiens?

A **half-strength balanced or low-nitrogen flowering feed every 2 weeks** during the growing season. Avoid lawn-fertiliser drift, fresh manure, and any fertiliser with nitrogen as the highest number. In containers, supplement with a slow-release fertiliser at planting time and switch to liquid feeds from week 6 onwards.
