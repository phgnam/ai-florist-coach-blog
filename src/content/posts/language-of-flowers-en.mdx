In the Victorian era, sending flowers was never as simple as it looked. A bouquet carried an entire conversation — each bloom carefully chosen to say what propriety wouldn't allow to be spoken aloud. This system of floral messaging is called **floriography**, and while the strict Victorian codes have softened, the meanings behind flowers remain surprisingly alive in contemporary culture.

## Where floriography came from

The formal language of flowers emerged in Western Europe in the early 18th century, popularised largely through letters from Lady Mary Wortley Montagu, who encountered *selam* — a Turkish tradition of communicating through objects — in Constantinople in 1717. The idea captivated European society, and by the Victorian era, floriography had become a sophisticated cultural code with dozens of competing dictionaries, each slightly different in their interpretations.

The Victorians used flowers to communicate everything that social codes forbade them to say directly — declarations of love, rejections, warnings, gratitude, and longing. A red rose delivered to a suitor's door meant one thing; the same rose wrapped upside down meant the opposite. The direction a flower was held, whether it was tied with a ribbon on the left or right — all carried meaning to those who knew the code.

## The meanings of common flowers

Understanding even the most widely agreed-upon meanings can transform the way you give and receive flowers.

**Rose** is perhaps the most layered of all. A **red rose** means romantic love — passionate, consuming, enduring. A **pink rose** communicates admiration and affection, appropriate for someone beloved but not romantically. **Yellow roses** once meant jealousy in Victorian England but have largely shifted to mean friendship and warmth in modern usage — a shift worth knowing when giving to someone from an older generation. **White roses** mean purity, new beginnings, and remembrance — which is why they appear at both weddings and funerals. **Orange roses** signal fascination and desire.

**Lily** is associated with purity and refined beauty in white form, but lilies carry different meanings by colour. Orange tiger lilies represent confidence and pride; yellow lilies mean thankfulness; white Easter lilies are the classic symbol of purity.

**Peony** in Eastern tradition, particularly in China, represents prosperity, good fortune, and feminine beauty. In Western floriography, peonies mean bashfulness and romance. Their full, layered blooms have made them universally associated with abundance and happiness.

**Tulip** carries meaning by colour more than any flower except the rose. **Red tulips** declare perfect love. **Yellow tulips** once meant hopeless love in Victorian usage but now simply signal cheerfulness. **Purple tulips** represent royalty and admiration. **White tulips** ask for forgiveness.

**Sunflower** means adoration, loyalty, and longevity — a flower that follows the sun, a person who is unwavering in their devotion. They also represent warmth, positivity, and joy.

**Orchid** signals luxury, elegance, and rare beauty. In ancient Greece, orchids were associated with fertility and virility; in Victorian England they signified refined taste and a cultivated sensibility. To give an orchid is to say you consider the recipient extraordinary.

**Lavender** has long meant devotion and tranquillity. It was used in love potions in medieval Europe and remains associated with calm, purity, and gentle affection. It also carries the meaning of distrust in some older traditions — a reminder that floriography was never fully standardised.

**Iris** represents faith, wisdom, and hope. Named for the Greek goddess of the rainbow, irises appear on royal seals and state emblems precisely because of these associations.

**Hydrangea** carries a complicated history — Victorian dictionaries listed it as a symbol of frigidity and heartlessness (perhaps because of its showy, many-petalled blooms with no scent). Modern usage has inverted this entirely: hydrangea now typically means heartfelt emotion, gratitude, and abundance.

**Freesia** signifies trust, innocence, and friendship. Its delicate form and sweet fragrance have made it the default flower for 'thank you' arrangements in modern floristryculture.

**Sweet pea** says "thank you for a lovely time" and is associated with blissful pleasure and departure. Traditionally given at the end of a visit or at the close of a chapter.

**Dahlia** means elegance and dignity, but also personal commitment and a bond that will last forever. Its complexity and many-layered petals have made it a symbol of inner strength.

## Cultural variations matter

Floriography is not universal. **White flowers** are associated with grief and funerals in China, Japan, Korea, and Vietnam — never give all-white arrangements to celebrate a birth or marriage in these cultures. **Yellow chrysanthemums** are funeral flowers in many European countries (Belgium, France, Italy) but celebratory in other contexts. **Marigolds** are sacred in Indian Hindu tradition but associated with death in parts of Latin America and Southeast Asia.

When giving flowers across cultural contexts, the universal safe choices are: warm-coloured mixed bouquets for celebration, and single-colour arrangements in the recipient's known favourite colour for personal occasions.

## Using floriography intentionally today

The most meaningful modern use of floriography isn't strict Victorian code — it's the gesture of choosing flowers that carry a specific message and then telling the recipient what each flower means. A bouquet of **freesia, sweet pea, and iris** given to a mentor with a handwritten note explaining that freesia means sincerity, sweet pea means appreciation, and iris means wisdom — this creates a memory that a generic arrangement never could.

Flowers say what words sometimes can't. Floriography is simply the vocabulary.
