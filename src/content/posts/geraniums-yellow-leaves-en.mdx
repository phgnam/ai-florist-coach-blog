## Quick Answer

Geranium leaves yellow for five common reasons: overwatering (whole-leaf yellow then drop), underwatering (dry crispy yellow at edges), nutrient deficiency (yellow with green veins), cold or transplant shock (mottled yellow on lower leaves), and natural ageing (oldest leaves only). Read the pattern of yellowing — which leaves, what they look like — to identify the cause.

## What does yellow look like on geranium leaves, and why does the pattern matter?

"Yellow leaves on geraniums" is not one problem — it is at least five different problems that all happen to make leaves yellow. The single highest-leverage thing you can do as a diagnostician is **stop reacting to "yellow" as a single signal and start reading the pattern**.

Three things to observe before doing anything:

1. **Which leaves are yellowing?** The bottom (oldest) leaves only? The top (newest) leaves? Random leaves throughout? Each pattern points to a different cause.
2. **What does the yellow look like up close?** Uniform yellow across the whole leaf? Yellow between green veins? Yellow at the edges with green centres? Mottled?
3. **What is the leaf doing now?** Soft and limp? Crispy and dry? Dropping off when touched? Still firm? Yellowing while still attached vs already fallen?

These three observations narrow the cause to one or two possibilities every time, and the correct fix follows from the cause — not from generic "geranium care".

The five common causes — described in detail below — each have a characteristic signature:

| Cause | Which leaves | Pattern | Touch test |
|---|---|---|---|
| Overwatering / root rot | Lower then mid-plant | Whole leaf yellow, soft, drops off | Stem soft near soil; soil sour-smelling |
| Underwatering | Lower leaves first | Crispy yellow at edges, brown tips | Soil pulled away from pot edge; plant droops in heat |
| Nutrient deficiency | Lower leaves (N), upper leaves (Fe) | Yellow with darker green veins | Plant looks tired; flowering reduced |
| Cold / transplant shock | Lower leaves | Mottled yellow + reddish blotches | Recent move, transplant, or cold night |
| Natural ageing | Bottom leaves only | One leaf at a time, gradual | Plant otherwise healthy and flowering |

The next sections walk through each cause in detail.

## Are you overwatering your geraniums?

**Overwatering is the #1 killer of geraniums in pots** — by a large margin. Geraniums (more correctly *Pelargoniums*, see FAQ) evolved in the dry rocky hillsides of South Africa. Their roots cannot tolerate waterlogged soil for long. The cycle of damage:

1. Excess water in the pot fills air pockets in the soil
2. Roots cannot get oxygen and start dying
3. Root death looks above-ground as yellow lower leaves
4. Reduced root mass means the plant cannot uptake water properly
5. Owner sees wilting, assumes thirst, waters more
6. Cycle repeats until the plant collapses

**Overwatering signature:**

- **Lower and mid-plant leaves yellow uniformly** (whole leaf, not just edges)
- **Yellow leaves feel soft and limp** rather than crispy
- **Leaves drop with a gentle touch** even when fully yellow
- **Plant base / lower stems are soft, brown, or black** if you press gently with a finger
- **Soil stays wet for 5+ days** after watering
- **Drainage holes drip for hours** after watering
- **Sour, swampy smell** from the bottom of the pot

**The fix:**

- Stop watering immediately and let the pot dry out completely (3–7 days for a saucer-pooling pot)
- Empty all standing water from the saucer or decorative outer pot — geraniums must never sit in water
- Once the surface is dry, lift the plant from the pot and inspect roots: white = healthy, brown/black = rot
- If most roots are black and mushy: trim all rotten parts back to clean white tissue with sterile scissors, repot in fresh, free-draining compost (50/50 standard potting compost and perlite or coarse sand)
- Resume watering only when the top 3 cm of soil is dry to the touch
- For pots, water until liquid runs from the drainage holes, then immediately tip out the saucer

For mild overwatering caught early, the plant usually recovers within 2–3 weeks. For severe rot with most roots gone, take a 10 cm tip cutting from a healthy stem and propagate as a backup — the parent plant may not survive.

## Could it be underwatering or salt build-up in the pot?

The opposite mistake — keeping pots too dry — produces a different yellow signature.

**Underwatering signature:**

- **Lower leaves yellow first**, then crisp brown at the edges
- **Yellow areas feel papery or crispy** rather than soft
- **Soil pulls away from the pot edge** — water poured in runs straight through the gap without soaking the rootball
- **Whole plant droops on hot days** but recovers overnight
- **Stems may also yellow at the base**

**Salt build-up** (chronically underwatered or fertilised with hard water) produces a related problem:

- **White crusty deposits** on the soil surface and around drainage holes
- **Yellow leaf edges with reddish or brown burns** alongside the yellow
- **Plant looks "tired" overall** even with proper watering schedule

**The fix for underwatering:**

- Soak the entire pot in a bucket of water for 30 minutes — the soil will rehydrate evenly rather than letting water run through gaps
- After 30 minutes, lift out and let drain fully
- Resume watering once the top 2 cm of soil is dry — typically every 4–7 days in moderate weather, every 2–3 days in hot weather

**The fix for salt build-up:**

- "Flush" the pot — pour 3× the pot's volume of clean water through it slowly, letting it drain completely each pass
- This dissolves accumulated fertiliser salts and washes them out
- Use rainwater or filtered water for flushing if your tap water is hard
- Reduce fertiliser strength to half-strength going forward

## What does nutrient deficiency look like on geraniums?

Geraniums in pots exhaust their compost faster than gardeners expect. The two most common deficiencies — nitrogen and iron — show different visual signatures because the plant moves nutrients differently.

**Nitrogen deficiency (mobile nutrient — moved from old to new growth):**

- **Lower leaves yellow uniformly** — same pattern as overwatering, which is why many gardeners confuse them
- The difference: lower leaves stay **firm**, not soft, and don't drop easily
- Upper growth still green, but pale
- Plant overall looks pale, slow-growing, with reduced flowering

**Iron deficiency (immobile nutrient — stuck in old leaves):**

- **Upper, newest leaves yellow** with darker green veins still visible — the classic "interveinal chlorosis" pattern
- Lower mature leaves stay green
- Often associated with high-pH (alkaline) compost or hard tap water tying up iron in the soil

**Magnesium deficiency:**

- **Mid-plant leaves yellow between veins**, often with reddish or purple tinting
- More common in container-grown geraniums fertilised exclusively with high-nitrogen feeds

**The fix:**

- For nitrogen and general feeding: apply a balanced liquid fertiliser (NPK ~10-10-10 or 7-7-7) at half strength every 14 days during the growing season
- For iron deficiency: apply iron chelate (sequestered iron) as a foliar spray weekly until new growth comes in green; longer-term, repot into fresh, slightly acidic potting compost
- For magnesium: dissolve 1 tablespoon Epsom salts (magnesium sulphate) in 4 litres of water and apply as a soil drench monthly
- **Never feed a sick plant.** If the plant looks otherwise stressed (overwatered, root-rotted, heat-shocked), fix the underlying problem first. Feeding stressed plants accelerates decline.

## Could the yellowing be from cold, transplant shock, or root issues?

Three less common but distinctive causes worth checking when the obvious ones don't match.

**Cold shock:**

- Geraniums are tender; they suffer below 4–7 °C (40–45 °F)
- A single cold night exposes the plant; **lower leaves develop reddish-purple-yellow blotches** 5–10 days later
- Common after putting plants out too early in spring or leaving them out into autumn
- **The fix:** move plant to a warmer position (above 10 °C overnight). The damaged leaves won't recover but new growth will be normal. If most of the plant is affected, trim damaged stems and overwinter indoors.

**Transplant shock:**

- After potting up, repotting, or planting out, geraniums often drop or yellow some leaves for 2–4 weeks
- This is normal — the plant is rebuilding its root system in fresh soil
- **The fix:** don't fertilise until new growth resumes (typically 3–4 weeks). Water moderately. Don't relocate the plant during this period.

**Pot-bound roots:**

- Long-term pot-bound geraniums (3+ years in the same pot) develop root systems that fill every gap, leaving no room for new water absorption
- Yellow leaves emerge despite proper watering because the rootball can't take up the water it's given
- **The fix:** unpot the plant and inspect — if roots circle the pot in a tight mass, you are pot-bound. Trim 20 % of the root mass back, cutting away the densest outside layer, then repot one size up in fresh compost.

**Pest pressure (less common):**

- Whitefly and spider mites stress geraniums and can cause secondary yellowing
- Whitefly: small white flies fly up when the plant is shaken. Underside of leaves shows tiny white scale-like nymphs
- Spider mites: very fine webbing on undersides; tiny dots and stippling on the leaves
- **The fix:** spray undersides with insecticidal soap or 1 % neem oil weekly until population collapses (usually 3 weeks)

## How do you bring a yellowed geranium back to full green?

Once the cause is identified, the recovery plan is structural rather than reactive. The 6-step rescue sequence works for most cases:

1. **Identify and remove the cause** (see above sections). Don't proceed until this is done — feeding or repotting a plant whose problem is overwatering will kill it.
2. **Remove the worst-yellowed leaves** (those whose yellow is more than 70 % of the leaf area). The plant has already abandoned them; removing them lets it concentrate resources on healthy growth. Leave partially-yellow leaves alone — they can still photosynthesise.
3. **Stabilise the watering routine.** Water deeply when the top 2–3 cm of soil is dry to the finger-press test. Empty saucers immediately. Use the same schedule for at least 3 weeks before adjusting.
4. **Hold off on feeding for the first 14 days** if the cause was overwatering or root issues. Stressed roots cannot use fertiliser and the salt may damage them further. After 14 days of stable growth, resume balanced liquid feed at half strength every 2 weeks.
5. **Restore light gradually** if the plant has been in the wrong position. Move 30 % of the way toward better light (e.g. from north window to east window), wait 5 days for the plant to acclimate, then move again. Sudden moves to brighter light cause new shock.
6. **Pinch the growing tip** once the plant is producing healthy new growth — this triggers branching and quickly restores a full-foliage appearance. Pinch back the top 2 cm of each main stem once.

Most "yellowed" geraniums are fully green again within 4–6 weeks of correct treatment. Expect 2–3 weeks before any visible improvement — geraniums recover slowly and the first sign is usually new green growth at the tips, not the existing yellow leaves turning back to green (yellowing is irreversible at the leaf level).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should you cut off yellow geranium leaves?**
Only if they are more than 70 % yellow, soft and rotting, or covered in mildew. Partially yellow leaves still photosynthesise and contribute to plant energy — leave them alone. Use clean snips, cut at the base of the leaf stem (petiole) rather than mid-stem, and don't compost yellowed leaves if the cause was disease (bag and bin them instead).

**Will yellow geranium leaves turn green again?**
No. Yellowing happens when chlorophyll breaks down in the leaf, and the plant doesn't rebuild chlorophyll in fully-yellowed leaves. The plant recovers by producing new green growth at the tips, not by re-greening the old leaves. New growth normally appears within 2–3 weeks of fixing the underlying cause and is the reliable sign of recovery.

**Are geraniums and pelargoniums the same thing?**
Botanically no, but they're sold under the same name. The "geraniums" most people grow in pots and window boxes — the showy red, pink, white, and salmon ones — are actually *Pelargoniums*. True *Geranium* is a separate genus, mostly hardy garden perennials called "cranesbills" with smaller daintier flowers. The yellow-leaf advice in this article is for the common Pelargoniums; true Geraniums have their own different issues.

**How often should you water geraniums in pots?**
There's no fixed frequency — water based on soil moisture, not the calendar. Press a finger 3 cm into the compost; if it feels dry to that depth, water deeply until liquid runs from the drainage holes. Then empty any saucer or outer pot. In moderate weather this is every 4–7 days; in hot summer weather every 2–3 days; in winter (if overwintered indoors) every 14–21 days.

**Can geraniums be saved if they have root rot?**
Mild root rot (where roots are mostly white with some brown patches), yes — unpot, trim the rotten sections back to clean white tissue with sterile scissors, repot in fresh free-draining compost, and reduce watering. Severe root rot (most roots black and mushy, stem softening at the base) is usually fatal — the realistic move is to take a 10 cm tip cutting from any remaining healthy stem and propagate as a replacement.
