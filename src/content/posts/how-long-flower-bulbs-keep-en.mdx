## Quick Answer

Most spring-flowering bulbs (tulips, daffodils, hyacinths, crocuses) keep **2–6 months** in cool, dry, ventilated storage — long enough to bridge an autumn purchase to a spring or autumn planting window. Stored warm, humid, or sealed in plastic, the same bulbs can rot or sprout within weeks. Lily bulbs are the most fragile (storage measured in weeks). Allium and amaryllis are the toughest (months). The single best storage condition is **cool (10–15°C), dark, and breathable** in mesh bags or paper sacks — never sealed plastic.

## How long can each common bulb really survive in storage?

This depends heavily on the species and on the conditions, but a useful baseline at cool, dry, ventilated storage:

| Bulb | Typical safe storage | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|
| Tulip | 4–6 months | Keep below 16°C; warm storage triggers premature sprouting |
| Daffodil (narcissus) | 4–6 months | Tolerates a wider temperature range than tulip |
| Hyacinth | 3–4 months | Wear gloves — skin irritant |
| Crocus | 3–5 months | Small bulbs dry out fast in low humidity |
| Allium | 6+ months | Very tolerant; keep dry |
| Iris (reticulata, dwarf) | 3–4 months | Don't let them shrivel |
| Iris (bearded rhizomes) | 1–2 months | Rhizomes, not true bulbs; plant promptly |
| Lily | 2–6 weeks | The most fragile — never let them dry out |
| Amaryllis (Hippeastrum) | 6+ months | The standard "boxed" sales bulb is months old already |
| Dahlia (tubers) | 4–5 months over winter | Pack in dry sand or vermiculite |
| Gladiolus (corms) | 4–6 months over winter | Lift after frost, dry, store cool |

The big outlier worth remembering: **lily bulbs are not dormant the way tulip bulbs are**. They are still alive and metabolising; they cannot be left to dry out for months. Plant lily bulbs within 2–6 weeks of buying them, or pot them up and overwinter the pots in a cool place.

## What kills bulbs in storage?

Three threats, in order of how often they cause problems:

**Heat** is the worst. Bulbs stored above 20°C lose dormancy fast. Tulips in particular need cool storage — at 25°C they may flower poorly the following spring even if they look fine on planting day.

**Humidity** is second. Damp bulbs grow fungal rot (the white or grey fluffy mould you sometimes find on a forgotten bag). Once rot starts, it spreads to neighbouring bulbs.

**Sealed plastic** is third — it traps moisture released by the bulbs themselves and creates a humid micro-environment perfect for rot. Mesh and paper let bulbs breathe.

A bulb in **cool, dry, ventilated** storage can keep for months. The same bulb in a sealed plastic bag in a warm utility cupboard can be mush in three weeks.

## Where in the house should I store bulbs?

The ideal spot is:

- **Cool (10–15°C)**: a garage, shed, unheated cellar, or cool spare room
- **Dark**: light triggers sprouting
- **Dry but ventilated**: mesh bag, paper sack, or open cardboard box

Common mistakes:

- **Kitchen cupboard**: too warm, too humid (cooking steam)
- **Garden shed in summer**: temperature can climb above 30°C on hot days, especially with a metal roof
- **Sealed plastic bag in the fridge**: the cold is right but condensation inside the bag rots bulbs in weeks
- **Open paper bag in the fridge**: works well, but watch out for fruit (apples release ethylene which damages bulbs)

A garage or unheated utility room is the sweet spot for most of the storage window in temperate climates. The fridge (in a paper bag, away from fruit) works for shorter periods if your house is warm.

## How can I tell if a bulb is still viable?

The four tests:

**The squeeze test**: a healthy bulb feels firm. A soft or spongy bulb is rotting from the inside. Discard.

**The weight test**: a healthy bulb feels heavy for its size. A light, hollow-feeling bulb has dried out internally and probably won't sprout. Marginal.

**The skin test**: papery dry skin is normal and even desirable (it protects the bulb). Wet, slimy, or mouldy skin is bad. Cracked skin is fine if the bulb underneath is firm.

**The cut test (last resort)**: for one or two bulbs in a doubtful batch, cut one open. The inside should be solid white or off-white. Brown, grey, or hollow is dead.

If most of the batch fails one or more tests, plant the survivors immediately in pots rather than waiting for the bed.

## Can I rescue bulbs that are already starting to sprout?

Yes, often. A bulb with a small green shoot has broken dormancy but is not dead — quite the opposite. The fix is to plant it immediately, even if the timing is "wrong":

- **Sprouting tulip in February (when you wanted to plant in November)**: pot up indoors or plant outside as soon as the ground isn't frozen. You may get a smaller flower this year, but the bulb will reset for next year.
- **Sprouting daffodil in summer**: pot it up. Often gives a small autumn flower instead of spring.
- **Sprouting amaryllis at any time**: pot it up immediately — these are typically the most willing to flower whenever planted.

What you cannot rescue is a bulb that has both sprouted and started rotting. The sprout takes the last energy reserves and the rot takes the rest.

## Does refrigerating tulip bulbs make them flower better?

Yes, controllably. Pre-chilling tulip bulbs at 4–5°C for 8–12 weeks before planting accelerates flowering and is essential for "forcing" tulips indoors out of season. This is what commercial growers do for January-flowering tulips.

For garden planting, pre-chilling is unnecessary in temperate climates because the soil itself does the chilling for you over winter. But in mild-winter climates (e.g. parts of California, southern Spain, much of Vietnam), you must pre-chill tulips in the fridge for 10–12 weeks or they won't flower properly.

## FAQs

### I bought tulip bulbs in September but only planted them in February. Will they flower?

Probably. If they were stored cool (under 16°C) and dry, late planting is fine — they may flower a few weeks late and slightly shorter than usual. If they were stored warm, expect blind shoots (leaves only) or weak short stems.

### Can I store leftover bulbs from this year for next year?

Generally no. Spring-flowering bulbs are bought to be planted promptly. Leftover bulbs stored for an additional 6–12 months past their natural planting window almost always lose vigour. Plant them somewhere "extra" rather than holding them.

### Why do my dahlia tubers shrivel in storage?

Too dry and too warm. The fix is to pack them in slightly damp (not wet) sand, vermiculite, or wood shavings, in a cardboard box, in a cool dark place. Check monthly and lightly mist if they start to feel papery.

### Should I cure bulbs after lifting before storing?

Yes for tulips, daffodils, lilies, gladioli, and dahlias. After lifting, brush off loose soil (don't wash them) and lay them in a single layer in a dry, airy spot for 1–2 weeks before storage. This kills surface fungal spores and lets the skin dry properly.

### Can I store bulbs in the freezer?

No. Freezing damages the cells. Bulbs need cool (4–15°C), not cold. The fridge is fine; the freezer is fatal.

### My amaryllis is still in the box — has it died?

Usually not. Amaryllis bulbs are sold months ahead of when they flower, and they're surprisingly tolerant of long storage. Squeeze the bulb — if firm, plant it now. Even a shrivelled-looking amaryllis often revives once watered.
