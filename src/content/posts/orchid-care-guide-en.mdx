Orchids have a fearsome reputation for difficulty, and most of it is undeserved. The reality is that **orchids are killed by kindness far more often than neglect**. The most common mistake people make — watering too frequently — is the opposite of what an orchid needs. Understand a few core principles and an orchid will reward you with blooms for months, then rebloom year after year.

This guide focuses primarily on **Phalaenopsis** (moth orchids) — the most widely sold orchid worldwide, the one you'll find at supermarkets and garden centres — but many principles apply across orchid genera.

## Light: bright but never direct

Orchids need **bright, indirect light**. In their natural habitat (tropical rainforests), they grow attached to trees where they receive dappled light filtered through a canopy. Replicating this: a windowsill where the light is bright but not direct is ideal.

**South or east-facing windows** are usually best. North-facing windowsills typically don't provide enough light. West-facing windows can work but may deliver too much direct afternoon sun.

Signs your orchid has too much light: yellowing leaves, bleached or scorched patches on the foliage. Signs of too little light: very dark green, almost black-green leaves (the plant is producing extra chlorophyll to capture scarce light), and failure to rebloom.

The leaves should be **medium green** — not dark green, not yellow. This is your calibration point.

## Watering: less is more

This is where most orchids die. **Phalaenopsis orchids should be watered approximately once a week** during the growing season and once every 10–14 days in winter — but the key is not the schedule, it's checking the roots.

The clear plastic pots that most orchids come in (inside a decorative outer pot) are useful precisely because you can see the root colour. **Green roots** mean the roots have water. **Silver-grey or white roots** mean the plant is thirsty and ready for watering.

**The best watering method**: Remove the inner plastic pot from the decorative pot. Place it under a gentle stream of room-temperature water for 30 seconds, allowing water to flow through the bark medium and drain completely from the bottom. Never let the pot sit in standing water — this is the fastest route to root rot.

**Room temperature water matters**: Cold water can shock tropical orchids. Let tap water reach room temperature, or use rainwater if you have it.

**Never water the crown**: Water pooling in the central cup of the leaves causes crown rot, which is fatal.

## Humidity and air circulation

Orchids are tropical plants and prefer humidity of 50–70%. In centrally heated homes in winter, this can be difficult to maintain. Options:
- **Pebble trays**: Place the pot on a tray of wet pebbles (the pot sits above, not in, the water — this creates local humidity through evaporation)
- **Humidifier**: A small humidifier near your orchid collection
- **Grouping plants**: Plants grouped together create a more humid microclimate through transpiration

Air circulation matters too — orchids appreciate gentle airflow. Stagnant air encourages fungal diseases. A lightly opened window (not cold drafts) or a fan on its lowest setting helps.

## Feeding: less than you think

Orchids should be fed during the growing season (spring through summer) with a **specialist orchid fertiliser at half the recommended strength, every 2–4 waterings**. The commonly repeated advice is "weekly, weakly" — feed with a dilute solution at every other watering.

Do not feed in winter when the plant is resting, and do not feed a stressed or unhealthy plant — fix the underlying problem first.

## How to get your orchid to rebloom

This is the question most orchid owners ask. After the initial bloom fades (which can last 3–6 months on a healthy Phalaenopsis), there's an adjustment period before reblooming.

**Step 1**: When the last flower drops, don't cut the spike immediately. Wait and watch — sometimes a secondary spike will emerge from a node on the old spike. If the old spike turns yellow or brown, cut it off at the base.

**Step 2**: Give your orchid a **temperature drop of 5–10°C at night for 4–6 weeks** in early autumn. This is the natural trigger for spike development — in tropical environments, orchids experience a cool, dry season before blooming. A windowsill where night temperatures drop to 14–16°C while days remain 20°C+ is ideal.

**Step 3**: Continue normal watering and feeding during this period. Within 6–8 weeks, you should see a new spike emerging from between the leaves at the base.

**Step 4**: As the spike develops, you can stake it with a bamboo spike and soft ties to direct the direction of bloom.

## Repotting: every 1–2 years

Orchids should be repotted every 1–2 years — not because they've outgrown their pot (they prefer to be somewhat root-bound) but because the **bark medium breaks down over time**, becoming compacted and retaining too much moisture. Fresh bark drains freely; old bark becomes soggy and suffocating.

The best time to repot is **after flowering and before new growth begins**, typically in spring.

**How to repot**:
1. Remove the orchid from its pot and gently shake off the old bark
2. Trim any dead, mushy, or clearly rotted roots with a clean blade (sterilise between cuts)
3. Allow cut root surfaces to dry for 30 minutes
4. Place in a clean pot only slightly larger than the root system — orchids prefer snug pots
5. Fill around the roots with fresh orchid bark, tapping gently to settle

## The most common problems

**Yellow leaves**: Usually overwatering or too much direct sunlight. Check roots for rot.

**Root rot** (brown, mushy roots): The most common cause of orchid death. Cut out all affected roots, let the plant dry, treat with a dilute hydrogen peroxide solution (1:10 ratio) to kill bacteria, and repot in fresh dry bark. Adjust watering going forward.

**No new spike**: Either insufficient light, no temperature differential between day and night, or the plant is stressed. Move to a brighter spot and attempt the cool-night method.

**Bud blast** (buds dropping before opening): Sudden changes in temperature, drafts, or ethylene gas from nearby fruit. Stable conditions and good air circulation prevent this.

A well-cared-for Phalaenopsis will bloom twice a year and live for decades. Many orchid enthusiasts have plants that are 15–20 years old and bloom reliably every year. Once you understand the rhythm, orchid care is genuinely simple.
