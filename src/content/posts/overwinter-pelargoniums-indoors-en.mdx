## Quick Answer

Overwinter pelargoniums by one of three methods: keep mature plants on a bright cool 7–13 °C windowsill with reduced watering; dig up and store bare-rooted plants in paper bags in a frost-free shed at 5 °C; or take 10 cm tip cuttings in late August, root in dry compost, and grow on as small plants over winter. Cuttings produce the most reliable spring plants; the bright-windowsill method keeps mature plants flowering through winter.

## Why do "geraniums" need overwintering — aren't they hardy?

The plants that most people call "geraniums" are not the hardy *Geranium* genus (the perennial cranesbills that cope with hard frost) but *Pelargonium* species from southern Africa. Pelargoniums are tender perennials — they survive freely above 5 °C and die at sustained temperatures below freezing. Most temperate gardens lose them outdoors in the first hard frost.

The taxonomic confusion goes back to Linnaeus, who originally lumped both genera together. They were separated in 1789, but the popular name "geranium" stuck for the colourful summer bedding plant, while botanists kept "*Geranium*" for the cold-hardy perennials. The result: you'll see a plant labelled "geranium" in a garden centre that is genuinely a *Pelargonium*, and a hardy *Geranium* in the perennial section that won't bear any of the bright-red bedding plants people picture.

For overwintering, what matters is which plant you actually have:

- **Hardy *Geranium* (cranesbill):** stays in the ground; cuts down naturally each autumn; needs no special treatment.
- **Tender *Pelargonium* (zonal, ivy-leaved, regal, scented):** needs frost protection. This article is about these.

The good news: pelargoniums are unusually willing overwinterers. Even neglected plants in poor light often pull through, and one healthy plant typically yields 6–10 new plants from cuttings if you take any.

## Which overwintering method should you choose?

Three methods work, and the right choice depends on your space and how many plants you have.

| Method | Space needed | Effort | Result next spring |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bright cool windowsill | One bright cool spot per plant | Low (occasional water) | Mature flowering plant |
| Dry-storage in paper bags | Frost-free shed/garage | Lowest | Mature plant after spring revival |
| Cuttings in late August | One small tray | Medium (4–5 hours total) | Multiple young plants |

For 1–3 prized plants you want to keep, the windowsill method is best. For a dozen mature bedding plants where space is short, dry-storage is the classic answer. For multiplying a single prized plant into a whole garden's worth, take cuttings.

Many growers use a combination — take cuttings as insurance, then store the parent plant by whichever method fits the available space. If the parent fails over winter, the cuttings carry the variety into the next year.

## How does the bright cool windowsill method work?

This is the easiest method for keeping a mature plant alive and modestly flowering through winter. The plant goes through a partial dormancy with reduced growth, but it stays leafy and produces occasional flowers.

The procedure:

1. **Lift before the first frost.** Pelargoniums die at sustained sub-zero temperatures; lift while temperatures are still above 5 °C.
2. **Cut back hard** — to about half the original size, removing all flowers and any soft new growth.
3. **Repot into fresh peat-free compost** if the plant has been in the same pot for more than a season.
4. **Water in well**, then let the compost dry slightly before watering again.
5. **Place on a bright, cool windowsill** at 7–13 °C. A north-facing or east-facing kitchen window is often perfect.
6. **Water sparingly** — once every 10–14 days through November–February, allowing the compost to dry between waterings.
7. **Increase watering and feeding from late February** as light returns and the plant resumes growth.

The single biggest mistake is overheating the plant. A pelargonium on a south-facing windowsill above a radiator at 22 °C grows leggy soft growth all winter, drops lower leaves, and may fail. The cool 7–13 °C range is what keeps it dormant enough to skip the leggy growth phase.

Watering errors are the second cause of failure. Pelargoniums in winter dormancy use very little water; soggy compost rots the roots. Water only when the top 3 cm of compost feels dry and the pot feels light when lifted.

## How does the dry-storage method work?

The dry-storage method is older and slightly counter-intuitive. It treats the plant almost like a stored bulb — you lift, dry off, and stash the plant in a paper bag in a frost-free dark space, then revive it in spring.

The procedure:

1. **Lift the plant** before the first frost, shaking most of the soil from the roots.
2. **Trim the top growth back hard** — to about 10 cm of bare stem, removing all leaves and flowers.
3. **Hang upside down** in a frost-free shed or garage for 2–3 days to let the stems dry slightly.
4. **Place in a paper bag** with the roots facing down, label clearly with the variety and date.
5. **Store at 5–10 °C** in a dark, frost-free space — a shed, garage, or unheated cellar.
6. **Check monthly** for any signs of rot; remove affected plants immediately.
7. **In late February or early March**, take the plant out, soak the roots in water for an hour, repot in fresh compost, and water lightly.
8. **Keep in a bright cool spot** at 12–15 °C while new growth resumes. Move to full light by April.

The dry-storage method has a higher failure rate than the windowsill method (around 70–80 % survival vs 90–95 %), but it requires no winter window space. For a gardener with 20 mature pelargoniums, dry-storage is the practical answer.

The plants emerge looking pathetic — leafless brown sticks — and need patience. New growth appears within 2–3 weeks of soaking and repotting; flowering follows within 6–8 weeks.

## How do you take pelargonium cuttings for winter overwintering?

Cuttings are the most reliable insurance against losing a variety, and they often produce stronger plants than overwintered parents. The window is late August to early September — early enough that the new plants establish before short winter days slow growth, late enough that you're cutting from healthy mature wood rather than spring-sappy growth.

The procedure:

1. **Take 10 cm tip cuttings** from the parent plant in late afternoon. Use sharp snips. Cut just below a leaf node.
2. **Strip the lower leaves** off the cutting, leaving 2–3 leaves at the top.
3. **Pinch off any flower buds** — the cutting should focus on rooting, not flowering.
4. **Air-dry the cutting** on a windowsill for 2 hours before potting. This unusual step is what makes pelargonium cuttings work — the cut surface seals over and is far less likely to rot.
5. **Pot into 7 cm pots of free-draining compost** (50 % multipurpose / 50 % perlite or grit). One cutting per pot, around the edge of the pot, not in the centre.
6. **Water once** to settle the compost and keep slightly damp, not wet.
7. **Place on a bright but not direct-sun windowsill** at 18–20 °C.
8. **Water sparingly** — once a week at most, only when compost feels dry.
9. **Roots form within 3–4 weeks**. Test by gently tugging; resistance means rooted.

Once rooted, treat the new plants as overwinter pelargoniums on the cool windowsill method. They flower from May the following year.

Pelargoniums are unusual in that cuttings are best taken without rooting hormone. The natural rooting compounds in the cut sap are sufficient, and added hormone often slows or rots the cutting.

## What goes wrong, and how do you fix it?

Five common winter problems and the fix for each.

**1. Leaves yellow and drop in November.**
Normal partial dormancy if light watering and cool temperature. Concerning if the compost is wet — root rot. Reduce watering and let the plant dry out somewhat.

**2. Long pale stems with small leaves.**
Etiolation from low light + warm temperature. Move to brighter, cooler spot. In spring, cut back hard to force new compact growth.

**3. Black soft stem at base.**
*Botrytis* or stem rot from overwatering and high humidity. Remove the plant; it rarely recovers. Take any healthy tip cuttings before disposing.

**4. White flies or aphids on indoor plants.**
Common indoor pest pressure from the warm dry air. Wash with soapy water, repeat weekly. Severe infestations may need an insecticidal soap. The [aphids on roses (organic)](/posts/aphids-on-roses-organic-en/) post covers approaches that work indoors too.

**5. Plant emerges in spring but slow to flower.**
Insufficient light or insufficient feeding. Move to brightest available light, feed with balanced fertiliser at half strength every 10 days. Flowering follows within 4–6 weeks.

Pelargoniums are unusually resilient and often recover from problems that would kill other plants. A pelargonium that looks dead in March is worth giving 4 weeks of bright cool conditions before discarding — many sprout from the base.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I leave pelargoniums outside in mild winters?**
In a frost-free coastal climate (zone 9b or warmer), yes. They survive light frost down to about –2 °C briefly but die at sustained sub-zero temperatures. Anywhere with regular hard frost, lift them.

**How long can pelargoniums live?**
Many years. A well-maintained plant overwintered annually can be 10+ years old. Old plants get woody at the base; cuttings every 3–4 years rejuvenate the variety with younger plants.

**Should I cut off all leaves before storing?**
For dry-storage, yes — cut back to bare 10 cm stems. For the windowsill method, keep some leaves; just remove flowers and any soft new growth.

**Why are my pelargonium leaves turning red in winter?**
Cool temperatures slow chlorophyll production and unmask anthocyanin pigments. Cosmetic only — the plant is fine. The leaves return to normal green when temperatures rise.

**Can I overwinter a hanging-basket ivy-leaved pelargonium the same way?**
Yes. Ivy-leaved (trailing) pelargoniums respond to the same three methods. Take cuttings from the longest healthy trailing stems for the easiest result.
