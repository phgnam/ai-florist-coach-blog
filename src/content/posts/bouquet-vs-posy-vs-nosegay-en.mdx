## Quick Answer

A **bouquet** is a general term for a tied bunch of flowers; a **posy** is a small round bouquet (usually 15–25 stems, fits in one hand); a **nosegay** is historically a fragrant posy designed to be held to the nose. Modern florists also use **tussie-mussie** (a Victorian fragrant nosegay), **hand-tied** (loose round bouquet), **biedermeier** (concentric rings of flowers), and **presentation bouquet** (long-stemmed and arm-carried).

## Why are there so many words for "a bunch of flowers"?

The vocabulary developed because florists have, for centuries, made dozens of distinct flower forms — each with a specific shape, intended use, and price point. The main categories:

- **By size**: posy (small) → hand-tied bouquet (medium) → presentation bouquet (large)
- **By construction**: hand-tied (loose round) vs. wired and structured (cascade, biedermeier, shower)
- **By function**: bridal bouquet, sympathy bouquet, gift bouquet, celebration bouquet
- **By era / regional tradition**: nosegay (English), tussie-mussie (Victorian English), biedermeier (Central European), Brideswain (Northern European)

In contemporary florist usage, **bouquet** is the umbrella word and the others are more specific shapes within it. A florist quoting "a bouquet" without qualification almost always means a hand-tied round bouquet of medium size.

**Bouquet**: a general term. Any tied bunch of flowers can be called a bouquet. Sizes range from small (10–15 stems) to large (50+ stems). Most modern florists use "bouquet" by default for hand-tied round arrangements.

**Posy**: specifically a **small, compact, round** bouquet that can be held in one hand. Typical size: 15–25 stems, around 15–20 cm in diameter. The form has historically been associated with **bridesmaids, debutantes, communion children, and small gift bouquets**. Posies are usually wrapped tight and finished with ribbon or a lace doily.

The dividing line is fuzzy but practical:
- If you can hold it comfortably in one hand without your wrist straining, it's probably a posy.
- If you need both hands to hold it, it's a bouquet.

A **bridal posy** is the small handheld variant of a bridal bouquet — popular in vintage-style and outdoor weddings, where a large cascade is impractical. A **bridesmaid posy** is the most common type of bridesmaid bouquet today.

## What is a nosegay? Is it the same as a tussie-mussie?

**Nosegay** is an old English word (from "nose" + "gay" meaning "ornament"). The original nosegay was a **small fragrant bunch of flowers and herbs** held to the nose to mask street odours in the days before sanitation. By the 17th and 18th centuries, nosegays were both functional (against bad smells) and decorative (fashion accessories at court).

**Tussie-mussie** is a Victorian English term for the same thing — a small fragrant bunch, often with specific symbolic meaning (each flower in the tussie-mussie chosen for its meaning in the "language of flowers"). Tussie-mussies were traditionally held in a small silver or gold cone-shaped vase called a **posy holder** (or **tussie-mussie holder**), which preserved the flowers and could be worn as jewellery.

Modern florists use "nosegay" and "tussie-mussie" almost interchangeably, with two distinctions:

- **Nosegay** is more often a generic descriptive term for any small fragrant posy.
- **Tussie-mussie** specifically implies the **symbolic, language-of-flowers tradition** — each flower chosen for what it means.

## What's a hand-tied bouquet? A biedermeier? A cascade?

These are construction styles, not size categories:

**Hand-tied bouquet** — the modern florist default. Stems are gathered in the hand in a **spiral pattern** (each stem placed at a slight angle, all stems running the same direction), then tied at the binding point with twine or ribbon. The result is a domed round bouquet that can stand on its own when set down. Most gift bouquets, bridal bouquets, and presentation bouquets are hand-tied.

**Biedermeier (or "biedermeier-style") bouquet** — a tightly constructed posy with **concentric rings of single flower types** — for example, a centre ring of red roses, surrounded by a ring of lavender, surrounded by a ring of pink ranunculus. Originated in 19th-century Central Europe and was extremely fashionable in mid-Victorian England.

**Cascade bouquet** — a flowing, downward-trailing bouquet that drapes from the bride's hands toward the floor. Popular in the 1920s and 1980s, less common in current weddings but still requested for traditional or church weddings. Requires significant hand-wiring of stems and is considerably more labour-intensive than a hand-tied.

**Pomander** — a spherical flower ball, usually hung from a ribbon. Often used as flower-girl bouquets or as suspended decor at the ceremony.

**Crescent bouquet** — a long curving asymmetric shape, popular in the 1980s. Less common now.

**Presentation bouquet (or "pageant bouquet")** — long-stemmed flowers arranged so they can be carried on the arm rather than in front. Used in beauty pageants, music recitals, awards ceremonies, and theatrical productions ("flowers presented at curtain call").

For 2026 wedding-bouquet pricing across these styles, see our [wedding bouquet cost article](/posts/wedding-bouquet-cost-2026-en/) — a hand-tied bouquet typically costs 30–40% less than a cascade or wired structural bouquet at the same flower spec.

## Why do florists charge different prices for different bouquet styles?

Three reasons:

**Labour.** A hand-tied bouquet takes 30–60 minutes to assemble. A cascade or biedermeier with hand-wired stems can take 2–3 hours. At £30–60/hour for skilled labour, the labour difference alone is £30–120.

**Materials.** Wired bouquets and biedermeiers often need additional florist wire, gutta-percha tape, individual flower picks, and supporting framework. Cascade bouquets sometimes need a foam holder or armature.

**Conditioning time.** Some structured bouquets need to be made the day before and held in cool storage; others are made on the day. Day-before construction is more flexible for the florist (the bouquet is finished and only needs final cooling) but requires longer hold times in the chiller, which is also a cost.

A simple working rule: **doubling the labour roughly doubles the price**. If you want to reduce a wedding-flower budget, the cheapest move is to choose a hand-tied style across the entire wedding party rather than wired cascades for the bride and bridesmaids.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is a hand-tied bouquet the same as a posy?**
A posy is a small hand-tied bouquet, but not all hand-tied bouquets are posies. The words are sometimes used interchangeably for small handheld arrangements. A hand-tied bouquet can also be much larger — bridal hand-tied bouquets are typically 35–50 stems and would not be called posies.

**What's a sympathy bouquet — and is it different from a regular bouquet?**
Yes — a sympathy bouquet has historically been **larger, predominantly white**, and often delivered to a funeral or memorial service rather than to the bereaved family at home. Modern practice varies; smaller white-and-cream gift bouquets to the family are now common.

**Why are wedding bouquets often round and posy-shaped?**
The round, posy-shaped bouquet is photogenic from any angle, doesn't catch on the bride's dress, and complements most ceremony aesthetics. The round form became standard during the 20th century when wedding photography was the dominant cultural reference for bridal bouquets.

**What's a "biedermeier" — is the spelling right?**
Yes — Biedermeier is a term for a 19th-century Central European art-and-design movement. Biedermeier flower arrangements were highly structured, symmetrical, and used concentric rings of single flower types. The style was extremely fashionable in mid-Victorian England as well, where it was associated with bourgeois domestic culture.

**Are nosegays still used today?**
Rarely as functional fragrant bunches, but **the small fragrant posy is back in fashion** for vintage-style weddings, communions, and small celebrations. Some florists call these "modern nosegays" and design them around fragrant flowers (lily of the valley, sweet pea, jasmine).

**What's the simplest way to assemble a hand-tied bouquet at home?**
Gather 15–25 stems of mixed flowers and foliage. Hold the first stem in your non-dominant hand. Add each new stem at a 30–40° angle in the same direction (the spiral). After 8–10 stems you should see the spiral pattern emerge. Tie firmly at the binding point with garden twine, then trim all stems to the same length. Wrap with ribbon or paper.
