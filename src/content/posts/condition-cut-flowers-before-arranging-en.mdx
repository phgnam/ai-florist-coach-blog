## Quick Answer

To condition cut flowers, **unwrap them, remove all leaves below the waterline, recut stems by 1-2 cm with clean sharp tools, place them in clean water with flower food, and let them hydrate for 2-4 hours before arranging**. Woody stems need a longer diagonal cut; hollow stems need gentle handling; dirty buckets shorten vase life immediately.

## What does conditioning cut flowers mean?

Conditioning is the preparation stage between buying or cutting flowers and arranging them. It restores hydration, removes rot-prone foliage, and gives each stem the best chance to drink clean water.

Skipping conditioning is why many bouquets look tired the day after they arrive. Flowers may have travelled dry, sat in warm packaging, or developed blocked stem ends. A fresh cut and clean water reset the system.

## How should I condition a mixed bouquet step by step?

Use this sequence:

1. Remove packaging, elastic bands, and any wet paper.
2. Separate stems by type so delicate flowers are not crushed.
3. Strip leaves that would sit below the waterline.
4. Fill a clean bucket or vase with cool water and flower food.
5. Recut each stem by 1-2 cm.
6. Place stems into water immediately after cutting.
7. Rest the flowers in a cool room for 2-4 hours.

The timing matters. A cut stem starts sealing quickly when exposed to air. Put each stem into water right after recutting instead of trimming the whole bunch and dropping them in later.

## Should I cut stems straight or at an angle?

For most flowers, cut stems at a **45-degree angle**. This increases drinking surface and prevents the stem end from sitting flat against the bottom of the vase.

Use different handling for different stems:

- **Soft green stems**: clean diagonal cut with snips or a floral knife.
- **Woody stems**: longer diagonal cut; do not smash the stem.
- **Hollow stems**: gentle cut, then keep upright so stems do not split.
- **Milky sap stems**: seal sap first if needed, then hydrate separately.

Do not use blunt scissors that crush stems. Crushed tissue blocks water uptake and gives bacteria more surface to colonise.

## Which leaves and flowers should I remove?

Remove every leaf that would be underwater. Submerged leaves rot quickly, feed bacteria, and turn vase water cloudy.

Also remove:

- Broken petals.
- Brown guard petals if they are genuinely damaged.
- Side shoots that clutter the lower stem.
- Any stem that smells sour or feels slimy.

Keep healthy upper foliage if it improves the arrangement, but do not let it crowd the vase neck. Good airflow around flower heads reduces mould.

## Do I need flower food?

Yes, especially for bought flowers. Commercial flower food contains three useful things: sugar for energy, acidifier to improve water uptake, and a biocide to slow bacteria.

Use the packet at the correct dilution. Half a packet in a full vase is too weak; two packets in a small vase can be too strong. If you do not have flower food, clean water plus frequent changes is better than homemade mixtures with guessed sugar levels.

Change water every two days if the bouquet is mixed or if the room is warm. Recut stems at the same time if the ends look brown or slippery.

## How long should flowers rest before arranging?

Most flowers benefit from **2-4 hours** of hydration before arranging. Roses, hydrangeas, lilacs, and other thirsty stems may need overnight conditioning in a cool room.

Arrange immediately only when flowers are already fully hydrated and fresh from a florist bucket. If stems feel limp, leaves are soft, or heads are nodding, condition first.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I condition flowers in the final vase?**
Yes, if the vase is clean and spacious. For a complex arrangement, conditioning in a bucket is easier because stems can hydrate straight before being designed.

**Should I use warm or cold water for cut flowers?**
Cool room-temperature water works for most flowers. Very cold water can slow uptake, while hot water stresses delicate stems.

**Do I need to remove rose thorns?**
Remove only thorns that sit below the waterline or interfere with handling. Stripping too aggressively wounds the stem and can shorten vase life.

**Why do flowers wilt after I cut the stems?**
The stem may have been left out of water too long, cut with blunt tools, or placed in dirty water. Recut cleanly and hydrate in fresh water with flower food.

**Can I condition grocery-store flowers?**
Yes. Grocery flowers often need conditioning more than florist-prepped flowers because they may have spent longer in packaging and warm display areas.
