## Quick Answer

Plant most spring-flowering bulbs from late September through November, when the soil is below about 15 °C but before it freezes. Daffodils and crocus go in first, hyacinths next, tulips last (October–early December). Plant at three times the bulb's height deep, pointed end up, in well-drained soil, and water once after planting unless rain is forecast.

## Why do bulbs have to go in the ground in autumn?

Spring-flowering bulbs need a sustained cold period — typically 12 to 16 weeks below 9 °C — to trigger the chemical changes inside the bulb that produce a flower stem the following spring. This is called vernalisation. Without enough cold, you get foliage and a stunted, sometimes flowerless stem; with full vernalisation you get the proper flowering display the bulb was bred for.

Buying bulbs in autumn and waiting until spring to plant them does not work. The cold has to happen while the bulb is in damp soil where it can also begin growing roots. A dry packet on a kitchen shelf goes through the calendar cold period but cannot put down roots, so when warmth arrives there is nothing anchoring the future stem.

There are a few exceptions. Pre-chilled tulips and hyacinths sold for indoor forcing have already had their cold treatment in commercial chillers, and you can plant them in pots and bring them indoors after roots establish. The [grow daffodils in pots indoors](/posts/grow-daffodils-in-pots-indoors-en/) guide covers that pre-chill workflow for paperwhites and forced bulbs.

## What month should each bulb go in?

The right window depends on what part of the bulb's life cycle is most temperature-sensitive. Daffodils start rooting at warmer soil temperatures than tulips, so they go in earlier; tulips actively prefer cold soil and resist tulip-specific fungi better when planted later.

| Bulb | Best planting window (Northern Hemisphere) | Soil temperature |
|---|---|---|
| Daffodil (*Narcissus*) | Late Sept – early Nov | 15 °C and falling |
| Crocus | Sept – early Nov | 15 °C and falling |
| Snowdrop (*Galanthus*) | Sept – early Oct (or "in the green" in spring) | 15 °C |
| Hyacinth | Oct – early Nov | 12 °C |
| Tulip | Late Oct – early Dec | 10 °C |
| Allium | Late Sept – early Nov | 12 °C |
| Iris reticulata | Sept – Oct | 12 °C |
| Anemone (*A. blanda*, *A. coronaria*) | Sept – Oct (after 4-hour pre-soak) | 12 °C |

If the calendar is past the recommended window, plant anyway — late autumn planting often produces a slightly later, slightly shorter flower than ideal-timing planting, but it nearly always still flowers. The only true mistake is leaving the bulbs in the bag until spring.

For a southern-hemisphere garden, simply shift everything by six months: plant in March–May for an October flowering season.

## How deep and how far apart should bulbs be planted?

The standard rule is three times the bulb's own height deep, measured from the base of the bulb to the soil surface. A 5 cm tall daffodil bulb sits with its base about 15 cm below the surface; a 4 cm tulip bulb sits with its base about 12 cm below.

Spacing is usually two times the bulb's width between bulbs for a natural drift, or close-spaced (touching but not stacked) in pots and concentrated displays.

| Bulb | Planting depth (top of bulb) | Spacing in beds | Pot density |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daffodil large-flowered | 12–15 cm | 8–12 cm | Touching |
| Daffodil dwarf (*Tête-à-Tête*) | 8–10 cm | 5–8 cm | Touching |
| Tulip | 12–15 cm | 8–12 cm | Touching |
| Hyacinth | 10 cm | 8–10 cm | Almost touching |
| Crocus | 7–8 cm | 4–6 cm | Touching |
| Allium giganteum | 15–20 cm | 20–25 cm | Sparse |
| Iris reticulata | 7–8 cm | 5 cm | Touching |

Two refinements matter. **In heavy clay soils, plant slightly shallower** (top of bulb 8–10 cm down for tulips) so the bulb is not sitting in waterlogged ground. **In sandy soils, plant slightly deeper** (top of bulb 15 cm down for tulips) so winter cold does not chill them too aggressively and so squirrels have a harder time finding them.

For a layered "bulb lasagne" pot, plant the largest bulbs at the deepest level (tulips at 18 cm), the next size up (daffodils at 12 cm), and the smallest (crocus at 6 cm). Each layer pushes through the soil above and they flower in succession from late winter to mid spring.

## How do you stop squirrels and mice from digging bulbs up?

Crocus and tulip bulbs are the most heavily targeted by squirrels and rodents; daffodils and alliums are usually left alone because they contain bitter alkaloids.

Five practical methods, in order of how often they actually work:

1. **Bury the bulbs at full recommended depth.** Squirrels rarely dig deeper than 8 cm. A tulip at 15 cm is much safer than one at 8 cm.
2. **Cover the planting area with chicken wire** for the first few weeks. Pin the wire down with garden staples; remove it once shoots emerge in spring.
3. **Mix daffodil bulbs into the same hole.** The daffodil's bitterness deters digging across the whole patch.
4. **Plant in pots indoors over winter** and move outside in spring once the squirrel pressure eases.
5. **Sprinkle a bulb-deterrent spray** at planting (varies in effectiveness; expect to reapply after rain).

Avoid the classic mistake of leaving the empty bulb-papers on the soil after planting. The papery scales smell strongly and attract squirrels to the freshly disturbed earth, undoing the rest of your protection.

## Should you fertilise bulbs at planting?

Yes, lightly, but not nitrogen-heavy fertilisers. Bulbs already contain almost everything they need to flower in their first spring; the bulb is essentially a packed lunch for the plant. What helps is bone meal or a low-nitrogen bulb fertiliser at planting to support the root system that establishes through autumn and winter.

Apply a small handful of bone meal in the bottom of the planting hole (mixed lightly into the soil so it is not in direct contact with the bulb base), then plant on top. Avoid manure or high-nitrogen feeds; they encourage soft foliage growth at the expense of flower buds and can rot bulbs in cold wet soil.

After flowering in spring, give a liquid potassium-rich feed (the same tomato feed used for flowering plants) every two weeks for six weeks. This is what builds next year's flower inside the bulb. The [tomato feed for flowers](/posts/tomato-feed-flowers-en/) post explains the same chemistry for non-bulb flowers.

Cut off spent flowers but do not cut the leaves — let them yellow naturally over six to eight weeks. Premature leaf removal is the most common cause of "tulips that flowered the first year and never came back."

## How do you plant bulbs in pots that survive winter?

Pot-grown bulbs face two extra risks: the soil freezes solid faster in a pot than in the ground, and pots dry out in mild winter spells when ground-planted bulbs are still moist. Both are manageable.

Choose a pot at least 25 cm tall so the bulbs sit at full depth (12–15 cm down for tulips and daffodils). Use a peat-free bulb compost or 50/50 multipurpose compost and grit. Plant bulbs touching but not stacked, water once thoroughly, and place the pot in a cold frame, an unheated shed, or against a north wall where it will get cold but not the worst of the wind.

In freezing climates, group several pots together and wrap them in hessian or bubble wrap for the coldest weeks. Bring them out to a visible spot once the first shoots are 5 cm tall.

For balcony growers, the [autumn-planting balcony bulbs](/posts/balcony-flower-pots-fall-en/) post has aspect-specific advice for cold-hardy pots, and the [winterproofing balcony flowers](/posts/winterproof-balcony-flowers-en/) post covers protecting pots in deep-freeze regions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I plant tulip bulbs in December?**
Yes, as long as the soil is workable. Tulips planted in December usually flower a little later in spring but otherwise perform normally. They are the most cold-tolerant of the common spring bulbs.

**What if I find unplanted bulbs in January?**
Plant them anyway, as long as the ground is not frozen solid. They will likely flower late or short the first year and recover by year two. A late planting beats no planting.

**Do bulbs need full sun in spring?**
Most do, but they are flexible because spring sunlight is partial through bare deciduous trees. Daffodils, crocus, and snowdrops handle dappled shade well; tulips and hyacinths flower best in 6+ hours of direct spring sun.

**Why did my tulips flower one year and never again?**
Most modern tulips are bred for one strong show year, then declining returns. Either treat them as annuals, or choose perennial-tendency species tulips (*Tulipa tarda*, *T. clusiana*, *T. sylvestris*) for reliable repeats.

**Can I plant bulbs in the same spot every year?**
Daffodils, alliums, and crocus yes. Tulips no — replant in a different spot or refresh the soil each autumn to avoid soil-borne tulip fire (*Botrytis tulipae*).
