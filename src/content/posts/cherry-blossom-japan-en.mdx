## Quick Answer

Cherry blossoms (*sakura*) bloom across Japan from late March to early May, sweeping north from Okinawa to Hokkaido in a "cherry blossom front" the Japan Meteorological Corporation forecasts every year. Tokyo and Kyoto peak around early April; the bloom in any one location lasts only 7–10 days. Famous viewing sites include Mount Yoshino, Hirosaki Park, and Ueno Park.

## What is the cherry blossom and why does Japan celebrate it?

A cherry blossom is the flower of any species in the *Prunus* genus belonging to the cherry family — most often *Prunus serrulata* in Japan, with the cultivar *Somei Yoshino* being the variety most photographed and most planted. The flowers appear in spring, last about a week per tree, and fall before the leaves emerge.

Japan celebrates them because *sakura* is the cultural shorthand for the entire concept of *mono no aware* — the bittersweet awareness that beauty is fleeting. The annual *hanami* (flower-viewing) tradition, in which families and colleagues picnic under blossoming trees, dates back over a thousand years and is still observed nationally today.

## When do cherry blossoms bloom across Japan?

The bloom moves like weather. The Japan Meteorological Corporation publishes the *sakura zensen* ("cherry blossom front") forecast every January, predicting peak bloom dates for around 1,000 sites. Typical timing:

| Region | Typical first bloom | Typical peak |
|---|---|---|
| Okinawa (sub-tropical south) | mid-January | early February |
| Kyushu (Fukuoka, Nagasaki) | mid-March | late March |
| Kansai (Kyoto, Osaka) | late March | early April |
| Kanto (Tokyo) | late March | early April |
| Tohoku (Sendai, Hirosaki) | mid-April | late April |
| Hokkaido (Sapporo) | late April | early May |

The bloom is sensitive to temperature — a warm February can bring the date forward by a week, a cool spring can push it back the same. Plan with a 5–7 day window, not a single day.

## Where are the best places in Japan to see cherry blossoms?

Five viewing sites are repeatedly singled out by Japanese travel boards and *hanami* veterans:

- **Mount Yoshino (Nara prefecture)** — over 30,000 trees in four altitude bands, so the bloom moves up the mountain over 2–3 weeks. The *senbonzakura* ("thousand-tree view") is unmatched anywhere in the country.
- **Hirosaki Park (Aomori)** — the late-April bloom of 2,600 trees around Hirosaki Castle's moat, with petals carpeting the water in pink, is on every photographer's list.
- **Ueno Park (Tokyo)** — central, accessible, around 1,000 trees lining the main path. Crowded — go early morning.
- **Philosopher's Path (Kyoto)** — a 2 km canal walk lined with sakura, quieter at dawn than the city's temple sites.
- **Goryokaku (Hakodate)** — a star-shaped fort surrounded by 1,600 trees; the aerial view from the Goryokaku Tower is iconic.

## What does cherry blossom mean in Japanese culture?

*Sakura* carries a layered meaning in Japan that goes beyond "spring" or "beauty":

**Impermanence (*mujō*).** The week-long bloom is a Buddhist meditation made visible. Falling petals (*hanafubuki*) are read as the same letting-go that defines a life well lived.

**Renewal and the new year.** The Japanese fiscal and academic year both begin in April, the cherry-blossom month — first days of school and first days of work happen, traditionally, under the trees.

**Sacrifice.** Sakura was historically associated with samurai (and, less happily, with kamikaze pilots in World War II) for the same reason: a beautiful life that ends abruptly at full flowering.

## Can you grow cherry blossoms outside Japan?

Yes — *Prunus serrulata* and the more cold-hardy *Prunus × yedoensis* grow well anywhere with a temperate climate that has a defined winter chill (USDA zones 5–8 cover most of the cultivar range). Washington D.C.'s Tidal Basin trees, gifted by Japan in 1912, are the most famous non-Japanese example, but cherry blossom festivals now run in Vancouver, Bonn, Stockholm, Macon (Georgia), and Hanoi.

What you can't easily reproduce outside Japan is the *cultural* experience of *hanami* — the social ritual of the picnic under the trees with its etiquette around blue tarpaulin placement, food sharing, and silence at dusk. That's worth the trip even if your home city has a sakura festival of its own.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do cherry blossoms last on the tree?**
A single tree's bloom typically lasts 7–10 days from first opening to last petal fall. The exact span varies with weather: a warm calm week can produce a 14-day display, while a single hard rain can end the bloom in 48 hours.

**What is the difference between cherry blossom and plum blossom?**
Cherry blossom (*sakura*) flowers have a distinctive split at the tip of each petal and bloom on long stems clustered together; plum blossom (*ume*) flowers have rounded petals and grow directly from the branch with very short stems. Plum blossom blooms 4–8 weeks earlier, often through snow. See [What is the plum blossom?](/posts/plum-blossom-mei-hua-en/) for the full distinction.

**Where can I see cherry blossoms outside Japan?**
The most famous overseas displays are in Washington D.C. (Tidal Basin), Vancouver (Stanley Park), Bonn (Heerstrasse), Stockholm (Kungsträdgården), and Macon, Georgia. Hanoi's Lotte Center and Seoul's Yeouido area also host annual festivals.

**Are cherry blossoms safe for cats and dogs?**
Cherry blossom flowers themselves are non-toxic in small amounts, but the leaves, stems, and seeds (pits) of cherry trees contain cyanogenic compounds that are toxic to cats, dogs, and horses. Keep fallen branches out of reach. See the full reference in [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).

**Can you eat cherry blossoms?**
Yes — salt-pickled cherry blossoms (*sakura no shiozuke*) are a Japanese ingredient used to flavour rice, tea, and sweets. The blossoms must be the edible *yaezakura* variety and properly cured; supermarket-grade cherry trees may have been sprayed.
