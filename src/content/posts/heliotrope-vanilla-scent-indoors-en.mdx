## Quick Answer

Heliotrope (*Heliotropium arborescens*) only emits its strong vanilla-and-almond scent when the flowers are warm and bathed in direct light — in cool, dim indoor conditions, the volatile fragrance compounds simply do not evaporate fast enough to be noticed. To bring the scent back, place the plant in your sunniest indoor spot, ideally above 18 °C in mid-afternoon, and choose a known fragrant cultivar like 'Marine' or 'White Lady'. Modern compact bedding cultivars are bred for colour, not scent.

## What is heliotrope and why is it called the "cherry pie plant"?

*Heliotropium arborescens* is a tender shrubby perennial native to Peru, grown in temperate gardens as a half-hardy annual or container plant. The dark green crinkled leaves carry dense flat heads of small, deeply purple or white tubular flowers from late spring through to first frost.

The English nickname "cherry pie plant" comes from the scent. A warm, well-grown plant smells overwhelmingly of vanilla, almond, and warm pastry — the air around it on a still afternoon is genuinely reminiscent of a baking cherry pie. Victorian gardeners regarded heliotrope as one of the most fragrant flowers in cultivation and grew it both outdoors as bedding and indoors as a winter conservatory plant.

## Why does heliotrope smell strongly outside but barely indoors?

Floral fragrance in heliotrope (and most flowers) is produced and released by tiny epidermal cells on the petals. Several environmental factors decide how much scent reaches your nose.

**Temperature.** Volatile fragrance compounds (esters, terpenes, aromatic alcohols) evaporate faster at higher temperatures. Heliotrope's vanilla-like benzaldehyde and methyl benzoate compounds are especially temperature-sensitive — at 25 °C they release dramatically more aroma than at 16 °C. A warm summer afternoon is the peak. A cool spring morning or a 19 °C indoor room is muted.

**Light.** Heliotrope's fragrance production is partially light-driven. Flowers in full direct sunlight produce more aromatic compounds than the same flowers in shade. The species name *Heliotropium* (sun-turning) reflects the genus's strong solar orientation; this is not just movement but metabolism.

**Air movement.** Outdoors, light air currents disperse fragrance to a much wider zone than the immediate neighbourhood of the plant. Indoors, fragrance pools immediately around the flowers and is barely noticed unless you put your nose right in.

**Humidity.** Moderate humidity holds fragrance longer in the air. Very dry indoor heating evaporates and disperses the volatiles too quickly to be perceived as a scent.

The combined effect: a heliotrope in a hot, sunny, breezy garden in July smells extraordinary. The same plant in a cool, dim, dry living room in November smells almost like nothing.

## How do I bring the scent back indoors?

Several adjustments together usually rescue indoor heliotrope scent.

1. **Move to your brightest spot.** A south- or west-facing windowsill is essential. East-facing is acceptable in summer but not enough in winter.
2. **Maintain 18–24 °C through midday and early afternoon.** Cooler nights are fine — flowers release their highest scent during warm afternoons.
3. **Avoid central-heating dryness.** Group with other plants on a tray of damp pebbles to maintain local humidity around 45–55%.
4. **Smell the flowers in the warmest part of the day.** Mid afternoon, after a few hours of sun on the petals, is peak.
5. **Open a window briefly** when checking — a slow air movement helps you notice the scent rather than the warmer air collecting fragrance into a single blob right at the petals.
6. **Choose the right cultivar.** Modern compact bedding heliotropes (especially F1 hybrids bred for uniform pot performance) often have weak or absent fragrance. Older, taller cultivars retain the strong vanilla scent.

## Which heliotrope cultivars actually smell strongly?

The single biggest factor in indoor scent quality is which cultivar you bought. The newer the breeding line, the more likely fragrance was traded for compactness or colour intensity.

| Cultivar | Scent | Habit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 'Marine' | Strong vanilla | 50–60 cm | The classic fragrant cultivar; deep violet flowers. |
| 'White Lady' | Strong | 40–50 cm | Pure white, especially fragrant in warm weather. |
| 'Chatsworth' | Very strong | 40–60 cm | A well-loved heritage cultivar; deep purple. |
| 'Mary Fox' | Strong | 50 cm | Old cultivar; rich deep purple, large flower heads. |
| 'Princess Marina' | Strong | 40 cm | Deep purple with a slight cherry-pie note. |
| Heliotropium 'Aureum' | Mild | 40 cm | Yellow-green leaves; bred mostly for foliage. |
| 'Atlantis' / 'Marino Blue' | Weak to mild | 25–35 cm | Compact bedding hybrid; bred for colour and habit, not scent. |
| 'Iowa' / 'Nautilus' series | Weak | 25–30 cm | Bedding hybrids — almost no scent. |

If you cannot identify the cultivar in front of you, gently warm a flower head between your fingers for 10–20 seconds and sniff. If a noticeable vanilla note develops, you have a fragrant variety; if not, accept the plant as a colour subject only.

## How do I overwinter a fragrant heliotrope to keep it from year to year?

In its native Peru, heliotrope is a soft-stemmed shrub that lives for many years. In temperate gardens, it is treated as an annual only because it cannot tolerate frost. With a little effort, you can carry over the same plant indefinitely, which preserves the cultivar's flavour year on year.

**Method:**

1. **Bring the plant indoors before the first hard frost.** Once stems have been frosted, recovery is poor.
2. **Trim by about a third** to reduce the leaf area that has to be supported on a sunny windowsill.
3. **Repot if rootbound** into the smallest pot that still holds the rootball comfortably — heliotrope flowers better when slightly pot-bound.
4. **Place in your brightest cool window** (10–18 °C overnight is ideal).
5. **Reduce watering.** Let the top 2 cm of compost dry between waterings; avoid sitting in water.
6. **Feed lightly only when growth restarts** in spring — a half-strength balanced liquid feed every 4 weeks.
7. **Take softwood cuttings as insurance** in late summer; they root readily.
8. **Move outdoors** in late spring once frost risk has passed.

Stems on overwintered plants harden into woody trunks over 3–4 years, eventually forming a small standard. This is one of the joys of long-term heliotrope care — the plant becomes more attractive with age. For more on long-term container care, see [How do I overwinter a hardy fuchsia outdoors so it survives a wet, cold winter?](/posts/hardy-fuchsia-overwintering-en/).

## How can I propagate heliotrope from cuttings?

Softwood cuttings root easily and let you keep a particularly fragrant cultivar going forever.

**Method (best in late spring or late summer):**

1. Take 8–10 cm cuttings from non-flowering side shoots.
2. Strip the lower leaves, dip in rooting hormone (optional, speeds rooting).
3. Insert in damp gritty compost in a 9 cm pot.
4. Cover with a clear plastic bag or cloche; keep humid.
5. Place at 18–22 °C in bright but indirect light.
6. Roots form in 3–4 weeks.
7. Pot on once a small white root tip emerges from the drainage hole.

Always take more cuttings than you need — heliotrope cuttings sometimes sit motionless for 2 weeks before suddenly rooting, and a few losses are normal.

## Are heliotropes safe for homes with pets?

No — and this matters because the plant is most often grown indoors as a fragrant houseplant. *Heliotropium arborescens* contains pyrrolizidine alkaloids, the same family of toxic compounds found in some weedy *Senecio* species. Ingestion by cats, dogs, or grazing livestock can cause liver damage. Symptoms develop slowly and may not be obvious until the toxin has accumulated.

Practical guidance:

- Keep heliotrope on shelves, hanging baskets, or window ledges out of reach of curious cats and dogs.
- Do not place in households with cats that climb to drink from foliage water.
- Do not use heliotrope flowers in cut arrangements where pets might chew the spent stems.
- Wash your hands after handling, especially before food preparation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is my heliotrope flowering but the leaves are wilting in the afternoon?**
Almost always a sign of inadequate water on a hot day. Heliotrope wilts dramatically when dry but recovers within an hour of a thorough soak. Repeated severe wilting weakens the plant and can trigger early flower drop. Water deeply rather than little-and-often.

**My heliotrope flowers are fading to a dirty grey colour. Is that normal?**
Yes — older flowers naturally fade from saturated purple to pale grey-violet. Deadheading the spent heads (whole panicles, not individual flowers) keeps the plant looking fresh and encourages a second flush of stronger-coloured blooms.

**Can I grow heliotrope from seed instead of cuttings?**
Yes, but seed-grown plants are more variable in scent and flower colour than cuttings. Sow at 18–22 °C 10–12 weeks before last frost. Germination takes 14–28 days. Use the seedlings to test which plants in your batch are most fragrant, then take cuttings from those for the future.

**Why does my heliotrope leaves look greasy or sticky?**
Probably whitefly or aphid honeydew. Inspect under the leaves for tiny insects or sticky residue. Wash leaves with mild soapy water or apply an organic insecticidal soap. Indoor heliotropes are particularly prone in dry warm winter conditions.

**Is the scent of heliotrope the same as the artificial vanilla in baking?**
Surprisingly, yes — they share several aromatic compounds, including methyl benzoate and benzaldehyde, plus heliotrope's signature heliotropin (also called piperonal). Heliotropin is even sometimes used in commercial perfumery and food flavour to evoke a "warm pastry" note. The plant's nickname "cherry pie" is therefore not accidental.
