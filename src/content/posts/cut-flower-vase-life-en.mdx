## Quick Answer

Most popular cut flowers last between 5 and 14 days in a vase. Carnations and chrysanthemums top the list at up to 21 days; tulips and sweet peas sit at the short end at 4–7 days. The flower variety sets the ceiling; vase hygiene, water temperature, and removing submerged foliage decide whether you reach it.

## Why does cut flower vase life vary so much?

A "cut flower" is a wound: the bloom keeps respiring, transpiring, and trying to open after it has been severed from its root system. How long it lasts in your vase depends on three things working together. First is the **species and cultivar** — a chrysanthemum is genetically built for a long shelf life; a tulip is not. Second is **post-harvest handling** — the cooler the chain from the farm to your kitchen, and the fresher the flower at the point of sale, the more vase life you start with. Third is **what you do at home** — the cleanest of vases and the freshest of flowers will both fail in 48 hours if leaves rot below the waterline.

The numbers below assume professionally handled flowers and the basic care routine in [How to arrange supermarket flowers like a florist](/posts/how-to-arrange-flowers-en/). Treat them as ceilings, not guarantees.

## What habits actually extend cut flower vase life?

Five habits do more than every flower-food sachet combined:

**1. Strip every leaf below the waterline.** This is the single highest-leverage change. Submerged leaves rot within hours and produce the bacteria that block xylem vessels and kill flowers. A florist will spend longer stripping leaves than arranging stems.

**2. Re-cut stems on a 45-degree angle, under water if possible.** A diagonal cut increases the surface area for water uptake and prevents the cut end from sealing flat against the vase floor.

**3. Use clean, lukewarm water — and change it every 2 days.** Bacteria, not lack of food, is what kills cut flowers. A scrubbed vase with fresh water doubles vase life. The exception: bulb flowers (tulip, daffodil, hyacinth) prefer cold water.

**4. Keep flowers away from heat, direct sun, and ripening fruit.** Ripening fruit (especially apples and bananas) emits ethylene, which accelerates senescence in ethylene-sensitive flowers — that includes carnations, snapdragons, sweet peas, delphiniums, and most lilies.

**5. Use the flower-food sachet — but at the right dose.** Commercial flower food contains a sugar (energy), an acidifier (helps water uptake), and a biocide (controls bacteria). Under-dosing is worse than not using it at all because you get the sugar without the biocide and feed the bacteria. Follow the sachet's water-volume instructions exactly.

## Which cut flowers are worth the money for vase life?

If you measure cost per day of bloom, the winners are not glamorous: **carnation, chrysanthemum, and alstroemeria** are usually the cheapest stems in a florist's bucket and last 2–3 weeks. The AI Florist Coach app's [budget planner](/tools/budget-planner/) ranks every common cut flower by cost-per-day-of-bloom rather than cost-per-stem, which is the metric that actually matters for events.

The big-ticket flowers — peony, garden rose, ranunculus — are not bad value if you accept that you are paying for impact, not endurance. A peony in full bloom for 5 days is doing different work from a chrysanthemum for 21.

## Why do my cut flowers wilt in two days when they're supposed to last a week?

Almost always one of these:

- **Submerged foliage rotting in the vase.** Strip every leaf that sits below the waterline.
- **Stems that sealed before they reached water.** Cut a fresh 2cm off every stem at 45 degrees, then put them in water within 30 seconds.
- **A vase with old slime in it.** Scrub it with hot soapy water and rinse before refilling.
- **Heat or direct sun.** Move the arrangement; vase life on a sunny windowsill is half what it is in a cooler corner.
- **Buds cut too tight.** Some flowers (peony, ranunculus, lisianthus) sometimes ship with buds too closed to ever open. There is no cure once they're in the vase.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Which cut flowers last the longest in a vase?**
Carnations and chrysanthemums last the longest at 14–21 days, followed by alstroemeria at 10–14 days. Among "premium" flowers, orchids hold up best at 10–14 days. Garden-grown stems generally last less time than commercially-grown ones because they have not been through the cool chain.

**Which cut flowers have the shortest vase life?**
Sweet peas (3–5 days), tulips (4–7 days), peonies (5–8 days from soft bud), ranunculus and anemones (5–7 days). All five are bought for their beauty, not their endurance — accept the trade-off.

**Does sugar in the water actually help cut flowers last longer?**
Yes, but only when paired with a biocide. Sugar alone feeds bacteria and shortens vase life. The flower-food sachet is sugar plus a biocide plus an acidifier, dosed correctly. Home substitutes (a teaspoon of sugar, a drop of bleach, a splash of vinegar in 500 ml water) approximate the sachet but rarely match it.

**Should I refrigerate cut flowers overnight?**
For commercial florists, yes — cool storage at 2–5°C extends vase life dramatically. At home, only if you have a dedicated drinks fridge: most kitchen fridges store fruit, which produces ethylene and accelerates senescence. A cool, dark room overnight is a safer home equivalent.

**Why does the same flower last longer for some people than others?**
Three reasons: how fresh the flower was at point of sale (varies by retailer), how clean the vase is, and how diligently leaves are stripped below the waterline. Skill explains most of the difference; luck explains very little.

**Can I revive a wilted cut flower?**
Sometimes. Re-cut the stem 2 cm and stand it in lukewarm water for 30 minutes. For hydrangeas specifically, submerge the entire head in cool water for an hour — they rehydrate through their petals. Once a flower's petals have started to brown at the edges, it is gone.
