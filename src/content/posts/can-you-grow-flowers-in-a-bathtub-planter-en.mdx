## Quick Answer

Yes — an old bathtub makes a generous flower planter if you **drill 4–6 drainage holes**, add a 5 cm grit layer, fill with a peat-free compost-and-grit mix, and choose deep-rooting flowers like **dahlias**, **cosmos**, **zinnias**, **rudbeckia**, **calendula**, or **sweet peas** trained on a tripod. A 1.7 m enamel tub holds roughly 200 litres of compost — enough root space for 4–6 substantial plants plus trailing edges of alyssum or lobelia.

## Why does a bathtub make a good planter?

Most recycled containers fail one of three tests: too small, too shallow, or no drainage. A bathtub passes all three:

- **Generous root volume** — 150–250 litres depending on size
- **Depth of 35–45 cm** — fits the rootball of most cottage-garden flowers
- **Heavy and stable** — won't blow over on a windy patio

Cast-iron and enamel tubs add visual interest because they look both industrial and faintly elegant. Modern acrylic tubs are lighter and easier to drill but warm up faster in summer, which can stress roots.

The work is mostly the prep: drilling the drainage and getting the mix right.

## How do you drill drainage holes in a bathtub?

Required tools:

- **Diamond-tipped hole saw** (40–50 mm) for cast iron or enamel
- **Standard masonry bit** (10 mm) for acrylic
- **Cordless drill** with low-speed setting
- **Water spray bottle** to keep the bit cool

Process:

- Mark 4–6 hole positions, evenly spaced along the bottom
- Run a slow trickle of water on the marked spot to keep the drill cool
- Drill on low speed with steady pressure — let the bit do the work
- For enamel tubs, score the enamel with a centre punch first to prevent skidding
- Dust the drilled hole with a brush after — the area around the drainpipe is often already weakened, so leave the existing drain hole open as one of your drainage points

If you don't trust yourself with power tools, use the existing plug hole as the only drainage point and add a 10 cm gravel layer at the bottom for water reservoir. The compost stays soggier this way, so reduce watering and pick rot-tolerant flowers (calendula, marigold) rather than fussy ones.

## What mix should fill the bathtub?

A bathtub holds enough compost that buying premium bags becomes expensive. The cheaper, equally good answer:

- **70 percent peat-free multipurpose compost**
- **20 percent garden compost or well-rotted manure** for slow nutrients
- **10 percent grit, perlite, or coarse sand** for drainage

Layer:

- 5 cm of coarse gravel at the bottom for fast drainage at the holes
- Tub mostly filled with the compost mix, leaving 5 cm clear at the top for watering
- Top mulch with composted bark or fine gravel after planting to slow evaporation

Don't fill the tub to the brim with pure compost — you will lose 3–5 cm to settling within a week and the roots end up shallow.

## Which flowers grow best in a bathtub planter?

Match the bathtub to the available sun:

**Sunny tub (6+ hours direct sun)** — the best uses for a bathtub:

- **Dahlias** (compact 60–90 cm cultivars) — single tub holds 3 plants, big floral impact August–October
- **Cosmos** — tall 1.2 m airy stems, sown direct, blooming June–October
- **Zinnias** — heat-loving cut-flower workhorses
- **Rudbeckia 'Cherokee Sunset'** — long-lasting golden colour
- **Calendula** — self-sows for years
- **Sweet peas** on a 1.5 m tripod at one end of the tub

**Part-shade tub** — gentler choices:

- **Foxgloves** for vertical interest
- **Astrantia** for repeat shade-tolerant flowers
- **Fuchsias** at the trailing edge
- **Heucheras** for foliage colour

**Trailing edges** (every tub benefits):

- Sweet alyssum
- Lobelia
- Bacopa
- Trailing nasturtium

A productive plan for a 1.7 m sunny tub: 3 dahlia tubers in the centre, 2 zinnias at one end, a sweet-pea tripod at the other, alyssum spilling over both long sides.

## How often do you water and feed a bathtub planter?

A bathtub looks big but a hot summer day still strips moisture quickly because of the sun-exposed surface area.

- **Spring (cool weather)**: water every 4–6 days
- **Summer (hot weather)**: water every 2–3 days, daily in heatwaves
- **Autumn**: every 5–7 days
- **Winter**: only if compost dries out completely

Feed every 2 weeks once growth is active, with a balanced liquid fertiliser. Switch to high-potash tomato food when buds form. The compost-plus-manure base feeds the plants for the first 6–8 weeks without supplementary feeding; after that, regular liquid feed makes a visible difference to flower count.

## How do you stop the planter looking bare in winter?

A bathtub looks much sadder than a small pot when its summer flowers die back. Two strategies:

**Plant for year-round structure**: tuck a few small evergreens (boxwood balls, hebes, dwarf pittosporum) at the corners of the tub. Summer flowers fill the space between them; winter, the evergreens give shape.

**Replant for winter**: when summer flowers finish in October, pull most of them and add winter pansies, cyclamen, ornamental cabbages, or small heucheras. The spring bulbs (tulips, narcissus) layered underneath emerge from February onwards. The planter has three completely different looks across the year.

## What should you avoid in a bathtub planter?

- **Skipping drainage** — the most common cause of failure; standing water rots everything within weeks
- **Filling with topsoil from the garden** — too heavy, drains poorly, brings weed seeds and pests
- **Trees or shrubs over 1.5 m** — root pressure cracks the tub or destabilises it
- **Aggressive spreaders** like mint or running bamboo — they take over the volume in a season
- **Placing on a wooden deck without protection** — drainage water stains and rots wooden boards. Use small "feet" (bricks) under the tub

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a filled bathtub planter weigh?

Roughly 250–350 kg when fully wet. Place it on stable, level ground, ideally a concrete or stone slab. A wooden deck needs joist-load checking before installing.

### Does the tub rust over time?

Cast iron with the original enamel intact lasts decades. Chipped enamel exposes iron, which rusts cosmetically but takes 10+ years to weaken structurally. Acrylic tubs do not rust but can crack under heat stress.

### Can I leave the taps on for design?

Yes — old taps look great as a design feature. The tap holes can be sealed with silicone or used as additional drainage if positioned at the top edge of the tub. Don't leave the chrome polish — it looks better aged.

### Will it crack in winter freezes?

Cast iron and enamel can crack if water freezes inside. Either ensure full drainage (no standing water) or empty the tub and tarp it for winter. Acrylic tubs are more freeze-tolerant.

### Is there a tub size too small for flowers?

Below about 100 litre capacity (a child's tub or shallow utility sink), you are basically working with a planter trough. The same flowers work but you lose the deep-rooting advantage; use shallow-rooting choices like marigolds, alyssum, and pansies instead.

### Can I grow vegetables in it instead?

Yes — courgettes, dwarf tomatoes, salad leaves, and herbs all thrive in a tub. The flower advice in this article transfers directly. For mixed planting, edible flowers like calendula and nasturtium combine well with vegetables.
