## Quick Answer

Dragonflies are predators, not pollinators — they don't visit flowers for nectar. To attract them you grow plants that **attract their prey** (midges, mosquitoes, gnats, small flies) and that give them **perches and shelter**. The best combination is a **small still pond**, **water iris** and **rushes** at the margin, **yarrow**, **goldenrod**, **cosmos**, **borage**, and **agastache** in the surrounding border, and dense lawn-edge grasses for emergence.

## Why don't dragonflies visit flowers like bees do?

Adult dragonflies eat live flying insects — they catch midges, mosquitoes, hoverflies, and even small butterflies on the wing using their leg basket. They do not feed on nectar or pollen, so the bee/butterfly approach of "plant lots of nectar-rich flowers" misses the point.

What they need:

- **Prey availability** — abundant small flying insects in the air
- **Water** — almost all dragonflies breed in still or slow-moving freshwater
- **Sun-warm perches** — they are sun-loving and need to thermoregulate
- **Vertical emergence stems** — nymphs climb out of water onto reeds and flower stems before final moult into adulthood

So the flower question becomes: which flowers feed prey and provide perches?

## Which flowers attract the prey dragonflies eat?

Insects most likely to be hunted are small two-winged flies — midges, mosquitoes, gnats, hoverflies, fungus gnats. Plants that attract these:

- **Yarrow (*Achillea*)** — flat umbel heads support hoverflies, parasitic wasps, small flies
- **Goldenrod (*Solidago*)** — late-season insect magnet
- **Cosmos** — open daisy face draws hoverflies and small bees
- **Borage** — produces nectar 24/7, attracts a constant cloud of small visitors
- **Agastache** (anise hyssop) — beloved by hoverflies and small bees
- **Fennel and dill** flowers — umbels for small parasitoid wasps and hoverflies
- **Sweet alyssum** — surprisingly effective for hoverfly populations

Plant these flowers in **drifts of 5–9 plants** rather than singletons. The point is to create a sustained insect cloud that draws dragonflies in.

## Why is water the most important factor?

Without water, dragonflies pass through a garden but do not establish. The full life cycle requires fresh water for nymphs (which can live underwater 1–4 years) and emergence stems above water for adults to climb out.

A pond does not need to be large. The minimum useful size is **about 1 m² and 30 cm deep**. Even a half-barrel pond can host damselfly species. Key features:

- **Sunny position** — dragonflies are sun-warmth dependent
- **Sloping sides or beach** — for nymphs to climb out and adults to land for water
- **No fish** — fish eat dragonfly nymphs; a pond designed for dragonflies should be fish-free
- **Some open water** in the centre — for adults to skim and lay eggs
- **Marginal plants** — providing emergence stems and predator-shelter

## Which marginal flowers should grow at the pond edge?

These do triple duty: nymph emergence stems, adult perches, and prey-attracting blooms.

**Tall vertical stems for emergence** (essential):

- **Yellow flag iris (*Iris pseudacorus*)** — classic British native dragonfly plant
- **Pickerelweed (*Pontederia cordata*)** — purple flower spikes, dragonflies perch on them
- **Common rush (*Juncus effusus*)** — dense vertical stems
- **Bulrush (*Typha minima*)** — small-cultivar version of the bulrush
- **Sweet flag (*Acorus*)** — narrow upright leaves at the margin

**Pond-edge flowers that draw insect prey**:

- **Marsh marigold (*Caltha palustris*)** — early spring nectar for hoverflies
- **Water mint (*Mentha aquatica*)** — flowers attract hoverflies, scent deters mosquito breeding
- **Purple loosestrife (*Lythrum salicaria*)** — long flowering, hoverfly favourite (note: invasive in US wetlands, fine in UK gardens)
- **Skullcap (*Scutellaria*)** — neat blue flower for damp ground

A pond ringed with iris, rushes, and marsh marigold ticks every dragonfly need.

## How do you create dragonfly perches in the garden?

Dragonflies spend a lot of daylight perched on tall, exposed structures, scanning for prey or rivals. Good perches:

- **Tall flower stems** like agastache, cosmos, verbena bonariensis
- **Bare twigs** stuck into a pond margin or border
- **Tall grasses** like *Calamagrostis* or *Miscanthus*
- **Stout dead stems** left in beds over autumn

Verbena bonariensis is a particular favourite — the airy 1.5 m stems give a perfect dragonfly perch with full sky view, while the lavender flower clusters above attract hoverflies and small flies as prey.

## What flowers are best for the surrounding garden borders?

A border that supports dragonfly populations needs sun, height, and high insect activity. The package:

- **Verbena bonariensis** — perch stems plus pollinator magnet
- **Echinacea** — long-flowering, draws hoverflies and small bees
- **Helenium** — late-summer prey-insect attractor
- **Rudbeckia** — same role as helenium, golden colour palette
- **Liatris** — vertical perches with strong nectar pull
- **Sedum 'Autumn Joy'** — autumn boost when many borders fade
- **Cosmos and zinnias** — annual fillers between perennials

Aim for height variation: tall perch plants (1.2–1.5 m), middle-layer flowers (60–90 cm), and ground-cover hoverfly attractors (sweet alyssum, thyme).

## How do you avoid attracting mosquitoes alongside dragonflies?

A common worry: doesn't a small pond attract mosquitoes? It does — but a healthy pond also attracts the dragonflies, damselflies, frogs, and predatory water beetles that eat them.

To keep the balance:

- **Avoid stagnant water** — even a small pump or solar fountain creates surface movement
- **Skip fish** but encourage frogs and toads
- **Add water mint** — its scent is mildly mosquito-deterrent
- **Don't have other still water** in unused buckets, gutters, neglected pots — those are mosquito-only, no predators

A balanced pond produces fewer mosquitoes per garden than no pond at all, because the predators control them.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How quickly do dragonflies arrive after creating a pond?

Adults find new ponds within days when the pond is sunny and weather is warm. Nymph populations build over 1–3 years as eggs are laid and complete the underwater stage.

### Can I attract dragonflies without a pond?

You'll see passing visitors hunting in any insect-rich garden, but a resident breeding population needs water. If a pond is impossible, even a sunken half-barrel pond hosts smaller damselfly species.

### Do dragonflies sting or bite people?

No. They have no sting and rarely bite humans even when handled. They are completely safe to have in number and are excellent natural mosquito control.

### Which dragonfly species will I attract?

Depends on your region and pond size. Common visitors to small UK garden ponds include common darter, southern hawker, broad-bodied chaser, large red damselfly, common blue damselfly, and azure damselfly.

### Do native flowers matter?

Native marginal plants are best for nymph emergence because the timing of plant growth matches local dragonfly emergence cycles. Native flower borders also support a more diverse local insect prey base. Pair with the [flowers for bees guide](/posts/flowers-for-bees-en/) for a wider pollinator-friendly plan.

### Will a small wildlife pond freeze in winter?

Surface yes — the pond freezes over in cold winters but nymphs survive in the deep water. As long as the pond is at least 30 cm deep over the deepest part, dragonfly populations come through fine. Float a tennis ball on the surface to maintain a small ice-free patch in long freezes.
