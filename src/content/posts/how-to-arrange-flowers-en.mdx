Arranging flowers is one of those skills that looks intimidating from the outside but becomes instinctive surprisingly quickly. Professional florists follow a handful of core principles — and once you understand them, your arrangements will look immediately more deliberate, balanced, and beautiful.

## The tools you actually need

You don't need a studio full of equipment. Start with: a sharp pair of **floral scissors or secateurs** (clean cuts matter enormously), a clean vase, **floral foam** if you want structural arrangements, and a spray bottle for keeping delicate blooms misted. That's it. Most professional florists work with far fewer tools than beginners expect.

## Step 1: Prepare your flowers

Before anything touches a vase, preparation is everything.

Strip all **leaves below the waterline** — leaves left in water rot quickly, creating bacteria that blocks stem ends and kills flowers far ahead of schedule. Remove any leaves that will sit below the rim of your vessel.

Cut every stem at a **45-degree angle** under running water or submerged in a basin. The angle increases the surface area available for water uptake; cutting underwater prevents air bubbles from entering the stem and blocking the water channel. Use a clean, sharp blade — a crushed stem from dull scissors absorbs water far less efficiently.

Allow your flowers to **condition for at least two hours** (overnight is better) in deep, clean, cool water before arranging. This lets the stems fully hydrate and makes the flowers much more workable.

## Step 2: Establish your structure with focal flowers

Every arrangement needs a **focal flower** — the star that draws the eye first. Roses, peonies, dahlias, and large lilies all make excellent focal flowers. Place these first, distributing them unevenly through the arrangement (the eye finds odd numbers more natural than even numbers). Three or five focal points almost always look better than two or four.

Work in **triangular patterns**. Florists talk about creating an implied triangle across the arrangement — one bloom higher, two lower on either side. This gives depth without symmetry becoming rigid.

## Step 3: Add secondary flowers for volume

Secondary flowers fill the visual space around your focal blooms. Think **hydrangeas, spray roses, freesia, carnations, or stock**. These shouldn't compete with the focal flowers but should support and frame them. They fill holes, add colour variation, and help the overall shape feel generous rather than sparse.

Place secondary flowers at varied heights — resist the instinct to make everything the same length. The most common mistake in amateur arrangements is cutting all stems to the same height, which produces a flat, regimented look. Florists deliberately stagger heights to create depth.

## Step 4: Finish with texture and movement

The final layer is **texture and movement** — small, airy, or wispy elements that make arrangements feel alive rather than static. Baby's breath, eucalyptus, wax flower, sweet peas, and ornamental grasses all serve this purpose beautifully. These are often called **filler flowers**, but in the hands of a skilled florist they define the entire feeling of an arrangement.

Greenery is equally important. A few stems of **eucalyptus or ruscus** give arrangements a natural, organic feeling and draw the eye through the whole arrangement rather than letting it stop at individual blooms.

## Step 5: Shape and refine

Step back. Really step back — at least two metres — and assess the silhouette. Is it balanced? Does it have an interesting shape? Are there any flowers competing awkwardly for space?

Rotate the arrangement. Most arrangements are placed against a wall so they only need to look good from one angle — but rotating reveals holes and imbalances you'd miss otherwise.

Adjust any stems that look too stiff or too similar in height. **The mark of a professional arrangement is that it looks effortless** — even though a lot of small decisions went into it.

## Colour principles for better arrangements

You don't need to be a colour theorist, but three simple approaches always work:

**Monochromatic** (different shades of one colour) — elegant, sophisticated, hard to get wrong. Blush, mid-rose, and deep burgundy together is a classic.

**Analogous** (colours next to each other on the colour wheel) — warm and harmonious. Orange, peach, and yellow together; or lavender, purple, and deep blue.

**Complementary contrast** (opposite colours) — high impact. Purple and yellow; orange and blue; red and white. Use sparingly for maximum effect.

## A note on vessels

The vessel matters as much as the flowers. **The rule of thumb**: the arrangement should be approximately 1.5× the height of the vase. A heavy, low-centred arrangement in a tall vase looks wrong; a tall, airy arrangement in a small bud vase looks unstable.

Wide-mouthed vessels suit lush, romantic arrangements. Tall cylinders suit structured, architectural ones. Simple glass vases show stems beautifully and never compete with the flowers.

## Changing the water

Your beautiful arrangement will last significantly longer with one simple habit: change the water every two days, and re-cut the stems by 1cm each time. The bacteria that builds up in stale water is the primary cause of premature wilting — far more than light or temperature.

Flower arranging is one of those skills where practice genuinely and quickly produces results. Start with three flowers and one type of greenery. Then add one more element each time. Within a few arrangements, you'll have internalized the principles well enough that they become invisible — and your flowers will simply look good.
