## Quick Answer

*Penstemon barbatus* (beardlip penstemon) is one of the most reliably deer-resistant flowering perennials in cultivation. The wiry stems, slightly hairy foliage, and faintly bitter sap make it unappealing to deer, who almost always pass it for sweeter targets like hostas or daylilies. No plant is fully deer-proof — hungry deer in a hard winter will sample anything — but in normal seasons *P. barbatus* and most other dry-loving penstemons survive without fencing or repellent sprays.

## What is *Penstemon barbatus* and why is it called beardlip?

*Penstemon barbatus* is a clump-forming herbaceous perennial native to the dry mountain slopes of the southwestern United States and northern Mexico. The species name *barbatus* (Latin for "bearded") refers to the small tuft of yellow-orange hairs at the lower lip of each flower — a tiny landing pad that helps hummingbirds and long-tongued bees orient on the tubular bloom.

Three traits define the species:

**Tall, airy flower spikes.** Stems reach 75–120 cm and carry sparsely-spaced clusters of long tubular flowers in coral red, pink, or rarely white.

**Long-blooming.** Flowering begins in early summer and continues for 6–8 weeks if deadheaded. In hot climates, it may rebloom in autumn.

**Drought-tolerant.** Native to dry, rocky habitats, *P. barbatus* sulks in rich, moist garden soil. Lean conditions produce stronger, more upright stems.

## Why do deer mostly leave penstemons alone?

Deer browsing is unpredictable, but several plant traits consistently push deer toward easier targets. Penstemons combine most of them.

**Bitter sap.** Penstemon foliage contains low levels of bitter glycosides and tannins. Deer are not poisoned by these but they taste unpleasant on the tongue, and a deer that takes a single test bite usually moves on.

**Slightly hairy or rough foliage.** Many penstemons (including *P. barbatus*) have leaves with a faintly rough surface — fine hairs or a slightly leathery texture. Deer prefer smooth, succulent foliage like that of hostas, lilies, and tulips.

**Wiry, fibrous stems.** Penstemon stems are tougher than herbaceous-perennial average. Deer that browse with the upper-jaw-against-lower-lip motion (deer have no upper incisors) tear stems rather than cleanly biting them, which makes browsing harder work for less reward.

**Native habitat.** Most penstemons evolved in dry, exposed, deer-rich habitats. Their evolutionary success in those conditions itself signals that deer have not driven them out.

**Important caveat.** "Deer-resistant" is not "deer-proof". A pregnant doe or a young deer in early spring will sample almost anything, including penstemons. Local browsing pressure varies enormously — gardens with constant deer foot traffic and limited alternative forage can lose even penstemons. Talk to neighbours about which plants survive locally.

## Which other penstemons hold up well in deer country?

Beardlip is not the only deer-tolerant penstemon. The genus is huge (over 270 species) and several others are at least as tough.

**Western native species (high deer-tolerance):**

- *Penstemon strictus* (Rocky Mountain penstemon) — vivid blue-purple flowers, hardy to USDA 3.
- *Penstemon pinifolius* (pineleaf penstemon) — short shrubby form with needle-like leaves and orange-red tubular flowers.
- *Penstemon eatonii* (firecracker penstemon) — brilliant scarlet, ideal in dry rock gardens.
- *Penstemon palmeri* (Palmer's penstemon) — pale pink, mildly fragrant.
- *Penstemon pseudospectabilis* (desert beardtongue) — pink to magenta, very drought-tolerant.

**Garden hybrids (mixed deer-tolerance):**

- *Penstemon* × *gloxinioides* hybrids ('Husker Red', 'Garnet') — generally avoided by deer.
- 'Sour Grapes' and 'Stapleford Gem' — tolerable but more often nibbled than the species.
- Penstemon 'Raven' — deep purple-red, generally left alone.

**Lower deer-tolerance (caution):**

- *Penstemon digitalis* (foxglove penstemon) — softer, smoother leaves; sometimes browsed.
- Some woolly-leafed European-bred bedding penstemons — reportedly more attractive to deer than the wiry-stemmed species.

If you are in a high-pressure deer garden, plant the western species first and add hybrids only after you confirm what local deer tolerate.

## How do I plant penstemons for the best long-term results?

Penstemons are some of the easiest perennials to grow if you give them what they actually want. The classic mistake is treating them like cottage perennials and planting them in rich, moist border soil.

**Soil:** Lean, gritty, free-draining. A mix of native soil with one-third coarse grit at the planting hole is ideal. Pure compost or rich loam shortens the plant's life dramatically.

**Aspect:** Full sun. Six hours of direct sun is the minimum; eight is better. Light shade in hot climates is acceptable but reduces flowering.

**Spacing:** 30–45 cm apart. Penstemons need air movement at the crown to prevent rot.

**Watering:** Water in well at planting, then taper off. Established plants need only occasional deep watering during prolonged drought.

**Mulching:** Gravel or grit mulch is better than organic mulch. Composted bark holds moisture against the crown and shortens the plant's life.

**Feed:** Almost never. A light dressing of bone meal in spring is enough.

## How do I encourage maximum flowering and rebloom?

The right deadheading and pruning routine extends the season substantially.

**During the season:**

1. Watch each flower stem from the base upwards. Buds open from the bottom up.
2. As each spike finishes, cut the entire flower stem down to a leafy side shoot.
3. Side shoots produce a smaller second flush 4–6 weeks later.
4. Continue throughout summer.

**End of season:**

1. Leave the last flush of stems standing through winter to protect the crown.
2. Cut back to about 5–10 cm in early spring, just before new growth emerges.
3. Do not autumn-prune — it weakens the plant and reduces winter survival.

This is the same "leave-stems-standing-overwinter" approach that works for verbena and many other border perennials. For more on that pruning timing, see [When and how should I cut back Verbena bonariensis without killing it?](/posts/verbena-bonariensis-cutting-back-en/).

## How do I propagate and replace short-lived penstemons?

Many penstemons are short-lived (3–5 years), so propagation insurance is worth setting up early.

**Softwood cuttings (best in late summer):**

1. Take 8–10 cm cuttings from non-flowering side shoots.
2. Strip lower leaves, dip in rooting hormone, insert in damp gritty compost.
3. Keep humid under a clear cloche or bag.
4. Roots form in 4–6 weeks.
5. Pot on after rooting and overwinter in a cold frame.

**Division (less reliable):**

Penstemons do not divide as cleanly as most perennials — the woody crowns split unevenly. Try only on 3-year-old or older plants and only in early spring.

**Seed (slow but useful for natives):**

Most species produce viable seed. Sow in autumn for cold stratification or in early spring at 18–22 °C. Seedlings flower in their second year.

Take cuttings every year of any cultivar you want to keep — by the time the parent plant declines, you have replacements ready. For broader propagation advice, see [Best cottage garden flowers](/posts/best-cottage-garden-flowers-en/).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are penstemons safe for cats and dogs?**
Penstemons are generally regarded as low-toxicity and are not on common pet-poisoning lists, but the bitter glycosides that make them unappealing to deer may also cause mild gastric upset if a pet eats large amounts. For documented pet-safe alternatives, see [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).

**Do penstemons attract pollinators if deer don't eat them?**
Yes — the long tubular flowers are perfect for hummingbirds (in their American native range), bumblebees, and various long-tongued solitary bees. *Penstemon barbatus* in particular is a star hummingbird plant. For more pollinator-friendly choices, see [Best flowers for attracting bees and butterflies](/posts/flowers-for-bees-and-butterflies-en/).

**Why are my penstemons rotting in winter despite being "tough"?**
Almost always wet feet, not cold. Penstemons survive cold well but cannot tolerate sitting in saturated soil at low temperatures. Replant on a slight mound, mix coarse grit into the planting hole, and avoid organic mulch directly against the crown.

**Can I grow penstemons in pots in deer country?**
Yes — pots are an excellent solution where deer pressure is high enough to threaten even penstemons. Use deep (30 cm+) pots filled with a free-draining mix and place near the house where deer are less likely to venture.

**My local deer ate my penstemon anyway. What should I do?**
Treat it as a sign of unusual pressure (drought, deep snow, neighbouring construction). Apply a deer repellent spray for 6–8 weeks, replant fresh stock from cuttings, and consider adding a few traditionally deer-proof shrubs (boxwood, lavender, rosemary) at the edges of the bed as a "first taste" deterrent layer.
