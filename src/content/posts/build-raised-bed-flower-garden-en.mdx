Build a raised bed flower garden using **untreated timber** (cedar, larch, or Douglas fir), **30–45 cm tall**, filled with a mix of **60% topsoil, 30% compost, and 10% perlite**. The ideal width is **120 cm** (reachable from both sides) and any length you have space for. A single 120 × 240 cm bed costs $50–150 in materials and takes 2–3 hours to build.

## Why grow flowers in raised beds?

Raised beds solve several problems that in-ground gardens can't:

**Soil control**: You fill with exactly the soil mix your flowers need, regardless of your native soil. Heavy clay? Rocky subsoil? Compacted builders' fill? None of it matters — the raised bed contains its own growing medium.

**Drainage**: Raised beds drain faster than ground level, which most flowers prefer. Waterlogging kills more garden plants than drought.

**Ergonomics**: A 30–45 cm raised bed reduces bending and kneeling. For gardeners with mobility issues, beds can be built 60–90 cm tall for comfortable seated or standing access.

**Defined spaces**: Raised beds create neat, organized garden areas. They contain spreading plants, keep paths clear, and look intentional even when the planting is wild and exuberant.

**Warmer soil in spring**: Raised soil warms faster than ground level, giving you a head start on the growing season.

## What materials should you use for raised beds? ### Wood (most popular) **Best
choices**: - **Cedar** — naturally rot-resistant, lasts 10–15 years untreated.
Beautiful silver-grey weathering. The gold standard for raised beds. - **Larch**
— similar durability to cedar at a lower price. Weathers to a warm grey. -
**Douglas fir** — less rot-resistant than cedar (5–8 years) but affordable and
widely available. A good budget choice. **Avoid**: - **Pressure-treated timber**
— older CCA-treated wood contains arsenic. Modern ACQ treatment is safer but
still debated for vegetable gardens. Fine for purely ornamental flower beds. -
**Railway sleepers** — old ones are soaked in creosote (toxic). New "garden
sleepers" are usually safe but heavy to handle. - **Pallets** — unknown
treatment history. Only use if stamped "HT" (heat-treated, not chemically
treated). ### Alternative materials - **Corrugated metal** — modern, industrial
look. Galvanized steel lasts 20+ years. Gets hot in full sun, which dries soil
faster. - **Stone or brick** — permanent and beautiful but expensive and
labour-intensive. Best for a long-term garden. - **Composite lumber** — recycled
plastic/wood mix. Won't rot, no maintenance, lasts decades. Higher upfront cost.
## What are the ideal dimensions? **Width**: **120 cm maximum** — this allows
you to reach the centre from either side without stepping on the soil. If the
bed is against a wall or fence, limit to **60 cm** (accessible from one side
only). **Height**: - **30 cm** — minimum for flowers. Adequate for most plants
with roots reaching into native soil below. - **45 cm** — ideal general height.
Enough depth for almost any flower, including deep-rooted dahlias and roses. -
**60–90 cm** — accessible gardening height. No bending required. Excellent for
patios and areas with poor native soil you don't want to dig into. **Length**:
Whatever fits your space. Common sizes: 120 × 240 cm (standard), 120 × 360 cm
(long border), or 90 × 90 cm (square accent bed). ## How do you build a basic
timber raised bed? **Materials for a 120 × 240 × 30 cm bed**: - 4 boards: 2.5 ×
30 cm timber (2 at 240 cm, 2 at 120 cm) - 4 corner stakes: 5 × 5 × 35 cm - 40
wood screws (65 mm stainless steel or coated) - Drill/driver - Level - Landscape
fabric (optional, for the base) **Steps**: 1. **Choose a level spot** with full
sun (6+ hours). Clear grass or weeds. 2. **Screw corner stakes** to the inside
of the short boards, flush with the bottom edge. The stakes extend 5 cm below
the board to anchor into the ground. 3. **Attach long boards** to the corner
stakes, forming a rectangle. 4. **Level the frame** on the ground. Adjust by
digging under high corners. 5. **Optional**: Line the base with landscape fabric
to suppress weeds growing up through. Don't line the sides — you want lateral
drainage. 6. **Fill with soil mix** (see below). ## What soil mix should you
use? The classic raised bed formula: - **60% screened topsoil** — provides bulk,
minerals, and structure - **30% compost** — adds organic matter, nutrients, and
moisture retention - **10% perlite or coarse sand** — ensures drainage stays
excellent as compost decomposes For a 120 × 240 × 30 cm bed, you need
approximately **0.85 cubic metres** (about 1 tonne) of mix. Order in bulk from a
landscape supply yard — buying bags from a garden centre is 3–4× more expensive.
**Top up annually**: Raised bed soil settles 3–5 cm per year as organic matter
decomposes. Top-dress with 5 cm of fresh compost each spring. ## What flowers
grow best in raised beds? Almost anything thrives in the improved conditions of
a raised bed. Particularly good choices: **Cut flowers**: Dahlias, zinnias,
cosmos, snapdragons, sweet peas (with a trellis). Raised beds make excellent
cutting gardens because you control soil quality precisely. **Perennials**:
Lavender, echinacea, black-eyed Susan, salvia, catmint. These establish deep
roots and benefit from the excellent drainage. **Bulbs**: Tulips, daffodils,
alliums, dahlias. Raised beds prevent the waterlogging that rots bulbs in heavy
ground soil. **Roses**: Bush and shrub roses grow exceptionally well in raised
beds where you can provide the rich, well-drained soil they love. ## Frequently
Asked Questions **Do raised beds need drainage holes?** No — the open bottom
provides natural drainage into the ground below. If you're building on concrete
or paving, drill drainage holes in the base or add a 10 cm gravel layer at the
bottom. **How long do wooden raised beds last?** Cedar: 10–15 years. Larch: 8–12
years. Douglas fir: 5–8 years. Lining the inside with heavy-duty plastic (not
the base) extends life by 3–5 years by keeping wet soil off the wood. **Should
you put cardboard at the bottom of a raised bed?** Yes — a layer of cardboard on
the ground surface smothers existing grass and weeds. It decomposes within 6–12
months, by which time the weeds beneath are dead. Much easier than removing
turf.
