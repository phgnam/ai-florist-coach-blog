## Quick Answer

Soak ranunculus corms in cool water for 3–4 hours, then pre-sprout them in damp seed compost in a tray for 10–14 days at 10 °C. Once roots and shoots show, plant 5 cm deep in 25 cm pots filled with free-draining compost, claws facing down. Place outside in part sun, water sparingly, and expect flowers in 90 days. Lift the corms after foliage yellows and store dry until next autumn.

## Why pre-sprout ranunculus instead of planting the corms straight into the pot?

Ranunculus corms look like small dried claws or octopuses. Planted dry into a cold wet pot, they often rot before producing shoots — especially on a windy balcony where the compost stays cold. Pre-sprouting solves that risk by waking the corm in a controlled environment first.

The pre-sprouting workflow is short:

1. Soak corms claws-down in cool water for 3–4 hours, no longer. They double in size as they rehydrate.
2. Lay them in a shallow tray of damp seed compost or vermiculite, claws still pointing down, just covered.
3. Keep the tray at 8–12 °C in a cool but not freezing spot — an unheated porch, a cold frame, or the bottom shelf of a fridge.
4. Check daily. After 7–14 days you should see fine white roots and a green sprout pushing up from the centre of each corm.
5. Plant the pre-sprouted corms straight into their final pot.

Pre-sprouting roughly doubles the survival rate compared with direct planting in cold conditions and shaves about three weeks off the time to first flower. If you have a heated greenhouse it is less critical, but for unheated outdoor containers it is the single most important step.

For ranunculus growing in beds (not pots), the [grow ranunculus](/posts/grow-ranunculus-flowers-en/) post covers in-ground spacing and bed preparation; this article focuses specifically on container culture.

## What pot, soil, and depth do ranunculus need?

Pot size matters more than people expect. Ranunculus develop a wide, fibrous root system from the corm in the first six weeks; cramped pots produce short, weak stems and small flowers regardless of feeding.

A practical pot specification:

- **Minimum diameter:** 25 cm
- **Minimum depth:** 25 cm
- **Drainage:** at least three holes; an inch of broken terracotta or coarse gravel at the bottom
- **Material:** terracotta or thick plastic. Thin plastic pots cook the corms in early heat.

Three corms per 25 cm pot is the right density — enough that the display feels full but not so crowded that the corms compete for moisture. For a 30 cm pot, 4–5 corms; for a 35 cm pot, 6 corms.

The compost mix should drain freely but hold enough moisture to keep the corms from drying out completely:

- 60 % peat-free multipurpose compost
- 25 % horticultural grit or coarse perlite
- 15 % coir for moisture retention
- 5 g of slow-release fertiliser per litre of mix

Plant pre-sprouted corms 5 cm below the surface with the claws pointing down and the new sprout pointing up. Push the compost gently around the corm; do not press hard, because that compacts the root zone and slows establishment.

## When should you plant ranunculus in pots?

Timing depends on your climate. Ranunculus are cool-season plants — they grow strongly between 5 °C and 18 °C and stop or sulk in heat above 22 °C.

**Mild winters (Mediterranean, mild UK / west coast US, southern Europe):** Plant pre-sprouted corms in late October to mid-November. They grow steadily through winter and flower from late February to April.

**Cold winters (Northern Europe, central US, anywhere with hard freezes):** Two options. Plant pre-sprouted corms in February or early March for May–June flowering. Or plant in October but bring the pots into an unheated greenhouse, garage, or bright porch through the worst of winter.

**Warm climates (subtropical, hot summers):** Treat ranunculus as a winter flower. Plant pre-sprouted corms in November and expect a short January–March flowering season before heat ends them. They will not survive a hot summer outdoors.

The single rule across all climates: ranunculus must reach flowering size before sustained temperatures above 22 °C arrive. If your spring is short and the heat comes early, start earlier. If your spring is long and cool, start later for a longer season.

## How do you water and feed pot-grown ranunculus?

Watering is where most container-grown ranunculus go wrong. The corm rots almost as easily as it dries out, so the goal is "evenly damp, never wet, never bone-dry."

Watering rules through the season:

- **Pre-sprout phase:** keep compost just damp — about as wet as a wrung-out sponge.
- **Establishment (first 4 weeks after planting in pot):** water once a week in cool weather, twice a week in warmer spells.
- **Active growth (foliage 10–20 cm tall):** water as needed; pots in part sun typically want water every 3–4 days.
- **Flowering:** water more frequently to support the buds — every 2–3 days for outdoor pots in warming spring weather.
- **After flowering:** taper watering as the foliage yellows, then stop entirely.

Always check the top 2–3 cm of compost with a finger before watering. If the surface is still moist, wait a day. Letting the compost dry slightly between waterings is far less damaging than keeping it constantly wet.

Feeding is light. The slow-release fertiliser in the planting mix carries the plant through establishment. Once foliage is 15 cm tall, switch to a fortnightly liquid feed at half-strength — a balanced or slightly potassium-leaning blend. The [tomato feed for flowers](/posts/tomato-feed-flowers-en/) post explains the same chemistry for any flowering pot.

Avoid high-nitrogen feeds; they push leaf at the expense of flower buds and produce weak stems prone to flopping.

## What light and temperature do ranunculus pots prefer?

Ranunculus want plenty of light but not extreme heat. The sweet spot is morning sun and afternoon part shade, especially as spring progresses and afternoon temperatures climb.

For balcony and patio placement:

- **East-facing aspect:** ideal. Morning sun, afternoon shade — the [east-facing balcony flowers](/posts/east-facing-balcony-flowers-en/) post explains why this aspect suits cool-season flowers.
- **West-facing aspect:** workable in cool climates; in warm climates, move pots to part shade once afternoon temperatures hit 20 °C.
- **South-facing aspect:** good early in the season, but provide afternoon shade with a parasol or lattice screen once heat arrives. Pots cook faster on a south-facing aspect than ground-level beds in the same garden.
- **North-facing aspect:** marginal. Possible in mild climates but flowering is sparse; consider violas, primulas, or [north-facing balcony flowers](/posts/north-facing-balcony-flowers-en/) instead.

Temperature tolerance:

- **Below freezing:** young plants tolerate occasional frost down to about –4 °C, but corms in shallow pots can freeze through. Use a hessian wrap or move pots into a cold porch.
- **Above 22 °C:** flowers shorten, stems get weak, and the plant rapidly declines. Once heat hits, accept the season is ending.

A shaded position is a useful summer rescue: moving the pot into deep shade once temperatures spike often extends flowering by a week or two.

## What goes wrong with ranunculus in pots, and how do you fix it?

Five common failures, with the most likely cause and fix.

**1. Corms rot before sprouting.**
Cause: too wet, too cold, or both. Soak only 3–4 hours, not overnight. Pre-sprout in damp not wet compost. Discard any soft, mushy corms before they spread rot to the rest.

**2. Foliage grows but no flowers form.**
Cause: too warm during winter (above 18 °C steadily) or too much nitrogen. Move pots somewhere cooler if possible; switch to a balanced or potassium-leaning feed.

**3. Stems are short and floppy.**
Cause: low light, overcrowding, or heat. Move pot into more direct morning sun, thin to one corm fewer per pot next year, and accept the short stems if heat is the cause.

**4. Buds form but don't open.**
Cause: heat spike. Once daytime temperatures hit 22 °C steadily, ranunculus often pause or fail to open new buds. Move into part shade and water consistently — sometimes the buds resume in a cooler week.

**5. Yellow leaves halfway through the season.**
Cause: end-of-season senescence (normal) if plant has flowered. Cause: rot in the corm (bad) if plant has not yet flowered. Remove and inspect the corm; healthy corms are firm, rotted ones are soft and dark.

## How do you save ranunculus corms for next year?

In mild climates, ranunculus corms are perennial — you can leave them in the pot, dry them through summer, and they regrow each autumn. In hot or wet summer climates, lift and store corms is more reliable.

Lifting and storing procedure:

1. **Wait until foliage is fully yellow and dry.** Do not cut down green leaves; the plant is still translocating energy back into the corm.
2. **Tip the pot out and brush the corms clean.** Do not wash them.
3. **Discard soft, dark, or mushy corms.** Keep firm, dry, claw-shaped ones.
4. **Lay the corms in a single layer in a paper bag** with a label of variety and date.
5. **Store in a cool, dry, dark place** at 5–15 °C — a garage, cool cupboard, or unheated room. Avoid airtight containers, which trap moisture.
6. **Check monthly** for any sign of mould; remove affected corms immediately.

Replant the following autumn (or late winter, depending on your climate) using the same pre-sprouting workflow as the original planting. Stored corms regrow as well as fresh corms in their second year, then yields tend to decline by about 20 % per year afterward.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I grow ranunculus indoors?**
Not well. Ranunculus need cool nights (below 12 °C) for proper bud development, and most homes stay too warm. They will grow leaves indoors but rarely flower well.

**How long do ranunculus flowers last as cut stems?**
Ten to fourteen days in a vase, longer than most spring flowers. The [keep cut flowers fresh](/posts/keep-cut-flowers-fresh-en/) post covers vase-life basics that apply directly to ranunculus.

**Should I deadhead spent ranunculus flowers?**
Yes. Deadheading once flowers fade keeps the plant producing new buds for as long as the cool season lasts. Cut at the base of the stem, not partway up.

**Can I plant ranunculus corms in window boxes?**
Yes, if the box is at least 20 cm deep. Use 2 corms per 30 cm of box length and ensure good drainage.

**How long is the bloom season for pot-grown ranunculus?**
About 4 to 6 weeks once flowering starts, depending on climate. Cool consistent weather extends the season; an early heatwave shortens it dramatically.
