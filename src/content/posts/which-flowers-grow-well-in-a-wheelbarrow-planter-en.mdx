## Quick Answer

A wheelbarrow planter holds **40–80 litres** of compost — enough for 6–10 plants in a generous mixed display. The best performers are **trailing petunias, calibrachoa, alyssum, lobelia, dwarf cosmos, dahlias, and nasturtiums**: all sun-loving annuals that flower hard, look full fast, and tolerate the mobility/heat extremes of metal containers. **Drill 6–8 drainage holes**, line with landscape fabric, and water **once daily** in summer.

## Why is a wheelbarrow such a good planter?

Wheelbarrows hit a useful middle ground between small pot and full raised bed:

- **Generous soil volume**: 40–80 L holds roots, water, and nutrients
- **Mobile**: roll into sun, shade, or shelter as needed
- **Visual statement**: instantly reads as "garden feature", not "boring tub"
- **Reuses waste**: tired wheelbarrows that no longer carry rubble find new life
- **Easy access**: rim-height planting, no kneeling

The drawback: metal heats up in sun (so does plastic). Solutions exist (see below), but expect to water more than ground beds.

## What style of wheelbarrow works best?

Three categories, with different planting implications:

- **Old metal builder's barrow**: rusted patina, holds moisture once drainage drilled, looks rustic — best for cottage-style mixes
- **Wooden cart-style**: traditional appearance, slower to heat, but rots without lining
- **Decorative miniature wheelbarrows** (under 30 L): cute but limited — only suit small annuals like alyssum, viola, lobelia
- **Modern plastic barrow**: cheap, lightweight, plastic-y look — drill drainage, paint matt outside if appearance matters

For a serious flower display, choose **45 L+ capacity**. Anything smaller dries out within a day in summer.

## How do I prepare a wheelbarrow for planting?

The setup checklist:

- **Drill 6–8 drainage holes** in the bottom — 1 cm diameter, evenly spaced
- **Line the bottom** with landscape fabric or hessian — keeps soil in, water out
- **Add 5 cm gravel/broken pots** for drainage
- **Fill with peat-free multipurpose** compost mixed with **20% perlite** for lightness
- **Water-retaining gel crystals** optional but useful in hot summers
- **Slow-release fertiliser pellets** (6-month type) saves later feeding

A 45 L barrow needs about 35 L of compost (5 L drainage + 5 L plant rootballs).

## Which flowers actually thrive in a wheelbarrow?

The reliable annual mix, from front edge to back:

**Trailing edge** (cascading down):

- **Trailing petunias** (Wave or Surfinia type) — dependable summer-long flowering
- **Calibrachoa** ("Million Bells") — finer-leaved, masses of small trumpets
- **Bacopa** — dainty white/blue spreader that fills gaps
- **Trailing lobelia** — cool blues, spills over the edge

**Middle ground** (filling the body):

- **Geraniums (zonal pelargoniums)** — heat-tolerant, long bloom
- **Dwarf cosmos** ('Sonata' series, 45 cm) — airy and feathery
- **Dwarf dahlias** ('Mignon' or 'Bishop') — bold colour
- **Nemesia** — fragrant, prolonged flowering

**Back rise** (taller for height):

- **Snapdragons** (medium varieties)
- **Cleome** (spider flower)
- **Salvias** (annual types)

**Edible additions**:

- **Nasturtiums** — flowers and leaves both edible, peppery
- **Pot marigolds (Calendula)** — edible orange/yellow
- **Borage** — blue starflowers, attracts bees

A typical 45 L barrow holds: 3 trailing edge plants + 4 middle plants + 1–2 tall back plants = full display in 6–8 weeks.

## How should I lay out the planting?

The classic "thriller, filler, spiller" approach works well:

- **Thriller**: 1–2 tall plants near the back/handle end (the highest visual point)
- **Filler**: 4–5 medium-height plants filling the body
- **Spiller**: 3–4 trailing plants along the front edge, draping down

Rules of thumb:

- **Plant close** — 5 cm tighter than ground spacing for instant fullness
- **Group like with like** — three of one variety beats one each of three
- **Soft colour bridge** — alyssum or lobelia between bolder plants softens transitions
- **One bold accent** — single dahlia or salvia draws the eye

A common mistake: too many varieties, no theme. Pick a colour palette (e.g. pink + white + blue) and stick with it.

## How often do I need to water?

Wheelbarrow planters dry out fast. Expect:

- **Spring (cool weather)**: every 2–3 days
- **Early summer**: daily
- **Heatwave**: twice daily, morning and evening
- **Autumn**: every 3–4 days

Quick test: stick a finger 3 cm into the soil — if dry, water. If still moist, wait.

Watering technique:

- **Water at the base**, not over leaves
- **Slow trickle** until water exits drainage holes
- **Dawn or dusk** to minimise evaporation
- **Saucer underneath** is optional — improves drought tolerance but risks waterlogging in rainy spells

For weeks-away holiday cover, install a self-watering reservoir bottle or move to a partly shaded spot before leaving.

## Do wheelbarrows need feeding?

Yes — soil volume is small relative to plant biomass. Choose one approach:

- **Slow-release pellets** at planting (6-month formulation) — easy, lasts the season
- **Liquid feed every 7–10 days** — high-potash tomato feed (1/2 strength) keeps blooms coming
- **Dried fish/blood/bone scratch** at midseason — slow boost for tired soil

Stop feeding 4 weeks before plants are due to die back (autumn). Over-fed plants make foliage at the expense of blooms.

## How do I deal with the metal heating up?

Metal wheelbarrows can hit 50 °C surface temperature in midday sun, cooking shallow roots:

- **Paint outside white** or pale colour — reflects heat
- **Wrap in hessian** or cocofibre matting for rustic look
- **Stand in part shade** in afternoon — eastern aspect is ideal
- **Mulch the soil surface** — 2 cm bark or compost reduces evaporation
- **Move on rolling base** to track shade

For very hot climates, consider an internal ceramic or wooden liner — reduces heat transfer to the rootzone significantly.

## Can I leave a planted wheelbarrow over winter?

Most annuals die at first frost. For a year-round display:

- **Spring–summer**: annuals as above (April–October)
- **Autumn–winter swap**: replace with **pansies, violas, ornamental cabbage, heathers, cyclamen, ivy** for cold-season interest
- **Hardy bulbs**: layer tulips, daffodils, crocus underneath for spring surprise

Or empty in October, store the barrow upside-down (drainage), refill in March.

For continuous interest, plan **two seasonal makeovers** per year.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I plant directly into the wheelbarrow or use a liner?

Direct planting is fine for metal/plastic barrows once drainage holes are drilled. Wooden barrows benefit from a coir or hessian liner to slow rotting. Don't use waterproof plastic liners without holes — water collects and rots roots.

### How heavy is a planted wheelbarrow?

Surprisingly heavy. A 45 L barrow with damp compost and plants weighs 50–60 kg. Move it before filling, or roll it on its wheel. Check that wheel and tyre are sound before planting.

### Can I grow vegetables alongside flowers?

Yes — herbs (basil, parsley, chives) integrate beautifully with flowers. Lettuce, spinach, and dwarf chillies also work. See the [edible flowers guide](/posts/edible-flowers-guide-en/) for combining ornamental and edible.

### What's the best annual for total beginners?

**Trailing petunias** — fail-safe, flower from May to first frost with minimal fuss. Combine with **white alyssum** for filler and you have a stunning low-effort display. See the [strawberry pot guide](/posts/which-flowers-grow-well-in-a-strawberry-pot-en/) for similar small-container thinking.

### Can I plant flowers all year in a wheelbarrow?

Almost. Even in winter, pansies, heathers, and cyclamen keep colour going. Match plants to season. The [flowers that bloom all year guide](/posts/flowers-bloom-all-year-round-en/) outlines the rotation.

### My wheelbarrow has no wheel — does it still work?

Yes — treat as a fixed planter. Choose its position carefully (full sun for the recommended plants), and accept it can't move. Mid-season repositioning becomes impossible, so plan watering routes accordingly.
