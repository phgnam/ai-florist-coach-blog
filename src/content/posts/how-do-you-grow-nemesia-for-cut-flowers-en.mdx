## Quick Answer

Sow nemesia seed indoors **8–10 weeks before last frost**, prick out into modules at the two-true-leaf stage, harden off for 7 days, and plant out at **15 cm spacing** in rich, free-draining soil. Pinch the growing tip at 10 cm to force branching. Cut stems just above a leaf node when 5 of the lowest flowers are open — expect **6–8 stems per plant** every week through summer if you keep deadheading.

## Why grow nemesia for cutting?

Nemesia is overlooked as a cut flower because the stems look short on the plant — but the perfumed varieties (*Nemesia denticulata*, *N. caerulea*) and modern hybrids ('Wisley Vanilla', 'Aromatica' series, 'Sweet Pea Lavender') give you:

- **Soft sweet vanilla scent** — strongest in the white/cream cultivars
- **6–8 weeks of cutting per plant** with deadheading
- **25–35 cm stem length** — perfect for small jugs and posies
- **Light, airy texture** that fills out grass-and-rose arrangements

A 1 m² patch of 25 plants gives a hand-tied posy every 3–4 days through summer.

## When should I sow nemesia seed?

Two viable methods:

**Indoor sowing (most reliable)**:

- **8–10 weeks before last frost** (early February in mild UK, late February cooler regions)
- Sow on the surface of moist seed compost — light is needed for germination
- Cover with a thin sprinkle of vermiculite, not soil
- Germination at 18–20 °C in 7–14 days
- Prick out into modules when 2 true leaves form

**Direct sowing**:

- After last frost, when soil is workable and 12 °C
- Scatter onto a fine raked seedbed
- Press in with a board, do not cover
- Thin to 15 cm when 5 cm tall
- Flowers about 6 weeks later — later than indoor-sown plants

For continuous cutting, sow batches every 4 weeks until midsummer.

## Where should nemesia be planted out?

Nemesia is fussier about position than people expect. The right combination:

- **Full sun to very light shade** — at least 5 hours direct sun
- **Free-draining soil** — gravel beds, raised beds, sandy loams
- **Slightly acidic to neutral pH** — chalky soils need extra organic matter
- **Sheltered from strong wind** — fragile stems break easily

Avoid heavy wet clay — nemesia rot at the crown in cold wet conditions. If your soil is clay, build a raised bed 20 cm high with a lighter compost-and-grit mix.

## How do I prepare the bed for cut flower production?

Cutting puts heavier demand on a plant than border display. Prepare for it:

- **Dig in 1 bucket of well-rotted compost per square metre** before planting
- **Add a low-N base fertiliser** (fish blood and bone, or a 5-7-10 ratio) at planting
- **Mulch with composted bark** after planting to keep soil cool and moist
- **Plan irrigation** — a soaker hose at the base saves stems from being splashed when watering

Avoid manures with high nitrogen — they push leafy growth at the expense of flowers, which is the opposite of what cutting demands.

## How do I pinch and train nemesia for stem length?

Pinching is non-negotiable for cut-flower nemesia. The first pinch makes the difference between 8 short floppy stems and 6–8 strong straight stems per plant.

The method:

- When the seedling is **10 cm tall**, snap or snip the growing tip just above a leaf node
- Within 7–10 days, two side shoots emerge from the leaf axils below the cut
- When those side shoots reach 10 cm, **pinch them again** for an even bushier plant (optional second pinch)
- Stop pinching by 4 weeks before flowering

Twiggy support — pea sticks woven through the planting at 30 cm height — keeps stems vertical without visible stakes.

## How do I cut nemesia for the longest vase life?

The conditioning rules are critical:

- Cut **early morning** when stems are turgid
- Use sharp clean snips just above a leaf node
- Cut when **5 of the lowest flowers are open** but the spike still has unopened buds at the top
- Plunge stems immediately into a jug of cool water
- **Recut underwater** before arranging
- Strip leaves below the waterline
- Use cut-flower food at half strength
- Vase life: 5–8 days, longer in cool rooms

Nemesia drops the lowest flowers naturally as the spike ages — pick those off rather than tossing the whole stem.

## Are nemesia hardy or do I have to start over each year?

The answer depends on cultivar:

- **Nemesia strumosa** (the classic annual nemesia) — half-hardy annual, replant from seed yearly
- **Nemesia caerulea / N. denticulata** — short-lived perennials, hardy to about -5 °C in well-drained soil
- **Hybrid 'Aromatica' series** — perennial in mild winters, treated as annuals in colder regions

For a perennial nemesia patch in a colder climate, take softwood cuttings in late summer and overwinter rooted plants in a cool greenhouse — much more reliable than trusting them in the ground.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why do my nemesia stop flowering in midsummer?

Heat stress and missed deadheading are the main causes. Nemesia prefer cool nights — they slow in temperatures above 28 °C and push back into flower as autumn cools. Cut hard back by half in late July, water and feed, and they restart by mid-August.

### Can I grow nemesia in pots for cutting?

Yes — a 25 cm pot holds 5–7 plants. Use a free-draining peat-free compost with extra grit. Water more often than you think (small pots dry fast in sun) and feed weekly with half-strength balanced fertiliser. Vase yield is lower than from beds but the cutting works.

### How long do nemesia plants last in the bed?

A well-fed, deadheaded nemesia produces from May to October — about 5 months of cutting. Plants from spring sowing are spent by autumn frost. Perennial cultivars in mild gardens may live 2–3 years before going woody at the base.

### What companions for nemesia in a cut flower bed?

Nemesia mixes well with **scabiosa**, **cosmos**, **cornflower**, **sweet peas**, **dianthus**, and **alchemilla**. Keep aggressive heavy feeders like dahlias separate so they don't outcompete the nemesia roots.

### Are nemesia flowers edible?

The flowers of common cultivars are non-toxic but flavourless and not commonly used in cuisine. Treat as ornamental only unless you have a specifically edible cultivar verified by a reputable source.

### Can I save seed for next year?

Modern hybrids ('Aromatica' series, 'Wisley') do not breed true from seed. Older species nemesia (*N. caerulea*, *N. denticulata*) seed is reliable — collect dried seed pods in late summer and sow the following year.
