## Quick Answer

Poinsettia leaves drop after Christmas mostly from cold drafts, dry indoor heat, overwatering, or shock from the move home from the garden centre. Yes, you can keep the plant alive — keep it at 18–22 °C, away from radiators and doors, water only when the top inch of soil is dry, and reduce light gradually as the bracts fade.

## Why do poinsettias start dropping leaves so soon after Christmas?

Poinsettias (*Euphorbia pulcherrima*) are tropical Mexican shrubs sold as disposable holiday décor — but they are far more sensitive than the supermarket display suggests. Six causes account for almost all post-Christmas leaf drop:

1. **Cold shock during purchase or transport.** A poinsettia exposed to outdoor air below 10 °C — even briefly walking from the shop to the car — drops leaves 1–2 weeks later. This is the most common cause and is invisible at the moment of damage.
2. **Cold draft at home.** A draughty hallway, a windowsill above a leaky single-glazed window, or proximity to an exterior door that opens to freezing air. Leaves drop within days of the cold exposure.
3. **Hot, dry air from radiators or wood stoves.** Poinsettias evolved in humid Mexican cloud forests at 60–80 % humidity; centrally-heated UK and continental European homes drop to 25–35 % humidity in winter. The plant transpires faster than its roots can replace water and the leaves drop in protest.
4. **Overwatering.** The single most common slow killer of post-Christmas poinsettias. Decorative foil sleeves trap drainage water; roots sit in the puddle, suffocate, and rot. Leaves yellow and drop from the bottom up.
5. **Underwatering.** Less common but real — wrapped peat-based potting mix dries hard quickly in heated rooms, and water then runs straight down the gap between soil and pot rather than rehydrating the rootball. Leaves wilt, dry, and drop.
6. **Sudden light change.** Garden centres grow poinsettias under bright greenhouse light. Moving to a dim corner of a living room is a major light shock; the plant sheds the leaves it can no longer support.

The pattern of drop and the appearance of the dropped leaves tell you which cause it is — diagnosed in the next section.

## How do you tell which cause is to blame for the leaf drop?

Each cause has a recognisable signature.

**Cold (draft or transit shock):**

- **Whole leaves drop, often green**, sometimes with a bluish tint or curled edges.
- **Drop happens 3–14 days after the cold exposure**, not immediately. This delay is why most owners blame "the room" rather than "the trip home from the shop".
- **Bracts (the red coloured "petals") may also drop** in severe cases.

**Overwatering / root rot:**

- **Lower leaves yellow uniformly** before falling.
- **Soil stays soggy 5+ days after watering.** Lift the foil sleeve and feel — if water pooled at the bottom, that is the cause.
- **Stem near the soil line softens** in advanced cases. Sour, swampy smell from the drainage hole.

**Underwatering / dry heat:**

- **Leaves wilt limp first**, then go crispy at the edges before falling.
- **Soil pulls away from the pot edge.** Watering then runs through the gap and out without soaking the rootball.
- **Lower leaves drop**, but feel papery rather than soft.

**Light shock (after a move from greenhouse to dim corner):**

- **Lower, oldest leaves drop steadily** over 2–4 weeks even though everything else looks fine.
- **No yellowing or wilting beforehand** — leaves just abscise.
- **Plant looks "normal" but slowly thins from the bottom.**

**Combined cause (which is most real cases):** if you brought it home from a cold shop, parked it next to a radiator, and watered it through a foil sleeve, you have the trifecta and need to address all three.

## What temperature, water, and light does a poinsettia actually need indoors?

The full home-care specification is narrower than houseplant guides suggest, but it is also not difficult once you know it.

**Temperature:**

- Daytime: **18–22 °C** (65–72 °F). Stable.
- Night: **15–17 °C** (60–63 °F). Avoid drops below 13 °C overnight.
- **No drafts.** A 1 °C draught from a leaky window is more damaging than steady cool air.
- **Not next to radiators** or fireplaces — surface temperatures swing wildly.

**Water:**

- Check moisture by pressing a finger into the top 2 cm of soil. **Water only when it is dry to the touch.**
- Water from above with tepid water until liquid runs from the drainage holes. **Then immediately tip out any water collected in the foil sleeve, decorative pot, or saucer.** Roots cannot sit in water.
- Frequency varies wildly with room conditions — typically once a week in a cool room, twice a week in a heated room with low humidity. Never water on a fixed calendar; always test the soil first.
- **Never let the soil go bone-dry.** Underwatered poinsettias do not recover the way other plants do — leaves dropped from drying are gone permanently.

**Light:**

- **Bright, indirect light** for 6+ hours a day. East- or south-facing windows ideal in the Northern Hemisphere; north- or east-facing in the Southern Hemisphere.
- No direct midday sun in summer (it scorches the bracts).
- **Avoid moving the plant once you find the right spot.** Like fiddle leaf figs, poinsettias drop leaves when relocated — light angle changes confuse them.

**Humidity:**

- Aim for 40–60 % indoor humidity. A cheap hygrometer pays for itself.
- Place the pot on a tray of damp pebbles (with the pot above the water line, never sitting in it) to raise local humidity by 10–15 %.

## Can you make a poinsettia rebloom — turn its bracts red — next Christmas?

Yes, but it requires deliberate work and patience. The "red" parts of a poinsettia are not flowers but **modified leaves called bracts** that change colour in response to short days. The technical name for the trigger is *photoperiodism* — poinsettias are obligate short-day plants and need around 14 hours of complete darkness per night for 8–10 weeks before they start colouring up.

The full year-round schedule:

- **January–March (recovery):** keep at indoor conditions above. Bracts will gradually fade; many leaves will drop. This is normal and not a sign of dying.
- **April (cutback):** when bracts have fully faded, cut all stems back to ~10 cm from the soil. Reduce watering to once every 10–14 days. Plant looks like a stick — this is fine.
- **May (resume growth):** fresh green leaves emerge. Resume normal watering. Begin diluted balanced liquid fertiliser (NPK ~10-10-10) every 3 weeks.
- **June (repot if needed):** if rootbound, repot one size up in fresh, free-draining peat-free potting mix. Move outdoors to a sheltered spot once nights are reliably above 13 °C.
- **July–August (pinching):** pinch out the tips of each stem when they reach 15 cm to encourage bushy branching. Stop pinching by mid-August.
- **September (move indoors):** before nighttime temperatures fall below 13 °C, bring the plant back inside.
- **Late September to mid-December (the dark treatment):** this is the crucial step. Every evening at 6 p.m., place the plant in a closet or cover with a black box that admits zero light, until 8 a.m. the next morning. Strict 14 hours of total darkness, every night, for 8–10 weeks. Even brief light from a hallway lamp resets the clock. Daytime: bright indirect light as usual.
- **Late November / early December:** the bracts begin colouring. Once fully red, regular indoor positioning resumes — and the plant is back in bloom for Christmas.

This works in real homes — the limiting factor is human consistency on the dark treatment, not plant biology.

## What do you do when most leaves have already fallen off?

Strip back the rescue plan to the essentials and don't expect immediate recovery.

**If the stems are still green and firm:**

1. **Move to ideal indoor conditions** (18–22 °C, no drafts, bright indirect light). Resist the urge to move it again "to try a better spot".
2. **Stop watering until the top 2 cm of soil dries**, then water deeply once and tip out drainage water.
3. **No fertiliser.** Stressed plants rot faster when fed.
4. **Let the bracts fade and drop naturally.** Don't cut the plant back yet.
5. **Once spring conditions return**, follow the rebloom schedule from April. Many "lost" poinsettias come back fully from this point.

**If the stems are blackened, soft, or smell bad:**

The plant has root rot beyond home recovery. The realistic move is to take a 10 cm tip cutting from the firmest remaining stem, dip the cut end in rooting hormone, plant in fresh peat-free seed compost, cover with a clear plastic bag, and try to root it as a new plant. Otherwise, dispose of the plant and any contaminated soil.

**Practical reality check:** about half of post-Christmas poinsettias under home care never make it to spring even with optimal conditions, because the stress accumulated in shop transit and early-week handling is already too much. Don't take it personally if a rescue fails — start fresh next year with a healthier specimen using the buyer's checklist below.

## How do you choose a healthy poinsettia at the garden centre to avoid this in the first place?

The single biggest determinant of how long your poinsettia lasts is the condition of the plant *at point of purchase*. The full buying checklist:

- **Buy from a heated indoor display only** — never from outside the shop entrance, never from a transit pallet near loading doors, never from a discount end-of-line shelf. Cold exposure already happened on those plants.
- **Inspect the underside of the leaves** for whitefly (small white flies that lift off when disturbed). Whitefly is the #1 pest on poinsettias and typically signals the plant has been stressed at the grower.
- **Check the centre of the bracts** for the small yellow-green flower buds (called *cyathia*). Tightly closed yellow cyathia mean the plant is at peak freshness and will hold colour longest. Open or fallen cyathia mean the plant is past peak — already 2–4 weeks closer to bract drop.
- **Avoid plants with yellowing lower leaves** — overwatering damage is already underway.
- **Avoid plants packed into tight cellophane sleeves** for long display periods. The sleeve traps ethylene from the plant itself and accelerates leaf drop. If you must buy sleeved, ask the shop how long it has been wrapped.
- **Wrap the plant for transport.** Even a 5-minute walk to the car at -2 °C causes leaf drop two weeks later. Use a paper bag or wrap the plant loosely in newspaper for any outdoor exposure below 10 °C.
- **Place it in its final location at home immediately** rather than moving it around the house "to find a spot". Each move stresses the plant.

Following these rules typically extends a home poinsettia's bract-display from the all-too-common 2–3 weeks to 8–12 weeks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are poinsettias actually poisonous to cats and dogs?**
The popular myth says yes; the modern evidence says no, only mildly irritating. Poinsettia sap (*Euphorbia* milky latex) can cause vomiting, drooling, and mild mouth irritation in pets that chew on the leaves, but the LD₅₀ studies in the 1970s showed it would take a small dog eating roughly 500 leaves to risk serious effect. Treat ingestion as you would any minor plant nibble — wash the mouth, watch for distress, call the vet if symptoms persist — but no, poinsettias are not the deadly Christmas hazard old guides suggested.

**How long should a poinsettia last from purchase if cared for well?**
A healthy plant bought before peak ethylene drop, transported warm, and placed in stable indoor conditions typically holds its red bracts for **8–12 weeks** from purchase. Supermarket plants bought from outdoor displays in November often drop bracts within 2–4 weeks regardless of subsequent care, simply because the cold damage was already done.

**Should you repot a poinsettia after Christmas?**
Not immediately. Repotting a stressed, leaf-dropping poinsettia adds shock on top of shock. Wait until **April or May**, after you have cut the stems back and new growth is starting, then repot one size up in fresh, free-draining peat-free potting mix. Over-potting (jumping two sizes up) causes root rot — go one size at a time.

**Why do poinsettias drop leaves on the cold trip home from the shop?**
Below about 10 °C, poinsettia leaves form ice crystals between the cells of the leaf base and trigger an abscission response — the plant deliberately sheds the affected leaf as a chemical signal of damage. The tricky part is that the abscission is delayed: the cells damage on Day 1, the abscission hormones build over the next 7–14 days, and the leaves drop on Day 14 in your warm living room. The owner usually blames the room conditions, but the cause was the trip home.

**Is it worth keeping a supermarket poinsettia, or buy fresh next year?**
If the plant is in good condition by mid-February (still has most of its leaves, stems firm, no rot), absolutely yes — keeping it is satisfying and free, and reblooming it for the second Christmas is a quietly impressive feat. If by mid-February the plant has dropped most of its leaves and only sticks remain, the realistic odds of survival to next December are roughly 30–40 %. Buying a fresh, healthy plant next November is a perfectly sensible default.
