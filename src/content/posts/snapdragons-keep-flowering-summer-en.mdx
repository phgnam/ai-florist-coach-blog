## Quick Answer

Keep snapdragons blooming all summer by deadheading every few days before seed pods set, cutting plants back hard to about 15 cm in mid-July when flowering slows, watering deeply once a week, and switching to a potassium-rich feed every 10 days. With this routine, modern *Antirrhinum* cultivars produce three flushes of flowers from May to October instead of one short May–June display.

## Why do snapdragons stop flowering by midsummer?

Snapdragons are short-lived perennials almost always grown as annuals. They flower hardest in the cool weeks of early summer and slow dramatically as soon as two things happen: temperatures climb above 25 °C consistently, and the first seed pods finish forming on the spent flower spikes.

The plant's flowering hormones flip the moment seed production becomes the priority. Once seeds are ripening at the base of an old spike, the plant stops producing the lateral side spikes that give you the summer-long display. From that point on, growth goes into seed, not new flowers.

Heat is the second trigger. Above 25 °C, snapdragons enter a partial dormancy where stems lengthen, leaves yellow at the base, and bud production slows or stops. The plant is alive and the roots are healthy, but the flowering machinery is paused. In hot climates, this can happen by late June; in cooler climates, not until August.

Both triggers are reversible. Deadhead before seed sets and the plant keeps producing lateral spikes. Cut the plant back hard during the hot pause and it bounces back when temperatures cool again — usually with a stronger autumn flush than spring.

## How and when do you deadhead snapdragons?

Snapdragon flowers open from the bottom of the spike upward over about two weeks. The bottom flowers fade first; by the time the top flowers open, the bottom of the spike is already starting to set seed pods.

The most useful deadheading rule is: **cut the whole spike off as soon as the bottom third has finished flowering, not when the whole spike is spent.** Most home gardeners wait too long, and by then the seed-production signal has already gone out.

The cut goes back to a strong leaf node low on the stem — typically 8–12 cm above where the spike emerged. New side branches develop from that node within 7–10 days, each carrying its own flower spike. A well-deadheaded plant produces 3–5 spikes from the spot where there was one, dramatically extending the display.

Three deadheading habits that pay off:

- **Walk the plants every 2–3 days** in peak season, not weekly. Three days is enough for seed pods to form on a finished spike.
- **Use sharp snips, not fingers.** Tearing the stem leaves a ragged wound that takes longer to heal and resprout.
- **Leave the bottom-most pair of leaves on the side stem.** Cutting too low (into woody tissue) sometimes prevents new growth from that node.

For the mechanics that apply across most cut-flower annuals, the [how to deadhead flowers](/posts/how-to-deadhead-flowers-en/) post is a good general reference; the specific cut-back-to-a-node rule above is what makes it work for snapdragons.

## Why does cutting snapdragons back hard in midsummer trigger more flowers?

The classic mistake with snapdragons is babying them through the midsummer slump. Plants that look ragged in late July rarely recover with deadheading alone. What works is a deliberate hard cut-back — a "haircut" that resets the plant to bushier growth.

The procedure is short:

1. **Wait until the plant is clearly flagging.** Most spikes finished, leaves yellowing at the base, no new buds forming.
2. **Cut every stem back to about 15 cm tall.** This looks brutal; it is the right thing to do.
3. **Water deeply** and feed with a half-strength balanced or potassium-leaning liquid feed.
4. **Keep evenly moist** for the next two weeks while the plant recovers.

Within 2–3 weeks, multiple new shoots emerge from the cut stems. Within 5–6 weeks, those shoots are flowering. The autumn flush from a cut-back snapdragon is often heavier than the spring flush because the root system is already mature and the cooler late-summer weather suits the plant better.

In mild climates, well-cut-back snapdragons can flower well into November, and some cultivars even survive the winter and flower again the following spring.

## What watering and feeding routine keeps snapdragons producing?

Snapdragons are not heavy drinkers but they are sensitive to drought during bud formation. A plant that dries out repeatedly produces short, sparse spikes; one watered deeply once a week produces tall, dense spikes for weeks longer.

A practical schedule:

| Stage | Watering | Feeding |
|---|---|---|
| First 4 weeks after planting | Daily light water | Slow-release fertiliser at planting |
| Active flowering | Deep water once a week | Half-strength liquid feed every 10 days |
| Hot weather (above 25 °C) | Deep water twice a week | Hold off feeding; resume after cool-down |
| After hard cut-back | Deep water twice a week for two weeks | Half-strength feed at the end of week 1 |
| Autumn flush | Deep water once a week | Half-strength feed every 14 days |

The key is "deep water once a week," not light daily sprinkles. A 20 cm root system needs the top 15 cm of soil saturated, then allowed to dry slightly. Light surface watering keeps roots shallow and makes the plant much more drought-vulnerable.

For feeding, aim for balanced or slightly potassium-leaning ratios — the [tomato feed for flowers](/posts/tomato-feed-flowers-en/) post explains why high-nitrogen fertilisers push leaf at the expense of buds. A half-strength general flower feed every 10 days during active flowering supports the plant without producing the soft, sappy growth that aphids love.

## Should you let snapdragons self-seed for next year?

It depends on what you want. Letting some plants set seed late in the season produces volunteer seedlings that flower the following spring — often with surprising colour mixes if you grew several cultivars together.

The trade-off is that any plant allowed to set seed almost always stops flowering for the rest of the season on that stem. So the best approach is to choose sacrifice plants:

- **Deadhead the front-row plants relentlessly** to keep the display going.
- **Let one or two back-row plants set seed** in late August and September.
- **Collect dry seed pods** in October when the pods are brown and rattle when shaken.

Self-sown snapdragon seedlings are extraordinarily hardy in most climates — they germinate in autumn, overwinter as small rosettes, and flower from late April to June the following year. The flowers from self-sown plants are often shorter and looser than F1 hybrid bedding plants but have the charm of unexpected colour combinations.

If you want predictable colour and form, treat snapdragons as annuals: deadhead all season, replace from fresh seedlings each year, and skip the self-seeding entirely.

## What goes wrong, and how do you fix it?

Five common problems and the fix for each.

**1. Tall single spike, no side branches.**
The plant was not pinched at planting. Pinch the growing tip when the seedling is 10 cm tall to force lateral branching. For plants you have already let grow tall, cut the leader back to a leaf pair at 15 cm and side shoots will form.

**2. Lots of leaves, no flowers.**
Too much nitrogen. Stop any feeding and wait. The plant uses up the excess nitrogen within 2–3 weeks and starts forming buds again.

**3. Spikes droop at the top.**
Heat stress combined with overwatering, or a fungal infection at the stem base. Check the bottom 5 cm of stem for soft brown tissue (rust or stem rot); if present, remove the plant. If the stem is firm, the plant is just heat-stressed and will recover with cooler weather.

**4. Yellow lower leaves with rust-coloured pustules.**
Snapdragon rust (*Puccinia antirrhini*). Remove and bin the worst leaves, improve airflow by thinning crowded plants, and avoid overhead watering. Severely infected plants are best pulled and replaced with fresh seedlings; rust spores overwinter in the soil and the cycle repeats next year.

**5. Plant collapses in a heatwave.**
Hard cut-back to 10 cm, water deeply, and shade lightly for 4–5 days. Most plants recover and produce a strong autumn flush.

Snapdragons attract aphids, especially on the new soft growth after a hard cut-back. The [aphids on roses (organic methods)](/posts/aphids-on-roses-organic-en/) post covers control approaches that work directly on snapdragons too.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do snapdragons live?**
Treated as annuals, one full season. Some cultivars survive a mild winter and flower again the following spring; the second-year display is usually shorter than the first.

**Can I grow snapdragons in pots and still get all-summer flowering?**
Yes, but feed every 10 days because pot soil exhausts faster than ground soil. Use a 25 cm pot for 3 plants, deep water once a week, and follow the same deadhead-and-cut-back schedule.

**Do bees and pollinators like snapdragons?**
Yes. Bumblebees in particular are big enough to push the snap-dragon mouth open and reach the nectar. Smaller bees can't access most cultivars. The [snapdragon pollination](/posts/snapdragon-pollination-en/) post covers this in detail.

**What does "pinching" a snapdragon mean?**
Removing the top 2–3 cm of the central growing stem when the seedling is 10 cm tall. This forces the plant to branch sideways and produces 4–6 flowering spikes instead of one tall single spike.

**Why are my self-seeded snapdragons different colours from the parent plants?**
Most modern snapdragons are F1 hybrids. Their seed produces F2 plants with a range of colours, often duller than the parent. This is normal genetic segregation, not a problem.
