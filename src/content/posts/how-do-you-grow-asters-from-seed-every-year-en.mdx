## Quick Answer

China asters (*Callistephus chinensis*) are easy to grow from seed every year: **sow indoors in March**, plant out in late May after frosts, deadhead summer flushes through August, and **leave 1–2 spikes per plant to set seed** in September. Collected seed sown next March restarts the cycle. Each plant gives 50–100 viable seeds, more than enough for a free annual display.

## What's the difference between China asters and Michaelmas daisies?

Two very different plants both called "aster":

- **China aster (*Callistephus chinensis*)** — annual, sown each year, flowers August–October on single tall stems, classic cut flower
- **Michaelmas daisy (*Symphyotrichum* / *Aster*)** — perennial, lives for years, flowers September–November on bushy mounds

This article is about **China asters** — the annual you grow fresh each year and which gives the most reliable seed harvest. Michaelmas daisies divide rather than seed for propagation.

## When and how do I sow asters?

The standard schedule for UK and most temperate climates:

- **March**: sow indoors at 18 °C
- **April**: prick out into 9 cm pots
- **May (mid-late)**: harden off
- **Late May after frost**: plant out
- **June–July**: pinch tips for branching
- **August–October**: flowers
- **September**: leave seed heads to ripen
- **October**: collect seed
- **Store seed dry** for next year

The sowing details:

- **Compost**: peat-free seed compost, fine surface
- **Depth**: 5 mm — barely cover with vermiculite
- **Tray or modules**: 6-cell trays work well
- **Temperature**: 18–22 °C for germination, 7–10 days
- **Light**: bright as soon as seedlings emerge
- **Move to cooler 15 °C** once true leaves appear, keeps stems sturdy

A pinch of seed (5–6 seeds) per cell, thinned to the strongest 1 seedling.

## How big should plants be at planting out?

Plant out at **15 cm tall, 4–6 true leaves**, hardened off:

- **Hardening off**: 1 week of progressively longer outdoor stints
- **Spacing**: 25–30 cm apart, both ways, in rows or staggered
- **Soil prep**: fork in compost, add a sprinkle of bone meal
- **Water in well** after planting
- **Pinch growing tip** at planting if not already done — encourages branching

A weakly-stemmed seedling planted out windswept will struggle for weeks. Stocky compact ones with thick stems establish in days.

## What growing conditions do they need?

China asters want what most cut flowers want:

- **Sun**: 6+ hours direct sun, ideally 8 hours
- **Soil**: fertile, free-draining, ph 6–7
- **Water**: regular through summer, especially in flower bud stage
- **Feed**: high-potash liquid every 2 weeks once buds form
- **Avoid**: heavy clay, dry shade, water-logged spots
- **Stake**: tall varieties need pea sticks at 30 cm height

Crop rotation matters: don't grow asters in the same spot 2 years running — fungal aster wilt builds up in soil. Move to a different bed each year.

## How do I get the most flowers per plant?

Three simple practices double the flower count:

- **Pinch out the central growing tip** when the plant is 15 cm tall — this triggers 5–7 side branches, each carrying flowers
- **Cut all flowers regularly** — for vases or just to deadhead. Cutting a flower triggers another one to form below the cut
- **Liquid feed every 2 weeks** with a tomato fertiliser from bud-set onwards

A pinched and well-cut China aster gives 15–25 flowers in a season. An un-pinched, un-deadheaded one gives 5–8.

## Which varieties give the best seed?

For seed-saving, choose **open-pollinated varieties** rather than F1 hybrids:

**Reliable open-pollinated cultivars**:

- **'Single Mix'** — daisy-form flowers, breeds true from seed
- **'Princess Mix'** — cushion-centred, long stems, great cut flower
- **'Tall Mixed'** — assorted colours, classic cottage garden look
- **'Matsumoto Mix'** — long-lasting in vase, semi-double
- **'Ostrich Plume'** — fluffy double form, beautiful for weddings

**Avoid for seed-saving**: F1 hybrids (often labelled "F1" or "Improved"). These don't breed true — saved seed gives variable, often disappointing offspring.

If you grow only one open-pollinated variety, your saved seed will breed true year after year.

## When and how do I collect seed?

Seed collection is the easy part:

- **Stop deadheading** in early September on 1–2 flower stems per plant
- **Let flowers fade fully** to a brown, dry, fluffy seed head
- **Wait for a dry day** to harvest — damp seed moulds in storage
- **Cut whole stems** with seed heads attached
- **Hang upside down** in a paper bag for 1–2 weeks indoors
- **Shake the bag** to release seed
- **Pick out viable seeds** — small, dark, slightly elongated
- **Discard chaff** (the fluffy flower remains)

Each fading flower head produces 10–25 seeds. Two seed heads per plant gives 20–50 seeds — easily enough for next year's row.

## How do I store seed for next year?

Stored properly, China aster seed remains viable for 2–3 years:

- **Container**: paper envelopes inside a sealed glass jar, or small plastic snap-lock bags
- **Dry the seed for 1–2 weeks** before storing — moisture is the enemy
- **Add a small silica gel pack** for extra dryness
- **Label** with variety name and year of collection
- **Store in a cool dark dry place** — 5–15 °C ideal, like an unheated cupboard
- **Don't store in fridge unless using sealed jars** — humidity issues

A Tupperware of mixed labelled envelopes in a kitchen cupboard works perfectly.

## What problems should I watch for?

Three issues affect China asters most:

- **Aster wilt** — fungal soil-borne disease causing wilted limp plants from base upward; rotate planting site, choose resistant varieties
- **Aphids on buds** — common pest, squash by hand or spray with diluted soap solution
- **Powdery mildew** — white film on leaves in dry summers; increase airflow, water at base not overhead
- **Slugs on seedlings** — young plants are vulnerable, use barrier methods
- **Poor germination** — old seed (over 3 years) or sown too deep

Crop rotation is the single biggest preventive measure. Move asters to a different bed every year and most problems disappear.

## What about other annuals you can save seed from?

Once you've mastered China asters, the same technique works on:

- **Cosmos** — same family, same easy seed collection
- **Zinnias** — large dry seed heads, very reliable
- **Larkspur** — sow autumn for early flowers
- **Cornflowers** — open-pollinated only
- **Calendula** — self-seeds freely, almost too easily
- **Nigella (love-in-a-mist)** — beautiful seed pods, easy collection

A garden full of saved-seed annuals costs almost nothing each year and gives a genuine connection to the plants over generations.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will China aster self-seed in the garden?

Yes, sometimes. Drop seed in autumn often germinates the following spring. But germination is unreliable in cold wet winters, and slug damage to autumn seedlings is heavy. Saving seed and re-sowing in spring is more reliable than relying on self-seeding.

### Can I sow direct in May instead of starting indoors?

Yes — direct-sow in late April–May. Plants flower 2–3 weeks later than indoor-started ones, but germination is generally good. Thin to 25 cm spacing once 4 leaves form.

### How long do the cut flowers last?

5–10 days in a vase if conditioned well: cut at dawn, plunge into water, recut at angle indoors. See the [conditioning cut flowers guide](/posts/condition-cut-flowers-before-arranging-en/) for tips.

### Are asters good for beginners?

Yes — among the easiest annual cut flowers from seed. Forgiving of mistakes, fast germinating, reliable flowers. Good first project for a child's garden patch.

### Do I need a greenhouse?

No — a sunny windowsill or kitchen counter works for indoor sowing. A cold frame or unheated greenhouse helps for hardening off, but a sheltered porch or shaded outdoor spot also works.

### How does this compare to growing from plug plants?

Plug plants from a garden centre cost £4–8 for a tray of 6 — about the same as a packet of seed but with only 6 plants. Seed gives 50+ plants per pack and you can save seed each year for free thereafter. The tradeoff is a few weeks more work.
