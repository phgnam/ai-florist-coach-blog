## Quick Answer

Delphinium spikes fall over because the stems are hollow, the spike is top-heavy with 80+ florets, and the wind catches the flat face of the open spike. Stake at the right time — when the spike is just emerging at 30 cm tall — using a 4-cane ring stake or a sturdy linking stake. Thin to 3–5 main stems per crown, water deeply but not overhead, and cut spikes for the vase before forecast storms.

## Why are delphinium stems so prone to snapping?

Hybrid delphiniums (*Delphinium elatum* group) are bred for spectacular flower spikes — 1.5 to 2 metres tall, with 80–120 individual florets opening from the base of the spike upwards over 3–4 weeks. The trade-off for that height is structural: the stems are hollow, slender relative to the spike length, and the leverage on a fully open spike during a windstorm is enormous.

Three structural facts explain the break problem:

- **Hollow internodes.** Delphinium stems have a cavity through the middle of each internode, like a bamboo cane. The wall is just 2–3 mm thick on a flowering stem. Bend it 30 ° and the cavity collapses; bend it 45 ° and the stem snaps clean.
- **Asymmetric spike face.** Florets open all around the spike but the largest faces are aligned. Wind catches the flatter side and rotates the spike, which transfers the force to the base of the stem.
- **Heavy upper-third weight.** A fully open spike of 80 florets weighs 200–400 g; the bottom of the stem is hollow, narrow, and trying to support all of it.

Add a thunderstorm with 60 km/h gusts, and even well-staked spikes can snap. Add no staking and a 30 km/h breeze, and the spikes go down within minutes.

The solution is not heavier staking but earlier staking. A spike supported from 30 cm tall develops a stiffer base; a spike supported only when it's already wobbling at 1.5 m has thin tissue at the bottom and breaks at the support point.

## When is the right time to stake delphiniums?

Most gardeners stake too late. The classic mistake is waiting until the spike is at 1 m tall before pushing canes in — by which time the lower stem is hollow and growing fast, and the cane tie ends up exactly where the stem is most likely to snap.

The right timing is much earlier:

- **Stage 1: shoots emerge in early spring.** Mark each crown with a small stake (a 30 cm cane) so you don't accidentally hoe through it.
- **Stage 2: shoots reach 30 cm.** Install the main staking — a 4-cane ring or a peony-style support — around the crown. The growing stems will rise up through the support, and the stems thicken at the points where they pass through.
- **Stage 3: spike reaches 60–80 cm.** Add ties on the side facing the prevailing wind, loose enough that the stem can flex 5 cm without strain.
- **Stage 4: spike reaches 1.2 m.** Add a final tie 60 cm above the previous one, again loose. Check all ties weekly; tighten if loose, never over-tight.

A spike that has been growing through its support since 30 cm tall behaves completely differently from one staked late. The internodes thicken, the stem develops a stiffer woody base in the lower 30 cm, and even a major storm rarely breaks it.

For the same staking-early principle on different tall flowers, the [staking tall dahlias](/posts/staking-tall-dahlias-en/) post applies parallel reasoning to dahlias.

## What kind of staking actually holds a delphinium up?

Three staking methods work well, in increasing order of investment.

**1. The 4-cane ring stake** (homemade, free):

- Push 4 bamboo canes (1.5 m tall) into the soil 30 cm apart in a square around the crown.
- Run twine around the outside of the canes at 30 cm and 60 cm height.
- The growing spikes rise up through the square frame.
- Tie each spike loosely to the nearest cane at 60 cm and 120 cm height.

Pros: free, simple, looks tidy. Cons: each spike still needs an individual tie at the top.

**2. The peony-style ring support** (commercial, £8–£15 per support):

- A circular metal hoop on 3–4 metal legs, sold as "peony support" or "perennial support."
- Push the legs into the soil over the dormant crown in early spring.
- The hoop sits at 60–90 cm height; spikes grow up through the hoop.
- The hoop catches the spikes naturally; minimal individual tying.

Pros: tidy, fast to install, supports multiple spikes from one crown. Cons: cost adds up for a large delphinium border.

**3. The linking stake system** (commercial, £4–£8 per stake):

- L-shaped metal stakes that link to each other to form custom shapes.
- Push 4–6 around the crown in early spring.
- Adjustable height; can lift the supports as the spike grows.

Pros: very flexible, handles odd-shaped clumps. Cons: more fiddly than ring supports.

For all three methods, the rule is the same: install before the spikes need them, not after they've started leaning. A delphinium leaning more than 20 ° has already developed an asymmetric stem thickening that won't fully straighten.

## Why does thinning improve the structural strength?

A vigorous delphinium crown can produce 8–12 spikes; a thinned crown produces 3–5 stronger spikes. The thinning is counter-intuitive — fewer stems sounds like a weaker plant — but each remaining spike develops a thicker base because the crown's resources concentrate on fewer stems.

The procedure:

1. **In early spring**, when shoots are 15 cm tall, identify the strongest 3–5 shoots per crown.
2. **Snap or cut off the weaker shoots** at ground level.
3. **Discard the thinned shoots** (they are not robust enough to use as cuttings).
4. **The remaining shoots** develop noticeably thicker stem bases over the next 4–6 weeks.

The flower display is barely smaller — the 3–5 strong spikes carry larger, fuller florets than the 8–12 thin spikes would have. The wind resistance improves dramatically.

For young plants in their first 1–2 years, do not thin; the plant is still establishing and benefits from all the growth. From year 3 onwards, thinning is the single most effective strength improvement.

## How does watering and feeding affect the stem strength?

Stem strength depends partly on potassium availability. Plants given high-nitrogen feeds produce tall, soft, fast-growing stems with thin walls; plants given balanced or potassium-leaning feeds produce slightly shorter, stouter stems with thicker walls.

A practical feeding schedule:

| Stage | Feed |
|---|---|
| Early spring (shoots emerging) | Top-dress with garden compost; no liquid feed |
| Shoots at 30 cm | Half-strength balanced liquid feed |
| Spikes at 60 cm | Half-strength tomato (potassium-leaning) feed |
| Spikes at 120 cm | Stop feeding; plant uses stored nutrients |
| After flowering | Half-strength tomato feed monthly to support next year's crown |

Watering matters too. Delphiniums want deep, even moisture but cannot handle overhead splashing. Three rules:

- **Deep watering once or twice a week** rather than light daily sprinkles. Deep watering forces roots down and produces stockier plants.
- **Water at the base, not from above.** Water on the spike adds weight to the upper stem; in a storm, that weight is what often snaps the stem.
- **Mulch with 5 cm of compost or composted bark** in early spring to retain moisture without overhead application.

## What do you do when a spike has already broken?

A snapped delphinium spike is not lost — it usually still flowers in a vase, and the lower stem can sometimes regrow secondary flowers later in the season.

For the broken spike:

1. **Cut the spike cleanly** at the break point with sharp snips.
2. **Strip the lower 10 cm of leaves** for vase work.
3. **Place in a tall vase** with cool water and standard cut-flower care. The [keep cut flowers fresh](/posts/keep-cut-flowers-fresh-en/) post covers vase-life techniques that apply directly to delphiniums.
4. **Display indoors** for 7–10 days.

For the remaining lower stem on the plant:

1. **Cut the broken stub** back to a healthy node 5–10 cm above ground.
2. **Apply no fertiliser** — the plant needs to focus on regrowth, not new top growth.
3. **Watch for side shoots** to emerge from leaf nodes within 2–3 weeks.
4. **Stake any side shoots** as they grow; they often produce smaller secondary spikes within 6–8 weeks.

The crown itself is unaffected. Next year's growth comes from the underground crown, not from this year's stems, so a broken spike has no effect on the following year's display.

For storm-prone gardens, cutting the spikes for the vase pre-emptively before a forecast storm is the most reliable defence. Spikes cut at half-bloom (bottom third of florets open) finish opening in the vase and last 7–10 days.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are some delphinium cultivars less prone to falling than others?**
Yes. The Pacific Giant series and the New Millennium hybrids are bred for stronger stems compared with the older 'Black Knight' and 'King Arthur' varieties. For storm-prone gardens, choose the newer breeding.

**Should I cut spent spikes back to the ground after flowering?**
No, cut the spent spike back to the lower leaf node only. The remaining plant photosynthesises through summer and feeds the crown for next year. Cut to the ground only at the very end of the season when foliage yellows.

**Can I grow delphiniums in pots and avoid staking?**
Pots cannot really avoid staking — the spikes are still 1–1.5 m tall and the pot wall doesn't help. A 30 cm pot with a 1.5 m cane and shorter cultivars (Magic Fountain series, 70–90 cm) is a workable compromise.

**Why are my delphiniums shorter this year than last?**
Common causes: less water in spring, partial shade from a neighbouring plant that has grown, or crown exhaustion if the plant is 5+ years old. Lift and divide every 4–5 years to maintain vigour.

**Do delphiniums need acidic or alkaline soil?**
Slightly alkaline (pH 7.0–7.5) is ideal. Acidic soils produce shorter, weaker spikes; very alkaline soils are also fine. A handful of garden lime in early spring on acidic soil makes a noticeable difference.
