## Quick Answer

Coleus naturally grows tall and single-stemmed unless you pinch it. Start when the plant has 3–4 sets of true leaves: pinch out the very tip just above a leaf node with your thumb and finger. This forces two side shoots from the leaf axils. Repeat every 2–3 weeks through the growing season, removing about 1–2 cm of soft tip each time, and pinch off any flower spikes as they appear. Plants treated this way stay bushy and full all summer.

## What is coleus and why does it grow leggy?

*Coleus* (now formally *Plectranthus scutellarioides*, but everyone still calls it coleus) is a tender perennial in the mint family grown almost exclusively for its dramatic patterned leaves. Cultivars come in lime green, magenta, deep burgundy, brown-and-yellow, and almost-black, with patterns ranging from solid to mottled to nerved.

Left to its own devices, coleus grows in the shape its species evolved for — a tall, upward-reaching stem competing for sunlight in a tropical understory. The first signs of legginess:

- Long internodes (stem segments between leaves) of 4–6 cm or more.
- A single dominant central stem with no side branches.
- Lower leaves yellowing and dropping.
- A "lollipop" silhouette with a tuft of leaves at the top of a bare stem.

In a pot or bedding scheme this looks weak. Pinching is what turns coleus from a tall single stem into the dense, mound-shaped plant most gardeners want.

## What does "pinching" actually mean?

Pinching is the simplest pruning technique in gardening: removing the very growing tip of a stem with your thumb and forefinger, or with sharp scissors. The cut should be just above a leaf node — the small bump on the stem where leaves emerge.

**Why it works:** Plants concentrate growth hormones (auxin) at the very tip of each stem. This hormone suppresses the side buds in the leaf axils. Remove the tip and the suppression is lifted; the two side buds at the topmost remaining node activate within days and grow into two new stems. Each of those stems can then be pinched again to produce two more, and so on.

The mathematics are dramatic. A single pinch turns one stem into two. A second pinch on each new stem turns those two into four. A third round produces eight. Three rounds of pinching can take a single seedling to an eight-tipped bushy plant in 8–10 weeks.

## When should I make the first pinch?

The first pinch is the most important. Done too early or too late, you lose the benefit.

**Too early.** A seedling with only its cotyledons (seed leaves) does not have axillary buds yet. Pinching now removes the only growing tissue and stalls the plant.

**The right moment.** Wait until the plant has at least 3 sets of true leaves above the cotyledons. The lower leaf nodes will have visible side buds — small green bumps in the leaf axils. Now is the time.

**Too late.** Once stems are over 15 cm long with 6+ sets of leaves, pinching still works but the plant is already shaped wrong. Side branches will fan out from the lower stem and the centre will look thin.

For nursery-bought plants, the first pinch should happen as soon as the plant has settled in (about a week after potting on), unless the grower has already pinched — many commercial coleus arrive already branched.

## How exactly do I pinch — and what about the cuttings?

The technique:

1. **Find the topmost healthy leaf pair** on each main stem.
2. **Pinch the soft growing tip** just above that leaf pair, removing 1–2 cm of stem and the tiny tip leaves.
3. **Use a clean cut** — clean fingernails are fine, but if any plants in the area show signs of viral disease, use scissors wiped between plants.
4. **Do not cut into hard wood** — coleus stems harden over time at the base. Pinching only works on the soft, green tip.

**The cuttings.** Every pinch produces a free cutting, and coleus is one of the easiest plants in the world to root.

1. Trim the cutting to a 5–8 cm length below a leaf node.
2. Strip the lower leaves so two leaves remain at the top.
3. Drop the cutting in a glass of water.
4. Roots emerge in 5–10 days at room temperature.
5. Pot up in damp compost once roots are 2 cm long.

In a single season you can multiply a particularly attractive cultivar from one plant to dozens by repeatedly rooting the pinched tips. Save them through winter on a bright windowsill to overwinter colour combinations.

## How often should I keep pinching through the season?

Pinching is not a single event. The plant will keep extending if you let it.

**Frequency:** Every 2–3 weeks through the active growing season (late spring through early autumn).

**Method on later pinches:**

- Inspect each plant.
- Find any new tip that is extending faster than its neighbours.
- Pinch it back to keep the silhouette mounded.
- Continue until you are happy with the shape.

The aim is a plant where the top is roughly flat or domed, with all stems flowering tipless at about the same height. Even the most disciplined pinching tends to leave one or two stems escaping; pinch those weekly if you want a precise mound.

**Stopping pinching.** Once nights cool in autumn (below about 12 °C), growth slows and pinching has less effect. Stop pinching about 6 weeks before your expected first frost. New growth will not have time to harden, so leave the plant in the shape it has.

## Should I pinch off the flowers too?

Yes, almost always. Coleus flowers are slim spikes of pale blue or violet — pretty in their own right but a sign that the plant is shifting energy from foliage growth (the show) to seed production (a one-shot finale).

Once a coleus flowers heavily, leaf colour fades, leaves shrink, and the plant begins to die back. Some named cultivars are notably "flower-shy" but most modern coleus will produce flower spikes by late summer.

**The fix:**

1. Watch for elongating tips that look different from the leaf-bearing tips — narrower, paler, with smaller leaves.
2. Pinch them off as soon as you spot them, before any flowers open.
3. Continue weekly inspection through late summer.

This is part of the reason regular pinching matters: it prevents the plant ever reaching the flowering stage in most growing seasons.

## What conditions does coleus need to respond well to pinching?

Pinching only works if the plant has the resources to push new growth. Pair the technique with the right conditions.

**Light:** Bright but not baking. 4–6 hours of soft sun is ideal; pure deep shade leaves plants weak. Hot midday sun fades the colour and can scorch leaves.

**Water:** Steadily moist. Coleus wilts dramatically when dry but recovers quickly. Repeated severe wilting causes leaf drop. Do not let pots dry to dust.

**Feed:** Light, balanced liquid feed every 2 weeks during active growth (a 10-10-10 or similar). Heavy nitrogen produces lush leaves at the expense of colour saturation.

**Temperature:** Coleus is a tropical and resents temperatures below 12 °C. Bring plants indoors well before the first frost.

**Pot size:** Generous. A 2–3 L pot supports one bushy plant; a 25 cm hanging basket holds 3 plants comfortably.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My coleus is already leggy — is it too late to pinch?**
No, but the shape will not fully recover. Pinch back hard (cut every stem to about 5–8 cm above the soil, just above a strong leaf pair). The plant looks bare for 2–3 weeks but produces multiple new shoots from each cut node and is bushier within a month.

**Why are my coleus leaves losing colour?**
Three common causes: too much direct sun (bleaches the dark cultivars), too much nitrogen feed (greens out the patterned colours), or near-flowering (the plant shifts energy and leaf colour fades). Address each in turn.

**Can I overwinter coleus indoors?**
Yes. Either bring whole pots indoors before the first frost and grow on a bright windowsill, or take 5 cm cuttings in late summer and root them indoors. Cuttings are usually easier — they take less space and produce fresh, vigorous plants for spring.

**Are coleus toxic to cats and dogs?**
Yes — coleus contains essential oils (including diterpenes) that can cause vomiting, drooling, and skin irritation in cats and dogs. Keep indoor plants out of reach. For documented pet-safe alternatives, see [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).

**Why are my pinched cuttings rotting in water before they root?**
Usually the water is too warm or the lower leaves are touching it. Use cool fresh water, change it every 3–4 days, strip lower leaves cleanly, and place the glass in bright but indirect light at 18–22 °C.
