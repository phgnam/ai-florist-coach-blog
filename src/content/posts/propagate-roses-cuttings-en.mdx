Take **15–20 cm semi-hardwood cuttings** from healthy rose stems in late summer (August–September), dip in rooting hormone, and plant in a 50/50 mix of perlite and potting soil. Keep moist under a humidity dome or plastic bag. Roots develop in **4–8 weeks**, and cuttings can be transplanted the following spring.

## When is the best time to take rose cuttings?

Timing matters more than technique:

**Late summer (August–September)** is ideal. Stems are **semi-hardwood** at this point — firm enough to handle but still young enough to root. The current season's growth has matured past the soft, floppy stage but hasn't become fully woody.

**How to identify semi-hardwood**: Bend a stem. If it snaps cleanly, it's too mature (hardwood). If it bends without resistance, it's too young (softwood). If it bends and then snaps with a fibrous break, it's perfect semi-hardwood.

**Softwood cuttings** (late spring/early summer) root faster but are more prone to wilting and fungal problems. **Hardwood cuttings** (winter) are the easiest but slowest — they root over winter in the ground and leaf out in spring.

## How do you take and prepare rose cuttings? **Step 1 — Select the right
stem**: Choose a healthy, disease-free stem that has recently finished
flowering. The stem should be about pencil-thickness and 20–25 cm long. **Step 2
— Make your cuts**: - Cut just **below a leaf node** (the bump where a leaf
attaches) at the bottom — this is where root cells concentrate - Cut just
**above a leaf node** at the top, leaving the cutting 15–20 cm long - Make the
**bottom cut at a 45° angle** (increases surface area for rooting and helps you
remember which end is down) - Make the **top cut straight** (so you can tell top
from bottom) **Step 3 — Prepare the cutting**: - Remove all **lower leaves**,
keeping only the top 2–3 leaves - If remaining leaves are large, **cut them in
half** to reduce moisture loss - Remove any flower buds or hips — you want
energy going to roots, not reproduction - Scrape the bark lightly along the
bottom 2 cm with a knife (this "wounding" exposes more cambium tissue for
rooting) **Step 4 — Apply rooting hormone**: - Dip the bottom 2 cm in **powdered
rooting hormone** (IBA — indole-3-butyric acid) - Tap off excess — a thin
coating is enough. Too much can actually inhibit rooting ## What is the best
method for rooting rose cuttings? ### Soil method (most reliable) 1. Fill a pot
with **50/50 perlite and potting soil** (or 100% perlite for maximum drainage)
2. Water the mix thoroughly 3. Use a pencil to poke a hole 5–7 cm deep 4. Insert
the cutting (hormone-dipped end down) — don't push it in directly or you'll
scrape off the hormone 5. Firm the soil gently around the cutting 6. Cover with
a **clear plastic bag** or place inside a clear plastic bottle with the bottom
cut off (instant humidity dome) 7. Place in **bright indirect light** — no
direct sun, which would overheat inside the plastic 8. Keep soil moist but not
waterlogged. Mist the inside of the plastic bag if it dries out 9. **Remove the
plastic briefly** every few days for air circulation (prevents mould) 10. Roots
typically develop in **4–8 weeks** **How to check for roots**: Gently tug the
cutting after 6 weeks. If you feel resistance, roots have formed. You can also
look for new leaf growth — this almost always indicates successful rooting. ###
Water method (easier but less reliable) 1. Place prepared cuttings in a jar of
clean water, submerging the bottom 5 cm 2. Change water every 3–4 days to
prevent bacterial growth 3. Place in bright indirect light 4. Roots appear in
**3–6 weeks** as white threads 5. Once roots are 3–5 cm long, transplant to soil
The water method is fun and visible, but water roots are more fragile than soil
roots. Transplant carefully and keep the soil consistently moist for the first 2
weeks while roots adapt. ## How do you care for rooted cuttings? Once rooted,
the cutting is a baby plant that needs gentle treatment: 1. **Pot up** into
individual 10–12 cm pots with quality potting soil 2. **Keep in a sheltered
spot** — protected from wind, harsh sun, and frost 3. **Water consistently** —
the small root system can't handle drought 4. **Don't fertilise** for the first
6–8 weeks — tender roots burn easily 5. After 8 weeks, start feeding with
**half-strength** liquid fertiliser every 2 weeks 6. **Transplant to the
garden** the following spring after hardening off (gradually exposing to outdoor
conditions over 1–2 weeks) ## Frequently Asked Questions **Can you root a rose
cutting from a bouquet?** You can try, but success rates are low (10–20%).
Florist roses are often treated with preservatives and may have been out of
water for days. For best results, take the cutting as soon as possible, use
rooting hormone, and accept that most won't take. **Do all roses root from
cuttings?** Most do, but success rates vary. Old garden roses and shrub roses
root easily (60–80% success). Hybrid teas are more difficult (30–50%). Grafted
roses will root but produce a plant on its own roots, which may behave
differently from the grafted original. **How long until a cutting-grown rose
blooms?** Typically **1–2 years** after rooting. Some vigorous varieties may
produce a few small blooms in the first year, but it's better to pinch off early
buds so the plant focuses on root and stem development.
