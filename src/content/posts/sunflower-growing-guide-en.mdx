Sunflowers are one of gardening's most straightforward pleasures — large seeds, fast germination, dramatic growth, spectacular flowers. They are genuinely hard to kill if you give them sun, and they reward even the most casual gardener with flowers that seem disproportionately magnificent for the effort involved. But there's a significant difference between a standard sunflower and an exceptional one, and the gap is largely explained by a handful of choices made before the seed ever goes in the ground.

## Choosing the right variety

The sunflower family (*Helianthus annuus*) has been bred into hundreds of varieties with dramatically different characteristics. Understanding the main categories helps you choose for your specific goals:

**Giant single-stem varieties** — *'Russian Giant', 'Mongolian Giant', 'American Giant'* — produce the classic enormous single flower on a tall stem. Heights of 2–4 metres are typical; flower heads of 40–60cm across are achievable in good conditions. These are the sunflowers of childhood imagination and village fairs.

**Multi-branching varieties** — *'Velvet Queen', 'Procut Orange', 'Soraya'* — produce a central flower followed by a succession of side branches, each bearing smaller flowers over an extended period. Much more productive as cut flowers; a single plant of 'Velvet Queen' can produce 12–20 stems over 6–8 weeks.

**Dwarf varieties** — *'Teddy Bear', 'Big Smile', 'Elf'* — compact plants of 30–60cm suitable for containers, small gardens, and front borders. Flowers are proportionally smaller but densely petalled and very cheerful.

**Pollenless varieties** — *'Procut' series, 'Sunrich' series* — bred specifically for the cut flower market, these produce no pollen, which means they don't shed (vital for vase use, as pollen stains are extremely difficult to remove from fabric and surfaces) and they last longer in the vase because the plant doesn't receive the "mission accomplished" hormonal signal that pollen release triggers.

**Unusual colour varieties** — beyond the classic yellow: *'Chianti'* (deep burgundy-red), *'Lemon Queen'* (pale yellow), *'Claret'* (dark wine red), *'Chocolate'* (rusty brown), *'Strawberry Blonde'* (coral-peach).

## When to sow

Sunflowers cannot tolerate frost and should not be sown outdoors until **the last frost date has passed** and soil temperatures are reliably above 10°C (usually May in most temperate climates, though this varies significantly by location).

For earlier flowers, **sow indoors 2–3 weeks before the last frost date** in individual pots (sunflowers develop a taproot and strongly dislike root disturbance — never grow in flat seed trays to be pricked out). Use 9cm pots minimum; larger is better.

**Succession sowing** — sowing a new batch of seeds every 2–3 weeks from May through June — dramatically extends the flowering period, producing a continuous supply of cut flowers through August and September.

## How to sow

Sunflower seeds are large and straightforward to handle. Sow at a depth of **2–3cm** (about twice the seed's own diameter). In the ground, sow 30–45cm apart for branching varieties; 45–60cm for giant single-stem varieties. In pots, one seed per 9cm pot.

Germination is fast — typically **7–14 days** in warm conditions. The seedlings grow rapidly; in good conditions, a sunflower can add 5–10cm of height per week.

**Birds and slugs** are the primary threats to young plants. Cover direct-sown seeds with a cloche or fleece until they're well established, and protect young transplants from slug damage with copper tape, wool pellets, or regular slug patrols in the evenings.

## Site, soil, and feeding

**Full sun is non-negotiable**. Sunflowers need a minimum of 6–8 hours of direct sunlight daily. A plant in partial shade will lean aggressively toward the light and produce smaller, weaker flowers. Choose your sunniest spot.

**Soil**: Sunflowers are tolerant of most soil types but perform best in **well-drained soil with moderate fertility**. Very rich soil (heavily manured vegetable beds) produces impressive leafy growth but sometimes at the expense of flowers. Sunflowers in moderately fertile, well-drained soil tend to flower more abundantly than those in very rich soil.

**Support**: Giant varieties will need staking in exposed or windy gardens. A bamboo cane and soft ties protect the stem from wind rock.

**Watering**: Young plants need regular watering to establish. Once mature (with a root system that extends 60cm or more into the soil), sunflowers are surprisingly drought-tolerant — their deep roots access groundwater that shallow-rooted plants cannot reach. In very hot, dry summers, watering the base of the plant once a week is usually sufficient.

**Feeding**: A dilute liquid fertiliser (a balanced NPK fertiliser, or a specialist flower fertiliser with higher potassium) applied every 2 weeks once the plant has reached 30–40cm encourages strong flowering. Stop feeding once the flower buds have formed.

## Cutting sunflowers

**Cut in the morning**, when the stems are turgid with water. Use sharp, clean secateurs or scissors. Cut the stem as long as possible — you can always shorten it later.

**When to cut**: For the longest vase life, cut sunflowers when the **bud is just beginning to open** — showing colour but not yet fully unfurled. Fully open flowers look spectacular but have a shorter vase life. A cut bud will open fully in the vase within 24–48 hours.

**Vase care**: Re-cut stems at a 45-degree angle, remove all leaves below the waterline, and place in clean, cool water immediately. Change water every 2 days. Vase life of well-conditioned sunflowers is typically **7–12 days**.

For branching varieties, cutting the central stem encourages side branches to develop — the more you cut, the more it grows. Regular cutting genuinely increases total yield.

## Saving seeds

Sunflowers are among the easiest plants from which to save seed. Allow the flower head to dry on the plant until the back of the head turns brown and the seeds begin to loosen. Cut the head and hang it in a dry, ventilated space to finish drying. Seeds can then be stored in a paper envelope in a cool, dry location and sown the following year.

Note: F1 hybrid varieties (marked as such on seed packets) will not come true from saved seed — the offspring will revert to more mixed characteristics. Open-pollinated or heritage varieties are the best choice for seed saving.

A well-chosen, well-sited sunflower is one of those plants that makes gardening feel both effortless and deeply satisfying. Few flowers deliver such an unambiguous expression of summer.
