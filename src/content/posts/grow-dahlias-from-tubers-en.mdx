Plant dahlia tubers **10–15 cm deep** with the eye (growth point) facing up, in **full sun** and **rich, well-draining soil**, after all frost risk has passed. Space 45–90 cm apart depending on variety. Don't water until shoots emerge, then water deeply and consistently. Pinching the central stem at 30 cm height doubles your bloom count.

## When should you plant dahlia tubers?

Timing is critical — dahlias are frost-tender and will rot in cold, wet soil:

- **Outdoors**: Plant after your last frost date when soil temperature reaches **15°C** (60°F). For most temperate zones, this means **mid-April to late May**.
- **Indoor start**: For earlier blooms, pot up tubers indoors **4–6 weeks before last frost** in a warm, bright spot. Transplant hardened-off plants after frost danger passes.
- **Too early is worse than too late.** A tuber planted in cold, soggy soil will rot. One planted 2–3 weeks late will catch up quickly in warm summer soil.

Check soil temperature with a soil thermometer — this is more reliable than calendar dates, especially in unpredictable spring weather.

## How do you prepare and plant dahlia tubers? **Inspect tubers** before
planting. Each tuber must have at least one **eye** — a small pink or green bump
at the base of the old stem. Tubers without eyes won't sprout. Discard any that
are shrivelled, mushy, or mouldy. **Planting steps**: 1. Dig a hole **15 cm
deep** and 30 cm wide 2. Mix a handful of **compost** and a slow-release
fertiliser (bone meal or balanced 5-10-10) into the bottom 3. Lay the tuber
**horizontally** with the eye facing up 4. Cover with soil but **don't water
yet** — dormant tubers sitting in wet soil rot. Wait for shoots to emerge (1–3
weeks) 5. Once shoots are 5–10 cm tall, begin watering regularly **Spacing**:
Small varieties (under 90 cm): 45 cm apart. Medium (90–120 cm): 60 cm. Dinner
plate types (120 cm+): 90 cm. **Staking**: Install a sturdy stake at planting
time — not after the plant is large and the roots are established. Tie stems
loosely as they grow. ## How do you pinch dahlias for more flowers? Pinching is
the single most impactful technique for abundant blooms: 1. When the central
stem reaches **30 cm tall** with 3–4 sets of leaves, pinch out the **growing
tip** (the top pair of tiny leaves) 2. This forces the plant to branch from the
lower leaf nodes, creating **2–4 stems** instead of one 3. Each stem produces
flowers, so pinching effectively **doubles or triples** your bloom count 4. The
trade-off: first blooms arrive **1–2 weeks later** than unpinched plants. Worth
it for the vastly greater flower production For cut flower growers, pinching is
non-negotiable. For gardeners who want one early bloom, you can skip pinching on
a few plants and pinch the rest. ## What problems should you watch for? **Slugs
and snails**: The number one pest of young dahlias. Emerging shoots are
irresistible to slugs. Use copper tape around pots, beer traps, or organic slug
pellets from planting day. **Earwigs**: These insects love dahlia petals and can
shred blooms overnight. Trap them with upturned pots stuffed with straw placed
on stakes near plants — earwigs hide inside during the day and can be emptied
away from the garden. **Powdery mildew**: White powdery coating on leaves in
late summer. Improve air circulation (don't overcrowd), water at the base (not
overhead), and remove affected leaves. **Leggy, weak growth**: Not enough sun.
Dahlias need **minimum 6 hours** of direct sun daily — 8+ is ideal. ## How do
you store dahlia tubers over winter? In zones where the ground freezes (zone 7
and colder), tubers must be lifted and stored: 1. After the first frost blackens
the foliage, **cut stems to 10 cm** 2. Carefully dig tubers with a garden fork —
work 30 cm out from the stem to avoid damage 3. Rinse off soil and let tubers
dry for **2–3 days** in a sheltered, frost-free spot 4. Pack in slightly damp
**peat moss, vermiculite, or wood shavings** inside a cardboard box 5. Store in
a **cool (5–10°C), dark, frost-free** place — garage, basement, or unheated room
6. Check monthly for rot (discard affected tubers) or excessive drying (mist
lightly if shrivelling) In zones 8–10, tubers can stay in the ground year-round
with a thick layer of mulch for insulation. ## Frequently Asked Questions **Can
you grow dahlias from seed?** Yes, but seed-grown dahlias won't be identical to
the parent plant and take longer to bloom. Tubers give you guaranteed colour,
form, and earlier flowers. Seed-grown dahlias are fun for experimentation but
impractical for specific varieties. **How many flowers does one dahlia tuber
produce?** A well-grown, pinched dahlia plant produces **15–30 blooms** over its
season (July–October). Cut flower varieties can produce 20+ stems with regular
harvesting. **Why are my dahlia tubers not sprouting?** Common causes: no eye on
the tuber (it can't sprout), soil too cold (wait for 15°C+), soil too wet (tuber
rotting), or planted too deep (try 10 cm instead of 15 cm for heavy soils).
