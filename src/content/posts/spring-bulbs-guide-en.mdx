Spring bulbs are one of gardening's great rewards — plant them in autumn, forget about them through the dark winter months, and then watch as the garden transforms in late winter and spring with almost no effort required. They are among the best-value plants available: a single tulip bulb can produce a flower worth several pounds at a florist, and a well-chosen mixed bulb planting can give colour from February through to May with a single afternoon's work in October.

## Understanding spring bulbs

All the familiar spring bulbs — tulips, daffodils, crocuses, hyacinths, alliums, fritillaries — share one characteristic: they need a **cold period (vernalisation)** to flower properly. This is why they're planted in autumn: the cold winter months in the ground provide exactly the chilling period they need. Without this cold period, bulbs planted in spring simply don't flower.

The cold period also explains why tulips bought at the supermarket and grown indoors in water produce smaller, weaker flowers than bulbs grown through a proper outdoor winter — the indoor "forced" bulbs experience an artificial, abbreviated cold period.

## When to plant

**October and November** are the optimal planting months in most temperate climates. This gives bulbs time to root before the ground freezes while ensuring the soil is cool enough to prevent premature sprouting.

Tulips specifically benefit from being planted **as late as possible** — even into December in many climates — because of their susceptibility to tulip fire (*Botrytis tulipae*), a fungal disease that overwinters in soil. The later you plant, the less time the bulbs spend in soil conditions that favour the disease before the cold kills the fungal spores.

Daffodils are less sensitive and are better planted **earlier in autumn** (September–October) to give them more time to establish.

## How deep to plant

The standard rule is to plant bulbs at **three times the depth of the bulb's diameter**. A tulip bulb 5cm in diameter should be planted at 15cm depth; a small crocus corm of 2cm at 6cm depth.

Depth matters for several reasons:
- Too shallow and bulbs are vulnerable to frost damage, squirrel predation, and drying out
- Too deep and they struggle to push through the soil to flower
- The right depth also helps bulbs flower year after year (see naturalising, below)

## The best spring bulbs to plant

**Tulips** are the stars of the spring bulb world. The variety available is extraordinary — from the simple single-flowered species tulips of March to the spectacular parrot, double, and frilled varieties of late May. Choosing varieties with different flowering times (early, mid, and late season) extends the display by 6–8 weeks.

Some outstanding tulip groups for cut flowers:
- **Triumph tulips**: The classic tulip shape, mid-season, outstanding cut flower stems. Varieties like 'Apricot Beauty' and 'Purple Prince' are consistently excellent.
- **Parrot tulips**: Flamboyantly fringed and frilled petals in extraordinary colour combinations. 'Black Parrot' is one of the most spectacular.
- **Double late tulips**: Dense peony-like blooms that produce extraordinary cut flowers. 'Angelique' (pale pink) is a perennial favourite.
- **Species tulips**: Small, elegant, early-flowering, and the most reliable perennial tulips. *Tulipa sylvestris* and *T. clusiana* naturalize and return reliably for years.

**Daffodils** (Narcissus) are the easiest spring bulbs — they naturalise readily, deter pests (all daffodil species are toxic, which means squirrels and deer leave them completely alone), and return reliably year after year. They're excellent for naturalising in grass. Note: daffodil sap contains toxic crystals that can irritate the skin of other flowers — always keep daffodils in a vase alone, or condition them for 24 hours in their own water before mixing with other cut flowers.

**Crocuses** flower earliest — often in February — and are vital for early pollinators. They naturalise in grass beautifully, creating the effect of a tapestry when planted in quantity. *Crocus tommasinianus* is the best species for naturalising in grass.

**Hyacinths** offer the most intense fragrance of all spring bulbs. A single hyacinth can perfume an entire room. They're excellent in containers near doorways and entrances.

**Alliums** (ornamental onions) flower later than most bulbs — May and June — bridging the gap between spring and summer. Their globe-shaped flowerheads on tall stems are spectacular in borders and exceptional as cut flowers.

## Planting in containers

Containers allow you to grow spectacular spring displays even without a garden, and the technique of **lasagne planting** multiplies the display from a single pot:

1. Place a layer of bulbs at the bottom of the pot (the largest, latest-flowering bulbs go deepest — alliums or late tulips)
2. Add a layer of compost
3. Place a second layer of bulbs above, offset from the first layer so they're not directly above each other (mid-season tulips or daffodils)
4. Add more compost
5. Place the earliest-flowering, smallest bulbs at the top (crocuses, scillas, grape hyacinths)
6. Top with a final layer of compost and firm gently

This creates a pot that flowers in successive waves from early spring through late spring, from a single afternoon's work in autumn.

## Naturalising — making bulbs return year after year

Most tulips are treated as annuals — planted fresh each autumn — because standard hybrid tulips don't reliably return. However, some bulbs naturalise beautifully:

**Daffodils**: Will naturalise and spread in grass and borders indefinitely. The key is to allow the foliage to die back naturally (at least 6 weeks after flowering) before cutting — the leaves are converting sunlight into stored energy for next year's bulb.

**Species tulips**: Unlike hybrid tulips, species tulips are reliably perennial. *Tulipa sprengeri*, *T. clusiana*, and *T. sylvestris* all reliably return and slowly spread.

**Crocuses**: Naturalise freely in grass and multiply over time.

**Alliums**: Return reliably and often multiply, both through bulb offsets and self-seeding.

For tulips you want to try to keep: allow the foliage to die completely, lift the bulbs after they've died down, allow to dry in a warm shed or garage, and store in paper bags in a cool, dry place until autumn.

Spring bulbs are a form of garden optimism — planting them in autumn is an act of faith in the spring that's still months away. And when that spring comes, they never disappoint.
