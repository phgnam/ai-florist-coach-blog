## Quick Answer

Impatiens stop flowering for one of four reasons: too much direct sun causing heat stress, excess nitrogen pushing leaf at the expense of flowers, downy mildew (a serious disease that has decimated *I. walleriana* since 2011), or root binding in pots. Diagnose by checking the leaf signature: yellow undersides with white fuzz means downy mildew; pale crispy edges mean sun damage; dark lush leaves with no buds mean too much nitrogen. Each cause has a different fix.

## Why are impatiens such a finicky bedding plant compared with twenty years ago?

Until about 2011, *Impatiens walleriana* — the standard pink, red, white, and orange bedding impatiens — was one of the easiest summer-long shade plants in temperate gardens. A wave of *Plasmopara obducens* downy mildew that swept through Europe and North America in the early 2010s changed that. Many gardens that grew impatiens for decades suddenly couldn't keep them flowering past mid-July, and the cause looked the same as the symptoms of nitrogen problems and sun stress.

Today, "impatiens not flowering" almost always falls into one of four categories:

- **Sun and heat stress** — *I. walleriana* genuinely cannot flower in 6+ hours of direct afternoon sun.
- **Excess nitrogen** — over-fed plants put energy into leaves, not flowers.
- **Downy mildew (*P. obducens*)** — the modern disease problem.
- **Pot-bound roots** — common in container plants by midseason.

Two newer Impatiens groups complicate the picture. *I. hawkeri* (New Guinea impatiens) and the SunPatiens hybrids tolerate more sun and are nearly mildew-resistant; if you bought "impatiens" recently, check the label, because these behave differently from traditional *I. walleriana*. Most of this article focuses on classic walleriana behaviour, with notes where New Guinea types differ.

## How do you tell the difference between the four common causes?

The leaf signature gives away the cause in most cases. Spend two minutes inspecting one healthy and one struggling leaf and you can usually tell.

| Symptom | Likely cause | Action |
|---|---|---|
| White fuzzy underside, leaves curl down, then drop | Downy mildew | Remove plants, replace with resistant variety |
| Crispy pale edges, leaves wilt midday even when watered | Sun/heat stress | Move to deeper shade or shade with cloth |
| Dark green lush leaves, no buds, plant looks healthy | Excess nitrogen | Stop feeding for 3 weeks |
| Leaves yellowing inward from edges, plant looks tired | Pot-bound, drought | Repot one size up, deep-water |

Other less common signatures:

- **Brown leaf spots with halos** — bacterial leaf spot; remove affected leaves, improve airflow.
- **Stems collapsing at base** — root rot from overwatering; check drainage.
- **Holes in leaves** — slug or caterpillar damage; check at dusk.

If the diagnosis is downy mildew (white fuzzy undersides), the disease is essentially incurable on *I. walleriana*. The only reliable response is to pull the plants, bag them, dispose in the bin (not compost), and replant the area with a resistant alternative — New Guinea impatiens, SunPatiens, or a different shade flower entirely such as begonias, fuchsias, or torenia.

## Why does too much sun stop *I. walleriana* from flowering?

*Impatiens walleriana* evolved on the shaded forest floor of East African mountain forests. Direct afternoon sun above about 22 °C is genuinely too much for it; the plant prioritises survival (closing stomata, reducing photosynthesis) over reproduction (forming buds and flowers).

The signs of sun stress:

- **Wilting at midday even when soil is moist.** The roots cannot pull water fast enough to replace transpiration in direct sun.
- **Crispy pale margins on leaves**, especially the side facing the sun.
- **Bud abortion** — small buds form but never open, dropping off as tiny brown sticks.
- **Stunted plant** — overall growth stalls during the hot weeks.

Where to plant *I. walleriana* successfully:

- **Morning sun, afternoon shade** is the sweet spot.
- **Dappled shade under deciduous trees** is ideal.
- **Full deep shade** works but produces leggy stretched plants with sparser flowers.
- **Eastern aspect** with a wall blocking afternoon sun is reliable.
- **Western aspect** in full sun: not recommended; the plant survives but rarely flowers well.

For impatiens already planted in too much sun:

1. **If in a pot:** move the pot to a shadier spot.
2. **If in the ground:** install temporary shade cloth over the bed for the worst weeks of summer.
3. **If you have other shade plants in pots:** rearrange to put the impatiens behind them where the taller plants block direct sun.

Recovery from sun stress takes 1–2 weeks once shade is provided. Buds resume forming within 7 days; full flowering returns in 2–3 weeks.

For sun-tolerant alternatives, the SunPatiens series and New Guinea impatiens (*I. hawkeri*) handle 4–6 hours of direct sun without bud drop. They are slightly less prolific bloomers than walleriana in shade but more flexible across light conditions.

## Why does too much nitrogen prevent flowering?

Plants given too much nitrogen produce abundant lush green leaves and very few flowers — the textbook "all leaf, no bud" pattern. Impatiens are particularly sensitive to this because their natural habit is moderate growth in low-nutrient soil; the rich potting compost and weekly feeds many gardeners give them push them well beyond their preferred fertility level.

Three sources of excess nitrogen:

**1. High-nitrogen liquid feed.** General-purpose feeds with N–P–K ratios like 24-8-16 are nitrogen-heavy. For impatiens, prefer ratios closer to 5-10-10 or 10-30-20 (the "tomato feed" or "bloom-booster" range).

**2. Fresh compost or manure top-dressing in summer.** Organic matter releases nitrogen as it breaks down. A well-fed garden bed already has enough nitrogen; adding more in mid-season tips it over.

**3. Slow-release fertiliser pellets in pots.** A slow-release pellet at planting is fine; reapplying mid-season often produces the lush-leaf-no-bud pattern.

The fix is simple: stop feeding for 3 weeks. The plant uses up the excess nitrogen, returns to a balanced state, and starts forming buds again within 2–3 weeks. Once flowering resumes, restart feeding with a balanced or slightly potassium-leaning ratio at half-strength every 14 days.

## What does downy mildew look like, and is there any cure?

Downy mildew (*Plasmopara obducens*) on *I. walleriana* is the modern impatiens disease that gardeners need to recognise. Once a plant is infected, no spray cures it; the plant gradually defoliates and dies within 2–4 weeks.

Diagnostic signs:

- **White fuzzy growth** on the underside of leaves, often visible only with a magnifying glass.
- **Yellowing of leaves** that progresses from the centre outward.
- **Leaves curl downward** like a fishhook before dropping.
- **Bud drop** before flowers open.
- **Stems weaken** and may collapse near the base.

The disease spreads via airborne spores that thrive in damp humid conditions. Once present in a garden, it can persist in the soil for several years as resting spores, infecting any *I. walleriana* planted in the same bed.

If you confirm downy mildew:

1. **Remove all affected plants immediately.** Pull, bag, dispose in the bin (not compost).
2. **Wash hands and tools** before working on other plants.
3. **Do not replant *I. walleriana*** in the same bed for at least 3–4 years.
4. **Replace with resistant alternatives:**
   - New Guinea impatiens (*I. hawkeri*) — high resistance.
   - SunPatiens hybrids — high resistance plus sun tolerance.
   - Begonias — different family, no cross-infection.
   - Coleus — dramatic foliage as an alternative shade colour.
   - Torenia (wishbone flower) — small but mildew-free.

The disease has slightly subsided since the worst years (2011–2015) but remains a regular threat in humid summers. Some seed companies now sell "*Beacon*" and "Imara" series of *I. walleriana* bred specifically for downy mildew resistance, which restores the option of using traditional walleriana in problem areas.

## What about pot-bound and drought-stressed plants?

In containers and hanging baskets, impatiens often slow flowering by mid-July not because of sun, nitrogen, or disease, but because the roots have filled the pot and the plant is fighting to maintain itself.

Signs of pot-binding:

- **Pot feels suspiciously heavy** for its size.
- **Water runs straight through** without soaking in.
- **Yellow lower leaves** even with regular watering.
- **Plant wilts within hours of watering.**

The fix is to repot one size up:

1. **Slip the plant out of the pot.** If roots are tightly circling, that confirms pot-bound.
2. **Tease the outer roots loose** to break the spiral and encourage outward growth.
3. **Repot in a pot 5 cm larger in diameter**, with fresh peat-free compost.
4. **Water deeply and place in shade** for 2–3 days while the plant recovers.
5. **Resume normal care.** Buds form within 1–2 weeks.

If repotting is impractical mid-season, the alternative is to root-prune in place: lift the plant, slice off the outer 2 cm of root mass with a sharp knife, and replant in the same pot with fresh compost filling the gap. Recovery takes a week longer than full repotting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I deadhead impatiens to keep them flowering?**
Modern walleriana cultivars are self-cleaning; spent flowers drop off without help. New Guinea impatiens benefit from light deadheading. Neither is strictly necessary for continued blooming.

**Can I grow impatiens from cuttings?**
Yes, easily. 10 cm tip cuttings root in water within 7–10 days. This is a useful way to overwinter favourite cultivars indoors and propagate for next year.

**Why are flowers smaller on shaded impatiens than on sunnier ones?**
Light intensity affects flower size; deeper shade produces smaller flowers but more of them on traditional walleriana. The trade-off is normal.

**Should I pinch impatiens to make them bushier?**
Yes, when transplanting and once when the plant is 10 cm tall. Pinch the central growing tip; side branches develop within a week.

**Can impatiens bloom in winter indoors?**
Yes, with bright indirect light and 18–22 °C. Cuttings rooted in autumn flower through winter on a bright windowsill. The display is lighter than summer outdoor flowering but pleasant.
