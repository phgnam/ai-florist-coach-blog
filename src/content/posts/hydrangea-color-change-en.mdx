## Quick Answer

Mophead and lacecap hydrangeas (*Hydrangea macrophylla*) shift between blue and pink based on **soil pH and aluminium availability**. Acidic soil (pH below 6.0) makes aluminium soluble — the plant absorbs it and flowers turn blue. Alkaline soil (pH above 6.5) locks aluminium up — flowers turn pink. White cultivars and most non-macrophylla species don't change colour at all.

## What's actually happening when a hydrangea changes colour?

The colour change isn't the plant deciding to switch palettes. It's a chemistry experiment running in real time.

The pigment in hydrangea sepals — what we casually call "petals" — is **delphinidin**, an anthocyanin. Delphinidin alone is pink. But when it binds with **aluminium ions (Al³⁺)** inside the flower tissue, the molecule shifts and we perceive the colour as blue.

Whether the plant can pick up aluminium depends on **soil pH**:

- **Acidic soil (pH < 6.0)** — aluminium dissolves in the soil water, the roots take it up, the flowers go blue.
- **Neutral soil (pH 6.0–6.5)** — aluminium is partially available, flowers tend toward purple, mauve, or muddy mid-tones.
- **Alkaline soil (pH > 6.5)** — aluminium binds tightly to soil particles and is not available to the plant, flowers go pink.

The soil chemistry is doing the colour reveal. The plant is just the indicator strip.

Only some hydrangeas respond to soil chemistry. Knowing which is which prevents a lot of frustration:

**Will change colour with pH:**
- *Hydrangea macrophylla* mopheads (the classic round-headed garden hydrangea)
- *Hydrangea macrophylla* lacecaps (flat-headed cousins of mopheads)
- *Hydrangea serrata* (mountain hydrangea — similar mechanism, more cold-hardy)

**Will NOT change colour with pH:**
- *Hydrangea paniculata* ('Limelight', 'Vanille Fraise', 'PeeGee') — start cream or lime-green, age to pink or red regardless of soil pH. The colour shift is **temperature- and age-driven**, not soil-driven.
- *Hydrangea quercifolia* (oakleaf hydrangea) — white to pink to russet brown, regardless of soil
- *Hydrangea arborescens* ('Annabelle') — white to lime-green, doesn't take aluminium
- White cultivars of *macrophylla* (e.g. 'Mme Emile Mouillère') — the gene that produces delphinidin is suppressed, so there's no pigment to shift. They stay white regardless of soil.

A common confusion: *paniculata* hydrangeas like 'Limelight' do shift colour over the season — from cream to pink to dusty red. People assume this is the soil at work. It isn't. It's the plant's own ageing process and the night-time temperatures dropping. Try to make 'Limelight' blue with sulphur and you'll only stress the plant.

## How do I deliberately turn my hydrangeas blue?

To shift a hydrangea from pink toward blue, you need to **acidify the soil** AND **make aluminium available**.

The standard tools:

**Aluminium sulphate (Al₂(SO₄)₃).** Dissolve 30g (1 oz) per 4 litres of water. Drench the soil around the root zone in early spring and again in late autumn. This adds aluminium directly and acidifies the soil mildly. **Do not exceed the dose** — too much aluminium is toxic to the plant.

**Garden sulphur (elemental sulphur).** Slower acting (takes 2–4 months for soil bacteria to convert it to acid) but gentler. Use ~30g per square metre, worked into the top 10cm of soil in early spring.

**Acidic mulches.** Pine needles, pine bark, coffee grounds (in moderation), and oak leaf mould all gradually push soil pH downward. They are slow but they compound.

**Avoid wood ash, lime, and bone meal.** All raise pH and lock aluminium up. Even neighbouring plant feeds containing lime can sabotage a colour-shift project on a nearby hydrangea.

The shift is not instant. A pink hydrangea moved into truly acidic soil will produce purple flowers in year one and clear blue flowers from year two onwards. Subsequent years will deepen if the regime is maintained.

## How do I turn hydrangeas pink instead?

To shift toward pink, you do the opposite: **raise pH** so aluminium is no longer available.

The standard tools:

**Garden lime (calcium carbonate)** or **dolomitic lime**. ~150g per square metre worked into the soil in autumn or early spring. Effects appear gradually over 3–6 months.

**Wood ash** (from untreated wood) — naturally alkaline, adds potassium. Apply lightly; wood ash is potent and easy to over-do.

**Bone meal** — both alkaline and a slow-release phosphorus source.

A common pattern: gardeners with pink hydrangeas growing happily in front of an old brick or limestone wall don't realise the wall is leaching lime into the soil and keeping the colour pink. Move the same plant 6 metres away and the same cultivar will turn purple within two seasons.

## Why are my hydrangeas a muddy purple — neither blue nor pink?

A muddy mauve, dusty purple, or split-personality bush (some heads pink, some blue) almost always means:

**The soil pH is sitting around 6.0–6.5**, which is the transition zone where aluminium is partially available. The plant is producing partly-shifted pigment.

**Aluminium availability is patchy across the root zone.** Roots on one side of the plant might be in slightly more acidic soil and pulling up aluminium; roots on the other side might be in alkaline soil and not. The result is two-tone heads.

**The cultivar is genuinely mauve.** Some modern cultivars have been bred for stable purple colour and won't shift cleanly to either end. Check the cultivar name before assuming it's a soil problem.

To clean up the colour, decide which direction you want and commit to it. Half-measures produce half-colours.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long does it take to change a hydrangea's colour?**
Visible shifts in year one (especially toward blue, where aluminium sulphate works fast). Full saturated colour takes 2–3 years of consistent pH management. Reverse the regime and the plant will reverse over the same timescale.

**Can I change the colour of a single bloom on a cut stem?**
No — cut hydrangeas don't take up enough aluminium fast enough to shift colour in the vase. The colour you cut is the colour you get. Some florists tint hydrangeas with floral dye in the water, but that's a different mechanism (and rarely produces a natural-looking blue).

**Are coffee grounds enough to turn hydrangeas blue?**
By themselves, no. Coffee grounds are mildly acidic and slowly improve soil structure, but they don't add enough aluminium fast enough to drive a colour shift. Use them in combination with aluminium sulphate or acidifying mulches, not as a standalone treatment.

**Why are my white hydrangeas turning pink?**
Most likely they're a *paniculata* cultivar like 'Limelight' or 'Vanille Fraise', which naturally age from white through pink to dusty red as the season progresses. Or they're an oakleaf hydrangea (*Hydrangea quercifolia*) doing the same thing. This is normal and not a soil issue.

**Why are my newly-bought blue hydrangeas turning pink in my garden?**
Commercial growers often dose container hydrangeas with aluminium sulphate at the nursery to produce the deepest possible blue at point of sale. Once planted out into typical garden soil (often neutral or alkaline), the aluminium leaches away and the plant reverts to its pH-driven colour, which is pink in most domestic soils. Maintain the aluminium and the colour will hold.

**Is it safe to add aluminium sulphate near other plants?**
In moderation, yes. Aluminium sulphate is widely used in horticulture and water treatment. Avoid drenching root zones of plants that prefer alkaline soil (lavender, clematis, lilac) and don't dump heavy concentrations into vegetable beds. Always follow label dose rates.
