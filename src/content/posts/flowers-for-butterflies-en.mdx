## Quick Answer

Butterflies feed best on **flat or clustered flowers** with shallow nectar — buddleia, verbena bonariensis, lantana, zinnia, echinacea, sedum, and milkweed are the most reliable. Long-tubed and double-flowered cultivars often look right but exclude butterflies because the proboscis can't reach the nectar. To raise butterflies you also need **host plants** for caterpillars (parsley, fennel, milkweed, nettles).

## Why are some flowers genuinely butterfly flowers — and others just look like they should be?

A butterfly's mouthpart is a long coiled tube called a **proboscis**, designed to drink nectar from open or shallow flowers. Three properties make a flower genuinely useful to butterflies:

**A flat or clustered landing platform.** Butterflies need to perch while feeding — they can't hover for long. Composite flowers (echinacea, zinnia, daisy) and umbel-clustered flowers (buddleia, verbena bonariensis, sedum) provide a stable perch.

**Accessible nectar.** Single, open flowers expose nectar at the base of short tubes. Heavily-bred double cultivars (e.g. double impatiens, ruffled marigolds) often have the nectaries buried or replaced with extra petals — they look impressive but yield nothing.

**Bright, broad colour.** Butterflies see well into the ultraviolet, but they also detect strong reds, oranges, yellows, pinks, and purples. White flowers are visited but less reliably.

A common mistake is to buy "butterfly bush" varieties chosen entirely on the colour and form a human likes — and end up with a low-nectar cultivar. Heritage and species-form plants are almost always more useful to butterflies than modern double or compact cultivars.

## Which flowers are the most reliable butterfly plants?

The most reliable butterfly plants in a temperate-climate garden:

- **Buddleia (butterfly bush)** — the genus name says it. Plant *Buddleja davidii* or compact 'Buzz' series for small gardens.
- **Verbena bonariensis** — see-through tall stems, flat purple clusters; the universal butterfly magnet.
- **Echinacea (coneflower)** — flat-topped daisies with raised central cones. Excellent landing pad and nectar source.
- **Sedum 'Autumn Joy'** — late-season nectar source, when many other flowers are over.
- **Lantana** — heat-tolerant annual or perennial in zone 9+, long flowering season, multi-coloured clusters.
- **Zinnia** — single-flowered cultivars only (avoid the doubles like 'Benary's Giant'). Bright open daisies.
- **Aster (Symphyotrichum)** — autumn nectar source, especially important for migrating monarchs.
- **Milkweed (Asclepias)** — both nectar source and the host plant for monarch caterpillars.
- **Lavender** — moderately attractive; bees prefer it but small butterflies (skippers, blues) feed regularly.
- **Joe Pye weed (Eutrochium)** — tall, pink flat-topped flowers in late summer.
- **Phlox paniculata** — fragrant, pink-purple, attracts swallowtails especially.
- **Cosmos** — single-flowered varieties; easy from seed.

For a wildlife-garden contrast with our [hummingbird flowers article](/posts/flowers-for-hummingbirds-en/) and [flowers for bees](/posts/flowers-for-bees-en/), butterflies overlap most with bee plants but skew toward flatter, taller, daisy-form blooms.

## What flowers should I avoid if I want butterflies?

Some commonly-bought "pollinator garden" plants underperform for butterflies specifically:

- **Heavily-bred double or pompom flowers.** Double impatiens, double dahlias, double zinnias, frilly petunias — the extra petals replace nectaries.
- **Modern compact bedding plants.** Many F1 hybrids prioritise uniform form and long bloom over nectar production.
- **Most modern hybrid roses.** Roses are not significant nectar plants for butterflies.
- **Tubular hummingbird flowers.** Salvia, fuchsia, trumpet vine — accessible only to long-billed birds, not butterflies.
- **Wind-pollinated plants.** Grasses, conifers, oaks — produce no nectar at all.
- **Many double-flowered begonias and impatiens.** Bedding-plant trays at supermarkets are often selected for human visual appeal, not nectar yield.

A useful test: stand by the flower for 5–10 minutes on a warm sunny morning. If no insects visit at all, it's not a nectar plant — regardless of what the label said.

## Why do I need host plants and not just nectar plants?

Adult butterflies need nectar; **caterpillars need leaves** — and most caterpillars are wildly picky about which leaves.

- Monarch caterpillars eat **only milkweed (Asclepias)**.
- Swallowtail caterpillars eat **parsley, fennel, dill, carrot tops** (Apiaceae family) or **citrus leaves** (different swallowtail species).
- Painted lady caterpillars eat **thistles, hollyhocks, and mallows**.
- Red admiral and tortoiseshell caterpillars eat **stinging nettles**. Yes, you have to allow nettles.
- Cabbage white caterpillars eat **brassicas** (which is why most gardeners try to keep them away — but they still produce butterflies).
- Common blue caterpillars eat **bird's-foot trefoil and clover**.

A garden with only nectar plants attracts adult butterflies passing through. A garden with both nectar and host plants raises the next generation. The difference is order-of-magnitude — a host-plant garden produces 10–50× more butterflies year-on-year than a nectar-only garden in the same region.

## How should I lay out a butterfly garden?

Three layout principles:

**Plant in big drifts.** A 2–3 m² block of one species is far more attractive than the same plants spread thinly. Butterflies see large blocks of colour from much further away.

**Stagger flowering across the season.** Spring (lilac, alyssum, native violets), summer (buddleia, verbena, echinacea), autumn (aster, sedum, ivy). A continuous nectar supply means continuous visitors.

**Provide sun, shelter, and water.** Butterflies need warmth (most won't fly below 16°C), shelter from wind (they're poor fliers in gusts), and a damp patch of mud or sand for "puddling" — they extract minerals from wet soil.

A south-facing border with at least 6 hours of direct sun, anchored by buddleia, verbena bonariensis, and echinacea, with milkweed or fennel patches, will produce a noticeable butterfly garden within one season.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are butterfly bushes (Buddleia) invasive?**
In some regions, yes — especially in parts of the US Pacific Northwest, the UK, and New Zealand. Sterile cultivars (e.g. 'Buzz' series, 'Lo & Behold' series) are bred to produce no viable seed and are accepted in regions where the species is restricted. Check your local invasive-species list before planting.

**Do butterflies actually pollinate flowers, or just feed on them?**
They are minor pollinators compared to bees, but they do transfer pollen — especially for tubular and brightly coloured flowers like phlox, lantana, and zinnia. Their pollination contribution is roughly 5–10% of insect pollination in most temperate gardens.

**Will planting nettles in my garden attract butterflies?**
Yes — common nettle (*Urtica dioica*) is the host plant for at least four common butterfly species in the UK and Northern Europe (red admiral, peacock, comma, small tortoiseshell). A small designated nettle patch, kept at the back of a border, is one of the highest-impact things a small garden can do.

**Why aren't butterflies coming to my garden even with the right flowers?**
Three common reasons: pesticides (any neonicotinoid-treated plant is toxic to butterflies — buy only "butterfly safe" or organic plants), too much shade, and lack of host plants nearby. Butterflies need a 200 m–2 km radius of suitable habitat to thrive; an isolated garden in a sea of mowed lawns will see fewer visitors than the same garden in a more naturalised neighbourhood.

**Are there butterflies in winter?**
A few species overwinter as adults (peacock, comma, brimstone) and emerge on warm winter days. Most overwinter as eggs, caterpillars, or chrysalises and re-emerge in spring. A garden with leaf litter, dead-looking stems, and unmown grass corners hosts overwintering butterflies that a tidy garden does not.

**Do hummingbirds and butterflies feed on the same flowers?**
Some overlap — both visit lantana, phlox, and verbena — but most hummingbird flowers (long tubular salvia, fuchsia) exclude butterflies, and most butterfly flowers (buddleia, echinacea) are too shallow to be high-value for hummingbirds. The overlap is meaningful but smaller than people assume.
