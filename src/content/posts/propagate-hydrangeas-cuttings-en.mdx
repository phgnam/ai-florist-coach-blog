## Quick Answer

Take **10–15 cm softwood cuttings in early summer (June–July)** from non-flowering shoots. **Cut just below a leaf node**, remove the lower leaves, and reduce remaining leaves by half to limit water loss. **Dip the cut end in rooting hormone**, insert into moist seed-starting mix or perlite, and **cover with a clear plastic bag or dome** to maintain humidity. Place in **bright indirect light** at **18–24°C** with the soil kept consistently moist (not soggy). **Roots typically form in 4–6 weeks** for *Hydrangea macrophylla* and most common types. Once rooted, harden off gradually before potting up. **Best species for beginners:** *H. macrophylla* (mophead/lacecap) and *H. paniculata* — both root reliably with this technique.

## Which type of hydrangea are you propagating?

Different hydrangea species root at different rates and respond best to slightly different cutting techniques. Knowing which type you have helps set expectations.

**Common hydrangea species:**

| Species | Common name | Rooting difficulty | Best timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| ***H. macrophylla*** | Bigleaf, mophead, lacecap | Easy (95% success) | Early summer |
| ***H. paniculata*** | Panicle / "Limelight" / "PeeGee" | Easy (90% success) | Early summer |
| ***H. arborescens*** | Smooth / "Annabelle" | Moderate (75% success) | Early-mid summer |
| ***H. quercifolia*** | Oakleaf | Moderate (70% success) | Mid summer |
| ***H. petiolaris*** | Climbing | Difficult (50% success) | Late summer |
| ***H. serrata*** | Mountain | Easy (90% success) | Early summer |

**The mophead vs. lacecap distinction:**

Both *H. macrophylla* mopheads (round flower clusters) and lacecaps (flat clusters with central tiny florets surrounded by larger sterile florets) propagate identically. **Cuttings produce identical clones** of the parent — including all colour and form characteristics.

**For colour preservation:**

- **Pink-blue colour** depends on soil pH (see existing [hydrangea color change](/posts/hydrangea-color-change-en/) and [hydrangea color change soil](/posts/hydrangea-color-change-soil-en/) posts).
- **Cuttings will turn whatever colour the soil dictates** when planted — not the parent colour.
- **White and red varieties** maintain their colour regardless of soil.

**Why species matters:**

- ***H. macrophylla*** is the most common, propagates the fastest, and is most accessible.
- ***H. paniculata*** is hardier and more drought-tolerant; same technique works.
- ***H. arborescens*** "Annabelle" is the white-flowering smooth hydrangea — straightforward to propagate.
- ***H. quercifolia*** produces large oak-leaf-shaped foliage; takes a bit longer to root.
- ***H. petiolaris*** climbing hydrangea is challenging — many gardeners use layering instead of cuttings.

For broader hydrangea context, see the existing posts on [why hydrangea leaves turn brown](/posts/why-do-hydrangea-leaves-turn-brown-en/) and [hydrangeas wilting in vase](/posts/hydrangeas-wilting-in-vase-en/).

## When exactly is the best time to take cuttings?

Timing affects success rate dramatically. **Softwood cuttings in early summer** root much better than woody cuttings later in the year.

**The "softwood" stage:**

- **Early summer (June–July) in temperate climates** is the standard window.
- **Stems are still flexible** but firm enough to hold their shape.
- **The wood is "this year's growth"** — bright green, not yet hardened to brown.
- **Bend test:** stem bends without snapping = softwood. Stem snaps clean = semi-hardwood (also works but slower).

**Why softwood works best:**

- **Soft tissue contains active growth hormones** (auxins) that trigger root formation.
- **Cells are still actively dividing** — easier to convert to root tissue.
- **Higher internal moisture** keeps cuttings alive while roots form.
- **Cooler weather** of early summer reduces transpiration stress.

**Visual indicators of softwood readiness:**

- **New growth emerging** at branch tips.
- **Leaves still glossy and supple**.
- **Stem is light green** (this year's wood) below 1–2 leaf nodes.
- **No flower buds yet** on the chosen stem (or just starting).

**Timing by climate:**

- **Cool temperate (UK, US Pacific Northwest):** late May–early July.
- **Continental temperate (US Midwest, Eastern US):** late May–mid July.
- **Mediterranean (Southern Europe, California):** April–June.
- **Subtropical (Southern US, Australia):** April–May or September–October (avoiding peak heat).
- **Tropical:** any cool season, avoiding wet humid stretches.

**Avoid these times:**

- **Spring before active growth** — cuttings have no growth hormone and don't root.
- **Late autumn/winter** — plant is dormant; cuttings don't initiate roots.
- **Heat of midsummer** (above 30°C) — cuttings dehydrate before rooting.
- **During flowering** — energy is going to flowers, not new tissue.

**Window length:**

The softwood window is typically **3–4 weeks long** in any given location. **Late softwood** (just transitioning to semi-hardwood) still works but with slightly lower success.

**Multiple attempts:**

- **Take cuttings every 1–2 weeks** during the optimal window.
- **More attempts = more successful plants**.
- **Failed batches don't waste resources** — just compost them.

**Time of day:**

- **Early morning** (before 9 AM) is best — stems most turgid.
- **Cool temperatures** matter — avoid hot midday cuttings.
- **Cloudy days** are ideal for cutting and processing.

## How do you actually take and prepare the cutting?

The cutting preparation steps determine 80% of success. Sloppy preparation reduces rooting from 90%+ to 30%.

**Step 1: Select the right stem**

- **Choose a healthy non-flowering shoot** — flowering stems put energy into blooms.
- **Pencil-thin to slightly thicker** — too thin (stringy) or too thick (woody) reduces success.
- **Mature green leaves**, no yellowing or pest damage.
- **From the current year's growth** — bright green, not brown or grey.

**Step 2: Make the cut**

- **Cut 10–15 cm from the tip** of the chosen stem.
- **Cut just below a leaf node** (the swollen point where leaves attach).
- **Use sharp clean secateurs or pruning shears** — clean cuts root better than crushed.
- **Cut at a 45° angle** for maximum surface area.

**Step 3: Trim the cutting**

- **Remove all leaves below the bottom 4–6 cm** of stem.
- **Cut large remaining leaves in half** horizontally — reduces water loss without eliminating photosynthesis.
- **Keep top 2 leaves** intact (or in halves).
- **Result:** stem with 4–6 cm bare bottom, 2 partial leaves at top.

**Step 4: Wound the base (optional but helpful)**

- **Make a 1 cm vertical cut** on the lower side of the stem with a sharp blade.
- **Or scrape away a small patch** of bark on one side.
- **This wounding** stimulates more root formation along the cut surface.
- **Adds 10–15% to success rate** for difficult species.

**Step 5: Apply rooting hormone**

- **Dip the cut end** in rooting hormone powder, gel, or liquid.
- **Tap off excess** — too much can actually inhibit rooting.
- **Use IBA-based hormone** (most common) — labelled for softwood cuttings.
- **Liquid hormone** (10–15 second dip) is more concentrated; powder is gentler.

**Step 6: Insert into rooting medium**

- **Use seed-starting mix or 50/50 perlite-vermiculite** — well-draining and sterile.
- **Make a hole** with a pencil or stick (don't push the cutting in directly — strips off the hormone).
- **Insert cutting** so 4–6 cm of bare stem is buried, leaves above the surface.
- **Press soil firmly** around the stem.

**Common mistakes:**

- **Cutting too long** (over 20 cm) — inefficient use of plant energy.
- **Cutting too short** (under 8 cm) — not enough buried length for root formation.
- **Leaving too many leaves** — cutting wilts and dries.
- **Not removing leaves below soil line** — they rot and contaminate.
- **Bottom too dry** when applying hormone — hormone doesn't adhere properly.

**The "many cuttings" strategy:**

Take 5–10 cuttings at once because:

- **Some will fail** even with good technique.
- **Multiple cuttings** give multiple successful clones.
- **Failed cuttings cost nothing** to compost.
- **More cuttings = more practice** at the technique.

## What environment and care do cuttings need to root?

Once the cutting is in the rooting medium, the next 4–6 weeks determine success. The key is **maintaining humidity without rot**.

**Humidity:**

- **High humidity (80–90%)** is essential for unrooted cuttings.
- **Cover with clear plastic bag, dome, or plastic bottle** with the bottom cut off.
- **Don't seal completely** — some air circulation prevents fungal disease.
- **Mist daily** if humidity drops or leaves wilt.

**Temperature:**

- **18–24°C (65–75°F)** is ideal.
- **Bottom heat** (heated propagation mat) speeds rooting by 30–50%.
- **Avoid direct sun** — cooks the cuttings under their cover.
- **Stable temperature** is more important than ideal — avoid swings.

**Light:**

- **Bright indirect light** — north-facing window or shaded outdoor location.
- **Avoid direct sun** especially through plastic cover (overheat risk).
- **Filtered light through curtains** or fluorescent grow lights work well.
- **Light is needed for photosynthesis** — leaves still need to make food while rooting.

**Watering:**

- **Keep medium consistently moist** — not soggy.
- **Bottom-watering** by setting tray in shallow water for 30 minutes is gentlest.
- **Mist top occasionally** if mix dries out.
- **Avoid overhead splashing** — promotes fungal disease.

**Air circulation:**

- **Lift cover briefly daily** for fresh air exchange.
- **Or leave cover slightly ajar** — partial covering still maintains high humidity.
- **Stagnant air** + warmth + moisture = fungal disease (the main failure mode).

**Containers:**

- **Small individual pots** (5–7 cm) — one cutting per pot.
- **Trays with cells** also work — multiple cuttings, easy to monitor.
- **Drainage holes essential** — water must drain freely.
- **Clear plastic** lets you see roots forming through the sides eventually.

**The rooting timeline:**

| Week | What's happening | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | Cutting taken, in medium, covered | Initial setup |
| 1–2 | Cutting may wilt slightly; calluses form | Maintain humidity, mist if needed |
| 2–3 | Tiny root primordia form (invisible) | Don't disturb |
| 3–4 | Visible roots may begin to emerge through drainage holes | Reduce cover gradually |
| 4–6 | Roots fully developed; new growth at tip | Remove cover; harden off |
| 6–8 | Cutting is rooted plant | Pot up to larger container |

**Visual indicators of success:**

- **Stem stays green and firm**.
- **Slight new growth at the tip** by week 4–6.
- **Roots visible at drainage holes** (don't always check by tugging — disturbs the cutting).
- **Resistance when gently tugged** at week 4+ (light tug only, doesn't dislodge cutting).

**Visual indicators of failure:**

- **Stem turns brown or black** at the base — bacterial/fungal rot.
- **Leaves yellow and drop** without new growth — cutting failed.
- **Stem becomes soft and mushy** — irrecoverable.
- **No resistance when tugged** at week 6+ — no roots formed.

**Failure recovery:**

- **Throw out failed cuttings** — don't try to revive.
- **Sterilise the rooting medium** before reusing.
- **Take new cuttings** if the parent plant still has good softwood available.
- **Most gardeners** have 60–80% success on first try; this improves with experience.

## How and when do you transplant the rooted cutting?

Once roots have formed, the cutting becomes a young plant that needs gradual transition to normal growing conditions.

**Hardening off (week 6–8):**

1. **Remove the cover** for increasingly long periods over 1 week.
2. **Day 1–2:** uncover for 1 hour, then recover.
3. **Day 3–4:** uncover for 4 hours.
4. **Day 5–6:** uncover for 12 hours.
5. **Day 7:** remove cover entirely.

**First repotting:**

- **Wait until roots fill the propagation pot** — usually 8–10 weeks after taking cutting.
- **Move to 10–12 cm pot** with regular potting mix.
- **Be gentle with roots** — they're fragile.
- **Water in immediately** after repotting.

**First outdoor exposure:**

- **Shade outdoor location** for the first 2 weeks.
- **Gradually increase sun** over 4 weeks.
- **Indoor → covered porch → shade outside → dappled sun → full conditions**.

**Final planting:**

- **Wait until plant is 30+ cm tall** with well-developed roots — typically 6–12 months after the cutting was taken.
- **Plant in autumn** (October–November) for spring growth, or early spring (March) before active growth.
- **Same depth as in the pot** — don't bury the stem.
- **Mulch heavily** for the first winter.

**First year care:**

- **Water consistently** through the first growing season.
- **Light feeding** — half-strength balanced fertiliser monthly.
- **No flowering yet** — plant focuses on root and structure development.

**Year 2:**

- **First flowers** typically appear in year 2 or 3.
- **Light pruning** if needed (most hydrangeas don't need pruning until established).
- **Continue regular watering and feeding**.

**Year 3+:**

- **Full flowering** as a mature plant.
- **Maintenance pruning** depending on species (different rules for *H. macrophylla* vs *H. paniculata*).
- **Plants live 30–50+ years** with reasonable care.

**Container vs. ground:**

- **Container plants** can be moved as needed and protected from harsh conditions.
- **Ground-planted hydrangeas** establish faster but commit to one location.
- **Many propagators** keep cuttings in containers for the first 1–2 years before final placement.

## What about layering as an alternative?

For large hydrangeas where you have one or two specific stems available for propagation, **layering** is an alternative to cutting. It's slower but with very high success rates.

**How layering works:**

- **Bend a low branch** to the ground without breaking it.
- **Bury a portion** under soil (the buried section forms roots).
- **Leave the tip exposed** above ground.
- **Wait 6–12 months** for roots to form.
- **Cut the rooted section** from the parent and transplant.

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Choose a low flexible branch** in spring or early summer.
2. **Bend the middle of the branch** to the ground 30–60 cm from the parent.
3. **Make a small cut** on the underside of the buried section (wounding stimulates rooting).
4. **Apply rooting hormone** to the cut.
5. **Bury 5–10 cm** of the branch under soil; pin with a U-shaped wire if needed.
6. **Leave the tip 15–30 cm exposed** above ground.
7. **Water regularly** for the entire layering period.
8. **Wait 6–12 months** before checking for roots.
9. **When rooted**, cut from parent and transplant.

**Advantages of layering:**

- **Higher success rate** (95%+) than cuttings.
- **Lower maintenance** — no humidity dome, no mist required.
- **Larger initial plant** — you get a full branch's worth of growth.
- **Works for difficult species** like climbing hydrangea (*H. petiolaris*).

**Disadvantages:**

- **Slower** — 6–12 months vs. 4–6 weeks for cuttings.
- **Limited number of plants** — can only layer 1–2 branches per parent.
- **Requires accessible low branches** — not all plants have them.

**Best for:**

- **Difficult-to-root species** (climbing hydrangeas, oakleaf).
- **Gardeners who want guaranteed success** rather than maximum quantity.
- **Plants with naturally low branching habit**.

**Combining strategies:**

The most productive gardeners combine both:

- **10 cuttings in a propagation tray** for quantity.
- **2–3 layered branches** for guaranteed success.
- **Result:** many plants with varied success patterns; minimum 5–10 successful clones.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will my hydrangea cutting be the same colour as the parent?

**Pink-blue varieties:** the cutting genetics are identical, but **bloom colour depends on soil pH** at the new location. **Acidic soil (pH 5.5 or lower)** produces blue flowers; **alkaline soil (pH 6.5+)** produces pink flowers. **White and red varieties:** cuttings maintain colour because their colour isn't pH-dependent. **For colour control**, see the existing [hydrangea color change soil](/posts/hydrangea-color-change-soil-en/) post for amendments.

### Can I take hydrangea cuttings in autumn or winter?

**Generally no — softwood cuttings are far more successful.** **Late summer/early autumn semi-hardwood cuttings** can work but with lower success rates (50–70% vs 90%+ for softwood). **Winter cuttings** rarely work because the plant is dormant. **For best results**, plan around the early summer window. **If you missed the window**, try semi-hardwood in late summer or wait until next year's softwood season.

### Do I need rooting hormone for hydrangea cuttings?

**Strongly recommended for best results, but not strictly necessary.** **Without hormone**, success rate drops from 90% to about 60–70% for *H. macrophylla*. **For difficult species** (oakleaf, climbing), hormone is much more important. **Rooting hormone is inexpensive** (£5–10 per container, lasts years) and significantly improves results — well worth the small investment.

### How big should the new plant be before planting in the ground?

**At least 30–40 cm tall** with well-developed roots filling a 12 cm pot. **First-year planting** (when cutting is still small) often results in winter losses. **Best practice:** keep cuttings in containers through the first year, planting out in autumn of the cutting's first calendar year (year 2 of life). **Plants then have 6+ months** to establish before harsh weather.

### Can I propagate from store-bought cut hydrangea flowers?

**Sometimes yes — but only fresh stems with intact base nodes.** **Most cut flowers** have woody stems trimmed for vase use, lacking the soft nodes needed for rooting. **If you have access to recently-cut stems** (from a wedding, garden cutting, or florist), try the standard propagation technique. **Success rate is much lower** (20–40%) than cuttings from a living plant. **Worth attempting** for sentimental varieties (e.g., wedding flowers).

### Are hydrangeas toxic to pets?

**Yes — hydrangeas contain cyanogenic glycosides** which release cyanide when chewed. **Toxic to dogs, cats, horses, and humans.** **Symptoms** include vomiting, diarrhoea, lethargy, and rarely tremors. **Severity** is usually moderate because the plant tastes bitter and pets typically don't eat large amounts. **Plant in areas pets don't access** if your pets are prone to chewing plants. Contact your vet if a significant amount is ingested.
