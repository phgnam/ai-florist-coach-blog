Most people who love flowers have noticed that being around them simply makes them feel better. It turns out this observation is not sentimental — there is a growing body of scientific research examining exactly how and why flowers and plants affect human psychological states, and the results are more substantive than you might expect.

## The emotional response to flowers

In 2005, behavioural researcher Jeannette Haviland-Jones and her colleagues at Rutgers University published research showing that people who received flowers as gifts displayed the Duchenne smile — the genuine smile involving the muscles around the eyes — immediately and consistently. This is significant because the Duchenne smile is associated with authentic positive emotion and cannot be reliably faked. The study also found that the positive mood effects of receiving flowers persisted for days, not just in the immediate moment of receiving them.

A follow-up study by the same team found that people who had flowers in their homes reported higher levels of positive mood, fewer episodes of anxiety and depression, and greater feelings of compassion toward others. The presence of flowers in a living space was associated with increased energy and enthusiasm specifically in the morning — suggesting that having flowers in a bedroom or kitchen meaningfully affects how people begin their day.

## Hospital recovery and nature exposure

Perhaps the most compelling scientific evidence comes from healthcare research. In a widely cited 1984 study, researcher Roger Ulrich compared recovery outcomes for surgical patients in rooms with a window view of trees versus rooms with a view of a brick wall. Patients with the natural view recovered faster, required less pain medication, and were discharged an average of almost a full day earlier.

This research contributed to a major shift in hospital design philosophy and triggered decades of further investigation. More recent studies have examined the presence of plants and flowers specifically within hospital wards. A 2009 Korean study found that patients in rooms with plants reported significantly lower pain levels, anxiety, and fatigue, and had lower systolic blood pressure readings than patients in rooms without plants. A 2016 Dutch study of intensive care patients found that natural views and indoor plants were associated with improved patient outcomes and reduced length of stay.

## The stress reduction mechanism

The mechanism behind these effects appears to involve multiple pathways. Exposure to natural elements — including flowers and plants — activates the parasympathetic nervous system (the "rest and digest" system), reducing cortisol levels and heart rate. This is the same system that counteracts the stress response, and flowers appear to trigger it relatively consistently.

There is also evidence that certain flower fragrances have specific neurological effects. Lavender has been studied most extensively: multiple trials have found that lavender scent reduces state anxiety (situational anxiety) and improves sleep quality, with effects that appear to be mediated through the limbic system via olfactory pathways. Jasmine has been found in some studies to have sedative properties comparable to mild tranquilisers. Rose fragrance has been associated with improved mood and reduced anxiety in several trials.

## Flower-arranging as therapeutic practice

In Japan, ikebana — the ancient practice of flower arrangement — has been formally integrated into therapeutic programmes for depression, dementia, and post-traumatic stress. The mindful focus required to select, prepare, and arrange flowers appears to function similarly to other mindfulness practices in calming the default mode network (the "overthinking" brain) and promoting present-moment awareness.

In the United Kingdom and United States, horticultural therapy — using gardening, plant care, and flower-related activities as structured therapeutic interventions — is increasingly used in psychiatric facilities, prisons, and palliative care settings. The evidence base is growing: a 2017 systematic review in the Journal of Clinical Medicine found that horticultural therapy produced significant improvements in depression, anxiety, quality of life measures, and social functioning across diverse clinical populations.

## Colour psychology and flowers

The colour of flowers matters beyond aesthetic preference. Research in colour psychology has consistently found that:

**Warm colours** (red, orange, yellow) are stimulating and energising, activating the sympathetic nervous system and increasing alertness. Warm-coloured flowers — sunflowers, marigolds, orange lilies — are genuinely energising to be around.

**Cool colours** (blue, purple, white) are calming and associated with reduced heart rate and blood pressure. Lavender, blue irises, and white flowers create environments that promote relaxation.

**Green** — which surrounds flowers in their natural habitat and in arrangements — is associated with recovery, balance, and the specific kind of restored attention described by Attention Restoration Theory (ART), which holds that natural environments restore directed attention capacity depleted by mental fatigue.

## Gardening and mental health

Growing flowers — rather than simply receiving or displaying them — appears to have even stronger mental health effects. A landmark 2007 study by Chris Lowry and colleagues at the University of Bristol found that *Mycobacterium vaccae*, a bacterium present in soil, stimulated serotonin production in mice when inhaled. The researchers hypothesised that digging and working with soil may function as a form of natural antidepressant by stimulating the same serotonergic pathways.

Multiple subsequent studies of gardening as an intervention for depression have produced positive results. A 2014 meta-analysis found that gardening interventions produced significant reductions in depression, anxiety, and body mass index, with improvements in life satisfaction and quality of life.

## What this means practically

None of this is to say that flowers cure serious mental illness. They don't, and presenting them that way would be misleading. What the evidence does support is that:

- Receiving flowers produces genuine, not merely performative, positive emotion
- Having flowers and plants in your living and working environment is associated with lower day-to-day stress and anxiety
- Flower-related activities (arranging, gardening) have documented therapeutic benefits when used as structured interventions
- Specific fragrances (lavender, jasmine) have measurable neurological effects on anxiety

The next time someone dismisses buying flowers as a frivolous luxury, there is now more than sentiment to push back with. There is science.
