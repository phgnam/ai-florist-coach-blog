## Quick Answer

Heuchera leaves usually curl and crisp at the edges for one of three reasons: vine weevil grubs eating the roots, frost heave lifting the crown out of the soil, or sun scorch on a position that has become too hot. Check the roots first by gently lifting the plant — if the rootball crumbles or grubs are visible, the cause is vine weevil. If the crown is sitting proud of the soil, replant deeper. If neither, move to a cooler aspect.

## What does a healthy heuchera leaf look like?

*Heuchera* (coral bells) is grown almost entirely for foliage. The flowers are slim wand-like sprays of small bells, charming but secondary. The leaves are the show — often lobed or scalloped, in colours ranging from lime-green and silver through caramel, peach, plum, and almost-black.

A healthy leaf should be:

- Firm and flat from petiole to tip.
- Showing the cultivar's true colour evenly across the blade.
- Free of brown tips, curled edges, or papery patches.
- Held by a flexible petiole that springs back when pushed sideways.

When leaves curl, crisp, brown at the edges, or fade dull, something has gone wrong below the soil or above. The first job is to identify which.

## Could it be vine weevil — and how do I check?

Vine weevil (*Otiorhynchus sulcatus*) is the single biggest cause of mysterious heuchera collapse, especially in pots. The adult weevils chew "C-shaped" notches in the leaf edges at night; the legless white grubs, which hatch in the soil, eat the roots and crown.

The classic sequence:

1. **Late summer, autumn:** Notches in leaf edges, often on multiple plants in the area.
2. **Late autumn to spring:** Leaves curl and crisp as roots fail.
3. **Spring:** Whole rosette pulls cleanly out of the soil with a gentle tug.
4. **In the rootball:** White grubs (5–10 mm long) curled in C-shapes, with a tan-orange head capsule.

**The check:**

1. Hold the crown of a poorly-looking heuchera firmly between two fingers.
2. Gently rock the rosette side to side.
3. If the entire plant lifts cleanly out of the soil with little or no resistance, vine weevil is almost certain.
4. Lift fully and tip the soil into a pale-coloured tray.
5. Look for white grubs against the dark soil.

If you find grubs, the plant is unlikely to recover from advanced damage. Treat the soil and replant fresh stock — see "How do I treat vine weevil?" below.

## Could the problem be frost heave instead?

In cold winters, repeated freeze-thaw cycles lift heuchera crowns clean out of the soil. The exposed crown then dries in winter wind and emerges in spring with severely curled, crispy leaves and a wobbly base.

Signs of frost heave:

- Crown sits 2–5 cm above the surrounding soil surface.
- Roots visibly exposed and partially dried.
- Plant is loose and rocks easily — but feels solid below soil level (no grubs).
- Leaves curl and crisp from the rim inward, often asymmetrically (the side most exposed to wind suffers worst).

**The fix:**

1. **In early spring**, when the soil has thawed but before significant new growth.
2. Lift the plant cleanly — heuchera generally has a fibrous, easily-handled rootball.
3. **Replant deeper.** The crown should be flush with the soil or slightly below, never proud of it.
4. **Mulch around the crown** with 3–5 cm of compost or composted bark to insulate against future heave.
5. **Water in well** to settle the soil and remove air pockets.

This is the easiest of the three causes to fix and the one most likely to be overlooked because the symptoms can look like a more dramatic problem.

## What about sun scorch and dry soil?

Heuchera is widely sold as a "shade plant", but the truth is more nuanced. Most modern cultivars — particularly the highly-coloured ones with caramel, lime, peach, or red-purple foliage — actually need 3–5 hours of soft morning sun to develop full colour. Pure deep shade dulls them to flat green.

The problem is the opposite extreme. A plant that gets harsh midday or afternoon sun on dry soil scorches.

Signs of sun scorch:

- Crispy brown patches on the upper leaf surface, especially in the centre of the leaf rather than the edges.
- Faded, "bleached" colour.
- Damage worst on south- or west-facing leaves.
- Soil dries to dust within hours of watering.

**The fix:**

1. Move the plant to a position with morning sun and afternoon dappled shade.
2. If moving is impossible, mulch generously and water deeply (not lightly) twice a week in dry weather.
3. Replace the soil immediately around the crown with compost-rich soil that holds moisture.
4. Trim away the worst-burned leaves — fresh leaves emerge from the centre within weeks.

## How do I treat vine weevil if I find it?

Treatment depends on whether the plant is in the ground or in a pot.

**In pots:**

1. Lift the plant entirely and discard the old compost (do not compost it — vine weevil grubs survive most home compost heaps).
2. Wash the rootball under a strong jet of water to remove any remaining grubs.
3. Trim damaged roots cleanly.
4. Replant in fresh, sterile compost.
5. Water in with **biological control** nematodes (*Heterorhabditis bacteriophora* or *Steinernema kraussei*) at the soil temperatures specified on the product (usually above 5 °C in spring or autumn).

**In the ground:**

1. Drench the planting area with the same nematodes when soil temperatures suit.
2. Repeat in autumn and spring.
3. Replant fresh stock once the area has been treated for at least one nematode cycle.

Avoid synthetic chemical insecticides for vine weevil unless absolutely necessary — biological controls work very well in this case and have no impact on bees or other beneficial insects.

For prevention going forward:

- Use 5 cm of grit on top of pots — adult weevils struggle to find soil to lay eggs.
- Monitor neighbouring heuchera and similar root targets (*Tiarella*, *Saxifraga*, primulas, sedums) early in the season.
- Reinspect rootballs at every potting-on or transplant.

## Are there resistant cultivars or related plants to switch to?

If your garden has a chronic vine weevil problem and heuchera keep failing, consider closely related but more resistant alternatives.

**Heucherella (× *Heucherella*).** Hybrid between *Heuchera* and *Tiarella*. Often more vigorous and slightly more weevil-resistant in practice. Look for cultivars 'Sweet Tea', 'Stoplight', 'Tapestry'.

**Tiarella (foam flower).** True species *Tiarella cordifolia* and named cultivars are reported to be less attractive to vine weevil grubs than heuchera. Pretty starry white flowers as a bonus.

**Tellima grandiflora (fringe cups).** Less coloured foliage but tougher and rarely seriously damaged by weevil.

**Brunnera macrophylla.** For "silver-and-blue-flower" effect in shade — different look, but similar position and unaffected by heuchera-specific pests.

## How do I revive a heuchera that has lost most of its leaves?

If you catch the problem early — before the crown has died — heuchera is remarkably resilient.

1. **Identify and remove the cause** (weevil, sun scorch, or frost heave).
2. **Trim away every damaged leaf**, leaving only firm, healthy ones in the centre.
3. **Replant deeper** if the crown is exposed.
4. **Water in with a half-strength balanced liquid feed** to encourage root recovery.
5. **Top-dress with 2–3 cm of compost.**
6. **Monitor for 6–8 weeks.** New leaves should emerge from the centre crown within 4 weeks.

If after 8 weeks no new leaves have appeared and the crown feels mushy, the plant is dead. Replace with fresh stock in a different spot.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I cut back the leaves of my heuchera in winter?**
Lightly — remove dead or browned leaves, but leave the centre rosette intact. The remaining live leaves protect the crown through winter. Remove fully damaged or rotting tissue only.

**Why is my heuchera flopping outwards from the centre?**
Heuchera develops a "leggy" central crown that lifts out of the soil over 3–4 years. Lift, divide, and replant the outer rosettes, discarding the woody centre. Spring is the best time.

**Do heuchera flowers have any value, or should I deadhead them?**
The flowers are subtle but loved by hummingbirds (in their American native range) and bumblebees. Leave the flower spikes for pollinators if you can; trim spent stems back at the base once seed heads form.

**Can I grow heuchera in pots, and how do I avoid vine weevil there?**
Yes — pots work well as long as you (1) use sterile potting compost, (2) top-dress with 5 cm of grit, (3) water with nematodes in spring and autumn, and (4) inspect roots when potting on. The grit barrier alone is the single most effective preventative.

**Are heuchera safe around pets?**
Heuchera is generally regarded as low-toxicity and is not on the most common pet-poisoning lists, but no plant is risk-free in quantity. Keep arrangements out of reach of pets that chew. For documented pet-safe options, see [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).
