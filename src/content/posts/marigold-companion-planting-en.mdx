## Quick Answer

Marigolds repel pests through chemical compounds called thiophenes produced in their roots, leaves, and flowers. French marigolds are the strongest nematode suppressors, while African marigolds deter whiteflies and aphids above ground. Plant them as borders around tomatoes, peppers, beans, and brassicas for effective organic pest control without sprays.

## How do marigolds actually repel pests?

Marigolds are not simply unpleasant to insects. They wage chemical warfare through several distinct mechanisms, each targeting a different group of pests.

**Root exudates kill nematodes.** The most powerful defence happens underground. Marigold roots release thiophenes — sulphur-containing compounds toxic to root-knot nematodes, lesion nematodes, and other microscopic worms that devastate tomatoes, carrots, and potatoes. French marigolds (*Tagetes patula*) are the most studied for this effect. Planting them as a cover crop for a full season can suppress nematode populations for two or three years.

**Leaf and flower scent masks host plants.** The pungent terpenoids in marigold foliage can confuse flying pests that navigate by smell. Whiteflies seeking tomato foliage and carrot flies following carrot scent are both disrupted when a strong-smelling marigold row sits nearby.

**Flower colour attracts beneficial insects.** The bright orange and yellow petals draw hoverflies, ladybirds, and parasitic wasps — predators that eat aphids, thrips, and caterpillar eggs. A marigold border is not only a repellent; it is a landing pad for pest-eating allies.

**Trap cropping.** Some pests prefer marigolds over the vegetable crop. Slugs, for example, may feed on marigold leaves first, giving you a chance to collect them before they reach lettuce.

## Which marigold varieties are best for companion planting?

Not all marigolds are equally useful. The genus *Tagetes* has three common garden species, each with different strengths:

**French marigolds (*Tagetes patula*)** are the top choice for nematode suppression. They are compact (15–30 cm), bloom heavily, and produce the highest concentration of thiophenes in their roots. Varieties like 'Sparky Mix', 'Durango', and 'Bonanza' are widely available and easy to grow from seed.

**African marigolds (*Tagetes erecta*)** grow taller (60–90 cm) and produce larger blooms. Their above-ground scent is stronger, making them better for deterring whiteflies, aphids, and other flying insects. Use them as tall border plants behind shorter vegetables. 'Crackerjack', 'Inca', and 'Antigua' are reliable garden varieties.

**Signet marigolds (*Tagetes tenuifolia*)** are smaller, lacier, and have edible flowers with a citrusy flavour. Their pest-repelling chemistry is weaker, but they attract pollinators strongly and work well as edible garnish borders. 'Tangerine Gem' and 'Lemon Gem' are popular picks.

**Mexican marigold (*Tagetes minuta*)** is the powerhouse for large-scale nematode suppression. It grows over a metre tall and produces extremely high levels of thiophenes, but it is vigorous enough to become weedy. Use it as a green manure crop rather than an ornamental companion.

## What vegetables benefit most from marigold companions?

Research and centuries of kitchen-garden tradition point to several strong pairings:

**Tomatoes.** The single most studied pairing. Marigolds suppress root-knot nematodes that attack tomato roots and repel whiteflies that spread tomato diseases. Plant French marigolds every 50–60 cm along the tomato row.

**Peppers and aubergines.** Same Solanaceae family, same nematode vulnerability, same benefit from marigold root chemistry.

**Beans and peas.** Marigolds deter Mexican bean beetles and attract hoverflies that eat bean aphids. Do not plant too densely — beans need air circulation.

**Brassicas (cabbage, broccoli, kale).** Marigolds can confuse cabbage white butterflies and attract parasitic wasps that prey on cabbage moth caterpillars.

**Carrots.** The scent of marigold foliage masks carrot scent from carrot root fly. Interplant or use a border row.

**Cucumbers and squash.** Marigolds attract pollinators that cucurbits need for fruit set, while discouraging cucumber beetles.

## Do marigolds have any downsides as companion plants?

Yes, a few practical points matter:

**They compete for water and nutrients.** In dry soil or tight spacing, marigolds can reduce yields of neighbouring vegetables rather than help them. Leave at least 20–30 cm between a marigold and its companion crop.

**They attract slugs.** While this can work as trap cropping, it can also mean more slugs near your lettuce if you do not manage them. Check marigold plants at dusk and remove slugs by hand.

**Some varieties self-seed aggressively.** African marigolds in warm climates can volunteer freely the following year, appearing where you do not want them.

**Spider mites.** In hot, dry conditions, marigolds themselves can attract spider mites, which may then spread to nearby crops. Keep plants well-watered and monitor the undersides of leaves.

**Short-lived.** Annual marigolds die at first frost. The nematode-suppressing effect from root exudates is strongest when plants grow for a full season, so planting marigolds mid-summer gives less soil benefit than planting them at the same time as your vegetables.

## How should I plant marigolds for maximum pest protection?

The placement and timing matter as much as the variety:

1. **Start early.** Direct-sow marigold seeds after the last frost or transplant seedlings at the same time you plant your main crop. The earlier the roots are active, the sooner thiophenes build up in the soil.
2. **Use borders and interplanting.** A single row of French marigolds bordering a tomato bed is the classic approach. For stronger effect, alternate marigold and vegetable plants within the bed.
3. **Do not remove spent marigold plants at season end.** Chop them and dig the foliage and roots into the soil as green manure. The decomposing tissue continues releasing thiophenes.
4. **Rotate.** Plant marigolds in a different bed each year to spread nematode suppression across the garden over time.
5. **Full sun.** Marigolds bloom and produce the most chemistry in at least 6 hours of direct sun. Shaded marigolds grow leggy and produce fewer defensive compounds.
6. **Deadhead regularly.** Removing spent blooms keeps the plant flowering and producing scent through the season, extending both pest confusion and pollinator attraction.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do marigolds really kill nematodes in the soil?**
Yes. French marigolds produce thiophenes that are toxic to several species of root-knot and lesion nematodes. Research from multiple agricultural universities confirms that growing French marigolds as a cover crop for a full season can reduce nematode populations significantly.

**Can I use ornamental marigolds from a garden centre for companion planting?**
Yes, as long as they are *Tagetes patula* (French) or *Tagetes erecta* (African). Avoid *Calendula officinalis* (pot marigold), which is a different genus entirely and does not produce the same nematode-suppressing chemicals.

**How close should marigolds be to vegetables?**
Plant them 20–30 cm from vegetable stems. Close enough for root zones to overlap and scent to mask the crop, but far enough to avoid water and nutrient competition.

**Do dried marigold petals repel pests?**
No. The pest-suppressing chemistry works through living root exudates and volatile leaf compounds. Dried petals are decorative but have no meaningful pest-control effect.

**Will marigolds attract bees to my vegetable garden?**
Yes. Marigolds attract honeybees, bumblebees, hoverflies, and other pollinators. This is especially beneficial for crops like tomatoes, peppers, squash, and beans that need pollination for fruit set.
