## Quick Answer

A "blind" bulb produces leaves but no flower. The four most common causes are: **bulbs too small to flower** (especially in shop-bought tulip dumps and self-divided clumps), **last year's leaves cut back too early** before the bulb could refuel, **overcrowding** so each bulb starves the next, and **insufficient feeding** during the leafy phase. The fix takes a full season: feed the existing leaves with high-potash fertiliser this year, lift and divide overcrowded clumps in autumn, and discard any bulbs smaller than walnut-sized.

## What does "blind" actually mean?

In gardening, a "blind" bulb is one that produces a normal-looking sheaf of leaves in spring but no flower stem at all — or a flower stem that aborts before the bud forms.

The leaves are usually green and full-sized, sometimes a bit floppy. There is no telltale brown or scorched spot where a bud failed; the plant simply skips the flowering step entirely.

Almost every gardener encounters this with naturalised daffodils and old tulip clumps, and almost everyone wonders if the bulbs have died. They haven't — they are alive but lack either the size or the energy reserves to flower.

## Are your bulbs too small to flower?

Bulbs need to reach a critical size before they can flower. Below that size, the plant produces leaves to grow itself bigger; above it, the plant has the resources to produce a flower as well.

Rough size thresholds:

- **Tulip**: roughly 10–12 cm circumference (about a walnut)
- **Daffodil**: roughly 12–14 cm circumference (a small chestnut)
- **Crocus**: 5–7 cm circumference
- **Hyacinth**: 14–16 cm circumference

Bulbs that come up blind are commonly:

- **Daughter bulbs** that split off from a flowering parent and haven't grown big enough yet (gives 1–2 blind seasons before they reach size)
- **Cheap tulips from a "post-display dump"** sold off after a public garden replanting — these are often spent bulbs that won't reach flowering size again for 2 years
- **Naturalised daffodils** in long-grass areas where mowing has cut back leaves before they could refuel

The fix is patience: feed the leaves well, leave them undisturbed for 6–8 weeks after flowering season, and most blind bulbs flower within 1–2 seasons.

## Did you cut last year's leaves back too early?

This is the second most common cause and the easiest to fix going forward.

After a tulip or daffodil flowers, the leaves spend the next 6–8 weeks photosynthesising and pumping sugars into the bulb to fuel **next year's flower**. The flower bud for next year is set during this leafy refuelling phase.

If you cut, mow, knot, or fold the leaves before they yellow naturally:

- The bulb doesn't get enough sugar
- It survives but cannot afford a flower
- Next year's display is blind

The rule: **leave bulb foliage alone until it yellows naturally**, usually 6–8 weeks after the petals drop. Then you can cut it back to ground level.

The "knot the daffodil leaves into a tidy bow" tradition is a classic way to ruin next year's display. So is mowing a daffodil-naturalised lawn before the foliage yellows.

## Are your bulbs overcrowded?

A clump of daffodils or tulips that started as 12 bulbs five years ago can become 100 bulbs without ever being divided. Each bulb produces 1–3 daughter bulbs per year, and over time the colony fills the soil so densely that no individual bulb gets enough water and nutrients to flower.

Symptoms of overcrowded bulbs:

- A clump that flowered well 3–5 years ago and is now mostly leaves
- Foliage looks crowded and slightly stunted
- A single bulb dug up reveals it is wedged between dozens of others
- Daffodils especially go through a "leafy clump" phase as a colony matures

The fix is **lift and divide**. The right time is when foliage starts to yellow (typically 6–8 weeks after flowering — May or June for daffodils, late May or early June for tulips). Lift the whole clump, separate the bulbs, replant the largest with 10–15 cm spacing, and either compost or move the smallest to a "growing on" bed.

## Are you feeding at the wrong time of year?

Bulbs need their feeding **during the leafy phase**, not when you plant them in autumn.

- **Autumn planting fertiliser** at the time of planting: helps new root growth but doesn't fuel next year's flower bud
- **Spring fertiliser as foliage emerges** (February–April): the right time
- **Post-flowering foliar feed** while leaves are green (April–June): even better, especially for naturalised drifts

Use a high-potash (high-K) fertiliser like tomato food, rose food, or specialist bulb food. Liquid feed every 2 weeks while foliage is green is more effective than a single dry application.

Avoid high-nitrogen fertilisers (most lawn fertilisers, balanced "all-purpose" feeds): they push leaf growth at the expense of flowers — the same outcome as not feeding at all.

## Are tulips just genetically short-lived?

This one is partly outside your control. Most modern hybrid tulips are bred for **single-season impact in a public-garden display**, not multi-year garden performance. They flower spectacularly in year 1, modestly in year 2, and often blind out by year 3.

If you want tulips that come back reliably, look for:

- **Species tulips** (Tulipa clusiana, T. tarda, T. saxatilis) — small, perennialise reliably
- **Darwin Hybrid tulips** — large, the most reliable of the showy types
- **Fosteriana ("Emperor") tulips** — early, good perennialisers
- **Triumph and Lily-flowered tulips** — moderate reliability

Avoid for perennialising:

- **Parrot tulips** — very short-lived
- **Double late tulips** — heavy heads, often blind in year 2
- **Most novelty cultivars** — bred for spectacle, not longevity

Daffodils, by contrast, are long-lived almost by default. A blind daffodil is almost always a feeding or overcrowding problem, not a cultivar problem.

## Was the autumn planting depth wrong?

Tulip and daffodil bulbs planted too shallow can be destabilised by frost heaving and freeze-thaw, which damages the developing flower bud inside. Planted too deep, the leaves emerge but the flower stem can't reach the surface and aborts.

The rule is **3 times the bulb's height**:

- A 5 cm tall tulip bulb: 15 cm deep
- A 4 cm daffodil bulb: 12 cm deep
- A 2 cm crocus: 6 cm deep

In sandy soil, slightly deeper. In heavy clay, slightly shallower.

## FAQs

### Can I cut blind tulip leaves back early to tidy the bed?

No. The leaves are doing the work to fuel next year's flower. Cutting them off now guarantees another blind year. Tolerate the messy phase for 6 weeks, then cut back when leaves yellow.

### Will blind bulbs flower again next year if I just leave them?

Sometimes, but not reliably. Leaving them alone with no other intervention gives maybe a 30–50 percent chance of flowering next year. Adding feed, lifting and dividing if crowded, and replanting the largest bulbs raises that to 80+ percent.

### How long should I leave naturalised daffodils before mowing?

Six to eight weeks after the petals drop, or until the foliage yellows naturally. In long-grass meadows, this means delaying the first mow until late June. The grass looks rough during that period; that is the trade-off.

### Can I rescue a tulip dump (mass-discard from a public garden)?

Marginally. Sort by size and replant only walnut-sized or larger bulbs. Expect 30–50 percent to flower in the first year and most of the rest in year 2, after a year of leaf-only growth and feeding.

### Why are daffodils more reliable than tulips year after year?

Daffodils are native or near-native to many temperate regions and have not been bred for short-burst display. Most tulip cultivars are descended from species adapted to dry continental summers and are inherently mismatched to wet temperate-maritime climates like the UK.

### Should I lift tulips every year and replant?

Some experts do — lifting tulips after flowering, drying them, and replanting in autumn. It improves longevity but is fiddly. A reasonable compromise: lift and divide every 3–4 years, or whenever a clump becomes leafy and blind.
