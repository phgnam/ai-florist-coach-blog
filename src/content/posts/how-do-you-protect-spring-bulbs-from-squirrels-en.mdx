## Quick Answer

Squirrels dig up tulips and crocus bulbs in autumn for food and bury them elsewhere. Protect by **planting 15 cm deep** (5 cm deeper than recommended), **covering the soil with chicken wire** pinned in place, **mulching with holly clippings or sharp gravel**, and **choosing squirrel-resistant bulbs** like daffodils, alliums, snowdrops, and fritillaries which they find bitter or toxic. A combination of two methods is more reliable than any single one.

## Why do squirrels dig up bulbs?

Squirrels treat freshly planted bulbs like a buffet for two reasons:

- **Food caching season** — autumn is when grey squirrels stockpile food for winter, and freshly turned soil is a clear signal that something edible was just buried
- **Energy-dense forage** — bulbs are calorie-rich underground storage organs, exactly what a squirrel wants to bury for later
- **Smell trail** — disturbed soil and the bulb's outer papery skin both leave scent that squirrels find at distances of 30+ metres

The damage is worst in **the first 2 weeks after planting**, before soil settles, smells fade, and roots anchor the bulbs. Once spring growth starts, squirrels lose interest.

## Which bulbs do squirrels target most?

Not all bulbs are equally vulnerable:

**High-risk** (squirrels prize these):

- **Tulips** — sweet starchy bulbs, top of the menu
- **Crocus** — small enough to dig out and eat whole
- **Lily bulbs** — large and palatable

**Low-risk** (rarely touched):

- **Daffodils and narcissus** — toxic alkaloids, bitter taste
- **Alliums** — onion family, deters most rodents
- **Snowdrops** — toxic
- **Hyacinths** — bitter and toxic when fresh
- **Fritillaries** — chemical defence makes them unpalatable
- **Muscari (grape hyacinth)** — generally ignored

If your garden has heavy squirrel pressure, lean toward the low-risk list and treat the high-risk list as a special-case planting that needs protection.

## How does chicken wire work?

Chicken wire over the soil is the single most effective deterrent. The principle: squirrels dig with their front paws, and wire blocks the dig entirely.

The method:

- **Plant bulbs at full depth** with normal spacing
- **Cut a sheet of chicken wire** to the size of the planted area
- **Lay flat over the soil**, pin in 4–6 places with U-shaped pegs or wire staples
- **Cover wire lightly with 1–2 cm of compost** to hide it visually
- **Remove the wire in spring** as shoots begin to push through (or bulb shoots will grow through the mesh — both work; pulled wire makes mowing easier)

This prevents virtually all digging. The downside is the labour for a large area; for small beds and pots it's quick.

## What about deep planting?

Planting bulbs 5 cm deeper than recommended makes a real but partial difference.

- **Tulips**: standard 10 cm → deep 15 cm
- **Crocus**: standard 8 cm → deep 12 cm
- **Lilies**: 15 cm → 20 cm

At 15 cm depth, squirrels are less likely to attempt digging because the calorie return for the effort is poor. They prefer easier shallow targets.

But deep planting has a side benefit: the bulbs are also more cold-protected, last longer in the ground, and are less prone to splitting into many small bulblets that flower poorly. Combine with chicken wire for the best result.

## Do scent deterrents actually work?

Mixed evidence. Some scent-based methods help, others are placebo. The ones with at least anecdotal traction:

- **Chilli or cayenne flakes** scattered on top of the soil — irritates a digging squirrel's nose, deters re-digging
- **Crushed holly leaves** mixed into the topsoil — sharp prickles physically deter paws
- **Mothballs (naphthalene)** — actually toxic, do not use; bird and pet risk
- **Predator urine (fox, coyote)** — short-lived, expensive, smells unpleasant
- **Garlic spray** — soil drench, repeats needed weekly

The chilli-and-holly combination on top of chicken wire makes a strongly deterrent surface. Reapply chilli after heavy rain.

## Are some bulbs naturally squirrel-proof?

Yes. The whole **Narcissus** family contains lycorine, an alkaloid that is bitter and mildly toxic to mammals. Squirrels learn quickly to avoid daffodils after a single bad-tasting attempt.

Other squirrel-proof choices worth knowing:

- **Allium 'Purple Sensation'** — onion-family deters digging
- **Camassia** — large bulbs but unappealing taste
- **Fritillaria meleagris (snake's head)** — chemical defence
- **Iris reticulata** — generally ignored
- **Eranthis (winter aconite)** — bitter, toxic
- **Galanthus (snowdrop)** — toxic

A bulb display made entirely of these requires no wire or chilli — and many of them are also some of the loveliest spring flowers anyway.

## What about planting in pots?

Container bulbs are vulnerable but easier to protect:

- **Cover the pot with chicken wire** secured around the rim
- **Place pots in a covered porch, shed, or wire cage** through autumn
- **Move out to the display position in early February** when shoots emerge
- **Tuck holly sprigs** into the pot soil as a physical barrier

Squirrels don't usually dig out emerged shoots — by February the protection can come off.

## What if a squirrel has already dug up bulbs?

If you find scattered displaced bulbs:

- **Replant immediately** at the original depth
- **Cover with chicken wire** to prevent return visits
- **Sprinkle chilli or cayenne** over the area
- **Check nearby beds** for buried "treasure" — squirrels often cache stolen bulbs in unexpected spots
- **Look for shoots in spring** — even displaced and reburied bulbs often grow if they ended up reasonably deep

A single attack rarely kills the bulbs entirely; multiple repeat raids do most of the long-term damage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will a cat keep squirrels away?

A patrolling cat helps moderately but not reliably. Squirrels are agile climbers and can usually escape. Don't rely on a cat alone — use chicken wire as the primary defence.

### Does pet hair scattered on soil work?

Mildly. Some gardeners report success with dog or cat fur sprinkled around plantings, presumably because predator scent deters squirrels. Effect lasts only until the next rain.

### Do squirrels eat tulip foliage and flowers in spring?

Some do — particularly if dry conditions limit other food. The chicken wire trick doesn't help once flowers emerge; for spring foliage protection, a bone meal-and-chilli mulch around stems sometimes works.

### What about mice and voles for bulbs?

Mice and voles attack from below, eating bulbs in place. Chicken wire on top doesn't help. For these, plant bulbs in a wire cage (mesh basket lined with chicken wire) or use repellent grains in the planting hole. See the [protect bulbs from mice in pots guide](/posts/protect-bulbs-mice-pots-en/) if separate.

### Is there a no-effort solution for a heavy squirrel garden?

Yes — switch to entirely squirrel-proof bulbs (daffodils, alliums, snowdrops, fritillaries). The display is excellent and you skip all protection work. Pair with the [autumn-planted flowers for early spring colour guide](/posts/which-autumn-planted-flowers-give-earliest-spring-colour-en/) for a low-conflict planting plan.

### Will squirrels return to the same bed every year?

Yes if successful. Once a squirrel has had a successful raid on your tulip bed, expect repeated visits each autumn. Persistent protection for 2–3 consecutive years usually breaks the habit.
