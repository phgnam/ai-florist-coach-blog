## Quick Answer

Begonia leaves usually curl and turn brown for one of four reasons: **humidity below 40%**, **inconsistent watering** (alternating bone-dry and soaked), **direct sun scorching the leaves**, or **damaged roots from cold or rot**. The pattern of damage tells you which: **crispy brown edges with curl** = humidity, **pale brown centres** = sun scorch, **soft brown bases with curling** = rot, **uniform dull brown then leaf drop** = cold or root damage. Move to bright indirect light, raise humidity to 50–60%, and water only when the top 2 cm of compost feels dry.

## What kind of begonia do you actually have?

Diagnosis depends on the type. Begonias are an enormous family with three quite different groups, and they don't all want the same care.

**Cane / wing begonias** (*Begonia coccinea*, *B. corallina*, "angel-wing" types). Bamboo-like upright stems, asymmetric polka-dotted leaves, drooping flower clusters. The most forgiving group; tolerates short dry spells and average household humidity.

**Rex / rhizomatous begonias** (*Begonia rex* hybrids). Grown for spectacular metallic, silver, or maroon foliage. **The most humidity-sensitive group**, and the one most likely to crisp at the edges in dry indoor air.

**Tuberous begonias** (*Begonia × tuberhybrida*, the big "rose-form" garden types). Outdoor or conservatory plants from underground tubers; go fully dormant in winter. Brown leaves in autumn are usually normal seasonal die-back, not a problem.

**Wax / semperflorens begonias** (the small bedding types in summer borders). Annual or short-lived perennials; outdoor heat and rain are the most common cause of leaf damage.

If you don't know which type you have, look at the leaves: heavily patterned colourful foliage = Rex, big symmetrical roses-like flowers = tuberous, asymmetric green or polka-dotted leaves on tall stems = cane.

The remainder of this article focuses on **Rex and cane begonias** (the most common indoor types), with notes on tuberous and wax types where relevant. For broader indoor begonia care covering setup and placement, see [growing begonias indoors](/posts/begonia-indoors-en/).

## What does the pattern of browning tell you?

Where the brown starts and how it spreads is the most useful diagnostic clue. Look at one or two damaged leaves before assuming a single cause.

**Crispy brown edges, slight inward curl, leaf otherwise green:** **dry air**. Almost always low humidity (below 40%), often combined with proximity to a radiator or heating vent. Common in winter when central heating runs hard.

**Pale tan or bleached patches in the leaf centre:** **direct sun**. Indoor begonias scorch dramatically in even mild direct sun — especially Rex types with thin leaves. Move out of direct light or filter with a sheer curtain.

**Yellow first, then brown, often with leaf drop:** **over-watering** with cold roots. The plant is suffocating; soil is too wet for too long. Tip out the saucer, let the top half of the pot dry, and check whether the lower stem is still firm.

**Soft brown patches at the leaf base or stem joint:** **botrytis / grey mould**. Indicates wet leaves combined with poor airflow. Improve ventilation and stop misting.

**Whole leaf wilting, then browning evenly:** **under-watering** in low humidity. The rootball has become bone-dry and is repelling water on rewatering. Soak the pot in a tray of room-temperature water for 20 minutes, then drain.

**Leaves curling without browning:** usually **temperature shock** (a cold draught, a recent move, or a rapid heating change), or early-stage **spider mite damage** on the leaf undersides.

**White powdery coating on leaves with curl:** **powdery mildew**, common on Rex types in stagnant air.

The fix follows the cause. Don't over-correct — if you mist a "dry edges" begonia twice a day for a week, you'll trade dry curl for botrytis. Adjust one variable at a time and wait 5–7 days before judging the result.

## How much humidity do begonias actually need?

Most "Why is my begonia browning?" cases come down to humidity. Indoor air in heated homes can drop to **20–30% relative humidity**; begonias evolved in tropical understorey forests at **70–90%**.

**Targets:**

- Cane begonias: **40–55%** is comfortable.
- Rex begonias: **55–70%** for clean leaf edges.
- Tuberous begonias: not relevant indoors; they go dormant in winter.

**Practical ways to raise local humidity:**

- **Pebble tray.** Sit the pot on a saucer of pebbles with water just below the pebble surface. Adds 5–10% local humidity.
- **Group plants.** Three or more plants together create their own humid microclimate via shared transpiration.
- **Small humidifier on a timer.** The most reliable solution; running a 1 L humidifier for 4–6 hours per day during winter heating raises room humidity by 15–25%.
- **Move location.** A cool kitchen window or bathroom often runs 10–20% higher humidity than the average sitting room.

**Avoid misting.** Direct misting wets the leaf surface, which encourages **botrytis** and **bacterial leaf spot** — both of which cause exactly the brown patches you were trying to prevent. Raise humidity in the surrounding air instead.

A cheap **digital hygrometer** (10–15 USD/EUR) is the single most useful investment for begonia keepers. Guess about humidity and you'll over-correct in one direction or the other.

## How should you water a begonia to avoid leaf damage?

Begonia roots are surprisingly fine and surprisingly fragile. Both extremes — bone-dry and waterlogged — cause leaves to curl and brown within days. The middle is narrow, but it is reachable.

**Watering principles:**

1. **Check the top 2 cm of compost** with a finger, not by leaf droop. If it feels dry, water; if it still feels damp, wait.
2. **Water at the base**, not over the foliage. Wet leaves invite mildew and bacterial leaf spot.
3. **Use room-temperature water.** Cold tap water shocks the roots and can cause cane-begonia leaf curl within hours.
4. **Drain thoroughly.** Empty the saucer 15–30 minutes after watering; never let the pot stand in water.
5. **Adjust frequency by season.** Weekly in summer, every 10–14 days in winter, longer in cool conservatories.

**Common watering mistakes:**

- **Watering on a schedule** (e.g. "every Sunday") regardless of pot moisture. The plant doesn't care about your calendar.
- **Top-watering hard plants** with crusty compost; water runs straight through and the rootball stays dry. **Bottom-water** these (sit the pot in water for 15–20 minutes) until the rootball rehydrates.
- **Mistaking cold leaf curl for thirst** and over-watering already-cold soggy roots. Always check the rootball before watering.

**Bottom-watering routine for fussy Rex types:** sit the pot in a few centimetres of water for 15–20 minutes once a week. The compost wicks up evenly without wetting the crown or splashing soil onto leaves. This single change fixes about 30% of "browning leaf" Rex begonia cases.

## Where should a begonia sit in the house?

Light and temperature stability matter as much as humidity for keeping leaves clean.

**Light:**

- **Bright indirect light.** An east window with morning sun, or a north window with consistent daylight, both work.
- **Filtered light from a south or west window** is fine if you keep a sheer curtain or a few feet of distance.
- **Direct midday or afternoon sun is too harsh** for most indoor begonias and produces bleached patches in days.
- **Deep shade is too dim**, especially for Rex types — leaves lose their colour and the plant becomes leggy.

**Temperature:**

- **18–22°C** is the comfortable range.
- **Below 12°C** triggers leaf curl and drop, especially on cane types.
- **Above 28°C** combined with low humidity crisps leaf edges.
- **Avoid drafts.** Cold air from a leaky window or hot air from a radiator vent on the leaves causes localised browning. Keep at least 50 cm from radiators.

**Stability matters as much as the absolute number.** A begonia that has been in the same spot for months will tolerate suboptimal conditions; the same plant moved suddenly to a new room often drops half its leaves to the new conditions before stabilising. Resist relocation when in doubt.

## How do you fix a damaged begonia and stop the spread?

Once you've identified the cause, the recovery routine is consistent.

**Immediate (day 1):**

1. **Remove the worst leaves.** Snip off any leaf that is more than 30% brown, plus any with mould patches. Sanitise the scissors between plants.
2. **Move the plant** out of direct sun and away from heat sources or cold drafts.
3. **Check the rootball.** Tip the plant gently from its pot. If roots are dark, mushy, or smell, the plant needs root rescue; if roots are firm and white, the issue is environmental.

**First two weeks:**

- Maintain stable humidity at the target for your type.
- Water only when the top 2 cm of compost is dry.
- **No feeding** while the plant is recovering — rich nutrients on damaged roots cause more damage.
- **No repotting** unless roots are clearly rotting; new compost is a stress on top of existing stress.

**Long-term recovery (4–8 weeks):**

- Once the plant is producing new healthy leaves, resume **half-strength balanced houseplant feed** every 3 weeks.
- Pinch off growing tips to encourage bushiness if the plant has lost density.
- Take **stem cuttings** from healthy growth as insurance — cane begonia cuttings root in water in 2–3 weeks; Rex begonias root from leaf cuttings in moist sand at 21°C.

If a Rex begonia has lost more than half its leaves, take leaf cuttings now even if the parent looks like it might recover; the cuttings often outperform a stressed parent over the following 6 months.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are my begonia leaves curling but not turning brown?

Curl without colour change is usually **temperature shock or early spider mite**. Check for fine webbing on the leaf undersides; if present, treat with a strong rinse and insecticidal soap. Otherwise, look for cold drafts from a window, a recent room move, or a sudden change in heating. Begonias dislike rapid environmental changes more than steady but suboptimal conditions.

### Should I cut off the brown leaves?

Yes, once the brown covers more than about 30% of the leaf. Snip the whole leaf at the base of the stem; do not just trim brown edges (the cut edges keep browning anyway). Removing damaged leaves redirects energy to healthy growth and makes pests easier to spot.

### Do begonias need to be misted?

**No, despite the common advice.** Direct misting wets the leaf surface, which encourages **botrytis grey mould** and **bacterial leaf spot** — the same brown spots people are trying to prevent. Raise local humidity with a pebble tray, plant grouping, or a humidifier instead.

### How often should I water an indoor begonia?

There is no fixed schedule. Check the **top 2 cm of compost** with a finger; water when dry, leave it alone when damp. In a warm summer room this typically means **every 5–7 days**; in a cool winter room, **every 10–14 days**. Bottom watering for Rex types is more reliable than top watering.

### Will my begonia recover from cold damage overnight?

It depends on severity. **Light cold damage** (a single cold night, leaves slightly curled but stems firm) usually clears within 1–2 weeks once the plant returns to 18–22°C. **Severe cold damage** (frosted leaves, blackened stems, root damage) often kills the plant or leaves only a small remnant; take cuttings from any healthy growth and discard the rest.

### Why do my begonia flowers brown before opening?

Usually **botrytis** in stagnant humid air, or **dehydration** in dry hot conditions. Improve airflow with a small fan, water more consistently, and remove already-affected buds to stop the fungal spread. Cane begonias typically resume flowering once airflow and watering stabilise.
