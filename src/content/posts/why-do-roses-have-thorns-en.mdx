Roses grow sharp outgrowths — technically called **prickles**, not thorns — primarily to defend against herbivores, to anchor climbing canes to supporting structures, and to reduce moisture loss by creating a micro-boundary layer around the stem. They are an evolutionary adaptation, not a flaw.

## Why did roses evolve prickles instead of true thorns?

True thorns (like those on hawthorn) are modified stems with vascular tissue. Rose prickles are outgrowths of the epidermis — the outer bark layer — and snap off cleanly without damaging the plant's core. This distinction matters because prickles are cheaper for the plant to produce: they require less energy and can be distributed densely along canes without diverting resources from flower production.

The evolutionary pressure is straightforward. Wild roses evolved in habitats shared with browsing mammals — deer, rabbits, goats. A plant that could discourage grazing survived longer and set more seed. Prickles make a stem unpleasant to chew and painful to brush against, which is exactly the point.

## Do all rose varieties have thorns?

No. Several cultivated varieties have been bred to be nearly or completely thornless. **Zéphirine Drouhin**, a Bourbon climber from 1868, is the most famous thornless rose. Modern examples include **Smooth Touch**, **Amadis**, and many of David Austin's English roses which have minimal prickles.

Wild species roses, however, are almost always well-armed. *Rosa sericea* (the Tibetan rose) has spectacular translucent red prickles that are grown ornamentally for their beauty rather than despite them.

The presence or absence of prickles is genetically controlled by multiple genes, which is why breeding for thornlessness sometimes comes with trade-offs in disease resistance or flower form.

## How do prickles help climbing roses?

Climbing roses don't twine like morning glories or grip with tendrils like clematis. Instead, they produce long, arching canes that lean against and hook onto surrounding vegetation or structures. The backward-curving prickles act as anchors — they catch on bark, fencing, or trellis wire and prevent the cane from sliding back down.

This is why climbing rose prickles are typically more hooked than those on bush roses. The shape is functional: a straight prickle defends against browsers; a curved prickle also serves as a grappling hook.

## Do rose prickles reduce water loss?

There's evidence that dense prickle coverage creates a boundary layer of still air around the stem surface, which slightly reduces transpiration. In arid habitats — where many wild rose species evolved — this marginal advantage compounds over a growing season. Some desert-adapted species like *Rosa stellata* are notably prickly, which supports this hypothesis.

The effect is minor compared to leaf stomata regulation, but evolutionary adaptations don't need to be dramatic — they just need to be consistent.

## Can you safely remove thorns from cut roses?

Yes, and florists do this routinely. The standard approach:

1. **Use a thorn stripper** (a small metal tool with a notch) — grip the stem near the base and slide upward to pop prickles off cleanly
2. **Or use a sharp knife** held at a shallow angle to shave prickles without gouging the stem
3. **Never tear** prickles downward — this strips bark and creates entry points for bacteria that shorten vase life

Removing prickles from the portion of stem that sits below the waterline is especially important for cut flowers, as damaged bark underwater accelerates bacterial growth and blocks water uptake.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are rose thorns poisonous?**
Rose prickles are not poisonous, but puncture wounds can introduce soil bacteria including *Sporothrix schenckii*, which causes sporotrichosis (rose gardener's disease). Always clean puncture wounds promptly and wear gloves when pruning.

**Why do some roses have more thorns than others?**
Prickle density is genetically determined and varies between species and cultivars. Wild species roses tend to be heavily armed; many modern hybrid teas have been selectively bred for fewer prickles to make handling easier.

**Do thornless roses produce fewer flowers?**
Not necessarily. Thornlessness is controlled by different genes than flower production. However, some thornless cultivars are slightly less vigorous because the breeding focus was on removing prickles rather than maximizing bloom count.
