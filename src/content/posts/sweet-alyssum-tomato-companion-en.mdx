## Quick Answer

Sweet alyssum (*Lobularia maritima*) is the best low-growing companion for tomatoes because its dense clouds of tiny flowers attract hoverflies and parasitic wasps — the most effective natural predators of aphids, the main early-season tomato pest. Sow alyssum directly into the tomato bed in spring, 15–20 cm from each tomato stem, in full sun. Keep it trimmed to under 15 cm so it acts as living mulch, holding moisture and shading the soil without competing with the tomatoes for light.

## What is sweet alyssum and why does it pair so well with tomatoes?

*Lobularia maritima* (sweet alyssum) is a small, low-growing annual or short-lived perennial in the cabbage family (Brassicaceae). It forms a dense low mound 5–15 cm tall, covered for months in tiny four-petalled flowers in white, pink, or pale violet. The flowers are honey-scented and attract small pollinators in huge numbers.

The combination with tomatoes works for several specific reasons:

**Predatory insect attraction.** The tiny flat-topped flowers are perfect for hoverfly (*Syrphidae*) feeding — adult hoverflies need nectar to fuel egg-laying, and their larvae are voracious aphid predators. Each hoverfly larva can eat 200–400 aphids before pupating. Parasitic wasps, especially small *Aphidius* species, also feed on alyssum nectar and parasitise tomato aphids and whiteflies.

**Living mulch.** A skirt of alyssum at the base of each tomato stem shades the soil, reducing evaporation and stabilising soil temperature. Studies in commercial Californian tomato fields have shown alyssum-interplanted rows yield 5–10% more fruit, primarily due to better pest control.

**Pollinator support.** Tomatoes are largely self-pollinating, but bumblebees and solitary bees buzz-vibrate the flowers for a meaningful yield bump. A bed in continuous flower from alyssum recruits these bees.

**Quick coverage.** Alyssum reaches flowering size from seed in 6–8 weeks. By the time tomatoes start producing serious leafy growth, the alyssum is already in flower and recruiting beneficial insects.

## When and where exactly should I sow alyssum around tomatoes?

Timing matters. The goal is to have alyssum already in flower when tomato aphid pressure starts.

**Outdoors:**

- Sow direct in late spring, 1–2 weeks before tomato planting-out date.
- Or sow in modules 4 weeks before tomato planting-out and transplant the seedlings on the same day as your tomatoes.
- Soil temperature 12 °C+ for reliable germination.
- Light covering of soil (alyssum needs some light to germinate).

**Greenhouse / polytunnel:**

- Sow in modules 6 weeks before transplanting tomatoes.
- Plant alyssum seedlings into the bed 1–2 weeks before tomatoes go in.

**Spacing relative to tomatoes:**

- 15–20 cm from the tomato stem on the south or west side (where it gets sun and does not shade the tomato).
- Avoid planting directly against the stem — air movement at the tomato base is important to prevent fungal disease.
- For greenhouse rows, plant alyssum in a continuous strip 30 cm wide along the tomato row.
- For container tomatoes, sow alyssum directly into the pot edges; it tolerates root competition surprisingly well.

## Which alyssum varieties work best as a tomato companion?

Not all alyssum cultivars are equally useful. The trade-off is between flower duration, height, and heat tolerance.

| Cultivar | Habit | Heat tolerance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 'Carpet of Snow' | 8–10 cm low spreading | Moderate | Classic white; reliable beneficial-insect plant. |
| 'Easter Bonnet' series | 10–12 cm | Good | Pink, lavender, or white; bred for vigour. |
| 'Snow Princess' | 15–20 cm trailing | Excellent | Sterile hybrid; flowers all summer without heat-pause. |
| 'Snow Crystals' | 10 cm | Moderate | Larger flowers; very fragrant. |
| 'Wonderland' series | 8–10 cm compact | Moderate | Wide colour range; good for container edges. |
| 'New Carpet of Snow' | 6–8 cm | Moderate | Very low — useful as living mulch under bushy tomatoes. |

**'Snow Princess' is the standout choice** if you live somewhere hot. Standard seed-grown alyssum often slows or stops flowering for several weeks during summer heat (an evolutionary adaptation called heat-induced flowering pause). 'Snow Princess' is a sterile hybrid that does not pause and continues recruiting beneficial insects through the hottest weeks. It must be propagated from cuttings (sold as plug plants); seed is not available.

## How do I keep alyssum flowering through summer heat?

Sweet alyssum's main weakness as a tomato companion is exactly when you need it most — high summer. The plant naturally pauses flowering when daytime temperatures consistently exceed 27 °C, and resumes only when nights cool again.

Strategies to extend flowering:

1. **Choose 'Snow Princess'** if you can. The single biggest improvement.
2. **Trim back at first flowering pause.** When flowering thins, cut the plant back by half with shears. This often triggers a second flush within 2 weeks.
3. **Provide afternoon shade.** Plant on the east side of the tomato row so afternoon shade falls on the alyssum.
4. **Water deeply and consistently.** Heat stress is worsened by water stress.
5. **Mulch lightly.** A 2 cm layer of fine compost between alyssum and tomato keeps soil cooler.
6. **Sow a second batch in midsummer.** Fresh young plants sometimes flower through heat better than older established ones.

## How does alyssum specifically reduce aphid pressure on tomatoes?

The mechanism is well-studied. Aphids (*Macrosiphum euphorbiae* on tomatoes, also others) reach damaging numbers fast in warm weather — populations can double every 5–7 days. Conventional aphid management uses contact insecticides; the alyssum approach uses biology.

The cycle:

1. **Hoverflies arrive at flowering alyssum** for nectar and pollen, often within days of first flowers opening.
2. **Female hoverflies lay eggs** singly on plants infested with aphids — a single female may lay 100+ eggs in her short adult life.
3. **Hoverfly larvae hatch in 2–3 days** and immediately begin eating aphids. Each larva consumes 200–400 aphids over its 7–14 day larval stage.
4. **Parasitic wasps arrive in similar numbers**, parasitising aphids by laying eggs inside them. Parasitised aphids die within 5–7 days.
5. **The cycle stabilises** at low aphid numbers — typically too low to damage tomato yield.

In commercial trials, alyssum-bordered tomato rows showed aphid populations 30–60% lower than non-companion rows, with no insecticide use.

## What are the risks of using alyssum near tomatoes?

The combination is largely positive but a few caveats deserve attention.

**Brassica family overlap.** Alyssum is a member of the cabbage family, which means it shares some pests with brassicas — notably flea beetles (mostly cosmetic damage on alyssum) and rarely cabbage white butterfly larvae. Neither pest crosses to tomatoes.

**Slug attraction.** Dense alyssum cover at the soil surface provides excellent slug habitat. In wet climates, slug damage to lower tomato leaves can increase. Manage with copper rings or evening hand-collection.

**Self-seeding.** Sweet alyssum self-seeds enthusiastically. In a permanent vegetable bed this can become a tidiness issue. Cut back ruthlessly before seed sets if you do not want volunteers next year.

**Allelopathy concern (minimal).** Some Brassicaceae plants release glucosinolates that inhibit other plants. Sweet alyssum's compounds are mild and tomatoes are unaffected in practice.

**Air movement at tomato base.** Avoid creating a complete dense cover right against the tomato stem. Tomatoes are vulnerable to early blight (*Alternaria*) and septoria leaf spot, both fungal diseases favoured by stagnant humid air at the base. A 5–10 cm gap between alyssum and tomato stem provides enough air flow.

## What other plants pair well with alyssum and tomatoes?

Once you have alyssum at the base of your tomatoes, you can layer in other companion plants for additional benefits.

**Basil.** Basil planted between tomatoes is folkloric companion planting; modern research suggests modest pest-deterrence (whitefly and thrips) and possible flavour benefit. Strong in the kitchen, weak in the science.

**Marigold (*Tagetes patula* or *Tagetes minuta*).** Reduces root-knot nematode populations in soil. Plant a row at the bed edge; effects build over 2–3 seasons.

**Borage.** Tall blue-flowered annual with deep nectar reserves; another star pollinator plant. Slightly competes for water; site at least 30 cm from tomatoes.

**Calendula.** Attracts a different suite of beneficial insects (ladybirds, lacewings) and trap-crops some aphid species.

**Nasturtium.** Trap crop for blackfly. Plant at the bed edges, not under tomatoes (nasturtium shades aggressively).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will alyssum compete with tomatoes for water and nutrients?**
Minimally, if you keep it trimmed and provide adequate water for both. Alyssum has a shallow fibrous root system; tomatoes root deeply. Competition is for surface water only, and alyssum's living-mulch effect more than compensates by reducing evaporation.

**Can I grow alyssum in pots with tomatoes?**
Yes — alyssum is excellent in tomato containers. Use pots at least 35 cm wide and sow alyssum at the edge once tomato seedlings are established. Both plants will share the pot happily, and the alyssum will spill attractively over the rim.

**Are sweet alyssum and tomato flowers safe for cats and dogs?**
Sweet alyssum is generally regarded as low-toxicity and is not on common pet-poisoning lists. Tomato foliage and unripe fruit, however, contain solanine and are toxic if eaten in quantity by cats or dogs. Keep pets out of vegetable beds. For documented pet-safe choices, see [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).

**Will alyssum still help if I only have a tiny patio garden with one tomato?**
Yes, scaled appropriately. A 30 cm pot with one tomato and a ring of alyssum at the rim still attracts hoverflies and benefits the plant. The effect is smaller because the alyssum biomass is small, but the benefit is real even at hobby scale.

**Why are my alyssum seedlings not flowering even after weeks?**
Probably either insufficient sun (alyssum needs at least 5 hours direct daily) or too much nitrogen (rich compost produces leafy plants with few flowers). Move into more sun and reduce feed.
