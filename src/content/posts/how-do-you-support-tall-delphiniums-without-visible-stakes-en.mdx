## Quick Answer

Tall delphiniums need supports at **40–50 cm tall** when foliage can still hide them. The four invisible methods: **single tall canes inserted behind each spike**, a **grow-through ring** placed before bud break, **pea-stick teepees** of natural twiggy hazel, or a **hidden jute grid** between short corner stakes. Tied early with stretchy garden twine, all become invisible by mid-June.

## Why do delphiniums always fall over?

Delphinium spikes carry too much weight for their stem strength:

- **Tall flower spikes** — modern hybrids reach 1.5–2 m
- **Heavy florets** — each spike has 50–100 dense flowers
- **Hollow stems** — the genus's stem structure is thin-walled
- **Top-heavy** — most weight is in the upper third of the spike
- **Wind catch** — the broad spike acts like a sail in a summer storm

A single heavy rain or strong wind in late June bends or snaps the entire stem, and you lose the year's display in a single afternoon. Staking is not optional for delphiniums — it's the difference between a spectacular display and disaster.

## When should I put supports in?

The right time is **early May**, when plants are 40–50 cm tall and foliage is starting to expand. By mid-June the supports must already be in place.

The reasoning:

- **Too late**: by July foliage is fully grown and the stake is awkward to insert without breaking branches; storms have already passed
- **Too early**: in March, plants are too small and you lose visibility of where to position stakes
- **Just right**: at 40–50 cm, you can see the central crown, foliage will hide stakes, and there's still time before the spike pushes up

If you missed the window, a strategic late stake is still possible — just push it in carefully alongside an emerging spike.

## What's the simplest invisible method?

A **single hardwood cane** behind each emerging spike. Cheap, fast, and almost completely invisible from the front.

The method:

- **Use a 2 m bamboo cane** painted dark green or natural-toned
- **Insert 30 cm deep** about 5 cm behind where the central spike is emerging
- **Tie spike to cane** with stretchy twine in a figure-of-eight at 3 heights: 50 cm, 1 m, 1.5 m
- **As the spike grows**, the cane stays just behind it
- **Foliage from the rest of the plant** hides the cane from front view

This works best in a deep border where the back of the plant isn't visible. From the front, only the spike shows.

## How does a grow-through ring work?

A grow-through ring is a circular metal frame on three legs. Place it over the crown in early May; the plant grows up through the open mesh.

Pros:

- **Most invisible method** of all — completely hidden once foliage fills in
- **No tying needed** — stems support themselves through the mesh
- **Quick** — one ring per plant
- **Reusable for years** — strong galvanised wire lasts decades

Cons:

- **Must go in early** before central foliage gets too thick
- **Limits spike count** — can over-restrict large clumps
- **Smaller delphiniums** prefer this method to canes
- **Must match plant size** — 40 cm rings for shorter varieties, 60 cm for tall hybrids

For mid-height delphiniums (1.2–1.5 m), the grow-through ring is by far the most discreet support.

## What about pea-stick teepees?

Pea sticks are twiggy hazel branches, traditionally cut from coppiced hazel in late winter. Three or four pushed in around the crown form an open teepee:

- **Cut hazel branches** 1.5–2 m long
- **Push 4 sticks 30 cm into the soil** spaced evenly around the plant
- **Bend tops gently together** and tie with garden twine
- **Add cross-bracing twigs** between the uprights for extra strength

Hazel pea sticks have a perfect aesthetic for cottage gardens — they fit the look even when partly visible, blending with foliage. They biodegrade after one or two seasons but are easy to replace.

If you don't have hazel, the same effect works with birch twigs or any flexible coppice cuttings.

## How do I make a hidden jute grid?

The hidden grid method is best for clumps with multiple emerging spikes:

- **Drive 4 short stakes** (50 cm wooden pegs or steel pegs) into the soil 30 cm in from the clump's edges
- **Tie jute or sisal twine** between stakes at 50 cm height to make a square outline
- **Add cross strings** from each stake to the opposite, dividing the square into 4 quadrants
- **Add another grid layer** at 1 m height
- **Add a third layer** at 1.5 m if your delphiniums are very tall

Each spike grows up through its own quadrant, supported by the grid on all four sides. By July, foliage hides the entire frame from the front.

## What kind of tie should I use?

Choice of tie matters as much as the support itself:

- **Best**: stretchy green garden twine (sometimes called "soft tie" or "rubber tie") — flexes as the stem thickens
- **Good**: jute twine in a loose figure-of-eight (allows growth, biodegrades after one season)
- **Avoid**: wire, plastic ties, or string drawn tight (cuts into stems within weeks)
- **Avoid**: anything that creates a 360° loop without slack — strangles the stem

For each tie point, leave 2–3 cm of slack between stem and stake. Re-check ties at mid-June and slacken if needed.

## How do I make supports look natural in the border?

Beyond the support type, three small touches help:

- **Choose dark colours** for canes — green or brown, not natural bamboo yellow
- **Cut canes flush** with the top of the spike — no canes towering above flowers
- **Hide bases** with low-growing companions like *Alchemilla mollis*, hardy geraniums, or *Nepeta*
- **Place the support behind**, not in front of, the spike from the most-viewed direction

A delphinium clump well-supported looks like a self-supporting flower; one badly supported looks like a tomato plant in a vegetable garden.

## What about "Chelsea chop" — does that help?

Yes, partially. Cutting back delphiniums by half in mid-May (the "Chelsea chop") produces shorter, multiple, slightly-later spikes:

- **Less staking required** — shorter spikes are easier to support
- **Later flowering** — by 2–3 weeks
- **More flower spikes** — the plant branches and produces more side spikes
- **Smaller individual spikes** — but more total flowers

For very tall hybrid delphiniums in exposed gardens, the Chelsea chop combined with a single cane per spike is the most reliable approach.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will a fallen delphinium recover?

If the stem is bent but unbroken, yes — gently lift, stake, and tie. If snapped or hollow at the break, cut at the base and let the plant push side spikes from the crown. These secondary spikes are smaller but still flower well in late summer.

### How do I stake delphiniums in pots?

Container delphiniums need a single tall cane pushed deep into the pot at planting. Use a heavier pot (40+ cm) for stability. Tie at 3 levels as the plant grows. Move pots into a sheltered position during forecast storms.

### Are there any short delphiniums that don't need staking?

Yes — the **'Magic Fountains'** series and **'Mini Stars' Pacific Hybrids** stay under 1 m and rarely need staking. Smaller spikes mean less weight. See [how can I keep flowers blooming all year](/posts/flowers-bloom-all-year-round-en/) for fitting them into a sequence.

### Do I need to stake larkspur (annual delphinium) too?

Less so. Annual larkspur reaches only 60–90 cm and has more flexible stems. A bit of pea-stick support among them helps in windy gardens but isn't always essential. For [growing larkspur in a cut flower bed](/posts/larkspur-cut-flower-bed-en/), light support keeps stems straight.

### How long do staking materials last?

Bamboo canes 3–5 years before splitting. Hazel pea sticks 1–2 seasons. Galvanised metal grow-through rings 20+ years. Jute twine one season. Plan annual replacement of organic materials.

### Can I leave supports in over winter?

Remove decorative supports for tidiness, but you can leave grow-through rings in place if you mulch over them. Bamboo canes tend to rot at the base if left in soil all winter — better to lift and store under cover.
