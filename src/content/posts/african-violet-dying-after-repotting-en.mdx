## Quick Answer

An African violet usually wilts after repotting because the new pot is too large, the potting mix is too dense and stays wet, or the crown was buried below the soil line. Move the plant to a slightly bigger pot — never more than 2 cm wider than the previous one — using a light, peat-based African violet mix, and keep the crown level with the soil. Water from the bottom, give it bright indirect light, and recovery usually takes one to two weeks.

## Why does an African violet wilt right after repotting?

Repotting shock is almost always a root and crown problem, not a leaf problem. African violets (*Saintpaulia ionantha*) have small, fibrous root systems that adjust slowly to a new container. Three things usually go wrong at the same time:

**The pot is too big.** A pot wider than the leaf canopy holds more water than the roots can drink, and the un-occupied mix stays soggy. This drowns the roots and triggers crown rot within days.

**The mix is too dense.** Standard houseplant compost compacts around the fine roots and starves them of oxygen. African violets need a fluffy, fast-draining mix — typically peat or coir blended with perlite and vermiculite.

**The crown is buried.** The fuzzy lower leaves and the central growth point sit just above the soil. If repotting buries the crown even a few millimetres, the leaf bases stay wet, fungus moves in, and the plant collapses from the centre outward.

A correctly repotted violet should look almost identical to its pre-repot shape, with crisp leaves, a level crown, and the soil line just touching the base of the lowest leaves.

## How big should the new pot be?

Smaller than most beginners assume. The classic rule of thumb is "one third the diameter of the leaf canopy." A violet with a 15 cm leaf spread is happiest in a 5 cm pot. Going up by more than 2 cm in pot diameter at one repot almost always backfires.

If you bought a violet in a small nursery pot, you can usually leave it there for at least six months while it settles in. Only move up a size when you can see roots circling the bottom of the rootball after a gentle slide-out, or when the leaf canopy has more than tripled the pot diameter.

Use a plastic pot rather than terracotta. Terracotta dries the mix unevenly and stresses the fine roots. A self-watering African-violet pot (an inner unglazed ceramic insert sitting in a glazed reservoir) is the most forgiving option for new growers.

## What potting mix should I use?

A real African violet mix is much lighter than ordinary potting soil. The simplest reliable recipe is one part peat or coir, one part perlite, and one part vermiculite by volume. Pre-blended commercial African violet mixes from reputable brands are also fine — check the bag for "perlite" or "vermiculite" listed as primary ingredients.

Avoid:

- Garden soil (too heavy, carries pests)
- Generic indoor compost (compacts too quickly)
- Coco coir alone (holds too much water without enough air)
- Mixes containing slow-release fertiliser pellets (can burn the fine roots)

If your only option is a generic peat-based houseplant compost, lighten it by adding 30–40 % perlite by volume before potting.

## How do I revive a wilted African violet step by step?

Work slowly. Most violets that look terrible after repotting are still alive at the crown.

1. **Inspect the rootball.** Slide the plant out of its pot. Healthy roots are white or pale tan; rotted roots are brown, mushy, and slip off when you tug. Remove all rotted roots with clean scissors.
2. **Trim damaged leaves.** Pull off any leaf that is yellowed, mushy at the base, or buried under soil. Take from the outer rosette first; never strip the centre.
3. **Dry the crown.** Let the plant sit on a paper towel for 30–60 minutes so any wet rot stops spreading.
4. **Repot smaller, not bigger.** Move it to a pot only slightly larger than the remaining rootball. Use fresh, dry African violet mix.
5. **Plant at the right depth.** The lowest healthy leaves should sit just above the soil. Do not bury the crown.
6. **Water lightly from below.** Stand the pot in 1 cm of room-temperature water for 15 minutes, then drain. Do not soak.
7. **Cover with a clear plastic bag for 5–7 days.** This high-humidity recovery tent reduces leaf wilt while the new roots regrow. Keep it out of direct sun while tented.
8. **Reintroduce light gradually.** After a week, move to bright indirect light — an east window or a metre back from a south window.

You should see the plant firm up over 7–14 days. New tiny leaves at the centre confirm the crown is recovering.

## How should I water a freshly repotted violet?

Bottom-watering is the safest method for at least the first month. Set the pot in a saucer with 1–2 cm of room-temperature water and let the mix wick moisture up for 15–30 minutes, then tip out any excess. Cold water on the leaves causes pale ring spots, and water sitting in the crown causes rot.

Frequency depends on light and pot size, but a small violet usually wants water every 7–10 days at first. Check by lifting the pot — a dry pot feels surprisingly light, a wet one feels heavy. Skip a watering whenever you are not sure.

## What about light, temperature, and feeding during recovery?

Hold off on fertiliser for at least four weeks after repotting. Damaged roots cannot use nutrients and accumulated salts make rot worse. Once you see new leaf growth, restart with a balanced African violet fertiliser at quarter strength, applied through the bottom-watering reservoir.

Keep recovering plants:

- At 18–24 °C, away from cold draughts and hot radiators
- In bright indirect light (an east-facing windowsill is ideal)
- Out of direct midday sun, which scorches the recovering leaves
- At 50–60 % humidity if possible — a pebble tray helps in dry winter rooms

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I actually repot an African violet?**
Once a year is usually enough. Most violets benefit more from refreshing the mix in the same pot than from sizing up. Slide the plant out, knock off old soil, trim any dead roots, and replant in fresh mix at the same depth.

**Why does my African violet have a long bare neck after repotting?**
The neck is the thickening lower stem that builds up over time. To bury it without raising the crown above the rim, scoop out a couple of centimetres from the top of the existing rootball, then lower the plant into the same pot so the lowest leaves sit at soil level. Top up with fresh mix.

**Can I save an African violet that has crown rot?**
Sometimes. If the centre is mushy and the leaves are pulling out cleanly, the parent plant is usually finished. However, you can often root individual healthy outer leaves in water or moist mix to start a new generation.

**Is it normal for leaves to droop for a few days after repotting?**
Yes. A small dip in turgor for two to four days is common while the roots establish. Wilting that lasts longer than a week, or that comes with mushy leaf bases, signals overwatering or crown rot rather than ordinary shock.

**Should I use rooting hormone when repotting an African violet?**
No. Rooting hormone is for cuttings, not for established plants. Using it on existing roots offers no benefit and can encourage uneven regrowth.
