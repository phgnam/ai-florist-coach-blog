Hydrangea flower colour changes because of **soil pH and aluminium availability**. In acidic soil (pH below 5.5), aluminium ions are soluble and absorbed by the plant, turning flowers **blue**. In alkaline soil (pH above 6.5), aluminium is locked up and unavailable, so flowers remain **pink**. The plant itself doesn't change — the chemistry of what it absorbs from the soil changes the pigment expression.

## How does soil pH actually change flower colour?

The mechanism is surprisingly specific. Hydrangea petals contain a pigment called **delphinidin-3-glucoside**. This pigment is naturally pink. When it binds with **aluminium ions**, it shifts to blue. So the question isn't really about pH directly — it's about aluminium:

- **Acidic soil (pH < 5.5)**: Aluminium in the soil dissolves into ions (Al³⁺) that roots can absorb. The aluminium travels to the flowers and binds with the pigment → **blue flowers**.
- **Neutral soil (pH 5.5–6.5)**: Some aluminium is available, some isn't → **purple or mixed flowers**.
- **Alkaline soil (pH > 6.5)**: Aluminium stays bound in insoluble compounds that roots can't absorb. No aluminium reaches the flowers → **pink flowers**.

This only applies to **Hydrangea macrophylla** (bigleaf hydrangea) and **H. serrata**. White hydrangeas (_H. paniculata_, _H. arborescens_) don't change colour because they lack the delphinidin pigment. White varieties always stay white regardless of soil conditions.

## How do you make hydrangeas blue? To turn pink hydrangeas blue, you need to
**lower soil pH** and **increase aluminium availability**: 1. **Apply aluminium
sulfate** — dissolve 1 tablespoon in 4 litres of water and drench the root zone.
Apply monthly from early spring through bud formation (March–June). This is the
most direct method. 2. **Add sulfur** — elemental sulfur lowers soil pH over
time. Work 100–200g per square metre into the top 10 cm. Takes 3–6 months to
take effect. 3. **Mulch with pine needles or oak leaves** — these acidic
materials gradually lower pH as they decompose. 4. **Use acidic fertiliser** —
products marketed for azaleas, rhododendrons, and blueberries also work for
hydrangeas. 5. **Water with collected rainwater** instead of tap water — tap
water in many areas is alkaline and gradually raises soil pH. **Important**:
Change doesn't happen overnight. It takes **one full growing season** to see
significant colour shift. Flowers that have already opened won't change — the
new colour appears on the next flush of blooms. ## How do you make hydrangeas
pink? To turn blue hydrangeas pink, you need to **raise soil pH** and **lock
aluminium away**: 1. **Add garden lime** (calcium carbonate) — 200–400g per
square metre, worked into the top soil layer. Raises pH gradually over 2–3
months. 2. **Apply dolomite lime** — similar effect plus adds magnesium. 3.
**Use high-phosphorus fertiliser** — phosphorus binds aluminium in the soil,
making it unavailable to roots. A fertiliser with high middle number (like
25-10-10 or superphosphate) helps. 4. **Avoid aluminium-containing products** —
don't use aluminium sulfate (obviously) and avoid acidic mulches. Pink is
generally easier to achieve than blue in most garden soils, since many soils are
naturally neutral to slightly alkaline. ## Can you have both blue and pink on
the same plant? Yes, and it's more common than you might think. It happens when:
- **Soil pH varies** around the root zone — one side of the plant roots in more
acidic soil than the other - **You're mid-transition** — changing soil pH takes
time, so blooms from different root zones may show different colours
simultaneously - **Container planting** — the confined root zone makes colour
control easier, but uneven watering or drainage can create pH pockets The
transitional purple shades and mixed blooms are actually stunning. Many
gardeners deliberately aim for this multi-toned effect. ## What other factors
affect hydrangea colour? **Variety**: Some cultivars hold their colour more
strongly. **Nikko Blue** turns blue readily in acidic soil. **Forever Pink**
resists bluing even in slightly acidic conditions. Check varietal tendencies
before planting. **Water source**: Hard tap water (high in calcium and
magnesium) gradually raises soil pH, pushing flowers toward pink. If you want
blue hydrangeas and have hard water, collect rainwater or use acidifying
treatments. **Fertiliser type**: Fertilisers containing ammonium (like ammonium
sulfate) acidify soil over time. Calcium-based fertilisers raise pH. **Container
vs. ground**: Potted hydrangeas are much easier to colour-control because you
control the entire soil environment. Use ericaceous (acid) compost for blue;
standard compost with added lime for pink. ## Frequently Asked Questions **Can
you change white hydrangeas to blue or pink?** No. White hydrangeas (H.
paniculata like Limelight, H. arborescens like Annabelle) don't contain the
delphinidin pigment that responds to aluminium. They're genetically incapable of
colour change. Only H. macrophylla and H. serrata varieties change colour. **How
long does it take to change hydrangea colour?** One full growing season minimum.
Start treatments in early spring, and you should see colour change in the
current or next season's blooms. Dramatic shifts may take 2 seasons. **Will
vinegar turn hydrangeas blue?** Vinegar temporarily acidifies soil but the
effect is fleeting — it's neutralized by soil buffers within days. It won't
produce lasting colour change. Use aluminium sulfate or sulfur for reliable
results.
