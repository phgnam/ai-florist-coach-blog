## Quick Answer

Bring potted geraniums (pelargoniums) inside **before the first hard frost**, cut them back **by half**, set them on a **bright, cool windowsill (10–15 °C)**, and water **only when the soil is fully dry — about every 2–3 weeks**. Pinch off any flower buds and yellow leaves through winter. In late February, increase watering, give a half-strength feed, and the plant restarts into bloom by May.

## Why do geraniums need to come indoors at all?

What gardeners call "geraniums" — the bedding and trailing kinds in red, pink, salmon, white — are actually *Pelargonium* species from southern Africa. They are **tender perennials**: not hardy below 1–2 °C, but easy to keep alive at indoor temperatures. The garden centres that sell them as annuals are pricing for the customer who throws them away each autumn. With minimal effort you can keep the same plant going for 5–10 years.

True geraniums (*Geranium* genus, hardy cranesbills) survive winter outdoors without help and are not the subject of this article.

## When should you bring them inside?

Watch the night-time forecast. Pelargoniums tolerate occasional dips to 4 °C without damage, but a single frost (below 0 °C) can kill the soft growing tips. The rule is: **bring indoors when overnight lows are forecast under 4 °C and not predicted to recover**.

In most temperate climates this is mid-October to early November. In sub-tropical or Mediterranean climates with frost-free winters, the plants can stay outside year-round and only need a light prune.

Before bringing in:

- **Inspect for pests** — aphids, whitefly, spider mites — and treat with a soft-soap spray
- **Trim back leggy growth by half** with sharp secateurs
- **Knock off loose soil** and check the bottom of the pot for slugs or vine weevil grubs

## How should you cut them back for winter?

Cutting back serves two purposes: reduces water loss through leaves over winter, and keeps the plant compact for the windowsill.

The method:

- Cut each stem to **roughly half its length**, just above a leaf node
- Remove all yellow or browning leaves
- Strip off any flower buds — the plant should not be flowering through winter
- Save the pruned tips as cuttings for free new plants (rooting rate 70 percent in damp gritty compost)

The plant looks bare for 2–3 weeks after pruning, then puts on slow new growth as it acclimatises to indoor light.

## Where on the windowsill should you place them?

Geraniums want **bright but cool**. The ideal indoor spot:

- **South- or west-facing window**, at the brightest spot of the windowsill
- **Cool-room temperature: 10–15 °C** during the day, no warmer than 18 °C
- **Distance from a radiator** — a hot dry windowsill above a heater is the most common reason indoor geraniums fail
- **Air circulation** — a slightly draughty cool windowsill is much better than a still hot one

If the only sunny windowsill you have is above a radiator, a small cardboard or wooden barrier between the radiator and the pot deflects rising hot air. A plant standing tray of damp pebbles also raises local humidity.

## How often should you water through winter?

Far less than you think. The most common cause of winter geranium death is **overwatering**, not cold or dry air.

The rule:

- **Check the soil every 7–10 days** by sticking a finger 2 cm in
- Water only if the top **half** of the pot feels dry
- When you do water, give it **enough to drain** from the bottom, then discard saucer water immediately
- In a cool 10–15 °C room, this is roughly **every 2–3 weeks**

A pelargonium with limp leaves and dry soil recovers within hours of watering. A pelargonium with limp leaves and wet soil is dying from root rot and may not be saveable.

Stop fertilising entirely from October to February. The plant is in semi-dormancy and does not use nutrients.

## Should you keep flowering through winter?

No — pinch off any flower buds that form. A plant flowering through winter on a low-light windowsill weakens itself for the spring flush that matters. The energy that would have gone into a few small winter flowers feeds the crown and roots instead, and the spring bloom is much heavier.

If a few flowers slip through, do not panic — but in general remove buds from October through January.

## How do you wake the plant up in spring?

From late February onwards, the days lengthen and the plant senses the change.

- **Increase watering** gradually — let the top 2 cm dry between waterings rather than the top half
- **Resume feeding** at half strength every 3 weeks with a balanced liquid fertiliser
- **Pinch the growing tips** in early March to encourage branching
- **Repot if rootbound** into a pot one size larger, with fresh peat-free compost
- **Move outdoors** after the last frost, gradually over 7–10 days (acclimatisation is essential or leaves scorch)

By mid-May a properly overwintered geranium is bushier and flowers more heavily than a freshly bought plant from the garden centre.

## Are there alternative methods if you don't have a windowsill?

Two old-school methods still work:

- **Bare-root storage**: lift the plant, shake off all soil, hang upside-down in a cool dry shed at 4–7 °C, and re-pot in March. Survival rate 50–70 percent.
- **Cutting-only overwintering**: take 10 cm cuttings in September, root in damp gritty compost, keep on a cool windowsill. Discard the parent plant. The cuttings stay small enough for any windowsill and bloom by June.

Bare-root storage is for gardeners with no indoor space at all; cuttings are the favourite method of professional growers because the new plants are vigorous and disease-free.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are my geranium leaves turning yellow indoors?

Yellow leaves in winter usually mean **overwatering** (lower leaves yellow, soil stays wet) or **too dark a spot** (whole plant pales). Reduce water and move closer to a brighter window. A few yellow leaves at the bottom is normal seasonal turnover; whole-plant yellowing is a warning.

### Should I keep the soil moist or let it dry?

Let it dry — geraniums prefer the soak-and-dry rhythm with longer dry intervals in winter. Moist soil at low temperatures encourages root rot and stem-base mould.

### Can I prune harder than half?

Yes, you can cut back to two-thirds. The plant grows back from the woody base by spring. Avoid cutting into the woody core itself — buds need green tissue to break from. Plants cut very hard look bare for 6–8 weeks but recover.

### Do geraniums need a rest period?

Yes — the cool, dry, low-feed winter is the rest. Plants that are kept warm, well-watered, and fed through winter often flower less heavily the next summer because they never paused.

### Will indoor geraniums catch pests in winter?

The common ones are whitefly and aphids on new growth. Inspect weekly, and treat early with a soft-soap spray if you spot any. Spider mite is rare in cool rooms but can appear in dry centrally-heated rooms — increase humidity if needed.

### How long can a single geranium plant live?

With annual overwintering and re-potting, 8–15 years is realistic. Some collectors keep named cultivars going for 30+ years by taking cuttings every few years and discarding the parent.
