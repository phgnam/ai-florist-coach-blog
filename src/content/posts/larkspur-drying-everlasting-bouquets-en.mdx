## Quick Answer

Larkspur (*Consolida ajacis*) is one of the very best garden flowers for drying because the petals retain colour exceptionally well — but only if you cut the stems at the right stage and dry them in the right conditions. Cut whole spikes when about half the lower flowers are open and the upper buds are still tight. Hang upside-down in small bunches in a dark, warm, dry, well-ventilated room for 10–14 days. Storing finished bunches out of direct light keeps the deep blue, purple, and rose colours vivid for several years.

## What is larkspur and why is it the gold standard for dried arrangements?

*Consolida ajacis* (annual larkspur, rocket larkspur) is a hardy annual in the buttercup family, native to southern Europe and naturalised across temperate regions. It is closely related to perennial *Delphinium* but easier to grow from seed and a quicker producer of cut-flower-quality stems. Plants reach 60–120 cm with tall spikes of densely-packed five-petalled flowers in deep blue, violet, lilac, pink, and white.

Several features make larkspur particularly good for drying:

1. **Genuinely retentive colour.** Many flowers fade dramatically when dried — larkspur fades only slightly. Modern saturated cultivars like 'QIS Dark Blue' or 'Imperial Blue' retain about 90% of their fresh colour through years of careful storage.
2. **Strong, stiff stems.** The dried stems hold their shape without wire, making bouquets easy to assemble.
3. **Architectural shape.** Tall spikes give vertical line and movement that's difficult to find in dried-flower work.
4. **Long-lasting.** Properly dried and stored, larkspur retains its appearance for 3–5 years before any obvious degradation.

## When exactly should I cut larkspur for drying?

Timing is the single biggest factor in colour retention. Cut too early and the upper buds never open; cut too late and lower flowers shed petals during drying.

**Ideal stage:** about half the flowers on a spike are fully open, the middle ones are partially open, and the upper third is still tight buds.

**Time of day:** Mid-morning, after the dew has dried but before the heat of the afternoon. Avoid cutting in rain — wet stems mould during drying.

**Stem length:** Cut the entire flowering stem at the base of the plant, leaving any side shoots that have not yet flowered. Long stems (60–100 cm) are far more useful in arrangements than short ones.

**Tools:** A clean sharp knife or florist's scissors. Strip lower leaves immediately — they shrivel unattractively if dried.

**Successive cuts:** Larkspur produces side shoots that flower 2–3 weeks after the main spike. Cut the main stem and the plant will flower again from the leaf axils, giving a second crop of useful drying material.

## How exactly do I hang and dry the stems?

Hanging is the traditional and best method for larkspur — it preserves stem rigidity and avoids the colour fade that can happen with desiccant drying.

### Step 1: Prepare bunches

- Group 6–10 stems per bunch — small enough that air can circulate around every spike.
- Stagger flower heads so spikes don't crush each other.
- Tie at the cut ends with twine, garden string, or rubber bands.
- Rubber bands are best because stems shrink as they dry; twine loosens and bunches fall.

### Step 2: Choose the drying space

The space matters more than people expect. The ideal:

- **Dark.** UV light fades colour fast. A windowless cupboard, attic, or shed is perfect.
- **Warm.** 18–25 °C is ideal. Cooler is fine but slower.
- **Dry.** Below 50% relative humidity. A dehumidifier helps in humid climates.
- **Well-ventilated.** Open access for air movement; a quiet fan in a small room dramatically improves results.
- **Dust-free.** Larkspur dust is hard to remove from finished bunches.

### Step 3: Hang upside down

Hanging upside-down preserves the natural shape of the spikes. Use:

- Strong twine strung horizontally between two hooks.
- Coat hangers with bunches tied to the bar.
- Drying racks with multiple hanging points.

Allow 5–10 cm between bunches for air movement.

### Step 4: Wait

Drying takes 10–14 days at typical room conditions. Larkspur is fully dry when:

- Stems snap rather than bend.
- Petals rustle when touched.
- No dampness when squeezed gently.

If a few petals shed, that's normal — larkspur sheds about 10–20% of its lowest petals during drying.

## Which larkspur cultivars dry best and hold colour longest?

Cultivar choice is critical for dried-flower work. Modern series bred for cut-flower production are vastly better than ordinary mixed seed.

| Cultivar | Colour | Drying performance | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 'QIS Dark Blue' | Deep navy blue | Excellent | Cut-flower bred; uniform spike. |
| 'QIS Carmine' | Deep rose-pink | Excellent | Holds pink colour better than most. |
| 'Imperial Series' (Blue, Lilac, White) | Range | Excellent | Dwarf spikes; best for small bouquets. |
| 'Sublime Series' (Dark Blue, Lilac, Pink) | Range | Excellent | Tall spikes (120 cm); commercial cut-flower grade. |
| 'Cannes Mixed' | Mixed | Good | Cheap; less uniform but useful for amateur drying. |
| 'Earl Grey' | Smoky violet | Excellent | Unusual colour; popular in modern bouquet styles. |
| 'Misty Lavender' | Soft lavender | Good | Lighter colour fades faster than darks. |
| 'Snow Cloud' | Pure white | Good | Whites can yellow over years; store in absolute darkness. |

**Deep blues and pinks dry best.** Pale colours (especially pale pink and white) fade more visibly over time. Deep saturated colours retain at least 80% of their fresh hue for 3+ years.

## How do I store dried larkspur to keep colour for years?

Storage is as important as drying. Done well, dried larkspur retains its colour for 3–5 years; done badly, you'll see fade in a single summer.

**The rules:**

1. **Keep out of direct light.** UV is the main agent of fade. A shaded room or a cabinet is fine; a sunny windowsill is fatal.
2. **Keep dry.** Re-absorption of moisture causes mould and breakdown. Keep below 50% relative humidity.
3. **Keep at moderate temperature.** Warmth is fine; extreme heat and cold both shorten dried-flower life.
4. **Box-store between displays.** Store unused bunches in tissue paper inside cardboard boxes between uses. This blocks light and dust.
5. **Avoid damp areas.** Bathrooms, conservatories, and unheated cellars all cycle humidity.
6. **Don't refresh with water.** Once dried, larkspur dies if rewetted. Avoid spraying with anything other than a fixative spray.

**Optional fixative:** A light mist of artist's matte fixative or hairspray (the cheapest brands work fine) reduces petal shed and dust uptake. Spray once when fully dry, hold the can 30 cm away, light pass only.

## Are larkspur and other Consolida flowers safe for homes with pets and children?

No — and this is important. *Consolida* and *Delphinium* are both highly toxic to humans and animals. They contain diterpenoid alkaloids (delphinine, methyllycaconitine, ajacine) that are dangerous in small amounts.

Practical precautions:

- **Keep dried larkspur out of reach of pets.** Cats are particularly sensitive; ingestion causes weakness, collapse, and potential death.
- **Do not allow children to play with stems or petals.** A small pet or child could ingest a fatal dose without obvious quantity.
- **Wash hands after handling.** Sap can irritate skin.
- **Do not use larkspur in arrangements where it might be eaten.** Avoid kitchen or dining-table displays where pets are present.
- **Discard responsibly.** Old dried larkspur should go to landfill or general waste, not garden compost where livestock or pets might encounter it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I dry larkspur with silica gel or sand instead of hanging?**
Yes — but the results are less natural. Silica gel preserves the flower-head shape exceptionally well but the stems become brittle and hard to arrange. Use silica only when you want individual flower heads (e.g. for resin embedding); use hanging for whole spikes for bouquets.

**My dried larkspur turned brown instead of staying blue. What went wrong?**
Almost certainly damp during drying. Stems sat too long in humid air or the drying space had above 60% relative humidity. Browning is permanent. Move drying to a drier space, or run a dehumidifier; expected drying time is 10–14 days.

**Can I cut larkspur and dry it after the spike is fully bloomed?**
Possible but disappointing. Fully-open spikes shed many lower petals during drying and the dried bunch looks ragged. Cut earlier, when only the bottom half is open.

**Does microwave drying work for larkspur?**
No — microwave drying is good for some flat flowers (pansies, hydrangea) but ruins larkspur. The high heat causes petals to brown. Stick with hanging.

**My larkspur seedlings emerged this spring but barely flowered. What should I do differently next year?**
Two things. (1) Sow earlier — late autumn or very early spring outdoors. Larkspur dislikes hot weather and will go to seed quickly if started too late. (2) Sow direct rather than transplanting. Larkspur resents root disturbance and grows much better from autumn-sown direct seed.
