## Quick Answer

To overwinter "garden geraniums" (technically *Pelargonium*), **lift the plants before first frost**, shake off most of the soil, and **cut foliage back by 50%**. Pot into fresh free-draining compost in a slightly smaller pot than the summer one. Keep at **10–15°C** in **bright indirect light** with **minimal watering** (every 2–3 weeks, just enough to prevent woody stems from shrivelling). Don't feed until spring growth resumes. Most overwintered pelargoniums return to flower by **April–May** and can survive 5+ years.

## Are "geraniums" and "pelargoniums" the same plant?

This is the first thing to get straight. The plants most gardeners call "geraniums" — the red, pink, white, or salmon flowers in summer window boxes and patio pots — are actually **Pelargonium**, not Geranium.

True hardy *Geranium* species (cranesbills) are perennial, frost-hardy, and **do not need to come indoors**. They die back to the ground in winter and return on their own.

*Pelargonium* species are South African in origin, tender, and **die at temperatures below about −1°C**. They are the plants this article is about. The naming conflict is so old that catalogues, garden centres, and even seed packets use "geranium" interchangeably with *Pelargonium*. If your plant is a tender summer container plant with rounded scalloped leaves and ball-shaped flower clusters, you have a *Pelargonium* — and it will need indoor protection to survive winter outside USDA zones 10–11.

The four common types behave slightly differently in storage:

- **Zonal pelargoniums** (the classic red/pink window-box type) — easiest to overwinter, most tolerant of dim conditions.
- **Ivy-leaved pelargoniums** (trailing types from hanging baskets) — overwinter well but resent dry indoor air.
- **Regal / "Martha Washington"** — large showy flowers; need cooler conditions in storage (8–12°C) than the others.
- **Scented-leaf pelargoniums** — overwinter beautifully and double as winter-fragrance houseplants.

## When is the right time to bring them indoors?

The cue is **night temperature**, not the calendar.

**Hard rule:** bring pelargoniums in **before night temperatures drop below 4°C consistently**. A single light frost (−1 to −3°C) blackens leaves and stems; a hard frost (−5°C or below) usually kills the plant outright.

**Practical sequence:**

1. **Watch the 10-day forecast** in early-to-mid autumn. The first appearance of overnight lows below 5°C is your signal to act.
2. **Stop feeding** about 2 weeks before lifting, so the plant slows growth and hardens new shoots.
3. **Lift on a dry sunny day** if possible. Wet soil clinging to the roots is heavy and rots more easily indoors.
4. **Pre-treat for pests** before they ride inside on the leaves (more on that below).

In mild-winter regions where night temperatures stay above 4°C all year, pelargoniums can stay outside all winter as long as they have shelter from cold rain. In most of Northern Europe, the UK, and the northern half of the US, October–November is the indoor-move window.

## How do you prepare and pot the plants for indoor storage?

Resist the temptation to bring them in "as is". A neat reduction at lifting time avoids most of the problems people run into mid-winter (legginess, rot, pest outbreaks).

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Lift the plant** with a fork, keeping a moderate rootball but shaking off about half the old soil. The goal is roots that fit a smaller winter pot.
2. **Cut back foliage by 30–50%.** Reduce each stem to a healthy outward-facing leaf node. This is the single biggest factor in clean overwintering — long, leafy stems flop, drop leaves, and become breeding grounds for fungus indoors.
3. **Inspect for pests** and discard any plant with whitefly, mealybug, or rust. A single infested plant will spread the problem to your entire windowsill collection.
4. **Repot into a slightly smaller pot** (e.g. from a 25 cm summer pot down to a 17–20 cm winter pot) using **free-draining compost**: 70% multipurpose, 20% grit, 10% perlite. The smaller pot holds less water and matches the reduced root system.
5. **Water once thoroughly** to settle the new compost, then leave alone for 2–3 weeks before watering again.

If you have **lots of plants and limited windowsill space**, an alternative storage method is **bare-root hanging**: lift each plant, shake off all soil, hang upside down by a string in a cool dark frost-free space (5–10°C, ideally a cellar or unheated garage), and replant in spring. This works for zonal types and scented-leaf types, less well for ivy-leaved and regal types.

## What conditions do indoor pelargoniums actually want all winter?

The ideal winter environment is **cool and bright**, which is the opposite of what most people offer indoor plants.

**Temperature:** 10–15°C is the sweet spot. Above 18°C the plant breaks dormancy and grows weak, leggy, leaf-shedding stems. Below 5°C and growth stops entirely; below freezing and the plant dies. A bright unheated porch, a cool spare bedroom, an enclosed but unheated balcony, or a cool stairwell window all work.

**Light:** the brightest indirect light you have. South or east windows are ideal. North windows are usable but the plant will be leggier by spring. A grow-light on a 10-hour timer is a good supplement in dark winter months.

**Humidity:** 30–50% is fine; pelargoniums tolerate dry indoor air better than most houseplants. Avoid constant high humidity (above 70%), which encourages fungal disease on the cut stem ends.

**Airflow:** essential. A small clip-on fan running on low keeps the air moving and dramatically reduces leaf-spot, rust, and grey mould. Stagnant warm humid air on a closed-window radiator-shelf kills more overwintered pelargoniums than cold ever will.

## How should you water and feed during the rest period?

This is the stage where well-meaning owners do most of the damage. Pelargoniums in winter are **slowly resting**, not actively growing — they need very little water and no food.

**Watering routine:**

- Water only when the **top half** of the compost feels dry. In most homes this is **every 2–3 weeks**, sometimes longer in cold conservatories.
- Water at the **base of the plant**, not over the foliage.
- Use **room-temperature water**; cold tap water shocks the slow winter root system.
- **Drain thoroughly** — never let the pot sit in a saucer of water for more than 30 minutes.

**Feeding:**

- **Stop all feeding** from the moment plants come indoors until the days lengthen in late February.
- Resume a **half-strength balanced feed** every 2–3 weeks once new growth appears — this signals the plant is breaking dormancy and demanding nutrients.

**Deadheading and tidying:**

- Remove yellow leaves and any flower stems still trying to bloom for the first 4–6 weeks. Don't cut into healthy green stems mid-winter; save heavier pruning for early spring.

If you took cuttings before the lift (a smart insurance policy), see related propagation principles in [how to propagate roses from cuttings](/posts/propagate-roses-cuttings-en/) and [growing lavender from cuttings](/posts/grow-lavender-from-cuttings-en/) — the "stem cutting + free-draining mix + bright indirect light" pattern is identical for pelargonium cuttings.

## How do you bring overwintered geraniums back into bloom in spring?

This is the rewarding stage. After 4–5 months of slow decline, you wake the plant up over a 2–3 week window and watch it transform.

**Six-week wake-up plan:**

**Weeks 1–2 (late February or early March):**

- Move pots to the **brightest spot you have** indoors.
- Begin watering when the **top 2 cm of compost is dry** (instead of waiting for half the pot to dry).
- Cut each main stem back to **2–3 nodes above the woody base**. This looks brutal but produces a much bushier spring plant.
- Resume feeding at **half strength** every 2 weeks.

**Weeks 3–4:**

- Watch for new pale-green shoots emerging from the cut stems.
- Repot into **fresh compost** if not already done — a one-pot-size step up is enough.
- Increase watering frequency as new leaves appear.

**Weeks 5–6:**

- Once 5–7 new leaves have unfurled, **start hardening off** by moving plants outside for a few hours on warm days.
- Pinch out the very top growing point on each new shoot to encourage further branching.

**Plant out** to summer pots when night temperatures stay reliably above 8°C (typically late April–May in temperate climates). Most overwintered plants flower **within 4–6 weeks** of going outside, and many flower more profusely than first-year plants because of the larger established root system.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will geraniums survive winter outside in pots?

Only in **frost-free climates** (USDA zones 10–11 and similar). True garden cranesbills (*Geranium*) are hardy and stay outside; the tender summer "geraniums" (*Pelargonium*) sold for window boxes will not survive a hard freeze in a pot, where roots are exposed and freeze faster than ground roots. In zones 8–9 with light freezes, a sheltered porch and protective fleece can sometimes carry plants through, but indoor storage is more reliable.

### How cold can pelargoniums tolerate?

Foliage tolerates brief drops to about **3°C** without damage. Leaves blacken at the first frost (−1°C). Stems usually survive a single light frost if foliage is removed, but a hard freeze (−5°C or below) kills most pelargoniums outright. The 4°C night-time threshold is your safe early-warning trigger.

### Do you need to cut back geraniums before bringing them inside?

Yes — and this is the single most common omission. Cut back foliage by **30–50%** to remove leafy growth that will shed indoors, reduce moisture loss, and prevent fungal disease. Long uncut plants become leggy and prone to rot in dim winter conditions.

### Can you store pelargoniums bare-root in a cellar instead of as houseplants?

Yes, for many types. Lift the plant, shake off all soil, and **hang upside down by string** in a frost-free, dark, cool spot (5–10°C). Inspect monthly and discard any with severe shrivelling. In spring, soak the roots in water for an hour, repot, and resume watering. This works well for zonal and scented-leaf types; less reliably for ivy-leaved and regal types.

### Why are my overwintering geranium leaves turning yellow?

Most often **over-watering** combined with low light. Yellow lower leaves with stems still firm signal too much water; remove the leaves, reduce watering, and improve light. Yellow with brown-edged leaves can indicate **cold draughts** or sudden temperature drops. Total yellowing with mushy stems is **rot** — usually fatal.

### Should I take cuttings before lifting in autumn?

Almost always worth it as insurance. Take **8–10 cm tip cuttings** from non-flowering shoots in late summer, strip the lower leaves, and root in a sandy free-draining mix at 18–20°C. By winter you have backup plants in case the lifted parent fails. Pelargonium cuttings root within 2–4 weeks and bloom the following summer.
