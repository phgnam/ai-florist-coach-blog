## Quick Answer

Pelargonium "geraniums" overwinter best as low-maintenance indoor plants in a cool (8–13 °C), bright spot with sparse watering — about a quarter of their summer routine. You can also dig them up, shake the soil off, and store them bare-root in paper bags in a frost-free shed at 5–8 °C. Both routes deliver vigorous spring regrowth and full flowering by early summer if you cut back hard and feed when daylight returns.

## Why do geraniums need to come indoors and not stay outside over winter?

Garden "geraniums" — almost always pelargoniums, zonal *Pelargonium × hortorum* and ivy-leaf *P. peltatum* — are tender perennials from southern Africa, not hardy *Geranium* cranesbills (for a related troubleshooting article, see [why geranium leaves go yellow](/posts/geraniums-yellow-leaves-en/)). They tolerate light frost briefly but die back permanently once the soil temperature drops below about −1 °C for any sustained period. In USDA zones 9 and warmer they can stay outside; in zones 8 and colder, an unprotected pelargonium left in a pot on the patio will be dead by January.

The good news: they are remarkably tough plants that overwinter successfully in three quite different ways, depending on how much light and indoor space you have:

1. **Bright windowsill** as a slow-growing houseplant. They keep most of their leaves and may even flower lightly through winter.
2. **Cool dormancy** in an unheated room, garage with a window, or stairwell. Plants drop most of their leaves but the woody stem stays alive.
3. **Bare-root storage** in a paper bag in a cool dark space. Plants lose all leaves and look dead, but the woody stem holds enough reserves to resprout strongly in spring.

Each route has very different watering, light, and temperature requirements. The biggest mistake new gardeners make is to bring a pelargonium indoors to a warm, dry, dim living room and water it like a normal houseplant — it then stretches into pale leggy growth, drops leaves from drought, and is unrecognisable by spring. Pick one of the three routes below and follow it consistently.

## How do you overwinter geraniums on a bright windowsill?

This is the most common method and the one that produces the earliest flowers next spring. It works well for anyone with a south- or west-facing windowsill in an unheated or lightly heated room.

**Bring them in.** Lift potted plants and dig garden plants 2–3 weeks *before* first frost. This gives plants time to acclimate while temperature swings are still gentle. Waiting until the night before frost almost guarantees stress and leaf drop.

**Inspect and cut back.**

1. Remove all yellowed and damaged leaves and any spent flowers.
2. Cut the plant back by half. This is not optional — it reduces transpiration losses, removes potential pest hotspots, and forces compact regrowth.
3. Check the underside of every remaining leaf for whitefly, [aphids](/posts/aphids-on-roses-organic-en/), and red spider mite. A 1 % insecticidal soap drench, including all leaf undersides and stems, is a useful insurance treatment as you bring the plant inside.

**Repot if needed.** If the rootball is rock-hard from a summer in a pot, refresh into the same-size pot with new free-draining compost (multi-purpose compost mixed 3:1 with horticultural grit). Do not pot up — pelargoniums flower better when slightly pot-bound.

**Position.** Brightest available windowsill. South- or west-facing window in cool-temperate climates is ideal. Keep the plant 5–10 cm away from cold glass on freezing nights — leaves touching glass at −5 °C suffer cold damage even though air temperature in the room is fine.

**Water.** This is where most people go wrong. Through the dark months, pelargoniums need only **a quarter** of their summer water. Wait until the top 3 cm of soil is bone dry and the plant looks slightly limp, then water moderately. Empty any saucer 30 minutes after watering — wet feet in winter cause stem-base rot that kills the plant outright.

**Feed.** None at all from late October to mid-February. From mid-February (when daylight is noticeably longer), restart half-strength tomato feed every fortnight to push spring growth.

**Expect.** Some leaf drop and yellowing through November–December as plants adjust. Possibly a few sparse winter flowers on a south-facing windowsill. Strong leaf flush as light returns from late February.

## What if you do not have a bright windowsill — can you store them dormant?

Yes — and arguably this method gives the best spring vigour because the plant rests fully rather than ticking over slowly all winter. You need a cool, dark or very dim, frost-free space: an unheated spare bedroom, a stair landing, an attached garage that does not freeze, or a basement with a small window. Target 5–8 °C, ideally not warmer than 10 °C.

**Cut back hard.** Reduce the plant to about 15 cm of bare woody stem. Remove all foliage. This sounds drastic but it is exactly how commercial growers winter their stock plants.

**Lift bare-root or keep in pot.** Either works. Bare-root: dig the plant, shake off most of the soil, and trim long roots back to about 10 cm.

**Storage option A — paper bags.** Wrap the bare-root stems individually in slightly damp newspaper (just damp enough that the paper does not crackle), then place each bundle in a brown paper bag with the plant's variety name written on it. Store in a cardboard box in a cool dark place at 5–8 °C. Check every 4 weeks: if a stem is beginning to wrinkle, mist it lightly; if any stem has white mould or feels mushy, discard it.

**Storage option B — pots in an unheated shed.** Cut back, water lightly so the soil is just barely damp, and stand pots together in a frost-free shed or garage. Cover the pots loosely with horticultural fleece. Water once a month with a small amount of water — just enough to stop the rootball drying to dust. Do not feed.

**Light.** A small amount of dim light is fine; complete darkness is also fine for paper-bag storage. The plant is conserving energy, not photosynthesising.

**Spring restart (late February to mid-March).**

1. Pot up bare-root plants into fresh compost. Water in. Place in a bright cool spot.
2. Cut back any further wrinkled stem tips to fresh green tissue.
3. Begin half-strength tomato feed every two weeks once new shoots are 5 cm long.
4. Move outside after the last hard frost in your area. Acclimate over 7–10 days (an hour the first day, two the next, etc.).

By early-to-mid May, dormancy-stored plants typically catch up with windowsill-overwintered ones in size and produce their first flowers about 2 weeks later — but with notably stronger, more compact growth.

## How do you take and root geranium cuttings as winter insurance?

Even if you successfully overwinter the parent, taking late-summer cuttings is the highest-leverage thing you can do as a hedge against losing a favourite cultivar. Pelargoniums root from cuttings extraordinarily easily — far easier than most flowering plants — and you can get half a dozen new plants from a single mature parent in 4 weeks.

**When.** Late August to mid-September gives the strongest spring plants. Cuttings taken in October still root but will be smaller next May.

**Take cuttings.**

1. Choose a non-flowering side shoot 8–10 cm long. Avoid hollow flowering stems and avoid floppy etiolated growth from a shaded plant.
2. Snip with a clean blade just below a leaf node.
3. Strip off the bottom two leaves and any flower buds.
4. Trim immediately below the lowest node.

**Root them.** No rooting hormone needed — pelargoniums root better without it. Insert 4 cuttings around the edge of a 9 cm pot of free-draining cuttings compost (multi-purpose mixed 1:1 with perlite or sharp sand). Water in once. Do *not* cover with a plastic bag — pelargonium cuttings rot in humid conditions; they prefer to be slightly dry.

**Place.** Bright but not direct sunlight, around 18–20 °C. A windowsill in a kitchen or bathroom is ideal because of the gentle warmth.

**Water.** Sparingly — keep the compost just barely damp. Once a week is usually plenty.

**Result.** Cuttings root in 3–5 weeks. New leaves at the tip are the visible sign roots have formed. Pot each rooted cutting into its own small pot and overwinter on the windowsill route above.

The same low-humidity, no-hormone, slightly-dry approach also works well for [propagating roses from cuttings](/posts/propagate-roses-cuttings-en/) and many semi-woody flowering plants.

## What spring routine brings overwintered geraniums into full flower?

Whichever overwintering route you used, the spring routine is what determines how heavily the plant flowers. Pelargoniums flower most reliably on plants that have been *cut back hard*, *fed steadily*, and *moved gradually outside* through April and May.

**Late February.**

- Cut back any windowsill plant to a tidy 20 cm woody framework.
- Repot bare-root stored plants into fresh compost.
- Begin half-strength tomato feed every 14 days.
- Place in the brightest available indoor spot. A south-facing windowsill or a cool conservatory is ideal.

**March–April.**

- Increase watering as light grows. Keep just-moist; never waterlogged.
- Pinch every new shoot tip when it reaches 8–10 cm to force branching. The same pinching principle also helps build bushy plants for many other flowering perennials and bedding plants.
- Move plants outdoors during warm sunny days and bring them back in at night. This hardens them off slowly.

**May.**

- After the last frost in your area, plant out into beds or pots permanently. South- or west-facing positions with 6+ hours of direct sun give the most flowers.
- Switch to full-strength tomato feed once a week through summer.
- Deadhead spent flower trusses by snapping the entire stem off at the base — pulling individual petals achieves nothing because the spent calyx is what the plant is trying to convert to seed.

**Expect.** First flowers from late April on early-cut-back windowsill plants; first flowers from mid-to-late May on dormancy-stored plants. Peak flowering June to first frost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I overwinter all my pelargoniums or just save cuttings?**
Both, ideally. Mature 2–3 year old plants often flower harder and earlier than fresh cuttings, but cuttings are the safest insurance against an overwintering disaster (a heating failure in the storage shed, an aphid infestation that kills the parent). Take 3–4 cuttings of any cultivar you really want to keep, regardless of which overwintering route you choose for the parent.

**Why are my overwintered geraniums leggy and pale by February?**
Almost always too warm and too dim. A pelargonium kept in a heated 21 °C living room with weak winter light stretches into pale lopsided growth — that is *etiolation*. Fix: move to the coolest bright room you have (an unheated bedroom is fine), and cut the plant back by half. Within 4 weeks new compact growth replaces the leggy stems.

**Can I leave my geraniums in their patio pots and just move them into the garage?**
Yes — this is option B of the dormancy method. The trick is the garage must stay above 0 °C all winter (even the coldest night) and you must water sparingly once a month. Pots that freeze solid kill the roots; pots that stay completely dry desiccate the woody stem. A cheap min/max thermometer in the garage is a useful insurance check.

**My geranium has lost all its leaves but the stem still feels firm — is it dead?**
Almost certainly not. Pelargoniums frequently shed all their leaves under stress (cold, transplant shock, low light) and look dead while still alive. Scratch a small section of bark with a thumbnail — green underneath means the plant is alive. Cut back to firm green tissue and treat as if it is in dormancy. Most plants resprout from the woody stem in 4–6 weeks.

**Is it worth overwintering scented-leaf geraniums the same way as zonal types?**
Yes. Scented-leaf pelargoniums (lemon, rose, peppermint) overwinter just as well as zonal types using any of the three routes. They tend to be hardier than zonals and tolerate the cold-bright-windowsill method especially well. They also make excellent kitchen-windowsill plants in winter — bruising a leaf releases the fragrance, which most zonals lack. For more on integrating scented-leaf pelargoniums with culinary uses, see the [scented herbs and edible flowers overview](/posts/edible-flowers-guide-en/).
