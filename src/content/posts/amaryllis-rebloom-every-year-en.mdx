To rebloom an amaryllis, let the plant **grow leaves all summer** in bright light with regular feeding, then **force dormancy in autumn** by cutting back water and storing the bulb in a cool, dark place (10–15 °C) for 8–10 weeks. Resume watering in late November or December and new flower stalks typically emerge within 4–6 weeks.
 
## Why do most people throw away amaryllis bulbs after flowering?
 
Amaryllis (*Hippeastrum*) bulbs are marketed as disposable holiday décor — buy a kit, watch it bloom for Christmas, discard in January. This is a waste. A healthy amaryllis bulb can rebloom reliably for **20–30 years** if you follow a simple annual cycle. The reason most people fail isn't that the plant is difficult — it's that they don't know the bulb needs a deliberate rest period to trigger new flower buds.
 
The cycle has three phases:
1. **Growth phase** (spring–summer): leaves photosynthesise and recharge the bulb
2. **Dormancy phase** (autumn): bulb rests in darkness, flower buds form internally
3. **Bloom phase** (winter): new stalks emerge and flower
 
Skip the dormancy step and the bulb will produce leaves indefinitely but never flower again. That's what confuses people — the plant looks alive but won't bloom.

## What should you do with an amaryllis after it finishes flowering?
 
This is the most critical window. What you do in the first few weeks after blooms fade determines whether the bulb will flower again next year.
 
**Step 1: Remove spent flowers, keep the stalk**
Snip off each flower as it wilts, but leave the green stalk standing. The stalk continues photosynthesising and feeding the bulb. Only cut it down once it turns yellow and dries out — typically 4–6 weeks after flowering.
 
**Step 2: Keep the leaves growing**
The strap-shaped leaves are the bulb's solar panels. They must stay green and active from late winter through the entire summer. This is where the energy for next year's flowers comes from.
 
- Place in the **brightest window** you have (south-facing ideal)
- Water when the top 2 cm of soil dries
- Feed every 2 weeks with a balanced liquid fertiliser (10-10-10 or similar)
- Move outdoors in late spring once night temperatures stay above 10 °C — full sun or bright shade
 
**Step 3: Resist the urge to repot**
Amaryllis actually bloom better when **slightly root-bound**. Only repot every 3–4 years or when the bulb is physically pushing out of the pot. When you do repot, choose a pot only 2–3 cm wider than the bulb diameter.
 
## How do you force an amaryllis into dormancy?
 
Dormancy is the secret to reblooming. Without it, the bulb never receives the signal to form new flower buds internally. Here's the process:
 
**Timing:** Start dormancy in **early to mid-September** for Christmas blooms, or adjust the calendar to target your preferred bloom window. Count back 12–14 weeks from when you want flowers.
 
**The dormancy process:**
 
1. **Stop watering completely** in early September. Move the pot to a cool, dry location.
2. **Stop fertilising.** The growth season is over.
3. **Let leaves yellow naturally.** As the soil dries, the leaves will turn yellow and wither over 2–3 weeks. This is normal — the bulb is reabsorbing nutrients from the dying foliage.
4. **Cut back dead foliage** once it's fully brown. Cut leaves to within 5 cm of the bulb top.
5. **Store the bulb** (in its pot or bare) in a **cool, dark place**: 10–15 °C (50–60 °F). A basement, unheated garage, or cool cupboard works. Darkness is important — light can interrupt dormancy.
6. **Wait 8–10 weeks.** Don't water. Don't check on it constantly. The bulb is alive but resting.
 
If you see a green shoot emerging from the top of the bulb before the 8 weeks are up, the bulb has decided it's ready. Move it back to light and resume watering — some vigorous bulbs need less rest.
 
## How do you restart an amaryllis after dormancy?
 
**Around late November or early December** (for holiday blooms):
 
1. **Move the pot to a warm, bright location** — a sunny windowsill at 20–24 °C is ideal.
2. **Water once, thoroughly**, until water drains from the bottom. Then wait — don't water again until you see new growth emerging (usually within 1–2 weeks).
3. **Resume regular watering** once a green shoot appears. Keep soil evenly moist but not waterlogged.
4. **Rotate the pot** a quarter turn every few days. Amaryllis stalks lean strongly toward light, and without rotation you'll get a dramatically lopsided plant.
5. **Do not fertilise yet** — wait until the flower stalk is 15–20 cm tall, then start feeding every 2 weeks.
 
New flower stalks typically reach blooming height (40–60 cm) within **4–6 weeks** of restarting. Some bulbs produce the flower stalk first, then leaves; others do both simultaneously. Either pattern is normal.
 
## How many times will an amaryllis bulb rebloom?
 
A well-maintained amaryllis bulb improves with age. Here's what to expect:
 
- **Year 1** (from a new bulb): 1–2 flower stalks, often pre-programmed by the grower
- **Years 2–3**: may produce 1 stalk as the bulb establishes in your conditions
- **Years 4+**: mature bulbs routinely produce **2–3 stalks** with 4–6 flowers each
- **Decades**: healthy bulbs can bloom for **20–30+ years** and produce offset bulbs (babies) that can be separated and grown into new flowering plants
 
The bulb diameter is a good indicator of bloom potential. Bulbs over **7 cm diameter** (measured across the widest point) have enough stored energy to flower. Smaller bulbs need another year of leaf growth before they'll bloom.
 
## What are the most common mistakes that prevent amaryllis from reblooming?
 
**1. Cutting off leaves too early**
The leaves must stay green all summer to recharge the bulb. Cutting them prematurely starves the bulb of energy for next year's flowers.
 
**2. Skipping dormancy**
No rest period = no flower-bud formation. The bulb needs those 8–10 weeks of cool, dark quiet.
 
**3. Not enough light during the growth phase**
A dimly lit windowsill won't cut it. Amaryllis leaves need strong light — ideally full sun outdoors during summer — to generate enough energy for flowering.
 
**4. Overwatering during dormancy**
The bulb should be completely dry during its rest. Even occasional watering can wake it prematurely or cause rot.
 
**5. Planting too deep**
The top third of the amaryllis bulb should sit **above the soil line**. Burying the entire bulb promotes rot and suppresses flowering.
 
## Frequently Asked Questions
 
**Can you leave an amaryllis in water instead of soil?**
 
Waxed and water-grown amaryllis are popular gifts, but they are essentially single-use. The bulb depletes its stored energy to produce flowers and cannot recharge without roots in soil. If you want to rebloom a water-grown amaryllis, pot it in soil after flowering, but expect it to take 1–2 extra years of leaf growth before it flowers again.
 
**Do amaryllis bulbs multiply?**
 
Yes. Mature amaryllis bulbs produce small offset bulbs (babies) around their base. Once offsets reach about 3–4 cm in diameter, you can separate them during repotting and grow them individually. They typically take 2–3 years to reach flowering size.
 
**Why did my amaryllis produce leaves but no flowers?**
 
The bulb didn't accumulate enough energy during the previous growth season, or it didn't get a proper dormancy period. Ensure bright light and regular feeding all summer, then enforce 8–10 weeks of cool, dark rest before restarting.
 
**When is the best time to buy amaryllis bulbs?**
 
October through December, when garden centres stock fresh Dutch-grown bulbs. Choose the largest bulbs available — bigger bulbs produce more flower stalks. Bulbs should feel firm and heavy for their size with no soft spots or mould.
