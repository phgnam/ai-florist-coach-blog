Lilies are among the most architecturally satisfying of all garden flowers. Their tall, straight stems, clearly defined buds, and large, open flowers have a structural quality that feels almost designed — as though a very deliberate hand chose exactly this angle for each bloom. They're also among the best cut flowers available, lasting 10–14 days in a vase with their buds opening successively over the full period.

Understanding the different types of lilies — and the specific care each requires — transforms what can seem like a complicated genus into a genuinely manageable and rewarding group of plants.

## The main types of lily

**Asiatic lilies** are the earliest-flowering lilies (typically June) and the most hardy. They produce upward-facing flowers without fragrance in an enormous range of colours from white through yellow, orange, pink, and deep burgundy-red. They're among the easiest lilies to grow and are the most common type found at supermarkets and garden centres as cut flowers. Because they face upward, they show beautifully in the garden but can collect water in their cups — in very rainy climates this can cause fungal issues.

**Oriental lilies** are the most fragrant lily group — a single open Oriental lily can perfume an entire room. They flower in mid-to-late summer (July–September) and produce large, reflexed (backwards-curving) petals in white, pink, red, and combinations thereof. The most famous Oriental lily is 'Stargazer' — a deep pink with white margins and an intense fragrance that has made it one of the most commercially successful lily varieties ever bred. Orientals require more warmth than Asiatics and are less tolerant of alkaline soils.

**Trumpet lilies** (*Lilium regale* and relatives) produce long, tubular flowers that flare at the tip like a trumpet. They're intensely fragrant — perhaps even more powerfully scented than Orientals — and flower in July. *Lilium regale*, the regal lily, discovered in a remote valley in Sichuan, China by plant hunter Ernest Henry Wilson in 1903, remains one of the most beautiful and widely grown lily species. Its white trumpets with pink reverses and yellow throats are extraordinary.

**LA hybrids** (Longiflorum-Asiatic crosses) combine the large flower size and fragrance of Oriental lilies with the hardiness and ease of Asiatics. They've become the dominant type in professional cut flower production — combining desirable traits from both parent groups.

**Tiger lilies** (*Lilium lancifolium*) are the familiar orange-spotted lilies of hedgerows and cottage gardens. They're extremely hardy, multiply readily (producing small black bulbils in the leaf axils), and flower in late July. They're not suitable for cutting (the pollen stains severely), but as garden plants they are almost indestructible.

## Planting lilies

**When to plant**: Lily bulbs can be planted in autumn (October–November) or spring (March–April). Autumn planting gives them more time to establish, but spring-planted bulbs from good nurseries flower perfectly in their first year.

**Depth**: Plant at **three times the bulb's height** — typically 15–20cm deep for a standard lily bulb. Lilies produce roots both from the bottom of the bulb and from the stem just above it (basal roots and stem roots) — planting deep enough allows both root systems to develop.

**Spacing**: 20–30cm apart depending on variety.

**Soil**: Most lilies prefer **well-drained, neutral to slightly acidic soil**. They are particularly intolerant of waterlogged conditions. In heavy clay soils, raise beds or add significant grit at and below planting depth. Oriental lilies specifically dislike alkaline soils — in chalky gardens, grow them in containers in ericaceous (acid) compost.

**Containers**: Lilies grow exceptionally well in containers, which allows you to control soil pH, move them to prominent positions when in bloom, and bring them under cover if the lily beetle (see below) is a particular problem in your area. Use a mix of 50% John Innes No.3 compost and 50% multipurpose compost, with added grit for drainage.

**Position**: Full sun to light shade. In hot climates, afternoon shade prevents the blooms from bleaching and extends their life.

## Feeding and aftercare

Lilies are hungry plants and benefit from regular feeding once growth begins in spring:

- Apply a balanced fertiliser as shoots emerge in spring
- Switch to a **high-potassium fertiliser** (tomato feed is ideal) once the buds form — this maximises flower size and stem strength
- Continue feeding every 2 weeks until flowering

**Deadheading**: Remove spent flowers promptly (preventing seed formation diverts energy away from the bulb), but **leave the stem and leaves intact until they die back naturally**. The foliage is photosynthesising and building up the bulb for next year's flowering. Cutting leaves early is one of the most common causes of poor performance in subsequent years.

## The lily beetle: the main pest

The **scarlet lily beetle** (*Lilioceris lilii*) is the primary pest of lilies in temperate gardens, particularly in the UK and Northern Europe. The adult beetle is a vivid scarlet red and unmistakable; the larvae are orange grubs that coat themselves in their own excrement as a disguise (and a deterrent to predators). Both adults and larvae consume lily foliage and flower buds, and a bad infestation can strip a lily plant completely.

**Control**:
- **Hand-picking** is effective but requires vigilance — check plants daily from April onwards. The adults fall and play dead when disturbed, landing on their black undersides and becoming almost invisible — cup your hand underneath before touching the plant.
- The larvae must be removed promptly before they cause serious damage
- **Neem oil** (organic) or **pyrethrin-based insecticides** applied early in the season can reduce populations
- **Biological control** with the parasitic wasp *Tetrastichus setifer* is available in some countries and provides longer-term reduction in beetle populations

## Lily pollen: a practical warning

The orange pollen of Oriental and trumpet lilies stains fabric **permanently** and clothing, curtains, and table linens are frequent casualties. The simple solution: use scissors to remove the anthers (the pollen-bearing tips of the stamens) from each flower as soon as it opens. This also extends the vase life of cut lilies, as a flower that has been pollinated begins to fade much faster than one that hasn't.

**Never touch lily pollen with bare hands** and then touch fabric. If pollen does land on fabric, resist the urge to rub it — let it dry completely and then lift it off gently with sticky tape. Water or rubbing sets the stain permanently.

A garden with well-grown lilies, opening through July and August in a succession of colours and fragrances, has a quality of theatrical punctuality — each bloom arriving exactly at the right moment, staying exactly the right length of time, and leaving its space to the next. It is a particularly satisfying kind of gardening.
