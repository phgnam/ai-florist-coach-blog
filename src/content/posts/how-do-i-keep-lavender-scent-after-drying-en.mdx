## Quick Answer

Pick lavender **as buds first start to open** in dry mid-morning weather, **hang in tight bunches** of 50–80 stems in a **dark, airy, room-temperature room** for 10–14 days, then **strip buds into airtight glass jars** stored away from light and heat. Properly dried lavender keeps strong scent for **2–3 years**; sloppy drying loses scent within months.

## When is the right moment to pick lavender?

Timing is the single most important factor for scent retention:

- **Best**: when bottom buds are just opening but most are still tightly closed (about 25% open)
- **Acceptable**: full bloom — but scent is already starting to volatilise
- **Too late**: most flowers fully open or starting to drop — dried product weak and sheds easily
- **Too early**: buds tight green — scent is undeveloped

The peak essential oil content occurs at this just-opening stage. Pick on a dry sunny morning **after the dew has dried** but **before midday heat**. Mid-morning (9–11 am) is ideal.

## How do I cut and bundle?

Cutting and bundling technique matters:

- **Cut whole stems** with sharp secateurs, taking the longest length possible
- **Strip lower leaves** to clean stems
- **Bundle 50–80 stems** per bunch — tight enough to keep airflow inside but not crushed
- **Tie firmly** with twine or a rubber band 2 cm from cut end
- **Re-tighten as bundles dry** — stems shrink, original bands loosen

A bundle too loose lets air flow through and dries unevenly. Too tight traps moisture and creates mould. The right size feels firm but not crushed when squeezed.

## Where should I hang them?

The drying environment must hit four targets:

- **Dark**: light bleaches colour and breaks down essential oils — a windowless cupboard, attic, or pantry
- **Airy**: gentle air movement prevents mould — not stagnant, not blowing
- **Cool to room temperature**: 15–22 °C ideal, never above 25 °C
- **Low humidity**: under 60% relative humidity

Good drying spots:

- **Garage** with ventilated window
- **Garden shed** away from direct sun
- **Spare bedroom** with curtains drawn
- **Airing cupboard** with door slightly open
- **Pantry** with good ventilation

Bad drying spots:

- **Kitchen** — humidity from cooking, smells transfer
- **Bathroom** — too humid
- **Sunny conservatory** — bleaches colour
- **Basement** — often too damp

Hang bundles upside down so essential oils flow into the buds, not out of them.

## How do I tell when lavender is fully dry?

Test for dryness before storage. Improperly dried lavender molds in the jar:

- **Stems snap cleanly** when bent — should not flex
- **Buds rub off easily** between fingers
- **Buds feel papery dry**, not soft or springy
- **Bunch makes a gentle rustle** when shaken

The drying time:

- **Hot dry summer**: 7–10 days
- **Cool damp weather**: 14–21 days
- **Humid coastal regions**: up to 4 weeks; consider a dehumidifier

If in doubt, leave another 2–3 days. Over-drying is rarely a problem; under-drying always is.

## Should I strip the buds or leave on stems?

Both have uses:

**Strip buds** — for sachets, potpourri, culinary lavender:

- **Hold a stem over a paper bag** or tray
- **Roll between fingers** or rub stems between palms
- **Buds fall off easily** if fully dried
- **Sieve out chaff** (tiny stem pieces) for clean buds
- **Yields 5–10 grams** of buds per stem cluster

**Keep on stems** — for visual displays, room scenting, gifts:

- **Trim stem ends evenly**
- **Re-tie bundles** tighter for finished look
- **Store flat or upright** in a closed box
- **Wave bundle gently** every 2 weeks to release scent into the room

For most home use, strip buds for storage; keep one or two whole bundles for display.

## How should I store dried lavender?

Storage decides whether scent lasts months or years:

- **Best container**: airtight glass jars or tins
- **Avoid**: paper bags (let scent escape), thin plastic (lets oxygen in)
- **Fill to 90%** — minimal air gap reduces oxidation
- **Label with year** of harvest
- **Store in a dark cool dry cupboard** — not above the cooker, not on a sunny shelf
- **Open occasionally** to refresh air briefly, then re-seal

A 500 ml glass jar holds about 60–80 grams of dried lavender buds — enough for 4–6 sachets or a year of small refresh uses.

## How long does the scent really last?

With proper drying and storage:

- **Year 1**: full strong scent
- **Year 2**: still strong, especially if jar opened sparingly
- **Year 3**: slightly faded but useable
- **Year 4+**: weak; refresh with essential oil drops

Compare with poor storage (open bowl on a sunny windowsill):

- **Month 1**: noticeable scent
- **Month 6**: faint
- **Year 1**: barely perceptible

The single biggest scent-retention factor is **light exposure** — even good airtight jars on a bright shelf fade within months.

## Can I refresh dried lavender that's lost scent?

Yes — two methods:

**Essential oil top-up**:

- 5–10 drops of pure lavender essential oil per 100 g of dried buds
- Mix gently in a closed jar
- Leave to absorb for 24 hours
- Buds smell strong again for several months

**Crush gently to release residual oil**:

- Rub a handful between palms
- Some retained essential oil escapes
- Brief revival, lasts a few hours
- Useful for sachet refresh

Refreshing only works on **buds**, not whole stems. Stems with no scent left are decorative only.

## What can I make with dried lavender?

The traditional uses, in order of difficulty:

- **Drawer sachets**: muslin bag with 30 g lavender, sewn shut
- **Pillow inserts**: 100 g in a small fabric pouch slipped inside a pillowcase
- **Lavender wands** (woven sticks of lavender): woven from fresh flexible stems while still soft
- **Potpourri**: combine with dried rose petals, citrus peel, cinnamon
- **Culinary lavender**: very small amounts in shortbread, ice cream, or syrup — only from organic plants
- **Bath sachets**: hang under hot tap for fragrant bath
- **Moth-deterring drawer bags**: 50 g in fabric bags between woollen clothes
- **Linen spray**: steeped in vodka with essential oil and water

The smaller the project, the longer your harvest lasts. A single shrub gives 200+ stems = enough for 30+ sachets per year.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Which lavender variety has the strongest scent?

**English lavender (*Lavandula angustifolia*)** — varieties like 'Hidcote' and 'Munstead' have the highest essential oil content and the cleanest "lavender" scent. **French (*L. stoechas*)** and **lavandin (*L. × intermedia*)** have a more camphoraceous note. For pure traditional scent, stick with English varieties.

### Should I dry lavender in a dehydrator or oven?

No — both produce too much heat too quickly, driving off essential oils. Air-drying at room temperature retains far more scent. The oven temptation comes from speed, but it costs scent.

### Can I dry lavender in a greenhouse?

No, usually. Greenhouses get too hot and too humid in summer — both bad for essential oil retention. A shaded shed or attic works much better.

### How long can I keep stems on display before storing?

Display whole bundles work for 2–3 months in a vase or on a shelf. After that, scent drops noticeably. Strip and store buds in airtight jars before scent fades, then make sachets as needed. See the [revive dusty dried flowers guide](/posts/how-do-you-revive-dusty-dried-flowers-en/) for cleaning displayed bunches.

### Can I dry from the supermarket lavender bunch?

Yes, but timing is uncertain — you don't know the exact picking time. Hang it the same way as garden-cut lavender as soon as you get it home. Quality varies; expect weaker scent than peak-picked garden lavender.

### Is dried lavender safe to use around pets?

Mostly yes for dogs; avoid for cats. Cats are particularly sensitive to lavender essential oil; even dried lavender contact can irritate them. For households with cats, keep sachets out of reach and avoid cat-area pillows.
