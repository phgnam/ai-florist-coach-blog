## Quick Answer

The four most common reasons a flower garden has no bees are: **most cultivars are sterile or double-flowered** (no nectar/pollen for bees), **bloom continuity is too narrow** (bees need flowers from March to October, not just June), **the garden is too tidy** (bees need bare soil and dead stems for nests), and **pesticide drift** from neighbouring gardens or farms. Roses bred for show, double dahlias, F1 marigolds, and most "patio bedding" are bee deserts. Single-flowered native species fix the problem fastest.

## Are your flowers actually attracting bees?

A surprising number of garden flowers are bred to look spectacular and produce no nectar or pollen. The pattern is consistent: **single flowers feed bees; double flowers don't.**

A "single" flower has a clear central bowl of stamens (male) and pistils (female) surrounded by a single ring of petals. Bees can land in the centre, work the flower in seconds, and move on. A "double" flower has the stamens converted into extra petal rows — visually dense, but functionally empty for a bee.

Common ornamentals that are essentially bee deserts:

- Most modern hybrid tea roses (the long-stemmed roses sold for cutting)
- Double dahlias and ball dahlias
- Double tulips
- Most patio petunias and pansies
- Pom-pom dahlia / pom-pom chrysanthemum
- F1 marigolds with stuffed flower heads

Common ornamentals that bees love:

- Single dahlias (look for the word "single" or "anemone-flowered" on the label)
- Cosmos (all are single-flowered)
- Open-flowered roses (Rosa rugosa, Rosa canina, single David Austin cultivars)
- Lavender (any variety)
- Echinacea, rudbeckia, helenium
- Foxglove, salvia, catmint
- Single zinnias
- Crocus, snowdrop, hellebore (early-season critical)

A garden that looks "perfect" with neat double dahlias and stuffed petunias is to a bee what a desert is to a thirsty hiker.

## Is your garden continuously in bloom?

A garden that explodes with colour in June and goes flat for the rest of the year is not a bee garden. Different bee species are active at different times, and they need food across the whole season:

**Early spring (March–April)**: queen bumblebees emerging from hibernation are starving. They need crocus, snowdrop, hellebore, willow catkins, lungwort, primrose, and early-flowering fruit (cherry, plum, blackthorn).

**Mid-spring (April–May)**: lots of forage available — apple blossom, dandelion, wild violet, comfrey.

**Early summer (May–June)**: the easy season — almost everything is flowering.

**Mid-summer (July)**: a quiet period in many gardens. Lavender, catmint, and salvia carry the load.

**Late summer (August–September)**: critical for the next generation of queens. Echinacea, helenium, sedum, ivy (yes, ivy in flower is a major nectar source), and asters.

**Autumn (October)**: late-flowering ivy and Michaelmas daisies are often the last forage before hibernation.

A garden with March, April, July, and October gaps will have far fewer bees than one with June bloom that runs from February to November. Plan for **continuity, not peak**.

## Is your garden too tidy?

Most garden bees in temperate Europe are **solitary species** — each female digs her own nest hole. The hole is usually in:

- Bare or sparsely vegetated soil (mining bees)
- South-facing dry banks
- Hollow plant stems left standing over winter (red mason bees, leafcutter bees)
- Old wood and dead branches (carpenter bees)

A garden that is mulched edge-to-edge with no bare soil, where every herbaceous stem is cut back to ground level in October, is a habitat desert for solitary bees regardless of how many flowers it has.

The fixes are easy and lazy:

- **Leave a few square metres of bare south-facing soil** somewhere out of the way
- **Cut back herbaceous perennials in February or March, not October** — leave the dead stems standing all winter
- **Drill 5–10 mm holes in a south-facing fence post** as instant solitary bee habitat
- **Tolerate a corner of "wild"** — long grass, nettles, dead wood

## Is there pesticide drift you don't know about?

Pesticides — especially neonicotinoids — are the third major reason for missing bees. You may garden organically and still have a contamination problem if:

- Your neighbour sprays roses or vegetables with systemic insecticides
- Your local council sprays street trees or roadside verges
- Your soil contains residue from previous garden owners
- The seedlings you bought from a garden centre were drenched in neonicotinoid before sale

The last is surprisingly common. Many "bee-friendly" lavender and salvia plants from large retailers are pre-treated with systemic neonicotinoid, which then translocates into the nectar and kills any bee that visits for several months.

The reliable workaround: buy plants from specialist nurseries or grow from seed yourself.

## Are you in a "bee desert" landscape?

If your garden is surrounded by lawn-only gardens, intensive farmland, or amenity grass with no flowering plants for hundreds of metres, the local bee population may simply be too low for any single garden to attract many. In a desert landscape, even a perfect bee garden may take 1–2 seasons to be discovered as bees slowly recolonise.

This is one place where a wildflower meadow strip — even a few square metres — has a disproportionate effect, because it offers density of bloom that pollen-strapped bees can find from much further away.

## What bees should I expect to see?

The two big groups:

**Bumblebees**: large, fuzzy, slow. Active from March (queens) through October. Most active in cool conditions. The buff-tailed and red-tailed bumblebees are the most common UK garden species. They tolerate light rain and cool mornings better than honey bees do.

**Solitary bees**: small, fast, often dark or metallic. There are roughly 250 solitary bee species in the UK alone, most under 1 cm long. They look like little flying ants more than bees. They are responsible for most fruit-tree pollination.

If you only ever see honey bees in your garden, that often means the wider bee community is depleted — honey bees are common because they are kept in hives, not because they are abundant in nature.

## FAQs

### How long does it take to attract bees to a new garden?

If your garden has the right plants and continuity, the first scout bees usually arrive within days. Building up a resident population of solitary bees that nest in your garden takes 1–3 seasons. Bumblebee numbers can change visibly within a single season as queens find your flowers.

### Is mowing the lawn bad for bees?

Frequent mowing removes dandelion, clover, and self-heal — three of the most important early forage plants in suburban gardens. "No mow May" or letting one section grow long all summer dramatically increases bee numbers in lawn-dominated gardens.

### Do hummingbird-style nectar feeders work for bees?

Not well. Most sugar-water feeders are designed for hummingbirds and don't suit bee mouthparts. Real flowers are far better. The exception is a shallow water dish with pebbles for landing, which bees do use, especially in hot weather.

### Why do bees ignore my buddleia (butterfly bush)?

Buddleia is a butterfly plant, not a bee plant — its nectar tubes are too long for most bees. Add lavender, salvia, or catmint nearby for bees, and keep the buddleia for butterflies.

### Should I plant non-native flowers if they attract bees?

Native plants tend to support more specialist bees, but some non-natives (lavender, catmint, sedum, echinacea) are excellent generalist forage. A 70% native, 30% non-native mix is a sensible target for most temperate gardens.

### Can pesticide-treated soil recover?

Yes, but slowly. Neonicotinoid residues in soil typically halve every 200–500 days. After 2–3 years of organic gardening with good compost additions, most residential soil tests within safe levels. Avoid bringing in topsoil of unknown origin to speed recovery.
