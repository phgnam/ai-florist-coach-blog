## Quick Answer

Cut *Verbena bonariensis* back hard in early spring, not in autumn. Wait until you can see new green shoots emerging from the base — usually March or April in temperate gardens — then cut every old stem to about 5–10 cm above the soil. Autumn pruning removes the natural winter protection that keeps the crown alive in cold, wet weather, and is the most common cause of "hardy" verbena dying over winter. Established clumps reward this single annual cut with stronger, more upright stems.

## Why is *Verbena bonariensis* so popular in modern borders?

*Verbena bonariensis* is a tall, slender, see-through perennial from South America that has become a fixture of modern naturalistic planting. Stems reach 1.5–2 m tall yet are so thin you can plant the species at the front of a border without hiding what is behind it.

Three traits make it a designer favourite:

**Vertical accent.** Long, almost leafless stems hold compact rosy-purple flower clusters at the very top, giving the plant a "purple cloud floating above the border" effect.

**Long flowering period.** It begins in early summer and continues into late autumn, often until the first hard frost. Few perennials match that range.

**Pollinator value.** The flat-topped clusters of tiny tube flowers are heavily worked by butterflies — small tortoiseshells, peacocks, painted ladies, red admirals — and by hoverflies and bumblebees. For more pollinator-friendly choices, see [Best flowers for attracting bees and butterflies](/posts/flowers-for-bees-and-butterflies-en/).

These traits also affect when and how it should be cut back. The plant is short-lived (often 3–5 years) but readily replaces itself by self-seeding, and the way you prune influences both the parent's longevity and the next generation.

## Why should I avoid cutting back *V. bonariensis* in autumn?

Tidy autumn pruning is the single most common cause of dead "hardy" verbena. The tall winter stems do important work for the plant.

**Insulation.** The dead stems trap fallen leaves and snow against the crown, raising soil temperatures by 2–3 °C in cold spells. The crown — the underground node from which next year's stems emerge — is the only living part that has to survive winter, and it cannot tolerate sudden, deep cold.

**Wind-shield.** Standing stems break up cold winds. Removing them exposes the bare crown to a desiccating winter wind that dries out fragile bud tissue.

**Drainage signal.** Erect dead stems lift water off the crown and shed it sideways, reducing the time the crown sits in saturated soil during winter rain.

**Bird food.** Goldfinches and similar small birds feed on the seed heads through winter, removing seed and reducing the next year's "verbena lawn" — which is useful if you want to control self-seeding.

A *Verbena bonariensis* cut back in November frequently fails to reappear in spring, especially in a wet winter or a cold garden. Leave the stems alone, even when they look tatty.

## How and when do I cut it back in spring?

The right timing is "as soon as you can see new growth, but no earlier".

In temperate climates, that usually means:

- **Early March** in mild, sheltered gardens (USDA 8–9, UK southern counties).
- **Late March to mid April** in colder gardens (USDA 6–7).
- **Late April to early May** in continental climates with prolonged winters.

Watch the base of the plant rather than the calendar. Pale grey-green new shoots appear in pairs at the soil line and start lengthening when daytime temperatures stay reliably above 8 °C. Cut once they are visible but before they reach 5 cm.

**The spring cut:**

1. **Identify the live shoots** at the base.
2. **Cut every old stem to about 5–10 cm** above the soil, just above any visible new growth.
3. **Use sharp secateurs or shears.** A clean cut prevents tearing the crown.
4. **Add the cut stems to the compost** — or shred them, since they take a year to decompose intact.
5. **Mulch around the crown** with 3–5 cm of compost or composted manure to feed the new flush.

That is the entire annual maintenance routine. Verbena does not need pinching, deadheading, or summer trimming.

## Should I deadhead *V. bonariensis* during the season?

Generally, no. The plant is essentially self-deadheading: each flower cluster fades, dries, and produces a small seed head that disappears into the broader purple cloud effect of the border. Trying to deadhead each stem individually is both impractical (each cluster is barely 2 cm across) and often counterproductive — you remove next year's seed source.

There are two cases where light deadheading helps:

**You want to prevent self-seeding.** Cut the entire spent flower cluster off as soon as the colour fades, before seed sets fully. Be aware this also reduces the food source for autumn finches.

**You want a slightly later, denser flush.** Cutting the top 30 cm off about a third of the stems in mid June (the "Chelsea chop" approach) produces shorter, bushier stems that flower a little later. Best done on at least 3-year-old clumps; first-year plants stay weedy if you do this.

## Will my *V. bonariensis* come back from a hard cut?

Yes — provided the crown is alive. The "hard cut" is essentially the same operation as the natural die-back of the stems, just performed by you rather than by frost.

After cutting, a healthy crown usually:

- Produces 5–8 strong stems by mid May.
- Reaches half its eventual height by mid June.
- Begins flowering by late June to early July.
- Continues to throw new flowering stems through August and September.

If no new shoots appear by mid May, scrape away mulch around the crown and check for visible bud tissue. A live crown is firm and pale; a dead crown is mushy and slips out of the soil with a gentle tug. If the original plant has died, look for self-seeded volunteers around the parent — there are almost always several within a metre or two, and these can replace the parent for free.

## Can I divide an established clump or take cuttings?

Both options work, although neither is essential. The plant short-lives whether or not you intervene, and self-seeding generally provides plenty of replacements.

**Division** (best in early spring, just as new shoots emerge):

1. Lift the entire clump with a fork.
2. Tease apart with two forks back-to-back, or cut with a sharp spade.
3. Replant the outer, more vigorous sections.
4. Discard the woody central core.

Each division should have at least 3 visible shoots. Water in well and shade for a few days.

**Softwood cuttings** (best in late spring):

1. Take 8 cm cuttings from non-flowering side shoots.
2. Strip the lower leaves, dip in rooting hormone, insert in damp gritty compost.
3. Keep humid under a clear bag or cloche.
4. Roots form in 3–4 weeks.

Cuttings root readily and let you preserve a particular form of the species — for example a particularly tall or compact specimen — without waiting for self-seeding.

## How do I prevent the clump dying after a wet winter?

In wet, cold climates the most common cause of loss is not pruning timing but waterlogged soil at the crown. Add the following to your protection routine:

- **Drainage.** Plant on a slight mound (5–10 cm raised) in heavy soils.
- **Grit at the planting hole.** Mix one part coarse grit to three parts soil to improve drainage.
- **Mulch.** Compost mulch over the crown in mid winter. Avoid soggy materials like grass clippings.
- **Avoid frost pockets.** Low spots collect cold air all winter.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is *Verbena bonariensis* a perennial or an annual?**
A short-lived herbaceous perennial. Individual plants typically last 3–5 years, but the species perpetuates itself indefinitely by self-seeding, so an established colony can persist in the same spot for decades.

**Will it self-seed too much in my garden?**
It can. In sunny well-drained borders, *V. bonariensis* seeds itself enthusiastically. Hoe out unwanted seedlings while they are still under 5 cm tall — they pull easily at that size and resent transplanting once larger.

**My verbena did not appear at all this spring. Is it dead, or just late?**
Probably late. Verbena is one of the latest perennials to break in spring; do not give up before the end of May in temperate climates. Scratch the soil around the crown gently — if you find pale, firm, pinkish bud tissue, the plant is alive and slow.

**Should I dig up self-seeded verbena seedlings and replant them?**
Verbena seedlings transplant tolerably well at the 4–6 leaf stage but resent disturbance later. Move them on a cool overcast day, with a generous root ball, and water in well.

**Are *V. bonariensis* flowers safe for pets?**
Verbena flowers are generally regarded as low-toxicity and the plant is not on most pet-poisoning lists, but no plant is risk-free in quantity. Keep arrangements out of reach of pets that chew.
