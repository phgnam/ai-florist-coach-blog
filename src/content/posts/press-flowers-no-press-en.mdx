## Quick Answer

You don't need a flower press. The three reliable household methods are: a **stack of heavy books with absorbent paper** (best results, 2–4 weeks), the **microwave** with paper-towel layers (90 seconds to 3 minutes, slightly less crisp), and a **dry iron on low heat** (10 minutes total, fastest). Thin flat flowers like pansies, violas and cosmos press best.

## What does "pressing" actually do to a flower?

Pressing a flower preserves it by **removing water as quickly as possible while holding the shape flat**. Two things have to happen at the same time:

1. **Moisture has to leave the petals and leaves.** Water inside the cells is what causes fresh flowers to wilt and brown; if it isn't removed, the colour rots out within a week.
2. **The flower has to be held flat while it dries.** A flower that dries in three dimensions becomes a brittle dried specimen, not a pressed one. The pressure prevents the petals from curling as they shrink.

Every pressing technique is some combination of (a) absorbent material to wick moisture away and (b) something heavy or flat to hold the shape. A purpose-built flower press is just plywood, blotting paper, and screws. Substitute parts and you have the same machine.

## Method 1 — Heavy books and absorbent paper

This is the highest-quality method for home pressing.

**You need:**
- A heavy hardback book (an old phone book, dictionary, or atlas — at least 5cm thick when closed)
- Two sheets of absorbent paper per flower: blotting paper, parchment paper, plain printer paper, or coffee filters. **Do not use kitchen roll** — the embossed pattern will print into the petals as they dry.
- 2–4 more heavy books to weight the top.

**Method:**
1. Lay the flower face-down on one sheet of absorbent paper. Spread petals into the position you want them to dry in — once you close the book they don't move.
2. Place the second sheet of absorbent paper over the top.
3. Close the book. Stack 2–4 more heavy books on top.
4. Wait 2 weeks for thin flowers (pansies, violas, ferns, leaves), 3–4 weeks for thicker flowers (small roses, daisies, larkspur).
5. Check at week 2: the flower should feel papery dry. If any soft spot remains, replace the absorbent paper and continue pressing.

The only common failure: not changing the paper. After 4–7 days, swap the paper for fresh sheets. Wet paper holds water against the petals and produces brown spots.

## Method 2 — Microwave (the fast method)

The microwave method dehydrates flowers in minutes rather than weeks. The trade-off is slightly less crisp colour and a shorter shelf life — pressed flowers from the microwave fade faster than book-pressed flowers.

**You need:**
- Two ceramic tiles, two flat hardback books, or a microwave-safe flower-press kit
- Absorbent paper (printer paper or coffee filters)
- A microwave
- Optional: a small weight (mug, jar) on top

**Method:**
1. Sandwich the flower between two pieces of absorbent paper.
2. Place the sandwich between two ceramic tiles or two flat books that fit in your microwave.
3. Microwave on **medium-low** (around 30% power) in **30-second bursts**, checking between each burst.
4. Total cooking time: typically 90 seconds to 3 minutes for a single thin flower; longer for thicker blooms.
5. Stop heating as soon as the flower feels papery. Overheating browns the petals and damages the colour.

This method is fastest for craft projects with a deadline. Use book-pressing for archival keepsakes and the microwave for "I need this for a card by Saturday."

## Method 3 — Iron (the express method)

The fastest of all, with the worst colour retention. Use it for flowers that don't matter much, or for last-minute crafts.

**You need:**
- A standard household iron, set to **low or medium** with **steam OFF**
- Absorbent paper (parchment paper works best because it doesn't stick)
- A flat hard surface (ironing board fine)

**Method:**
1. Place the flower between two sheets of parchment or absorbent paper.
2. Press the iron down (don't slide it) for 10–15 seconds.
3. Lift, pause 10 seconds, press again.
4. Repeat for around 10 minutes total. The flower is done when it feels stiff and dry.

The iron is the worst method for colour, but the fastest for emergencies.

## Which flowers press best?

Pressability depends on **petal thickness** and **water content**:

**Excellent for pressing** (thin, flat, low water):
- Pansy, viola
- Cosmos
- Larkspur, delphinium florets (individual)
- Daisy, fleabane
- Forget-me-not
- Most ferns and leaves
- Lobelia
- Verbena

**Acceptable but harder** (thicker, often need extra time and paper changes):
- Small roses (cut in half lengthwise first)
- Pansy daisies
- Small zinnias
- Cornflower

**Difficult to impossible** (succulent, bulbous, or three-dimensional):
- Tulip — too watery, browns badly
- Peony — too dense, holds water in the centre
- Lily — too three-dimensional and too watery
- Hyacinth — too succulent
- Rose (large heads) — middle won't dry; cut in half and press as a profile

For three-dimensional flowers like roses and peonies, an alternative method is **silica gel drying** — burying the flower in silica beads to preserve the 3D shape. That's a different craft and beyond the scope of this guide.

## How do I mount and display pressed flowers once they're dry?

Three durable display methods:

**Heavyweight cardstock with PVA glue.** Apply a small dot of glue to the back of the flower and press onto the card. Let dry under a book overnight. Best for cards, bookmarks, and framed art.

**Sealed in laminating pouches.** Run the flower through a desktop laminator. Produces durable bookmarks, place cards, and educational specimens. The sealed plastic prevents fading from light and humidity.

**Glass-on-glass framing.** The most durable display. Sandwich the pressed flower between two pieces of glass with paper edges sealed with framing tape. Hang in indirect light only — direct sun will fade pressed flowers within months.

Pressed flowers fade. Even archival book-pressed specimens lose colour over 5–10 years. Display them in indirect light, away from heating vents, and accept that fade is part of the medium.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do pressed flowers last?**
Book-pressed flowers, kept in a dark album away from sunlight, retain colour for 5–10 years and recognisable shape for decades. Microwave or iron-pressed flowers fade noticeably within 1–3 years. Direct sunlight fades any pressed flower within months.

**Why are my pressed flowers turning brown?**
Two causes: **the paper wasn't absorbent enough** (kitchen roll, glossy paper, or wax paper trap moisture against the petals), or **paper wasn't replaced** during long press cycles. Browning is the colour of trapped moisture starting to rot.

**Can you press flowers in a phone book?**
Yes — phone books were the original household flower press. The thin pages absorb moisture well and the book is heavy enough to flatten gently. Stack additional books on top for extra pressure.

**Do flowers press better fresh or wilted?**
Fresh, just-opened flowers press best. The colour is brightest and the petal structure is at its peak. Pick flowers in mid-morning after the dew has dried but before the heat of the day, and press them within 1–2 hours of picking.

**Can I press flowers with a heavy weight instead of a book?**
Yes — any flat heavy surface works. Old vinyl records, encyclopaedias, kitchen tiles, or plywood with bricks on top all replicate the function of a press. The two requirements are even pressure and absorbent layers.

**What's the best thickness of paper to use?**
Plain blotting paper is ideal. After that, parchment paper, plain printer paper, coffee filters, and watercolour paper all work. Avoid: kitchen roll (embosses petals), paper towel (same), wax paper (doesn't absorb), and glossy paper (doesn't absorb).
