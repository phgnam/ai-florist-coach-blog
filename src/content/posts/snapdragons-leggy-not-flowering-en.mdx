## Quick Answer

Snapdragons go leggy when they are grown without pinching, planted in too much shade, or fed too much nitrogen — all three push tall, soft stems with few flower buds. Pinch the central growing tip when seedlings reach 10–15 cm to force three to five branches, give them at least six hours of direct sun, switch to a low-nitrogen tomato feed, and deadhead spent spikes promptly to keep new flowers coming.

## Why do snapdragons go leggy in the first place?

A leggy snapdragon is one with a single tall, weak central stem, sparse foliage, and only a thin spike of flowers at the very top — instead of the bushy, multi-stem plant covered in fat flower spikes you see in seed-catalogue photos. Three causes account for almost every leggy snapdragon in a home garden:

- **No pinching at the seedling stage.** *[Snapdragon](/learn/snapdragon/)* (Antirrhinum majus) is genetically a single-stem plant unless physically forced to branch. Without an early pinch, it pours all its energy into one main stem, racing for height to set seed.
- **Insufficient direct sun.** Snapdragons need six or more hours of full sun. In partial shade they stretch (a phenomenon called etiolation), producing long internodes between leaves and a thin, floppy stem. The same packet sown in full sun produces sturdy 30–45 cm plants while one in dappled shade easily reaches 60 cm and falls over.
- **Too much nitrogen.** Standard balanced fertilisers (like 20-20-20) push leafy growth at the expense of flowers. A nitrogen-heavy feed in the first month of growth almost guarantees a tall, soft plant with delayed buds.

A fourth, less common cause is **planting from late, root-bound nursery plugs.** Snapdragons that sat in a 6-pack at the garden centre for an extra two weeks have already started flowering on a single stem, and once that apical bud has opened, pinching no longer triggers a full bushy response.

## When and how do you pinch snapdragons correctly?

Pinching is the single highest-leverage thing you can do to a snapdragon. Done correctly, one pinch turns a one-spike plant into a four-to-six-spike plant for the entire season.

**Timing.** Pinch when the seedling has 4–6 true leaves and is 10–15 cm tall — typically four to six weeks after sowing, before any flower bud is visible at the top. If you wait until you can see a bud forming, you have already missed the optimal window.

**Method.**

1. Identify the central growing tip — the cluster of small new leaves at the very top of the main stem.
2. Pinch it off with your fingernails or snip with clean scissors, taking the top 2–3 cm down to the next pair of healthy leaves.
3. Within 7–10 days, the two leaf axils just below the pinch will push out side shoots. Within three weeks you should see four to six branching stems instead of one.

**Should you pinch a second time?** For most garden snapdragons, one pinch is enough. For tall cut-flower varieties (Rocket, Madame Butterfly, Chantilly series), some growers do a second light pinch on each side branch when those reach 15 cm, producing an even bushier plant — at the cost of delaying flowering by 10–14 days.

**Already too tall and leggy?** It is not too late. Cut the entire plant back by half, even down to 15 cm if the lower stem is thick enough to have leaf nodes. The plant will look brutal for a week, then push fresh side branches and bloom three to four weeks later — usually right when ungroomed snapdragons are running out of steam in mid-summer.

## What feeding and watering schedule keeps snapdragons compact and flowering?

Once pinched, snapdragons need a feeding and watering routine that favours flower production over leafy stretch.

**Feeding.** Switch from any high-nitrogen starter fertiliser to a high-potassium, lower-nitrogen feed (such as a tomato feed at 1:1 dilution of label rate, or a 5-15-15 granular at half-strength) once branching begins. Feed every 10–14 days during active flowering. Stop feeding for two weeks if you see soft, pale, oversized leaves — that is nitrogen excess and the plant will rebound to compact growth.

**Watering.** Snapdragons prefer evenly moist, well-drained soil. Water deeply once or twice a week rather than shallow daily sips — shallow watering encourages weak surface roots and floppy stems. In pots, never let the rootball dry out completely; in beds, mulch with 3–5 cm of compost or fine bark to even out moisture.

**Avoid overhead watering** in the evening — wet foliage at night encourages *rust*, the most common snapdragon disease, which appears as orange-brown pustules on the underside of leaves. If rust appears, remove affected leaves and switch to drip or soil-level watering immediately.

## How do you keep snapdragons blooming all season?

Snapdragons bloom in two main flushes if you let them — one big one in late spring and a smaller one in autumn, with a gap when summer heat above 28 °C makes them sulk. With deadheading and a mid-summer cut-back, you can compress that gap and stretch the plant from late April to first hard frost.

- **Deadhead obsessively.** As soon as the lower flowers on a spike fade and the top still has fresh blooms, the plant is already deciding whether to set seed or push another flush. Snip the spent spike off at the next side branch. Leaving even one spike to set seed switches the entire plant into seed-production mode and stops new flowers within a fortnight. The same principle applies broadly to most cut flowers — see [How to deadhead flowers correctly](/posts/how-to-deadhead-flowers-en/) for the technique.
- **Mid-summer cut-back.** When mid-July heat causes flower spikes to shorten and pale, cut every stem back by one-third to one-half. The plant looks rough for 10 days, then rebuilds for a strong autumn flush right through to frost.
- **Cool roots.** Mulch helps. So does interplanting with shorter shade-cast plants like sweet alyssum or low-growing *[zinnias](/learn/zinnia/)* whose foliage shades the soil.
- **Final-frost gamble.** Snapdragons are remarkably hardy — *Antirrhinum* tolerates light frost down to about −4 °C and even occasionally overwinters as a short-lived perennial in zone 7 and warmer. Don't pull plants out at first frost; cover with fleece for unexpected late autumn warmth and you may get bonus blooms in November.

If your plant has stopped flowering despite all of the above, the issue is usually that pinching never happened and the apex flowered out — see the **What if my snapdragon already flowered on a single stem?** FAQ below for the rescue plan. A similar dynamic affects [sweet peas that won't flower](/posts/sweet-peas-not-flowering-en/) when their growing tips aren't pinched early.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I pinch snapdragon seedlings I bought already in flower?**
The textbook answer is yes, but you will sacrifice the open flowers on that central spike. Pinch the central spike down to the next set of healthy leaves; expect a 2–3 week pause before the side branches catch up and bloom. Many gardeners hate doing this and instead let the first spike finish, then cut it off and pinch the secondary buds. This delays the bushy form by another 2–3 weeks but feels less wasteful.

**How tall should snapdragons get if grown well?**
It depends entirely on the series. Floral Showers and Twinny dwarf snapdragons reach 20–25 cm. Mid-height border types like Sonnet stop at 50–60 cm. Tall cut-flower varieties like Rocket, Madame Butterfly, and Chantilly reach 90–110 cm and need staking or netting. Leggy is not the same as tall — leggy means the stem is *thin and floppy at the height it reached*, regardless of variety.

**What if my snapdragon already flowered on a single stem and the rest of the plant is bare?**
Cut the entire plant back hard — to about 15 cm or to the lowest pair of healthy leaves on the main stem. Water well and feed lightly with a tomato feed. Side shoots emerge from the leaf axils within 10–14 days, and the plant typically returns to full flower within four weeks. This rescue works as long as the lower stem still has live, green leaves.

**Are snapdragons annuals or perennials?**
Officially tender perennials (USDA zones 7–11 they often overwinter), but in practice most gardeners grow them as cool-season annuals. They bloom best when spring nights are still under 15 °C and decline quickly above 28 °C. In mild-winter areas, autumn-sown plants overwinter and put on a spectacular spring flush before summer heat takes them out.

**Why are my snapdragon flower spikes short this year compared to last year?**
The two most common reasons are insufficient sun (a tree leafed out and now shades the bed, or a neighbour's fence is taller this year) and old, depleted soil. Refresh beds with 5 cm of compost in autumn, switch any plants that now sit in less than six hours of sun, and rotate snapdragons to a different bed every two to three years to avoid the [build-up of soil-borne fungal diseases](/posts/zinnias-powdery-mildew-en/) that thin out spikes.

**Can I grow snapdragons in containers?**
Yes — short and mid-height varieties (Floral Showers, Sonnet) thrive in 25 cm pots. Use a free-draining potting mix, never let the pot dry out fully, and feed every 10 days with a tomato feed once branching begins. Tall varieties work in pots only if you can stake them; otherwise wind will snap the stems.
