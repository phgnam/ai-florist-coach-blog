## Quick Answer

Dahlia tubers rot in winter storage when they are lifted too late, packed before their cuts have healed, stored too warm and humid, or attacked by fungus on undetected wounds. To save them, dig after the first light frost, cure for one to two weeks, dust cuts with sulphur, and pack in dry vermiculite at 5–7°C.

## Why do dahlia tubers rot in storage?

Almost every storage failure traces back to one of four causes — and they are usually preventable, not bad luck.

**Moisture is the single biggest killer.** Tubers stored damp will sprout mould within weeks. Even tubers that look dry on the surface can hold water inside the neck (the join between tuber and old stem), where rot starts invisibly and spreads outward.

**Wounds that have not healed.** Every cut from your fork or knife is an open door for fungal spores. If you pack tubers before the cut surfaces have callused over, *Botrytis* and *Fusarium* walk straight in.

**Storage temperature too high.** Above about 10°C tubers wake up early, drawing on their own moisture, and any latent fungus on them speeds up. Below freezing they collapse outright. The narrow safe band is roughly 4–10°C.

**Hidden damage from the lift.** Crowns crushed by a careless spade often look fine in October and turn black by January. Inspect every tuber under good light before it goes into the storage box.

## When should you lift dahlia tubers for storage?

Timing matters more than most beginner guides admit. Lift too early and the tubers are immature and watery. Lift too late and they will be sitting in cold, wet soil where rot is already starting.

The classic cue is the first **light** frost — not a hard freeze. Once frost has blackened the foliage, cut the stems back to about 10 cm and **wait 7–14 days before digging**. That fortnight in the ground lets the eyes (next year's growth points) finish forming on the crown, which is what you actually need to keep alive.

Dig with a fork, starting 25–30 cm out from the stem so you slide *under* the tuber clump rather than spearing it. Lift the whole clump, shake off loose soil, and set it upside-down in a sheltered spot for a couple of hours so any water trapped in the hollow stem can drain out.

## How do you cure dahlia tubers before storing them?

Curing is the step most home gardeners skip — and it is the single biggest reason their tubers fail. The point of curing is to dry the skin and seal any cuts so fungus cannot enter during storage.

1. **Rinse, do not scrub.** A gentle hose-off removes mud without bruising the skin. Aggressive brushing damages the thin protective layer that you are about to depend on for five months.
2. **Dry the clumps for 24–48 hours** in a frost-free, well-ventilated space — a garage, shed, or covered porch is ideal. The neck of each tuber must be bone-dry to the touch before you continue.
3. **Divide now, or in spring — your choice, but be consistent.** Autumn division means the cuts have all winter to heal in dry storage; spring division keeps clumps intact but is harder because the crown has hardened.
4. **Dust every cut surface with elemental sulphur or cinnamon.** Both are mild fungicides that pose no risk to the tuber. Skip this step at your peril.
5. **Cure the divided tubers for a further 7–14 days** on a dry rack in the same cool space. The cut surfaces should look papery and dull before they go into the box.

## What is the best way to pack dahlia tubers for winter?

The packing medium has one job: hold the tubers in a humidity sweet spot — dry enough to suppress mould, moist enough to stop the tubers from shrivelling.

**Vermiculite (medium grade)** is the gold standard. It buffers humidity beautifully and is reusable for years. Use it slightly damp to the touch, not wet — squeezing a handful should not produce a single drop of water.

**Dry peat-free coir or wood shavings** are good alternatives if vermiculite is hard to find. Avoid sawdust, which packs too tight and stays damp.

**Cardboard boxes or open milk crates** breathe better than sealed plastic tubs. If you must use plastic, drill ventilation holes in the lid and sides.

Lay tubers in a single layer, cover with 3–4 cm of medium, then add the next layer. **Label every variety** with a permanent marker on the tuber itself — paper tags fade or fall off by March.

Store the box at **5–7°C in the dark**, ideally in an unheated garage, frost-free shed, or cool basement. A maximum/minimum thermometer on the box for the first month tells you whether the spot is actually as steady as you think.

## How often should you check stored dahlia tubers?

Once a month is the right cadence — often enough to catch a problem early, infrequent enough not to disturb tubers that are doing fine.

Lift the lid, sniff first (a sour or musty smell means rot somewhere), and look for soft, weeping, or blackened tubers. Pull any offender out immediately along with the medium directly touching it; one rotting tuber will infect its neighbours within weeks.

If tubers look **shrivelled**, the medium is too dry — mist it lightly with a spray bottle and re-cover. If you see **white mould or condensation**, the medium is too wet — tip everything out, let it dry on newspaper for 24 hours, and repack. By February most growers have done at least one corrective intervention; that is normal.

## Can you save tubers that have already started rotting?

Sometimes — if you catch it early and act decisively.

Cut away **every soft, brown, or weeping section** with a clean, sharp knife, going at least 1 cm into healthy white flesh. Sterilise the blade between cuts with rubbing alcohol so you do not spread the infection. Discard any tuber where the rot has reached the crown (the central stem-and-eye area); without an intact crown the tuber cannot sprout, no matter how much firm flesh remains below.

Re-dust the new cuts with sulphur, let them air-dry for 48 hours, and repack them in fresh, dry medium — never the same medium the rot came from. A tuber that has lost half its body but kept its crown can absolutely produce a full plant next summer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can you store dahlia tubers in the fridge?**
Yes, but only as a last resort. Use the crisper drawer, wrap each tuber loosely in lightly damp newspaper inside a perforated plastic bag, and keep them away from fruit — ethylene gas from apples and bananas will trigger early sprouting. Most fridges run cooler than the ideal 5–7°C, so check tubers monthly for shrivelling.

**Should you wash dahlia tubers before storage?**
Rinse them gently with a hose to remove loose soil, but do not scrub or soak. Soil left clinging is fine; what you must avoid is damp, hidden moisture in the crown. After rinsing, air-dry the clumps for at least 24 hours before any further handling.

**How long can dahlia tubers stay in storage?**
A healthy, well-cured tuber stored at 5–7°C in slightly damp vermiculite will keep for 5–6 months — easily long enough to bridge an October lift in temperate zones to an April or May replant. Beyond about seven months viability drops sharply, even when conditions look perfect.

**Why are my dahlia tubers shrivelled but not rotting?**
Shrivelling means the storage medium is too dry, the temperature is too warm, or both. Mist the medium lightly with water — never soak it — and move the box to a cooler spot if it is above 10°C. Mildly shrivelled tubers usually plump up and grow normally if you replant them in moist soil in spring.

**Do dahlia tubers need light during storage?**
No — store them in complete darkness. Light, especially combined with warmth, signals the eyes to break dormancy early, producing weak, etiolated sprouts that exhaust the tuber before planting time. A closed box in an unheated garage, shed, or cellar is ideal.
