## Quick Answer

In the Northern Hemisphere, **spring (Mar–May)** brings tulips, peonies, ranunculus, and lilac; **summer (Jun–Aug)** is dominated by roses, dahlias, sunflowers, and hydrangea; **autumn (Sep–Nov)** centres on chrysanthemum, dahlia, and amaranth; and **winter (Dec–Feb)** leans on amaryllis, anemone, hellebore, and forced narcissus. In-season flowers are cheaper, last longer, and look better.

## Why does flower seasonality still matter in a global supply chain?

A modern wholesaler can fly a Kenyan rose, a Dutch peony, and a Colombian carnation onto the same auction floor in 36 hours. From a buyer's perspective it can look as if every flower is available all year. In practice, the seasons still drive almost every decision worth making about a bouquet.

In-season flowers are dramatically cheaper. A peony in June costs roughly a third of the same stem in November, when supply has switched to refrigerated storage out of New Zealand or Chile. They also last longer in the vase, because they were harvested closer to home and spent less time in transit. And, less obviously, they *look* right — a bouquet of tulips in October reads as wrong to most people without their being able to name why. Seasonality is partly a calendar and partly a visual grammar.

The data we use below tracks the **Northern Hemisphere temperate calendar** (Europe, North America, much of Asia). Southern Hemisphere readers can shift everything by six months — January in Sydney behaves like July in London. Tropical climates have their own logic that this guide handles separately at the end.

The lists below are the cut flowers a temperate-climate florist genuinely expects to see at the wholesale market in each month. They are not exhaustive — exotics, imports, and forced stems can stretch most of these well outside their natural windows — but they are the flowers a sane buyer plans around.

**January** — Amaryllis, anemone, hellebore, hyacinth (forced), narcissus (paperwhites and forced), ranunculus (early), tulip (forced French and Dutch). Supplemented by evergreens: pine, eucalyptus, ivy, holly.

**February** — Anemone, mimosa (acacia), narcissus, ranunculus, tulip, hyacinth, hellebore, early daffodil. Mimosa is the surprise of February — fragrant, sunny yellow, available for roughly four weeks and gone.

**March** — Anemone, daffodil, fritillary, hellebore, hyacinth, lilac (forced), magnolia branches, ranunculus, tulip in full swing. The first English-grown stocks and sweet peas appear at the end of the month.

**April** — Cherry blossom, daffodil, fritillary, hyacinth, lilac, lily-of-the-valley, ranunculus, snowball viburnum, sweet pea, tulip, wallflower. April is the most generous month of the year for a UK or European florist — almost everything spring-flowering overlaps for two to three weeks.

**May** — Allium, columbine, foxglove, lilac, lily-of-the-valley, peony (early Sarah Bernhardt and Coral Charm), ranunculus, rose (first English garden roses), sweet pea, tulip (late, doubles). May is when peonies arrive and the wedding calendar visibly shifts.

**June** — Astrantia, cornflower, delphinium, English roses, foxglove, larkspur, peony (peak), poppy, scabious, snapdragon, sweet pea, sweet william. The peony window in any given country lasts roughly three weeks; June is its centre.

**July** — Cosmos, dahlia (early), delphinium, lavender, lisianthus, phlox, rose, snapdragon, statice, stock, sunflower (early), zinnia. The colour palette shifts from pastels to saturated warm tones almost overnight.

**August** — Aster, dahlia (peak), echinacea, helenium, hydrangea, lisianthus, rudbeckia, scabious, sunflower (peak), zinnia. August is a florist's "messy" month — abundance, slightly chaotic shapes, hot colours.

**September** — Amaranthus, aster, dahlia, hydrangea (mophead and lacecap going antique), Japanese anemone, rudbeckia, sedum, sunflower, zinnia. Foliage starts to do the heavy lifting.

**October** — Amaranth, aster, chrysanthemum (early), cosmos, dahlia (late), hydrangea (antique tones), Japanese anemone, sedum, ornamental kale, rosehips, hawthorn. The first chrysanthemums and the last dahlias coexist for about a fortnight; pair them.

**November** — Chrysanthemum (peak), berries (rosehip, hawthorn, snowberry), Japanese anemone, ornamental kale, evergreen foliage, viburnum tinus, late dahlia where the frost has held off. The single most important month of the year for chrysanthemum, especially across East Asia.

**December** — Amaryllis, anemone (forced), bouvardia, cyclamen (potted), hellebore, paperwhite narcissus, ranunculus (forced), tulip (forced). Plus the holiday botanical canon: pine, fir, holly, mistletoe, eucalyptus, hypericum berries.

## Which flowers are in season for weddings?

Wedding flowers are where seasonality has the largest financial impact, because the volume per booking is high and the substitutions are visible. A florist who plans the calendar around the seasons saves the couple money and almost always produces a better-looking bouquet.

**Spring weddings (Mar–May)** are built around tulips, ranunculus, anemone, sweet pea, lilac, lily-of-the-valley, and (from late May) the first peonies. Palette: soft pastels, white-and-green, dusty pink. Avoid: dahlias, sunflowers, chrysanthemums — they will look out of place and will be expensive.

**Summer weddings (Jun–Aug)** are the easiest to source. Roses (especially English garden roses), peonies (early June only), delphinium, lisianthus, scabious, sweet pea (early summer), dahlia (late summer), hydrangea, and an enormous range of foliage. Palette: anything goes. The only risk is heat — many summer flowers wilt in hot weather and need refrigerated transport on the day.

**Autumn weddings (Sep–Nov)** centre on dahlia, chrysanthemum, amaranth, hydrangea (going antique), rosehips, and rich foliage. Palette: burgundy, copper, cream, bronze, deep purple. Couples who book in autumn often save 20–30% on flower spend versus the same bouquet in June.

**Winter weddings (Dec–Feb)** are the trickiest but produce some of the most beautiful results when the constraint is leaned into. Amaryllis, anemone, hellebore, forced ranunculus, evergreens, berries, and dried elements (pampas, dried hydrangea, preserved foliage). Palette: white-and-green with deep accents, or full holiday saturation.

## Why are out-of-season flowers more expensive?

Out-of-season flowers are sourced from one of three places, all of which add cost.

**Long-haul air freight from the opposite hemisphere.** A November peony in London comes by air from New Zealand or Chile. The flowers are typically harvested tighter (more closed) to survive the flight, then re-hydrated on arrival. They look a little smaller and last a few days less than a June peony from Cornwall.

**Heated and lit greenhouses in the producing country.** Most Dutch tulips outside the natural March–May window are grown under supplementary light and heat, which is a non-trivial energy cost passed through to the buyer. The carbon footprint is sometimes worse than air-freighted flowers from a sunny climate.

**Cold storage from the local season.** Some flowers (chrysanthemums, carnations, alstroemeria) hold up well in refrigerated storage and can be sold weeks after harvest. These are the cheapest "out of season" option but the quality drops noticeably after about 3–4 weeks in a cold room.

A rule of thumb: **a flower bought 2–4 months outside its natural season costs 2–3× the in-season price** and lasts 30–40% less time in the vase. Anything sold more than 4 months outside its natural window is almost always either heavily forced or coming from the other hemisphere.

## How does seasonality work in tropical climates?

The Northern/Southern Hemisphere temperate calendar above does not apply in the tropics. Instead, tropical flower availability is driven by the **wet/dry seasonal pattern** and by altitude.

In Vietnam, Thailand, the Philippines, southern India, and equatorial South America, the major cut flowers — orchids (dendrobium, mokara, vanda), heliconia, ginger, anthurium, bird of paradise, lotus, hibiscus — are essentially **available year-round**, with quality dips during the heaviest rains. The wedding seasonality logic is therefore reversed: in tropical climates the question is "which weeks are *not* dependable," not "which weeks are in season."

Vietnamese florists mark a separate kind of seasonality around **Tết (Lunar New Year)**, when *hoa mai* (yellow apricot blossom) and peach blossom drive the entire flower market for two weeks — see our [hoa mai for Tết article](/posts/hoa-mai-vietnam-en/) for the detail. Similar lunar-calendar windows exist for Vu Lan, Mid-Autumn, and the Buddhist holidays, and they materially affect supply and price across Southeast Asia.

In equatorial Africa (Kenya, Ethiopia) the constant temperature and altitude allow **roses to be produced year-round at uniform quality**, which is why a January rose in Europe is almost certainly Kenyan and why Kenyan rose imports broke the European seasonal logic in the 1990s. The flower trade calendar in Europe was, in effect, rewritten around that supply.

## How should I actually buy flowers seasonally?

The simplest practical rule, for both florists and home buyers, is: **walk into the shop and buy what is in front of you in the largest quantity, in the best condition, at the best price.** That is, by definition, what is in season locally. If a flower has been flown in or held in cold storage, it will usually be obvious — the stems are slightly tired, the leaves are yellowing at the base, and the price has a story behind it.

For florists planning a week ahead, three habits make the seasonality work pay off:

- **Read the wholesale market list, not the retail catalogue.** The wholesale availability list is the season; the retail catalogue is whatever the shop has decided to advertise this week.
- **Build palettes around what's peaking, not what's named.** "We want peonies in November" is almost always solved better as "we want fluffy pale pink in November" — and the answer is forced ranunculus, garden roses with extra petals, or a layered chrysanthemum.
- **Use seasonality as the anchor, then layer.** Start with 60–70% in-season material, add 20–30% premium / out-of-season focal flowers if budget allows, and finish with 10% foliage. The bouquet reads as in-season even when individual stems aren't.

This is the same logic the AI Florist Coach app uses when it builds [combinations](/tools/combination-builder/) and [budget plans](/tools/budget-planner/) — start from the calendar, work outwards.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Which flowers are available all year round?**
Roses (mostly Kenyan and Colombian imports), chrysanthemums, carnations, alstroemeria, gypsophila, lisianthus, lilies, and most orchids are reliably available year-round in temperate-climate wholesale markets. Quality and price still vary, but stock-outs are rare.

**Are seasonal flowers really cheaper?**
Yes — typically 30–60% cheaper at peak season versus the same flower 2–4 months out of season, and 60–80% cheaper than the same flower at maximum out-of-season pricing. The price gap is largest for flowers that don't store or transport well, like peonies, lilac, and sweet peas.

**What is the cheapest month to buy flowers in bulk?**
For a temperate-climate florist, **April–May** is the best volume-for-money window — most spring flowers are at peak, and supply is high enough to push prices down. **November** is the second-best for autumn palettes (chrysanthemum, dahlia, foliage). The most expensive months globally are **December and February**, driven by Christmas, Lunar New Year, and Valentine's Day.

**Do imported flowers count as "in season"?**
Strictly, no — they are in season *somewhere*, just not where you are. For sustainability and vase-life purposes, "in season" means the flower is currently being harvested in your country or a neighbouring country and is reaching you within a few days of cutting. By that definition, a Kenyan rose in London in January is technically out of season, even though it's the same flower.

**Why do my supermarket flowers die so quickly compared to a florist's?**
Supermarket flowers usually spend longer in cold transport and storage, are cut tighter (less open), and are often handled at variable temperatures. Florist flowers come through a shorter cold chain. Seasonality compounds this — an in-season florist flower in May will routinely outlast an out-of-season supermarket flower by 5–7 days.

**Which flowers are in season for a wedding in October?**
Dahlia, chrysanthemum, amaranth, Japanese anemone, hydrangea (going antique), rosehips, ornamental kale, rich foliage. Palette skews burgundy, copper, cream, and bronze. October is one of the most cost-effective wedding months of the year because supply is high and demand has dropped off the summer peak.
