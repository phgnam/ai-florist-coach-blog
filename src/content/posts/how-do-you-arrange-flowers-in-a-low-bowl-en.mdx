## Quick Answer

To arrange flowers in a low bowl, **anchor with a metal pin frog (kenzan) or scrunched chicken-wire** at the base, fill with **2–3 cm of water**, build a **low dome of foliage** (rosemary, eucalyptus, ivy) for structure, then add **3 or 5 focal blooms** off-centre, **fillers** to bridge, and **delicate accents** that trail over the rim. Total height should be **roughly equal to the bowl's diameter**.

## Why is a low bowl harder than a tall vase?

Tall vases support stems naturally — gravity holds them in place. A low bowl gives:

- **Almost no vertical support** — stems flop without a holder
- **Limited water depth** — stems must drink from a shallow reservoir
- **Open visual exposure** — every stem and the mechanic shows
- **Awkward proportions** — heavy flowers want to topple

The fix: a **mechanical anchor** (pin frog or chicken-wire), tight stem placement, and short stems. Once you master the mechanics, a low bowl gives the most modern, sculptural look — perfect for dining tables where flowers shouldn't block sightlines.

## What is a pin frog and where do I get one?

A pin frog (Japanese: **kenzan**) is a heavy metal disc with closely spaced upright pins:

- **Material**: cast iron or brass — must be heavy enough not to float
- **Size**: 5–7 cm diameter for small bowls; 8–10 cm for centrepieces
- **Pins**: 1–2 cm tall, 3–5 mm apart, sharp tips
- **Cost**: £8–£20 from florist suppliers, online, or specialist craft stores

To use:

- **Place in centre of bowl**, pins up
- **Add water** to just below pin tips
- **Press stem ends straight down onto pins** — at least 1.5 cm of stem must stick onto pins
- **Angle stems** by leaning them slightly against pin pressure

A **florist's putty** under the kenzan locks it firmly to the bowl bottom. Without putty, kenzans can shift if you move the arrangement.

## Can I use chicken-wire instead?

Yes — and many florists prefer it for larger or asymmetric bowls:

- **Crumple a 30 × 30 cm piece** of chicken-wire into a loose ball
- **Press into the bowl** so wire wedges against rim and bottom
- **Tape down** with floral tape across rim if bowl is wide
- **Push stems through wire holes** — wire grips midway up the stem

Pros vs kenzan:

- **More flexible**: works in any bowl shape, including long ovals
- **Cheap**: £3 of wire makes 6 ball-anchors
- **Holds heavy stems** better than kenzans for big roses, dahlias

Cons:

- **Visible if not enough foliage**: wire shows through gaps
- **Harder to angle stems precisely**

For traditional ikebana-style minimal arrangements, a kenzan is essential. For full lush English-garden style, chicken-wire wins.

## What is the right ratio of bowl, height, and width?

The classic proportion:

- **Total height**: 1× to 1.25× the bowl's diameter (no taller)
- **Total width**: 1.25× to 1.5× the bowl's diameter
- **Foliage outline**: roughly elliptical or shallow dome, not pyramid
- **Focal blooms**: in the bottom third of overall height

For a 25 cm diameter bowl:

- Height ≈ 25–32 cm
- Width ≈ 32–37 cm

Going taller looks top-heavy. Going wider risks stems flopping over rim.

## What should I put in first?

The build order matters:

1. **Foliage base layer** — defines the shape, hides the kenzan
2. **Tallest stems** — set the height/edge of the dome
3. **Focal blooms** (3 or 5 large flowers) — placed asymmetrically off-centre
4. **Mid-fillers** — bridge focal points (snapdragons, lisianthus, smaller dahlias)
5. **Delicate textures** — astrantia, scabiosa, gypsophila for movement
6. **Trailing edge** — jasmine, ivy, or single ranunculus draped over rim

**Start central, work outward**. Don't add foliage at the end to "fix" gaps — it never sits right.

## Which flowers work best in a low bowl?

Best performers:

- **Roses** (garden type, 5–7 cm heads) — strong stem, clean lines
- **Ranunculus** — papery layered petals, perfect mid-bowl
- **Anemones** — bold colour, sturdy stem
- **Lisianthus** — long-lasting, multi-bloom stem
- **Dahlias** (smaller varieties) — striking focal point
- **Hellebores** — good for spring, moody colour
- **Sweet peas** — fragrance, light texture
- **Hydrangea heads** — instant volume

Foliage allies:

- **Eucalyptus** (especially populus) — blue-grey, forms structure
- **Italian ruscus** — deep green, narrow leaves
- **Rosemary** — fragrant, fine texture
- **Ivy** — trailing edge
- **Dusty miller** — silver leaves catch light
- **Scented geranium leaves** — bonus fragrance

Avoid for low bowls:

- **Very tall stems** (gladioli, delphiniums, foxgloves) — wrong scale
- **Tulips** — stems keep growing in water and bend the design

## How do I keep the arrangement looking fresh?

Daily care extends life:

- **Top up water** every morning — kenzans dry out fast
- **Refresh fully** every 2 days — empty bowl, rinse, refill
- **Trim stems** by 1 cm with a sharp knife at refresh
- **Pluck spent blooms** before they collapse into the bowl
- **Keep cool** — under 22 °C overnight, away from radiators
- **Out of direct sun and away from fruit bowls** — fruit ethylene kills cut flowers

A well-conditioned arrangement in a low bowl lasts **5–7 days**. See the [conditioning cut flowers guide](/posts/condition-cut-flowers-before-arranging-en/) for pre-arrangement preparation.

## Can I make a one-flower-type low bowl arrangement?

Yes — and it can be the most striking style:

- **All roses**: 5 same-colour garden roses + foliage = elegant, restrained
- **All hydrangeas**: 3 large heads densely packed = lush, modern
- **All ranunculus**: 9 mixed-pastel blooms = romantic, layered
- **All dahlias**: 5 bold-colour mediums = high-impact contemporary
- **All hellebores**: 7 stems, varied direction = moody, painterly

Single-variety arrangements demand colour repetition or shape variation to stay interesting. Use a 5–10% colour shift across stems (e.g. peach, blush, ivory roses) for natural depth.

## What about asymmetric/Japanese-style ikebana arrangements?

For minimalist ikebana-inspired pieces in a low bowl:

- **Three main lines**: heaven (tallest), human (medium), earth (lowest)
- **Negative space**: 50%+ of the bowl visible water
- **Asymmetry**: never centre, never equal triangles
- **Single focal**: one bloom is enough
- **Branch as primary line**: a curved branch defines the shape, flowers are accents

This style suits small bowls (15–20 cm diameter), short flowers (15–25 cm), and one or two stems of dramatic line material like flowering quince, magnolia, or curly willow.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much water should be in the bowl?

Just enough to cover the kenzan pins or the lower half of stems — about 2–3 cm in most low bowls. Too much water shows the mechanic if the bowl is glass; too little dries stems within hours. Top up daily.

### Can I use floral foam in a low bowl?

You can, but most modern florists avoid it for environmental reasons (floral foam is non-biodegradable microplastic). Pin frogs and chicken-wire are the eco-friendly modern standard and arguably look cleaner.

### My stems keep falling out of the kenzan — what's wrong?

Three common issues: stems are too thin (try thicker stems or wrap thin stems in a strip of foliage), stem ends aren't pressed deep enough (push at least 1.5 cm onto pins), or the kenzan is too small (heavy roses need an 8 cm+ kenzan).

### How long should I trim stems for a low bowl?

Cut stems to 1.5–2× the bowl's depth. For a 6 cm tall bowl, trim stems to 9–12 cm. Always cut at a 45° angle for maximum water uptake. See [conditioning cut flowers before arranging](/posts/condition-cut-flowers-before-arranging-en/) for proper stem preparation.

### Can I do a low bowl arrangement for a wedding?

Absolutely — low centrepieces are popular at long banquet tables because they don't block guests' sightlines across the table. Plan for 1 arrangement per 1.5 m of table length and brief the venue on table layout in advance.
