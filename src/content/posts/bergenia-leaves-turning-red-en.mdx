## Quick Answer

Bergenia leaves turn red in autumn because cooler nights and brighter low-angle sun trigger anthocyanin pigments in the leaf, the same chemistry that colours maple foliage. The shift is normal, healthy, and most pronounced on plants in full sun and lean soil. It only signals trouble when the redness comes with thinning, soft tissue, leaf spots, or root rot — those point to drought stress, vine weevil, or fungal disease, not seasonal colour change.

## Why does cool weather make bergenia leaves redden?

Bergenia (often called elephant ears or pigsqueak) is a tough evergreen perennial from cold mountain regions of central Asia. Many cultivars carry a dormant capacity to make red anthocyanin pigment in their leaves once short days and chilly nights return.

Three triggers stack up in autumn:

**Cooler nights.** Below about 10 °C, photosynthesis slows but sunlight still hits the leaves. The plant produces anthocyanins to shield surplus light energy and keep the chlorophyll system safe.

**Higher light at lower sun angles.** Deciduous trees lose their leaves and bergenia clumps that sat in summer shade now bake in autumn sunlight.

**Lower nitrogen availability.** Cold soil holds nitrogen tighter. Leaves with less nitrogen reveal more of the underlying anthocyanin colour.

The same pigments are responsible for the rosy purple of cooled flower wine, the deep burgundy of red cabbage, and the autumn flush in many other evergreens.

## Which bergenia cultivars colour up most strongly?

Not every bergenia turns the same shade. If you want a strong autumn red display, the cultivar matters more than the care regime.

| Cultivar | Autumn colour | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 'Bressingham Ruby' | Deep ruby and burgundy | One of the strongest colour-changers; flowers in early spring |
| 'Eric Smith' | Red-bronze with green undertones | Compact; tolerates dry shade well |
| 'Overture' | Bright red-purple | Reliable colour even in lighter shade |
| 'Rotblum' | Mahogany red | Long-flowering with magenta-red blooms |
| 'Bach' | Rich burgundy | Smaller leaves; good for front-of-border |
| 'Silberlicht' | Stays mostly green | Bred for white flowers, not autumn colour |
| 'Baby Doll' | Soft pink-tinged green | Dwarf; little red flush |

If you bought an unnamed clump from a garden centre and want autumn fire, check the cultivar label. A nameless bergenia that stays plain green probably will, no matter what you do to it.

## How can I tell healthy autumn red from stress red?

Healthy seasonal colour and trouble both produce reddish leaves, but they look very different up close.

**Healthy autumn flush:**
- Even colour across the whole leaf, often deeper at the edges
- Leaf surface stays firm and glossy
- Pattern starts at the outer rosette and works inward
- Crown and petioles look firm and pale green

**Drought stress:**
- Red-purple flush plus crispy leaf tips
- Foliage feels limp or papery
- New leaves come out small and curled
- Soil dust-dry several centimetres down

**Vine weevil damage:**
- Plants colour up earlier than expected
- A gentle tug pulls the rosette away from the soil
- Roots are gnawed off short with C-shaped cream grubs visible

**Fungal leaf spot:**
- Reddish-brown circular blotches with a darker outer ring
- Spotting is concentrated in damp, shaded clumps
- Older leaves drop early

If you see any of the trouble patterns, treat the cause rather than the colour: increase mulch and watering, drench with biological vine weevil control in autumn, or remove and destroy spotted leaves.

## Should I cut off red bergenia leaves?

Not until they turn brown or look battered. The red phase is doing a job:

- Protecting the chlorophyll layer through bright winter light.
- Keeping a thick evergreen mulch over the crown.
- Continuing photosynthesis on milder days.

In late winter or very early spring, before new leaves push, take out only the leaves that are obviously dead, ragged, or covered in fungal spots. Leave the rest. The plant will gradually shed older foliage on its own as new spring leaves emerge.

A clean pair of secateurs and a once-a-year tidy is usually all the maintenance bergenia ever needs. For a similar approach to evergreen winter foliage, see [Why are my hellebore leaves turning black in late winter?](/posts/hellebore-foliage-blackening-en/).

## How do I get the best autumn colour next year?

If your bergenia stays mostly green even in cool weather, three changes usually push the display:

**Move it into more light.** Bergenia tolerates shade but colours up best with at least 4 hours of direct sun. Edges of paths, low retaining walls, and sunny rockery pockets are perfect.

**Reduce nitrogen feeding.** A summer top-up of high-nitrogen fertiliser keeps leaves green and suppresses anthocyanin. Mulch with a thin layer of well-rotted compost in early spring instead.

**Keep the soil lean and free-draining.** Bergenia evolved on rocky slopes, not in rich woodland loam. Heavy organic mulches every year produce big leaves but dull colour.

## Is bergenia good for pollinators or for cut flowers?

Yes, modestly. The early spring flowers are valued by bumblebees emerging from hibernation when little else is open. The flower stalks are sturdy enough to use in small cut-flower posies — they sit comfortably alongside hellebores and early primroses in a mixed vase.

The leaves themselves are useful in arrangements year-round and especially good in autumn, when a few burgundy leaves give weight to a bouquet without needing to wait for the woodier autumn foliage. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will bergenia foliage stay red all winter?**
Often, yes — at least the deepest-colouring cultivars keep some red through to early spring, fading back to green as new leaves push. Very mild winters or warm exposures fade the colour faster.

**Why are only the outer leaves red and the centre still green?**
This is normal. Older outer leaves are first to colour because they have been in the light longest and have lower chlorophyll reserves. Younger inner leaves keep more chlorophyll and stay green longer.

**Can I divide bergenia in autumn?**
Yes. Early autumn is a good time to lift, split, and replant tired clumps. Cut the rhizome into pieces with at least one growth point each, replant at the same depth, and water in well. The divisions will recolour normally as cool weather arrives.

**Are bergenia leaves edible?**
No, not for casual eating. Bergenia leaves and rhizomes contain bergenin and tannins. They have folk medicinal use in Himalayan tea (sometimes called pashanbed) but are not a culinary herb.

**Why do my bergenia leaves get brown crispy edges instead of red?**
That is drought or sun-scald, not seasonal colour. Increase summer watering during heatwaves, mulch the root zone, and consider lifting the clump out of the harshest midday sun.
