## Quick Answer

Brick wall planters bake in summer and dry out fast — pick **drought-tolerant trailing plants** with shallow roots: **aubretia, ivy-leaved geraniums, calibrachoa, lobelia, creeping thyme, sedum, alyssum, and trailing nasturtium**. Use deep planting cells (**15 cm minimum**), moisture-retaining peat-free compost mixed with **20% perlite**, and water at the base in the morning. Expect to **water daily** through summer.

## What kind of brick wall planter are we talking about?

Three common types, each with different planting implications:

- **Hollow brick / breeze block planters**: square or rectangular cavities built into a low wall, 12–25 cm deep — small soil volume, dry out fast
- **Stepped pocket planters**: tiered cell-style raised planters built from brick — moderate volume, warm but not extreme
- **Cavity-wall flower bricks**: decorative bricks with planting holes around 8–10 cm deep — very limited, only suit succulents and tiny annuals
- **Wall-mounted brick troughs**: full troughs attached to brickwork — generous soil volume but exposed roots heat extremes

For each type, the smaller the cell, the more drought-tolerant the plants need to be. **Always check depth first** — under 15 cm depth means succulents only; 15–25 cm opens up annuals.

## Why do brick wall planters dry out so fast?

Three combining factors:

- **Heat absorption**: dark brick (especially red/brown) reaches 50+ °C in midday sun
- **Wind exposure**: walls catch breeze on both sides, accelerating evaporation
- **Limited soil volume**: small cells hold less water reserve
- **Vertical drainage**: gravity pulls water down and out faster than in a flat planter

Result: a 15 cm cell in full sun can dry from saturated to wilt-point in **8–10 hours** during a heatwave. Plants must either tolerate this or get watered twice daily.

## Which plants tolerate hot, dry brick conditions best?

Top performers for full-sun brick wall planters:

**Trailing edge** (cascading downward, the visual highlight):

- **Aubretia (purple/blue/pink)** — perfect for spring brick walls, drought-tolerant once rooted
- **Ivy-leaved geraniums (Pelargonium peltatum)** — heat-loving, flowers all summer, trails 50–80 cm
- **Calibrachoa "Million Bells"** — tolerates dry spells once established
- **Trailing lobelia** — shorter blooms but masses of flower
- **Bacopa** — fine white/blue spreader
- **Sedum (S. spurium, S. cauticola)** — succulent, copes with neglect

**Filling middle**:

- **Alyssum** — fragrant white carpet
- **Creeping thyme** — fragrant, drought-perfect
- **Sempervivums (houseleeks)** — succulent rosettes, indestructible
- **Echeveria** — for warmer regions
- **Saxifraga** — alpine-style, fine textured

**Compact uprights** (for taller pockets):

- **Erigeron karvinskianus (Mexican fleabane)** — daisy mass, self-seeds
- **Helichrysum (curry plant)** — silver-leaved, drought-tolerant
- **Gazania** — bright daisies that close in shade

**Edible additions**:

- **Trailing nasturtium** — peppery edible flowers, drought-tolerant
- **Strawberries** — many edible varieties trail well

Avoid for full-sun brick walls: hostas, ferns, fuchsias, primulas, impatiens — all moisture-lovers that wilt within hours.

## What about shaded or north-facing brick walls?

Different plant list entirely. North-facing or shaded brick stays cooler and damper:

- **Erigeron karvinskianus** (still works)
- **Aubretia** (tolerates part-shade)
- **Hardy ferns** (small types like *Asplenium*)
- **Lewisia** — alpine pink trumpets
- **Saxifraga** species
- **Campanula portenschlagiana** (Adriatic bellflower)
- **Heuchera** — colourful foliage, small flowers
- **Trailing fuchsia** — moist shade lover

Shaded brick planters need different watering — less than sun-baked walls, but not waterlogged. Aim for damp, never wet.

## How do I prepare a brick cell for planting?

The setup that makes the difference between thriving and dying plants:

- **Drill drainage** if not already there: 5 mm holes through the bottom mortar/concrete
- **Line the bottom** with permeable landscape fabric to keep soil in
- **Add 2 cm grit** for drainage
- **Fill with peat-free multipurpose compost** mixed with **20% perlite** (lightens, drains)
- **Add 10% water-retaining gel crystals** if available — buys 1–2 days of dry-out time
- **Slow-release fertiliser** at planting (6-month type)
- **Top with 1 cm gravel mulch** — reduces evaporation, hides bare compost

For pockets under 15 cm depth, use a 50/50 mix of compost and **John Innes No. 2** for extra moisture retention.

## How often will I need to water?

Watering schedule for sunny brick walls:

- **Spring (cool, damp)**: every 3 days
- **Late spring (warming)**: every other day
- **Mid-summer**: daily
- **Heatwave**: twice daily, dawn and dusk
- **Autumn**: every 4–5 days
- **Winter**: only if dry, rare in temperate climates

Quick test: stick a finger 3 cm into the soil. If dry, water. If still moist, skip.

Watering technique:

- **Direct at the soil**, not over leaves
- **Slow trickle** until water runs out drainage holes
- **Dawn or dusk** in summer
- **Use a long-spout watering can** for high or awkward cells
- **Drip irrigation** worth installing if you have multiple cells

For multi-cell walls, a simple battery-powered drip system on a timer transforms the maintenance burden.

## Do plants need feeding?

Yes — small soil volume runs out of nutrients fast. Choose one approach:

- **Slow-release pellets at planting** (6-month formulation) — easy, lasts the season
- **Liquid feed weekly** — half-strength tomato feed for flowering plants
- **Top-dress mid-season** — fresh compost layer scratched into surface

Stop feeding 4 weeks before frost or when plants naturally slow. Over-fed plants make leaves at the expense of flowers.

## How do I winter brick wall planters?

Most annuals die at first frost. Plan in two ways:

**Option 1: Seasonal swap**:

- **Spring–autumn**: annuals (May–October)
- **Late autumn–winter**: pansies, violas, ornamental cabbage, heathers, ivy
- **Winter–spring**: hardy bulbs (crocus, dwarf daffodils, tulips) layered for early colour

**Option 2: Permanent perennials**:

- **Aubretia** dies back but recovers each spring
- **Sedum, sempervivums, thyme** stay green year-round
- **Ivy** evergreen, fills gaps in winter
- **Heuchera** strong winter foliage

For lazy maintenance, plant 80% perennial mix and add a few seasonal annuals each spring/autumn for colour.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can plants damage the brickwork itself?

Most ornamental plants don't. Avoid plants with strong invasive roots (Russian vine, mature ivy on damaged mortar). Keep ivy small and trim regularly. Check pointing once a year — repair small mortar gaps before plant roots widen them.

### What's the smallest cell I can plant in?

8 cm minimum. Below that, only sedums, sempervivums, and tiny saxifrages survive. For real flower display, 15 cm depth × 15 cm width is the practical minimum.

### How heavy does a planted brick wall get?

A 30 × 15 × 15 cm cell with damp compost weighs about 5–7 kg. Multi-cell walls add up — check that the original wall was built to support the load, especially for retrofitted cells.

### Can I grow vegetables in brick wall planters?

Yes — herbs (thyme, oregano, marjoram, rosemary), salad leaves, dwarf chillies, strawberries, and dwarf tomatoes all work in 20+ cm cells. See the [edible flowers guide](/posts/edible-flowers-guide-en/) for combining ornamental and edible.

### Will the brick wall feel hot to plants in summer?

Yes — surface temperatures can hit 50 °C. Three mitigations: (1) light-coloured wash the inside of cells, (2) use white or pale gravel mulch, (3) water at base to cool. The [strawberry pot guide](/posts/which-flowers-grow-well-in-a-strawberry-pot-en/) covers similar small-pocket planning.

### What about the wheelbarrow planter approach to picking colours?

Same principle — pick a 2-3 colour palette and repeat across cells for visual unity. The [wheelbarrow planter flowers guide](/posts/which-flowers-grow-well-in-a-wheelbarrow-planter-en/) explains thriller/filler/spiller in detail; the same logic applies to wall pockets.
