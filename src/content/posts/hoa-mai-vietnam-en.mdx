## Quick Answer

Hoa mai (*Ochna integerrima*, also called the yellow apricot blossom or *mai vàng*) is the bright yellow, five-petalled flower that defines Tết — the Vietnamese Lunar New Year — in southern Vietnam. Growers strip the leaves around two weeks before Tết to force the bloom to open precisely on New Year's Day. Each open flower is read as a unit of luck for the household.

## What is hoa mai and why is it called the southern Tết flower?

Hoa mai is the flower of *Ochna integerrima*, a small deciduous tree native to mainland Southeast Asia (southern China, Cambodia, Laos, and especially southern Vietnam). The tree is unrelated to the European apricot — the "yellow apricot blossom" name is a translation convenience. Botanically it sits in the Ochnaceae family; the closest cultivated relatives are *Ochna serrulata* (the African "Mickey Mouse plant") and the white *Prunus mume* (Chinese *mei hua*) it is sometimes confused with.

It is the *southern* Tết flower because southern Vietnam's warmer climate suits *Ochna integerrima*'s tropical-deciduous rhythm: the tree drops its leaves in the dry season and flowers on bare wood, exactly when Tết falls. North of the 17th parallel the climate cools enough that growers shifted long ago to **hoa đào** (peach blossom) instead, and the two flowers now divide the country culturally — hoa mai south of Đà Nẵng, hoa đào north of it. For the Chinese cousin of the southern hoa mai, see [What is the plum blossom and why is it one of China's most loved flowers?](/posts/plum-blossom-mei-hua-en/).

## When does hoa mai bloom and how do growers time it for Tết?

The natural bloom window is **late January to mid-February**, which usually overlaps with Tết — but "usually" isn't good enough for a grower. The whole point of a hoa mai tree at Tết is that the flowers open on the **first morning of the new year**, ideally before the first guests arrive. Growers force the timing in three steps:

1. **Lá rụng (leaf-stripping), about 14 days before Tết.** Every leaf is removed by hand. The tree thinks it has hit the dry season and begins to throw flower buds.
2. **Water and warmth control, days −14 to −3.** Warmer water and full sun pull the bloom forward; cooler shade slows it.
3. **Final adjustment, days −3 to 0.** A tree that's running ahead is moved indoors, watered with cold water, and shaded; a tree that's running behind is bathed in warm water and given direct afternoon sun.

A skilled grower in Bến Tre or Bình Định gets within 24 hours of the target on a healthy tree. The skill is locally famous — the best growers are ordered out a year in advance.

## What does the number of petals on hoa mai mean?

Wild *Ochna integerrima* produces flowers with **five petals**. Five is the number Vietnamese and Chinese folk culture both treat as auspicious — the *ngũ phúc* (five blessings: longevity, wealth, health, virtue, and a peaceful death) and the *ngũ hành* (five elements). A standard hoa mai bloom is therefore already a small charm.

Cultivated forms break the rule on purpose:

- **Mai cánh nhiều (multi-petalled mai).** Bred over decades, these have 9, 12, 24, or even 60+ petals per flower. Each extra petal is read as an extra unit of luck. The 12-petal cultivar is especially popular because 12 is also the number of months in the year.
- **Mai tứ quý (four-season mai, *Ochna serrulata*).** A different species that flowers all year. Auspicious, but not strictly the Tết flower.

Counting blooms on the morning of *mùng một* (the first day) is a real, lived tradition: "How many flowers did your mai open?" is small talk, not metaphor. A tree that opens hundreds of flowers on day one is read as a specific forecast of the household's year.

## Where in Vietnam does hoa mai grow best?

Three regions are the centre of gravity for hoa mai cultivation:

- **Bến Tre (Mekong Delta).** The country's largest commercial hoa mai growing region. Thousands of farms train trees over 5–15 years into ornamental shapes (*thế cây*), then ship them up the Mekong to Saigon, Vũng Tàu, and Cần Thơ for Tết.
- **Bình Định (south-central coast).** Famous for the *mai bonsai* tradition — small, gnarled trees with deliberately twisted trunks, sold as miniatures rather than full-size garden trees.
- **Saigon (Ho Chi Minh City) and the suburbs.** Not a growing region but the **trading** centre. The Tết flower markets at Tao Đàn Park and along Nguyễn Huệ Street are the visible centre of the southern hoa mai trade in the week before the holiday.

Outside these regions hoa mai still grows ornamentally, but the **timing skill** is concentrated in Bến Tre and Bình Định. A pot bought from a non-specialist in Hà Nội or Đà Lạt is more likely to bloom a week early or a week late.

## How is hoa mai different from hoa đào, the northern peach blossom?

Both are Tết flowers; they are not the same plant.

| Trait | **Hoa mai** (south) | **Hoa đào** (north) |
|---|---|---|
| Species | *Ochna integerrima* | *Prunus persica* (peach) |
| Flower colour | Bright yellow | Pink (light to deep) |
| Petal count | 5 (wild) to 60+ (cultivated) | 5 |
| Bloom on bare wood? | Yes, after leaf-stripping | Yes, naturally |
| Climate | Tropical, hot dry season | Subtropical, cool winter |
| Region of cultural use | South of Đà Nẵng | North of Đà Nẵng |
| Leaf-stripping forced bloom? | Yes (mandatory for Tết timing) | Optional, less aggressive |
| Decorative pot or branch? | Whole tree more common | Cut branches more common |

The two are sometimes displayed together in central Vietnam (Huế, Đà Nẵng), where both climates blur. For a related East-Asian winter-bloomer with similar visual logic — flowers on bare wood — see the Chinese *mei hua* in [What is the plum blossom?](/posts/plum-blossom-mei-hua-en/).

## How do you care for a hoa mai branch or tree during Tết?

A few hours of care decide whether a hoa mai stays beautiful for the whole holiday or drops its petals on day three:

- **Recut the stem on a 45° angle** before placing it in water (for branches) or watering the pot (for trees). The same logic as any cut flower — see [how long do cut flowers last in a vase?](/posts/cut-flower-vase-life-en/) for the underlying mechanics.
- **Place in a bright, draught-free spot.** Direct afternoon sun in a Saigon apartment cooks the tree; an air-conditioning vent dries the buds before they open. A bright doorway with morning light is ideal.
- **Water lightly every other day** for potted trees. Hoa mai roots rot in standing water — the soil should be moist, not wet.
- **Mist the open flowers in the morning.** Cool, clean water on the petals doubles their lifespan and brightens the colour.
- **After Tết: don't throw the tree away.** Move it outdoors, prune the spent flowering wood, and let it leaf out naturally. A well-cared-for hoa mai will bloom again next Tết — and a tree that has been brought back to bloom three or four years in a row is itself a household symbol.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is hoa mai the same as cherry blossom?**
No. Cherry blossom (*sakura*) is *Prunus serrulata* and is pink; hoa mai is *Ochna integerrima* and is bright yellow. The flowers also bloom at different times — sakura peaks in late March to early April; hoa mai peaks in late January to mid-February at Tết. See [when and where can you see cherry blossoms in Japan?](/posts/cherry-blossom-japan-en/) for the full sakura distinction.

**Why does hoa mai have five petals?**
Wild *Ochna integerrima* naturally produces five-petalled flowers. The number is read in Vietnamese and Chinese culture as the *ngũ phúc* (five blessings: longevity, wealth, health, virtue, and a peaceful death). Cultivars with more petals — 9, 12, or 60+ — were bred deliberately to multiply that auspiciousness.

**Can you grow hoa mai outside Vietnam?**
Yes, in any frost-free tropical or sub-tropical climate that has a defined dry season. *Ochna integerrima* is grown ornamentally in Singapore, southern China, Taiwan, and the warmest parts of Australia and the southern United States. It will not survive a temperate winter outdoors. The flower-timing for Tết, however, is much harder outside its native range — the bloom rhythm is calibrated to the Mekong dry season.

**Are hoa mai flowers safe for cats and dogs?**
*Ochna integerrima* is not on standard veterinary toxicity lists, but the leaves and seeds of the related *Ochna serrulata* (mai tứ quý) are mildly toxic to small pets if eaten in quantity. Treat hoa mai as you would any non-food ornamental: keep fallen flowers out of reach of curious dogs and cats. For a full reference, see [which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).

**Can you keep a hoa mai tree alive year after year?**
Yes — and the household-tradition reward is real. After Tết, move the tree outdoors, prune off the spent flowering wood, and water normally through the wet season. The tree will leaf out, grow, and rebuild reserves. Strip the leaves again about two weeks before next Tết and the cycle repeats. Trees kept this way for a decade or more are a small status symbol in southern households.
