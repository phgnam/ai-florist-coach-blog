## Quick Answer

Yes — a florist *Cyclamen persicum* will reliably reflower next winter if you let it dry off through summer dormancy and then restart it in early autumn. After the last flowers fade, ease off watering, let the leaves yellow naturally, store the dry tuber in its pot somewhere cool and shaded all summer, and resume watering and feeding in late August. New leaves and buds appear by October and flowers from November through March.

## Why does my cyclamen look exhausted now that flowers are finished?

A florist cyclamen on its way out of bloom is *not* dying — it is following its native cycle. *Cyclamen persicum* evolved on Mediterranean and Middle Eastern hillsides where wet cool winters trigger leaf and flower growth, and dry hot summers trigger total above-ground dormancy. The tuber sits underground all summer, quietly storing energy, then resprouts when autumn rain returns. The plant in your living room is doing exactly the same thing, six months out of phase with its outdoor cousin.

What you are seeing in March or April is the start of that programmed dormancy. The plant looks tired because:

- Older leaves are yellowing as the plant withdraws nutrients back into the tuber.
- New flower buds have stopped forming — once spring light and warmth begin, *Cyclamen persicum* refuses to push more flowers.
- The compost is increasingly hard to keep evenly moist as the leaf canopy declines and the tuber takes up less water.

This is not failure on your part — it is the plant's evolved response to longer days and rising temperatures. The mistake most owners make is to treat the yellowing leaves as a watering or feeding problem and pour more water and feed on, which often rots the tuber. The right move is the opposite: slow down, let the cycle run, and prepare for dormancy.

A second common mistake is binning the plant on the assumption it is finished. A healthy florist cyclamen grown well can reflower for at least 3–5 winters from the same tuber, and many gardeners report 8–10 year tubers that flower more freely as they age.

## How do you wind a cyclamen down into summer dormancy correctly?

The transition from flowering plant to dormant tuber happens over about six weeks. Done correctly, the plant follows the same path it would on a Cretan hillside: leaves yellow gradually, watering tapers off, and the tuber goes to sleep with full reserves.

**Late March to early May (depending on your indoor temperature).**

1. **Stop deadheading.** Some growers continue twisting off spent flowers — fine if you are still being given new buds. The moment new buds stop appearing, leave the plant alone. Pulling and twisting now causes unnecessary tuber wounds.
2. **Cut feeding immediately.** No more tomato feed, no more African violet feed. The plant is winding down; feeding it now is like force-feeding someone going to sleep.
3. **Stop bottom-watering and switch to top-watering only when soil is dry to a finger 2 cm down.** The aim is gradually drying out the rootball, not abruptly stopping water.
4. **Let leaves yellow.** Do not pull them. As each leaf goes yellow and limp, give it a gentle twist at the base of the petiole — if it comes away cleanly, fine; if it resists, leave it for another week. Tearing at unripe leaves wounds the tuber.
5. **Move the plant to a cool, shaded windowsill.** Bedroom or hallway, away from direct sun. Target 13–18 °C.

**Late May to early June.**

By now most leaves should be off. Stop watering completely. Let the rootball dry out gently over 2–3 weeks. The tuber will be visible at the soil surface — a slightly wrinkled, dark-skinned disc. This is exactly what should happen.

**June to late August (full dormancy).**

- Stand the dry pot somewhere cool, shaded, and ventilated. A north-facing windowsill, a porch, an unheated bathroom, or under a garden bench in deep shade all work. Target 15–22 °C; avoid hot direct sun (the tuber cooks if pot temperature exceeds about 28 °C).
- Do not water at all through summer. The tuber survives on stored reserves.
- Do not repot or disturb the tuber. Any disturbance now will set the autumn restart back by 6–8 weeks.

If you live somewhere humid where the tuber risks rotting in stored compost, you can lift the dry tuber out of the compost in May, brush it off, and store it in a brown paper bag in a cool ventilated cupboard. This is the same principle as the [bare-root paper-bag overwintering for pelargoniums](/posts/overwinter-geraniums-indoors-en/) — fresh, dry, ventilated. Repot in fresh compost in late August.

## How do you restart a dormant cyclamen in late summer?

Restarting is just as important as resting. A clumsy restart can rot the tuber after it has survived an entire dormancy successfully. The key is gradual rehydration and resumed feeding before any leaves are visible.

**Late August (or whenever night temperatures fall below about 18 °C).**

1. **Refresh the compost.** Tip the dormant tuber out of its pot, brush off old compost, and inspect the tuber. It should feel firm. Soft, dark, mushy spots indicate rot — cut these out with a clean blade and dust with cinnamon powder before repotting.
2. **Repot one size larger only if the tuber is now obviously crammed in.** Florist cyclamen flower better in slightly small pots; do not pot up unnecessarily. Use a free-draining mix (multi-purpose compost mixed 3:1 with perlite or fine grit).
3. **Position the tuber correctly.** Half of the tuber should sit *above* the compost surface. Burying the tuber completely (a common mistake) causes crown rot and is the single biggest cause of failed cyclamen restarts.
4. **Place in a cool bright spot.** A north- or east-facing windowsill is ideal. Target 13–18 °C — too warm a restart spot causes leggy stretching and few flowers.

**First watering.**

- Water once thoroughly, then drain. *Bottom-water* by standing the pot in a saucer of room-temperature water for 20 minutes — this avoids splashing the tuber crown.
- Wait until the top 2 cm of compost is dry before watering again. In late August this is usually 5–7 days; in October it is more like 3–4 days.

**First feed.**

- Two weeks after the first watering, begin a quarter-strength balanced houseplant feed every 14 days.
- When the first flower buds are visible (usually mid-October), switch to half-strength tomato feed every 10 days through to mid-February.

**Expect.**

- New small leaves emerge from the tuber within 2–4 weeks of the first watering.
- The first flowers usually appear in November or early December.
- Peak flowering December to February, with sporadic flowers into March.

If at three weeks past the first watering nothing is happening, do *not* water heavily. The tuber may be slow this year, especially if it is over 5 years old. Patience is more reliable than extra water.

## How can you keep a florist cyclamen flowering longer through winter?

Once back into growth and bud, a few small environmental tweaks dramatically extend the flowering window. The plant will flower whether or not you do these — but doing them stretches the bloom from "December to early February" to "November to early April."

- **Cool nights.** *Cyclamen persicum* flowers more abundantly when nights stay below 15 °C. A heated bedroom at 21 °C through the night gives a shorter flowering season; an unheated kitchen or porch at 8–13 °C overnight gives the longest. If you must place it in a heated room, move it to a cool windowsill for the night.
- **Bright but indirect light.** Direct midday sun in midwinter is fine; direct summer-strength sun (rare in midwinter) causes leaf scorch. South- or east-facing windowsills are best in cool-temperate climates.
- **No drafts, no radiator updrafts.** Bud drop on cyclamen is most often caused by a sudden updraft of dry warm air from a radiator beneath a windowsill. The same airflow problem also explains [bud drop in tropical hibiscus](/posts/hibiscus-buds-dropping-en/) when buds dry out before they open.
- **Bottom-water religiously.** Watering from above onto the central tuber crown is the leading cause of crown rot. Always water by standing the pot in a saucer of cool water for 20 minutes, then drain.
- **Deadhead by twisting, not cutting.** Wrap your finger around the spent flower stem at the base of the tuber, then twist sharply. The whole stem comes away cleanly with a satisfying pop. Cutting leaves a short piece of petiole that rots back into the tuber crown and invites disease.
- **Room humidity.** Cyclamen prefers around 50 % RH. In a centrally heated room in midwinter, RH can drop below 30 %, causing crispy leaf edges and shortened flowering. A pebble tray under the pot (not touching the pot base) helps without risking root waterlogging.

If your plant develops a classic case of crown rot — sudden collapse of healthy-looking leaves, often with a foul smell at the tuber centre — diagnostics and recovery overlap heavily with rot in [tuberous and pseudobulb-style orchid roots](/posts/why-are-orchid-roots-growing-out-of-pot-en/). The fastest response is to lift the tuber, cut out all soft tissue, dust with cinnamon, and let the cut surfaces dry for 48 hours before repotting in fresh dry mix.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My cyclamen never went dormant — leaves stayed green all summer. Did I do something wrong?**
Probably not. In a cool home (below 22 °C all summer) some florist cyclamen behave as evergreen perennials and skip dormancy. If yours stayed leafy and healthy through summer, just continue watering and feeding lightly and expect autumn flowering as normal. The plant will eventually take a dormancy in a warmer year.

**The tuber went mushy in summer storage. Can I save it?**
Sometimes. Lift the tuber, cut away every soft section back to firm white tissue, and dust the cut surfaces with cinnamon powder. Let the wounds air-dry on a paper towel for 48 hours, then repot half-buried in fresh dry compost. Do not water for a further week. If the rot is more than 30 % of the tuber by mass, the plant is unlikely to recover and replacement is cheaper than nursing.

**Should I deadhead the seed pods that form after flowering?**
Yes if you want maximum tuber strength next year. The coiling spring-loaded seed pods (an evolutionary adaptation to scatter seed at ground level) draw real energy out of the tuber as they ripen. Pinch them off as soon as the petals fall, unless you specifically want seed.

**Can I plant a finished florist cyclamen out into the garden?**
*Cyclamen persicum* is not winter-hardy below about −2 °C, so it cannot survive winter outdoors in most cool-temperate climates. If you want garden cyclamen for autumn or spring colour, plant *Cyclamen hederifolium* (autumn-flowering) or *Cyclamen coum* (winter/spring-flowering) instead — both are fully hardy and naturalise into dense ground-cover carpets under trees and in shady borders.

**Will my cyclamen reflower in its first year, or only after a full dormancy cycle?**
A florist cyclamen bought in flower in November can definitely reflower the *following* winter (its second flowering for you, and 18 months after dormancy was last broken at the nursery). Many growers report the tuber actually flowers more abundantly in years 2 and 3 than in the first year, because nursery-forced cyclamen are pushed hard for that first display and the tuber needs a recovery cycle to settle into a normal rhythm.
