## Quick Answer

Yes, all parts of foxglove (*Digitalis purpurea*) are poisonous. They contain cardiac glycosides — primarily digitoxin and digoxin — that can cause serious heart rhythm disturbances if ingested. However, foxgloves are grown safely in millions of gardens worldwide. The risk is manageable with awareness: teach children not to eat any plant, supervise toddlers, and keep pets that chew plants away from foxglove beds.

## How poisonous are foxgloves exactly?

Foxglove toxicity is not theoretical. The cardiac glycosides in every part of the plant — leaves, flowers, stems, seeds, and even the water in a vase — affect the heart directly by disrupting the electrical signals that control heartbeat rhythm.

**Symptoms of ingestion** include nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, blurred or yellow-tinted vision, confusion, drowsiness, and irregular or dangerously slow heartbeat. In severe cases, cardiac arrest is possible.

**How much is dangerous?** There is no safe dose. Even a small amount of leaf material can cause symptoms in a child. The upper leaves tend to contain higher concentrations of glycosides than the lower ones, and dried plant material retains its toxicity.

**Historical context.** Digitalis extracted from foxglove was one of the first effective heart medications, used from the 18th century to treat heart failure and atrial fibrillation. The pharmaceutical drug digoxin is still derived from *Digitalis lanata*. The therapeutic dose is very close to the toxic dose, which is why medical digitalis requires careful monitoring — and why casual ingestion is so dangerous.

The plant does not cause harm through skin contact for most people, though some individuals report mild irritation after handling foxglove leaves. Wearing gloves when working with the plants is a sensible precaution.

## Is the risk realistic in a normal garden?

The vast majority of foxglove poisoning cases involve deliberate ingestion (self-harm or herbal misuse), not accidental garden exposure. Accidental poisoning from garden foxgloves is extremely rare for several reasons:

**Foxglove leaves taste terrible.** They are intensely bitter, hairy, and unpleasant to chew. A child or pet that mouths a leaf will almost certainly spit it out before swallowing enough to cause serious harm.

**The plant does not look edible.** Unlike berry-producing shrubs or fruit trees, foxgloves do not produce anything that resembles food. Children are far more likely to eat attractive berries than bitter, hairy leaves.

**Most pets ignore them.** Dogs and cats generally avoid foxgloves unless they are indiscriminate chewers. Livestock (horses, cattle, goats) are at greater risk because they may eat foxglove leaves mixed into hay or pasture.

That said, the potential severity of poisoning means the risk should be taken seriously, especially with toddlers who explore by putting things in their mouths, or with puppies that chew everything.

## How can you grow foxgloves safely in a family garden?

**Teach, do not remove.** The most effective safety measure is teaching children that they must never eat any plant from the garden without asking an adult. This rule protects against all toxic plants, not just foxglove.

**Plant away from play areas.** Place foxgloves in borders at the back of beds, behind lower-growing plants, rather than along paths or next to sandpits where toddlers crawl.

**Supervise young children.** Under-threes who are still in the mouthing stage need direct supervision in any garden, regardless of which plants are present.

**Wear gloves when handling.** Use gardening gloves when dividing, transplanting, or deadheading foxgloves. Wash hands afterwards.

**Do not put foxgloves in drinking-water vases.** The water absorbs toxins. If you use foxgloves in indoor arrangements, keep the vase out of reach and dispose of the water carefully.

**Know your pets.** Most adult dogs and cats ignore foxgloves. If you have a puppy or a dog that chews plants indiscriminately, either fence the plants or postpone planting foxgloves until the dog matures.

**Label the plants.** If other people care for your garden (babysitters, garden helpers), let them know which plants are toxic.

## What should you do if someone eats foxglove?

**Seek emergency medical help immediately.** Call your local poison control centre or emergency services. Do not wait for symptoms to appear.

**Do not induce vomiting** unless specifically instructed by a medical professional. Cardiac glycosides can cause further harm during vomiting.

**Bring a sample of the plant** to the hospital if possible. This helps medical staff identify the toxin and choose the correct treatment. The antidote for severe digitalis poisoning is digoxin-specific antibody fragments (Digibind/DigiFab), which is available in most emergency departments.

**For pets**, contact your veterinarian or an animal poison helpline immediately.

## Which other common garden plants are equally or more toxic?

Foxglove is far from the only toxic garden plant. Knowing the broader landscape helps put the risk in perspective:

**Yew (*Taxus baccata*)** — all parts except the red berry flesh are highly toxic, and ingestion can be fatal within hours. More dangerous than foxglove because the berries attract children.

**Lily of the valley (*Convallaria majalis*)** — contains cardiac glycosides similar to foxglove. All parts are toxic, including the red berries.

**Oleander (*Nerium oleander*)** — one of the most toxic common garden shrubs. All parts, including smoke from burning, are dangerous.

**Monkshood (*Aconitum*)** — extremely toxic. Even skin contact with the roots can cause numbness and, in rare cases, systemic poisoning.

**Autumn crocus (*Colchicum autumnale*)** — contains colchicine, which is toxic to humans and lethal to dogs in small amounts.

If you grow any of these — and most gardeners do — the same sensible precautions apply: teach, supervise, and plant thoughtfully.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can you touch foxglove leaves safely?**
Yes, for most people. Brief skin contact is harmless. Some individuals with sensitive skin may experience mild irritation. Wearing gloves when working with the plants for extended periods is a sensible precaution.

**Are foxgloves toxic to dogs?**
Yes. All parts of foxglove are toxic to dogs, and ingestion can cause vomiting, heart arrhythmias, and in severe cases, death. Most dogs leave foxgloves alone, but puppies and indiscriminate chewers are at risk.

**Do foxgloves spread aggressively?**
Foxgloves are biennials that self-seed freely. They can spread through a garden over time, but they are easy to pull out where unwanted. They are not invasive in the ecological sense.

**Can I compost foxglove leaves?**
Yes. The cardiac glycosides break down during composting. Fully composted foxglove material is safe to use in the garden. Do not add uncomposted leaves or stems to animal feed.

**Are there non-toxic foxglove alternatives with a similar look?**
Penstemon and veronica have similar spike-shaped flower forms without the toxicity. They are safe for gardens with children and pets, though they lack the dramatic scale of foxglove.
