Tropical flowers operate at a completely different visual scale from their temperate counterparts. Where a peony or rose speaks in whispers of pink and blush, a heliconia shouts in primary colour. Where a sweet pea delicately trails, a bird of paradise stands six feet tall and commands a room. Working with tropical flowers requires different instincts — and rewards those instincts with something unlike anything else in floristry.

## Bird of Paradise — *Strelitzia reginae*

The bird of paradise is among the most recognisable flowers in the world — its silhouette appears on the Los Angeles city seal and is the official flower of the city. Named for its resemblance to an exotic bird in flight, with orange petals forming the "wings" and a deep blue petal forming the "tongue," it is actually the modified sepals and petals of a South African native plant.

In nature, the bird of paradise is pollinated by sunbirds, which land on the blue petal (which functions as a perch) and are dusted with pollen in the process. This evolutionary design makes the flower extraordinarily architectural — it was built to support weight and maintain a specific pose.

As a cut flower, bird of paradise is exceptional. Vase life of 2–3 weeks is typical; properly cared for plants can produce flowers for 15–20 years. Each stem may have multiple blooms tucked inside the spathe (the green boat-shaped base), which open successively over several weeks. To release a hidden bud, gently reach into the spathe and ease it out.

**In arrangements**: Use as a focal point; single stems in tall cylindrical vases or grouped with ginger lily and protea in large-scale arrangements. Tropical floral design typically uses fewer, bolder stems rather than many small flowers.

## Heliconia — the lobster claw

Heliconias are among the most visually striking plants in existence. Their modified bracts (not technically the flower itself, but the brightly coloured leaf-like structures that surround the actual tiny flowers) grow in dramatic angles and folds in shades of red, orange, yellow, and combinations thereof. The most dramatic species hang in great pendulous clusters 60–90cm long; others grow erect in bright stacked zigzag formations that explain the common name "lobster claw."

Heliconias are native to tropical Central and South America and the Pacific Islands. They're closely related to bananas and birds of paradise — all members of the order Zingiberales — and share a similarly dramatic, architectural quality.

**In arrangements**: Their size and structural quality make them difficult to use in smaller arrangements. They excel in large-scale hotel lobby arrangements, event floristry, and as single statement stems in architectural interiors.

## Anthurium — the painted flower

*Anthurium andraeanum* is among the most commercially important tropical flowers globally, with major cultivation in Hawaii, the Netherlands, and throughout tropical Asia. The glossy, heart-shaped "flower" is actually a modified leaf called a spathe; the actual flowers are the tiny bumps on the finger-like spadix that protrudes from its centre.

Anthuriums are available in a range of colours from deep burgundy-red through coral, salmon, pink, white, and green. The lacquered, artificial-looking surface of the spathe is entirely natural and one of the plant's most distinctive qualities.

**In arrangements**: Anthuriums add a glossy, modern, slightly surreal quality to arrangements. They pair well with tropical greenery (monstera leaves, ti leaves), with other tropical flowers, and with modern sculptural arrangements. Vase life is 3–4 weeks.

## Protea — South Africa's national flower

Proteas are named for the Greek god Proteus, who could change his form at will — a reference to the extraordinary diversity of the genus, which includes several hundred species ranging from ground-hugging shrubs to towering trees, producing flowers that range from the size of a golf ball to the size of a dinner plate.

The most commercially significant cut flower is *Protea cynaroides*, the King Protea — South Africa's national flower. Its enormous, bowl-shaped blooms of pink-red bracts surrounding a central dome of fine flowers can reach 30cm across. They are extraordinary as cut flowers, with a vase life of 2–3 weeks.

Other commercially important protea include *Banksia* (Australian relatives with cylindrical cone-shaped flowers), *Leucospermum* (pincushion proteas with remarkable pin-like flowers), and *Leucadendron* (which produces more foliage-like bracts of silver, green, red, and gold).

Proteas dry exceptionally well, retaining their form and much of their colour for years. This makes them increasingly popular in dried flower arrangements.

## Ginger lily — *Hedychium* and related genera

The flowering gingers include some of the most fragrant tropical flowers available. *Hedychium* species produce tall stems topped with clusters of butterfly-like flowers, often in white or coral with prominent stamens. *Alpinia* (shell ginger) and *Etlingera* (torch ginger) provide more dramatic architectural forms.

Torch ginger (*Etlingera elatior*) is native to Southeast Asia and is used extensively in Malaysian and Indonesian cuisine as well as floristry. Its large, waxy pink-red buds open into spectacular layered flowers that are used in elaborate floral arrangements across the region.

## Tropical foliage: the unsung hero

Tropical arrangements depend as much on foliage as they do on flowers, and several leaves are indispensable:

**Monstera** (*Monstera deliciosa*) — the split leaf or Swiss cheese plant. The iconic perforated leaf has become one of the most recognisable plant images in contemporary design, appearing in arrangements and as a standalone decorative element.

**Ti leaf** (*Cordyline fruticosa*) — long, strapping leaves in green or burgundy-red, used as a structural element and to add height and line to tropical arrangements.

**Heliconia leaf** — the enormous paddle-like leaves of heliconia plants provide dramatic green backdrops for tropical arrangements.

**Banana leaf** — used in vast quantities in Southeast Asian floristry, event design, and food presentation for its graphic simplicity and impressive scale.

## Caring for tropical cut flowers

Tropical flowers evolved in warm, humid conditions and are damaged by:
- **Cold temperatures** — below 10°C causes chilling injury: blackened petals, collapsed stems, early deterioration. Never refrigerate tropical flowers in a standard cool floristry cooler designed for roses
- **Cold water** — use lukewarm water, never cold
- **Cold drafts** — air conditioning directed at tropical arrangements causes rapid deterioration

The ideal environment: warm room temperature (18–22°C), high humidity, bright indirect light, and regular misting for very fragile flowers.

Tropical floristry demands a willingness to think big — in scale, in colour, in form. The reward is arrangements that feel genuinely alive in a way that even the most beautiful temperate flowers sometimes don't achieve.
