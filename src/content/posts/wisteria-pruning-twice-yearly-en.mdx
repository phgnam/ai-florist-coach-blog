## Quick Answer

To get maximum flowers from wisteria, prune twice yearly. In July or August, cut all that summer's whippy green shoots back to 5–6 leaves from the main stem. In January or February, when the plant is bare, cut the same shoots back further to 2–3 buds. This concentrates energy into short flowering spurs instead of leafy growth, doubling next year's bloom.

## Why does an unpruned wisteria flower so poorly?

Wisteria is a vigorous climber that produces two completely different kinds of growth in a season:

- **Whippy long shoots** — fast, leafy, often 1–2 metres long in a single summer. These are the green stems that wave around in the breeze. They produce no flowers in their first year and only a handful if left.
- **Short flowering spurs** — stubby woody shoots, only 5–10 cm long, attached to the main stems. These produce the dangling racemes of flowers next spring.

If you don't prune, the plant pours its energy into the long whippy growth. You get a wisteria the size of a small house, with masses of leaves and almost no flowers — because the plant has not been forced to develop short spurs.

The whole point of pruning is to repeatedly cut back the long growth so that the plant has to make short, woody, flower-bearing spurs instead. The technique is well established (RHS recommends it) but most home gardeners do it once or not at all.

## When exactly do you do the summer prune?

The summer prune happens **in July or August**, about two months after the flowers have finished. The exact week is not critical, but the rules are:

1. Wait until the long whippy shoots are clearly visible — usually 60–90 cm long.
2. Don't wait so long that the wood at the base hardens; the cuts heal best when the shoot is still green and flexible.
3. Avoid pruning in a heatwave (>32 °C) — the plant is stressed and the cuts dry rather than callusing.

For each whippy shoot:

- Count the leaves from the base.
- Cut back to **5–6 leaves** from the point where the shoot joined the main stem.
- The cut should be just above a leaf node, angled away from the bud.

You will be removing 60–80% of every long shoot. The plant looks dramatically tidier afterwards. If you have an old wisteria covering a wall, the summer prune can take 2–3 hours and fill several garden bins.

The summer prune also lets light into the centre of the plant, which encourages the buds along the remaining stub to ripen — which is what produces the flowers next spring.

## What does the winter prune do that the summer prune did not?

The winter prune happens **in January or February**, when the plant is fully bare and you can see the framework clearly.

For each shoot you summer-pruned to 5–6 leaves:

- Find that stub.
- Cut it back further to just **2–3 buds** from the main stem.
- The buds you can see now are fat and round — those are the future flower buds. Aim to leave 2–3 of them, no more.

The winter prune is what separates a wisteria with hundreds of racemes from one with dozens. Without it, the stubs you left in summer will grow out into long whippy shoots again next year and the cycle of vigorous, flowerless growth resumes.

The winter prune is much faster than the summer prune (1 hour for an old plant) because there are no leaves and the structure is obvious. Always use sharp secateurs; wisteria wood is hard and a clean cut heals fastest.

For more on dormant-season pruning of woody climbers, see [propagate roses from cuttings](/posts/propagate-roses-cuttings-en/) for the same principle of dormant-season cuts triggering spring vigour.

## How do you tell flower buds from leaf buds in winter?

This is where the winter prune gets technical. On a wisteria stub in February, you'll see two kinds of buds:

- **Flower buds:** fat, round, plump, often greyish or silver, located on short woody spurs near the older wood.
- **Leaf buds:** thin, pointed, sharp-tipped, located along the longer last-summer growth.

When you cut back to 2–3 buds, the goal is to leave **fat round buds** at the tip of each stub. If the only buds left are pointy leaf buds, cut a bit further back; you should always be able to find at least one round flower bud per stub on a mature plant.

If your wisteria has been planted less than 5 years, you may struggle to find flower buds at all — young wisteria can take 5–10 years before flowering reliably. Patience plus annual pruning is the only solution; no fertiliser will speed this up.

## What other care helps wisteria flower more?

Beyond the twice-yearly prune, three things matter:

- **Avoid high-nitrogen fertiliser.** Lawn feed, manure, and general-purpose plant food drive leafy growth at the cost of flowers. Use a high-potassium feed (the same tomato feed used for [petunias and other heavy bloomers](/posts/deadhead-petunias-continuous-bloom-en/)) once in spring.
- **Don't move it.** Wisteria roots resent disturbance. Once planted, leave it.
- **Make sure it gets full sun.** A wisteria in dappled shade will grow vigorous foliage but flower poorly. South or west-facing wall is ideal.

If your wisteria is mature, in full sun, has been pruned correctly for 2 years, and still flowers poorly, the variety may simply be a non-flowering seedling type sold in the past. Replace with a named cultivar grafted onto a known rootstock — `Wisteria sinensis 'Prolific'` or `Wisteria floribunda 'Multijuga'` are reliable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can you prune wisteria once instead of twice a year?

You can, but you'll get fewer flowers. A single winter prune leaves the plant with too much summer foliage, which shades the developing flower buds and reduces next year's bloom. Two prunes — July plus January — is the proven combination. If you must skip one, skip the winter prune; the summer one matters more.

### Will pruning wisteria too hard kill it?

No. Wisteria is exceptionally vigorous and tolerates very hard pruning, including cutting old wood back to a basic framework. If the plant has gone wild, a renovation prune in winter — taking it back to a spare stub-and-framework structure — will reset it. You will lose one year's flowers but the plant will rebuild and flower again the following year.

### Why does my wisteria have masses of leaves but no flowers?

Three usual causes: not enough sun (less than 6 hours of direct sun); too much nitrogen feed; or insufficient pruning, allowing the plant to keep producing whippy growth instead of woody flowering spurs. Switch to twice-yearly pruning for two seasons and the flowers usually return.

### Is it too late to prune wisteria in March or April?

A late winter prune in early March is fine; the plant has not yet broken bud. By late March or April, when the buds are swelling and the racemes are starting to extend, leave it alone — pruning then will remove the flowers you were trying to encourage. Wait until the July summer prune.

### How long until a newly planted wisteria flowers?

A grafted, named cultivar typically flowers in years 3–5 after planting. A seed-grown wisteria may take 7–15 years and may flower poorly even then. Always buy a grafted plant with a named variety from a reputable nursery; the cost difference is worth it.
