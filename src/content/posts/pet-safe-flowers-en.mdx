## Quick Answer

Roses, sunflowers, orchids, gerbera daisies, freesia, snapdragons, lisianthus, and stock are safe for cats and dogs. Lilies — including all true lilies and daylilies — are extremely dangerous to cats and can cause fatal kidney failure. Tulips, daffodils, hyacinths, oleander, foxglove, and lily of the valley are toxic to both species.

## Why does flower toxicity matter for cut flowers, not just garden plants?

Many cut-flower toxicity guides are written for the *garden* — the assumption being that a curious dog might dig up a tulip bulb. The cut-flower problem is different and often worse. A bouquet sits at nose height on a table, the petals fall and are eaten, and — most dangerously of all — **lily pollen lands on a cat's fur and is licked off during grooming**. A cat does not have to bite a lily to be poisoned by one.

Florists, gift recipients, and pet owners all benefit from knowing which flowers belong in a multi-pet household and which absolutely do not. The reference below is based on the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center's plant database, cross-checked against the per-flower care guides already on this site (links inline).

## Which cut flowers are dangerous to cats?

The single most important rule for cat households: **no lilies of any kind, ever**. This is not a "keep them out of reach" situation — pollen drift alone can kill a cat.

| Flower | Severity for cats | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **All true lilies** (Asiatic, Oriental, Tiger, Easter, Stargazer) — see [lily care guide](/posts/lily-care-guide-en/) | **EXTREME** | Acute kidney failure within 24–72 hours, often fatal even with treatment. Pollen, water, and petals all toxic. |
| **Daylily** (Hemerocallis) | **EXTREME** | Same kidney mechanism as true lilies. |
| **Lily of the valley** | **EXTREME** | Cardiac glycosides — heart-rhythm collapse. Not actually a lily but acts like one. |
| **Oleander** | **EXTREME** | Cardiac glycosides; tiny amount lethal. |
| **Foxglove** (Digitalis) | **EXTREME** | Cardiac glycosides. |
| **Autumn crocus** (Colchicum) | **EXTREME** | Multi-organ failure. The spring crocus is mildly toxic; *autumn* crocus is severe. |
| **Cyclamen** (tubers especially) | High | Severe vomiting, heart-rhythm changes. |
| **Azalea / Rhododendron** | High | Vomiting, weakness, cardiac effects. |
| **Tulip / Hyacinth / Daffodil** | Moderate–High | Bulbs are the worst; cut stems still cause vomiting and drooling. |
| **Iris** | Moderate | GI upset, drooling, lethargy. |
| **Chrysanthemum** | Moderate | Vomiting, drooling, ataxia. |
| **Hydrangea** | Mild–Moderate | Vomiting, diarrhoea. |
| **Carnation** | Mild | Mild GI upset, dermatitis. |
| **Sweet pea** (seeds especially) | Mild–Moderate | Tremors, weakness in larger doses. |
| **Eucalyptus** (filler foliage) | Mild–Moderate | Salivation, vomiting, weakness. |

## Which cut flowers are dangerous to dogs?

Dogs handle lilies far better than cats — they typically experience GI upset rather than kidney failure — but most of the table above still applies, just at lower severity. Dogs are more likely than cats to eat large amounts of a flower and so are at higher risk for bulb-related poisoning.

| Flower | Severity for dogs | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Oleander** | **EXTREME** | Cardiac glycosides; small amounts lethal. |
| **Foxglove** | **EXTREME** | Cardiac glycosides. |
| **Lily of the valley** | **EXTREME** | Cardiac glycosides. |
| **Autumn crocus** | **EXTREME** | Multi-organ failure. |
| **Cyclamen** | High | Severe GI signs, possible cardiac effects. |
| **Azalea / Rhododendron** | High | Vomiting, weakness. |
| **Tulip / Hyacinth / Daffodil bulbs** | High | Bulbs are the danger zone; cut flowers cause GI upset. |
| **Sago palm** (sometimes used as foliage) | **EXTREME** | Liver failure. |
| **True lilies / Daylilies** | Moderate | GI upset, not the kidney crisis seen in cats. |
| **Iris** | Moderate | GI upset. |
| **Chrysanthemum** | Moderate | Vomiting, drooling, ataxia. |
| **Hydrangea** | Mild–Moderate | GI upset; cyanide compounds in large amounts. |
| **Carnation** | Mild | Mild GI upset. |
| **Begonia** | Moderate | Mouth irritation, vomiting (tubers worst). |

## Which cut flowers are safe for both cats and dogs?

Every flower on this list is non-toxic to both cats and dogs per the ASPCA database. They are safe to have in a vase at nose height, and safe if a curious pet chews a petal. "Non-toxic" does not mean "edible" — any plant material in quantity can cause mild GI upset — it means "not dangerous in normal exposure."

| Flower | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Rose** (incl. garden roses) — see [rose care](/posts/rose-care/) | The single safest "florist standard". Watch for thorns rather than toxicity. |
| **Sunflower** — see [sunflower growing guide](/posts/sunflower-growing-guide-en/) | Non-toxic petals and seeds. |
| **Orchid** (Phalaenopsis, Dendrobium, Cymbidium) — see [orchid care guide](/posts/orchid-care-guide-en/) | Safe for both species. |
| **Gerbera daisy** | Safe; cheerful and long-lasting. |
| **Freesia** | Safe and fragrant. |
| **Snapdragon** | Safe; tall feature flower. |
| **Lisianthus** (Eustoma) | Safe; rose-substitute look. |
| **Stock** (Matthiola) | Safe; strongly scented. |
| **Camellia** | Safe; long-lasting bloom. |
| **Zinnia** | Safe; vibrant cottage-garden colour. |
| **Aster** | Safe; daisy-form filler. |
| **Calendula** (pot marigold) | Safe; some pets will eat petals with no consequences. |
| **Statice** | Safe; classic dried-flower filler. |
| **Wax flower** (Chamelaucium) | Safe; tiny-flowered filler. |

For mixed bouquets, a reliable rule of thumb is to build around rose + sunflower + orchid + freesia and use only filler from the second list. ## What should I do if my pet eats a flower?

Three things, in order:

1. **Identify the flower.** Take the plant or a photo; the species is what determines treatment. (This is one of the few situations where AI flower identification is genuinely useful — see [Can AI identify a flower from a photo?](/posts/ai-flower-identification-en/) for what to expect from accuracy.)
2. **Phone a poison control hotline immediately.** In the US, the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center is **(888) 426-4435** (a fee applies but it's worth it). In the UK, the Animal PoisonLine is **01202 509000**. Don't wait for symptoms — for cats and lilies in particular, treatment in the first 6 hours dramatically improves outcomes.
3. **Get to a vet — bring the plant.** Even if your pet seems fine, severe poisoning (lily, oleander, foxglove, autumn crocus) can have a delayed onset. Don't wait for vomiting before acting.

Do **not** induce vomiting at home unless a poison control vet specifically tells you to. With some toxins (e.g. plant material with sharp edges, caustic compounds) vomiting causes more damage than the original ingestion.

## How can a florist offer a "pet-safe" bouquet without limiting the design?

Easier than most florists assume. The pet-safe list above contains a focal flower (rose, sunflower, lisianthus), a feature flower (orchid, snapdragon), a filler (freesia, stock, statice, wax flower), and foliage that's also safe (eucalyptus is mildly toxic — substitute leatherleaf fern, salal, or pittosporum). That gives the same structural template most modern bouquets are built on.

The only categories you genuinely lose are: lilies (a clean swap for orchids gives a similar focal-flower presence) and tulips/daffodils in spring (swap for ranunculus — *which is mildly toxic* — or, more conservatively, freesia and snapdragons). ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are roses safe for cats and dogs?**
Yes. Roses are listed as non-toxic by the ASPCA for both cats and dogs. The thorns are a bigger practical concern than the petals — a cat batting at a rose can scratch its eye or paw pads. Strip thorns from any rose stem you display at pet height.

**Why are lilies so dangerous to cats specifically?**
Cats lack the liver enzymes needed to metabolise the toxin in true lilies (Lilium spp.) and daylilies (Hemerocallis spp.). The toxin causes acute kidney tubular necrosis. Even pollen brushed onto fur and ingested during grooming can be fatal. There is no equivalent risk pathway in dogs.

**Are sunflowers and tulips safe to give to a pet owner?**
Sunflowers are safe. Tulips are not — the bulb is the most toxic part, but cut tulip stems cause drooling, vomiting, and GI upset in cats and dogs. Always check before sending mixed bouquets to pet households.

**My cat licked lily pollen. What do I do?**
Treat it as a medical emergency. Wash the pollen off the fur immediately to prevent further ingestion (use a damp cloth, not water), and call a poison control hotline or get to a vet within the hour. Treatment within the first 6 hours dramatically improves outcomes; waiting for symptoms is too late.

**Are dried flowers safer than fresh ones?**
Mostly yes, because the toxic compounds degrade somewhat as the flower dries — but not always. Dried lilies still pose a pollen risk to cats; dried oleander and foxglove are still highly toxic. Treat any dried arrangement to the same toxicity check as the fresh equivalent.

**What's the safest "florist's bouquet" recipe for a household with a cat?**
Roses + lisianthus + freesia + stock + wax flower, built around eucalyptus-free foliage (leatherleaf fern, salal, pittosporum). All non-toxic, structurally complete, available year-round in most markets, and visually indistinguishable from a conventional florist's design.
