## Quick Answer

A mandevilla (*Mandevilla*, formerly *Dipladenia*) with abundant foliage but few or no flowers usually has one of three problems: **too much nitrogen** (general or lawn fertiliser causing leafy growth at the expense of bloom), **not enough sun** (mandevilla needs 6+ hours of direct sun for prolific bloom), or **lack of pruning** (flowers form on new growth, so untipped plants stop producing). The fix: **switch to a high-phosphorus "bloom-boost" fertiliser** (5-30-5 or similar), **move to a sunnier spot** (south or west exposure), and **pinch growing tips** every 2–3 weeks to trigger lateral branching. Most plants begin blooming again within 4–6 weeks of correcting these issues.

## What kind of mandevilla do you have?

The mandevilla market is confused — multiple genera and species are sold under similar names, with significant differences in growth habit and bloom behaviour.

**Two main types:**

**True mandevilla (*Mandevilla* spp., especially *M. × amabilis*):**

- **Vining habit** — twining stems reach 3–6 metres in one season.
- **Large flowers** (8–10 cm wide) with prominent yellow throats.
- **Pink, red, or white** depending on cultivar.
- **Needs trellis or support** to climb.
- **Common cultivars:** "Alice du Pont" (classic pink), "Red Riding Hood", "Sun Parasol" series.

**"Dipladenia" (botanically also *Mandevilla*, especially *M. sanderi*):**

- **Bushy mounding habit** — trails to 60 cm but doesn't truly climb.
- **Smaller flowers** (5–7 cm wide) with subtler throats.
- **Pink, red, white, or yellow**.
- **No support needed** — works in hanging baskets and as patio plants.
- **Common cultivars:** "Rio" series, "Sundenia" series.

**Why this matters for "not flowering":**

- **True mandevilla** flowers on **new growth** of climbing stems — needs warmth + length.
- **Dipladenia** flowers on **lateral branches** — needs pinching to multiply branches.
- **Care is similar** but bloom mechanisms differ subtly.

If your plant is vining vigorously up a trellis but not flowering, it's likely true mandevilla and needs different attention than a bushy "dipladenia" with weak bloom.

## Are you giving it the wrong fertiliser?

Excess nitrogen is **the single most common cause** of non-flowering mandevilla. The plant produces lush leafy growth that looks healthy but suppresses flower development.

**The nitrogen problem:**

- **High-nitrogen fertiliser** (10-10-10, 20-10-10) feeds leafy growth.
- **Lawn fertiliser overspray** from adjacent grass beds.
- **Generic houseplant fertiliser** is often nitrogen-heavy.
- **Compost or fresh manure** in the soil supplies excess nitrogen.

**The right fertiliser for blooming mandevilla:**

- **High-phosphorus, lower-nitrogen formula:** 5-30-5, 10-30-20, or "bloom-boost" formulas.
- **Apply at half-strength every 2 weeks** during active growing season (May–September).
- **Stop fertilising** in autumn as the plant prepares for winter dormancy.
- **Resume light feeding** in spring as new growth begins.

**Why phosphorus helps:**

- **Phosphorus** is the macronutrient most directly linked to flower formation.
- **High-phosphorus fertiliser** signals the plant to invest in flowers rather than vegetation.
- **Visible response** within 2–4 weeks of switching fertiliser.

**Visible signs of nitrogen excess:**

- **Dark green, lush, oversized leaves**.
- **Long stretches of stem between leaves** (long internodes).
- **Vigorous tip growth** but few or no flower buds.
- **Lower leaves** may yellow as nitrogen is shunted to top growth.

**Visible signs of correct fertilising:**

- **Medium green, normally-sized leaves**.
- **Compact internodes** (leaves close together).
- **Both vegetative and flower growth** balanced.
- **Reliable bud set** at most leaf axils.

**The "starve to bloom" trick:**

For severely overfed mandevilla, **stop fertilising for 6–8 weeks** while increasing light and pruning. The plant uses up internal nitrogen reserves and begins flowering. **Resume fertilising at the new bloom-boost formula** once buds are forming.

**Container plants vs. in-ground:**

- **Container mandevilla** depletes nutrients faster — fertilise more often (every 2 weeks).
- **In-ground mandevilla** in rich soil may need no fertiliser at all — just refresh mulch annually.
- **Container plants in poor potting mix** may need more frequent feeding.

## How much sun does it really need?

Mandevilla is often grown as a partial-shade plant because it tolerates shade. But **toleration ≠ thriving**. For abundant bloom, it needs significantly more sun than most catalogue descriptions suggest.

**Sun requirements:**

- **6+ hours of direct sun daily** for prolific bloom.
- **South or west exposure** is best in temperate gardens.
- **Filtered afternoon sun** acceptable in very hot zones (USDA 9+) where leaves may scorch.
- **Less than 4 hours direct sun** = sparse bloom or no bloom.

**Indicators of insufficient light:**

- **Long stretches of stem between leaves**.
- **Pale or yellowish leaves** (light-deprived chlorosis).
- **Weak twining** — stems flop instead of climbing vigorously.
- **Few or no flower buds** despite healthy foliage.
- **Buds that form but drop** before opening.

**Indicators of correct light:**

- **Compact growth** with leaves close together.
- **Strong dark-green leaves**.
- **Vigorous twining** of climbing stems.
- **Continuous bud formation** during growing season.
- **Flowers open fully and last 3–5 days each**.

**Moving for more sun:**

- **Container plants** can be repositioned easily — try a sunnier spot for 4 weeks and observe.
- **In-ground plants** are difficult to move; consider transplanting in early spring before active growth.
- **Trellis position** matters — if vine is climbing into shade above, prune the top to keep growth in the sunny zone.

**Indoor mandevilla:**

- **Most homes are too dark** for blooming mandevilla.
- **South-facing window with direct sun** is essential.
- **Supplementary grow lights** (12 hours daily) can help.
- **Most "indoor mandevilla"** end up as foliage plants only.

**Summer outdoor strategy:**

In cold-winter zones where mandevilla is grown as an annual or overwintered indoors:

- **April–May:** start hardening off outside as nights warm.
- **May–September:** full sun outdoor location for blooming.
- **October:** move back indoors before frost.
- **November–March:** indoor location with maximum available light.

This rotation accepts that bloom only happens during the outdoor period, which is normal for the plant in temperate zones.

## How does pruning affect bloom?

Mandevilla flowers form on new growth. **An untouched plant produces fewer flowers** than a regularly pruned one because the plant doesn't trigger the lateral branching that creates more flowering wood.

**The branching principle:**

- **Each pinch of a stem tip** triggers 2 new lateral branches at the leaf axils below.
- **Each new branch** can produce flowers.
- **Compounding:** 1 main stem pinched → 2 branches → each pinched → 4 branches → 8 → ...
- **A regularly pinched plant** has 4–8x more flowering points than an unpinched one.

**Pinching technique:**

1. **Identify a stem tip** that has grown 10–15 cm without branching.
2. **Pinch off** the top 2 leaves with thumb and finger, or use small scissors.
3. **Pinch every 2–3 weeks** during the active growing season.
4. **Stop pinching** 6 weeks before you want the plant to flower most heavily.

**For climbing true mandevilla:**

- **Don't pinch every stem** — let some climb to fill the trellis.
- **Pinch lateral side-shoots** to bush them out.
- **Remove leggy unwanted growth** as you go.
- **Annual hard pruning** in early spring (cut back to 30 cm of established stem) rejuvenates the plant.

**For bushy dipladenia:**

- **Pinch all stem tips** every 2–3 weeks.
- **Goal:** create a dense rounded mound of branches.
- **More branches = more flowers**.
- **Annual hard prune** in early spring (cut back by half) maintains shape.

**Spring rejuvenation prune:**

- **Best timing:** early spring before active growth begins.
- **Cut back climbing stems** to 30–50 cm of healthy wood.
- **Remove all weak twiggy growth**.
- **Result:** vigorous new growth and abundant bloom 6–10 weeks later.

**Don't prune during active flowering:**

- **Cutting flowering stems** removes both current and future blooms.
- **Wait until autumn rest period** for major cuts.
- **Light pinching** of non-flowering tips is acceptable year-round.

**The "fewer pruning = fewer flowers" failure mode:**

A common mandevilla mistake is leaving the plant alone "because it's growing well." The plant develops long unbranched stems with sparse bloom. **The fix:** start pinching and pruning aggressively; bloom typically returns within 6–8 weeks.

## What about temperature and humidity?

Mandevilla is tropical and bloom is heavily affected by temperature. Cold conditions stop flowering; very hot conditions can also reduce bloom (counterintuitively).

**Temperature ranges:**

- **Active growth and bloom:** 21–29°C daytime, 18–24°C nighttime.
- **Below 15°C:** plant stops growing and bud formation halts.
- **Below 10°C:** leaves yellow and drop.
- **Below 4°C:** plant suffers permanent damage.
- **Above 35°C:** bloom slows; leaves may scorch in direct sun.

**Indicators of temperature issues:**

- **Slow growth in spring:** wait until consistently above 18°C night temperatures.
- **Leaf drop in autumn:** normal as temperatures cool below 15°C.
- **Bud drop in summer:** likely too hot; provide afternoon shade.
- **Yellow leaves with no obvious cause:** temperature stress.

**Humidity:**

- **40–70% relative humidity** ideal.
- **Below 30% (very dry indoor heating):** spider mite risk, slow growth.
- **Above 80%:** fungal disease risk if air circulation is poor.
- **Outdoor humidity** is rarely an issue except in very arid zones.

**Indoor humidity solutions:**

- **Pebble tray** under the pot.
- **Group with other plants** for shared humidity.
- **Small humidifier** in the room.
- **Avoid misting** — water on flowers causes spotting.

**Wind:**

- **Protect from strong wind** in container locations.
- **Wind dries out leaves rapidly** and reduces bud set.
- **Shelter near walls or fences** improves bloom.

**Cold protection:**

In cold-winter zones, mandevilla must be brought indoors or treated as an annual:

- **Bring indoors before first frost** (typically late September–early October).
- **Acclimatise** for 2 weeks by gradually reducing outdoor exposure.
- **Indoor location** with maximum light and minimum heating.
- **Reduce watering** significantly — plant goes semi-dormant.
- **Move outdoors** again after last spring frost when nights stay above 12°C.

This indoor overwintering is more demanding than treating as annual, but preserves the plant for multiple years.

## Could it be a watering issue?

Watering affects bloom subtly but consistently. Both extremes — too wet and too dry — reduce flowering, though the symptoms differ.

**The watering rule for mandevilla:**

- **Allow top 2–3 cm of soil to dry** between waterings.
- **Then water thoroughly** until water drains from the bottom.
- **Frequency:** every 3–5 days in summer, every 7–10 days in cooler weather, less in winter.

**Overwatering symptoms:**

- **Yellow leaves throughout the plant**.
- **Soft mushy stems** at base.
- **Soil constantly wet, mossy growth** on surface.
- **Reduced bud set**; existing buds drop.
- **Root rot** in severe cases (white fungal threads visible).

**Underwatering symptoms:**

- **Leaf wilt** that recovers when watered.
- **Flower buds drop** before opening.
- **Lower leaves yellow and drop**.
- **Stunted growth** with crispy leaf edges.

**Container drainage:**

- **Drainage holes mandatory** — plant in pots with multiple drainage holes.
- **Clay or terracotta pots** breathe and dry between waterings; ideal for mandevilla.
- **Plastic pots** retain moisture longer; water less frequently.
- **Saucers under pots** must be drained after each watering — never let plant sit in standing water.

**Soil mix:**

- **Well-draining potting mix** with added perlite or sand.
- **Slightly acidic to neutral pH** (6.0–7.0).
- **Refresh annually** by adding fresh compost on top, or repot every 2–3 years.

**Watering during bloom:**

- **Consistent moisture** during active flowering produces longer-lasting blooms.
- **Avoid splashing flowers** when watering — wet petals spot.
- **Water at soil level** with watering can or drip system.

**Signs of correct watering:**

- **Even green leaves**.
- **Firm but not soggy soil** below surface.
- **Steady bloom** through the season.
- **Vigorous but not lush growth**.

**The "missed week" recovery:**

If you've gone too long between waterings (plant wilted, soil bone dry):

1. **Water thoroughly** — pour water until it drains from the bottom.
2. **Wait 30 minutes** and water again.
3. **Don't fertilise** for 2 weeks while plant recovers.
4. **Plant typically resumes bloom** within 1–2 weeks if no permanent damage.

## Is your mandevilla too young or too old?

Plant maturity affects bloom potential. New plants need time to establish; old neglected plants may need rejuvenation.

**Young plants (1st year):**

- **Often bloom less than expected** in their first season.
- **Plant is establishing roots** rather than maximising bloom.
- **Light bloom in first year** is normal even with perfect care.
- **Be patient** — second year typically much better.

**Established plants (years 2–5):**

- **Best bloom years** for most mandevilla.
- **Vigorous growth and abundant flowers** with proper care.
- **Need annual feeding and pruning** to maintain.

**Older plants (year 6+):**

- **May begin declining** if not refreshed.
- **Annual rejuvenation pruning** becomes more important.
- **Repotting or in-ground refresh** with new soil/compost helps.
- **Plants can live 10+ years** with good care.

**Rejuvenating an old underperforming plant:**

1. **Hard prune in early spring** — cut back to 30 cm of main stems.
2. **Repot or replenish soil** with fresh potting mix or compost.
3. **Switch to bloom-boost fertiliser** at half-strength.
4. **Move to brighter location**.
5. **Resume regular pinching** of new growth.
6. **Result:** typically returns to vigorous bloom by midsummer.

**Replacing vs. rejuvenating:**

- **Plants over 8 years** may not respond to rejuvenation.
- **Container plants in same potting mix for 3+ years** likely need full repotting.
- **Take cuttings** from healthy stems before replacing — propagate the same plant.

**Cuttings propagation:**

1. **Take 10–15 cm cuttings** in spring or summer.
2. **Remove lower leaves**, dip cut end in rooting hormone.
3. **Insert into moist potting mix** and cover with plastic for humidity.
4. **Roots form in 4–8 weeks**.
5. **Transplant to permanent pot** when roots are 5+ cm long.

This way you can preserve a treasured plant indefinitely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will my mandevilla bloom indoors year-round?

**Probably not.** Most home interior light is insufficient for continuous mandevilla bloom. **Best results** come from outdoor sun during the growing season (May–September) with indoor overwintering as a foliage plant. **For winter bloom**, you'd need a south-facing window with direct sun plus supplemental grow lights — possible but uncommon. Most mandevilla owners accept seasonal bloom and treat winter as a rest period.

### How long do individual mandevilla flowers last?

**Each individual flower lasts 3–5 days** before naturally fading. **The plant produces continuous bloom** during the growing season — old flowers fade as new buds open, giving the appearance of constant flowers. **Healthy plants** bloom from late spring through autumn frost; **stressed plants** produce sporadic short-lived blooms.

### Should I deadhead spent mandevilla flowers?

**Optional but helpful.** **Deadheading** (removing spent flowers) keeps the plant tidy and may slightly increase bloom by preventing seed-pod development. **Pinch off the entire spent flower** including the calyx (small green base). **Don't cut entire flowering stems** unless they're severely past peak — cutting removes future buds along the same stem.

### Why are my mandevilla leaves turning yellow but no flowers dropping?

**Most common causes:** overwatering (most likely), nutrient deficiency (especially iron in alkaline soil), or natural autumn senescence as cooler weather approaches. **Check soil moisture first** — if constantly wet, reduce watering. **If soil moisture is correct**, try iron supplement or repot in fresh slightly acidic potting mix.

### Are mandevilla toxic to pets?

**Yes — mandevilla sap is mildly toxic** to dogs, cats, and humans, causing skin irritation and stomach upset if ingested. **Plant in areas pets don't access.** **Wear gloves** when pruning to avoid sap contact with skin. Symptoms in pets are typically mild (drooling, vomiting) but contact your vet if a large amount is ingested.

### Can I grow mandevilla in zone 5–6 outdoors permanently?

**No — mandevilla is hardy only to USDA zone 9+** (winters above −7°C). **In zones 5–6**, mandevilla must be grown as an annual or brought indoors for winter. **Indoor overwintering** requires a bright spot, minimum 10°C, and reduced watering. **Annual replacement** is also common — buy a new plant each spring rather than overwinter.
