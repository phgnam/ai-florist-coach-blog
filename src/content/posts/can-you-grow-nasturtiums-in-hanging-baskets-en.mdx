## Quick Answer

Yes, nasturtiums grow very well in hanging baskets, especially trailing varieties. Use a free-draining mix, avoid high-nitrogen fertiliser, give them at least 5-6 hours of sun, and water when the top few centimetres dry. Too much feed makes leafy baskets with fewer flowers.

## Why are nasturtiums good for hanging baskets?

Nasturtiums are good hanging basket flowers because they naturally trail, spill, and soften hard edges. Their round leaves cover the basket quickly, while the flowers hang forward where they are easy to see. That makes them useful for balconies, porches, railings, and small gardens where floor space is limited.

They also earn their space. Nasturtium flowers and leaves are edible, with a peppery flavour that works in salads and savoury dishes. For more food-safe flower options, see the [edible flowers guide](/posts/edible-flowers-guide-en/). The blooms also attract bees and other pollinators, so they fit well into a small wildlife-friendly planting plan alongside other [flowers for bees](/posts/flowers-for-bees-en/) and [flowers for butterflies](/posts/flowers-for-butterflies-en/).

The main limitation is that nasturtiums dislike pampering. A basket filled with rich compost and fed every week can become a huge green waterfall with only scattered flowers. Slightly lean conditions produce a better balance of leaves and bloom.

## Which nasturtium varieties work best in hanging baskets?

Choose trailing or semi-trailing nasturtiums rather than compact bush types. Trailing varieties can spill 60-150 cm from a basket, depending on cultivar, pot size, and weather. Compact types still flower, but they make a mound rather than a curtain.

Good basket choices include classic trailing mixes, "Jewel of Africa" types, and smaller trailing cultivars sold for containers. If the basket is exposed to wind, choose a semi-trailing type so the stems do not whip around too harshly. For a rail planter or small balcony, compact trailing nasturtiums are easier to manage than very long vines.

Nasturtiums are also one of the [fastest flowers from seed](/posts/fastest-flowers-from-seed-en/), so buying plug plants is optional. Direct sowing into the basket often works better than transplanting because nasturtiums dislike root disturbance.

## What basket size and compost do nasturtiums need?

Use a basket at least 30 cm wide for a full display. A 35-40 cm basket is easier to keep watered in summer and gives trailing varieties enough root space. Very small baskets dry too quickly and make nasturtiums flower for a shorter period.

The potting mix should drain freely. Use a general peat-free or multipurpose compost and add perlite, grit, or fine bark if it feels dense. Do not fill the basket with heavy garden soil because it compacts, drains poorly, and can pull away from the basket edge when dry.

Avoid rich manure-based compost and high-nitrogen fertiliser. Nasturtiums flower best in modest fertility. If your baskets usually need a weekly liquid feed for petunias or calibrachoa, treat nasturtiums differently. Feed lightly only if leaves turn pale, and choose a low-nitrogen or tomato-style feed rather than a lawn-type fertiliser.

## How should you sow nasturtium seeds in a hanging basket?

Sow nasturtium seeds directly into the basket after frost risk has passed and nights are reliably mild. Push each seed about 1.5-2 cm deep into moist compost. In a 30 cm basket, use 4-5 seeds spaced around the edge and one near the centre. In a 40 cm basket, use 6-8 seeds.

Soaking seeds overnight can speed germination, but it is not essential. Keep the compost evenly moist until seedlings appear. Once seedlings have several leaves, thin only the weakest ones if the basket is crowded.

If you start seeds indoors, use biodegradable pots or deep modules and transplant before roots become tangled. Nasturtiums resent being teased apart. ## How much sun and water do basket nasturtiums need?

Nasturtiums flower best with at least 5-6 hours of direct sun. Morning sun with light afternoon shade is ideal in hot climates. In too much shade, the leaves stay attractive but flowering drops sharply.

Water when the top 2-3 cm of compost feels dry. Hanging baskets dry faster than pots on the ground because wind reaches the sides and base. In warm weather, that can mean daily watering. In cool or rainy weather, wait until the compost actually starts to dry.

Water deeply until the basket drains. A quick splash wets the surface but leaves the root ball dry. If the basket becomes very dry and water runs straight through, take it down and soak it in a bucket or tray for 20 minutes, then let it drain before rehanging.

## How do you keep nasturtiums flowering instead of only making leaves?

Keep feeding low, give strong light, and remove seed pods if you want more flowers. Nasturtiums are annuals, so once they set plenty of seed, they begin to slow down. Picking flowers and removing old seed pods keeps the plant in a more active flowering mode.

Do not mistake leaf size for plant health. Huge soft leaves can mean the basket is too rich or too shaded. The best flowering baskets often look slightly leaner, with smaller leaves and more visible blooms.

If your goal is a mixed edible and ornamental basket, pair nasturtiums with other restrained plants rather than heavy feeders. Calendula, violas, and compact herbs can work. If you are using flowers around food crops, see the guide to [flowers around vegetables](/posts/what-flowers-should-you-plant-around-vegetables-en/) for companion planting ideas that include nasturtiums, calendula, marigolds, and alyssum.

## What problems affect nasturtiums in hanging baskets?

Aphids are the most common pest. They cluster on soft new growth and flower buds. A firm spray of water removes many of them, and small infestations can be pinched out. Nasturtiums are sometimes used as trap plants near vegetables because aphids prefer them, which is useful only if you check and manage the plants regularly.

Yellow leaves usually mean one of three things: natural ageing, drought stress, or waterlogged compost. Remove old yellow leaves from the base so air can move through the basket. If the compost smells sour or stays wet for days, improve drainage and reduce watering.

Heat can also shorten the display. In very hot summers, nasturtiums may pause flowering, make smaller leaves, or decline. Move baskets out of reflected afternoon heat if possible. For broader hot-weather choices, compare them with [flowers that handle heatwaves](/posts/flowers-that-handle-heatwaves-en/).

## Can nasturtiums share a hanging basket with other flowers?

Yes, but choose companions carefully. Nasturtiums grow fast and can overwhelm slower, smaller plants. They work best with flowers that tolerate similar sun, moderate watering, and not-too-rich compost.

Good companions include trailing alyssum, compact calendula, violas in cool weather, and small herbs such as thyme. Avoid pairing nasturtiums with very hungry basket plants that need constant feeding. Petunias and calibrachoa can work visually, but their feeding needs are different, so the basket often becomes harder to manage.

For a fuller balcony display, use nasturtiums as the spiller plant in the [thriller-filler-spiller formula](/posts/make-balcony-flowers-look-full-fast/). Let them trail from the front edge while a compact upright plant fills the centre.

## What are the most common questions about nasturtiums in hanging baskets?

**Do nasturtiums come back every year in hanging baskets?**
Usually no. Nasturtiums are grown as annuals in most climates. They may self-seed in mild regions, but hanging baskets are normally replanted each year.

**Can you eat nasturtiums from a hanging basket?**
Yes, if the plants have not been treated with unsafe pesticides or ornamental-only sprays. Flowers, leaves, and green seed pods are edible and peppery.

**Why are my nasturtiums all leaves and no flowers?**
The usual causes are too much nitrogen, too much shade, or a basket that is too rich. Stop feeding, move the basket brighter, and wait for leaner new growth.

**Should you deadhead nasturtiums?**
Yes, if you want more flowers for longer. Remove faded blooms and developing seed pods so the plant keeps producing buds instead of finishing its seed cycle.
