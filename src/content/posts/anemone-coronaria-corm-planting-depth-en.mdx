## Quick Answer

Plant *Anemone coronaria* corms 5 cm deep and 10–12 cm apart, with the slightly knobbly, pointed end facing up. The smoother, dimpled side is the bottom. If you cannot tell, plant the corm on its side — the shoot will reorient itself toward the surface. Soak corms in cool water for 3–4 hours before planting to rehydrate them; longer than 6 hours risks rotting. Expect the first flowers about 12–14 weeks later.

## What is *Anemone coronaria* and why do these corms look so confusing?

*Anemone coronaria* is the species behind several of the most popular cut-flower anemones, including the De Caen and St Brigid groups. Florists call them "poppy anemones" because the saucer-shaped flowers — in shades of red, blue, purple, pink, and white, all with a contrasting dark centre — look like flat-faced poppies on wiry stems.

The plant grows from a small, dried-up looking storage organ called a corm. To the eye these corms look like crumbled, irregular nuggets — nothing like the neat onion shape of a tulip bulb or the chunky tuber of a dahlia. That is normal. *A. coronaria* corms are deliberately dried hard for transport and shipping, then revived by soaking and a cool start.

## How can I tell which way is up on an anemone corm?

Anemone corms do not have an obvious pointy top like a tulip. Look for these features instead:

**The shoot end (top)** is usually slightly knobbly with a small raised crown or a paler scar where the previous season's stems detached.

**The root end (bottom)** is smoother, often with shallow circular dimples or a flat patch where the old roots emerged.

If the corm is genuinely unreadable, plant it on its side and let the shoot find its own way up. Anemone shoots are strongly negatively gravitropic and will turn upward within a centimetre or two of growth. Sideways planting wastes only a few days at most.

What you should not do is force a guess and plant the corm with the smooth side up. A corm planted upside down can still produce flowers but the shoot has to push twice as far through the soil and the plant emerges weak.

## Should I soak the corms before planting?

Yes. Dry-shipped *A. coronaria* corms perform much better if rehydrated for a few hours before they hit the soil.

The protocol:

1. **Cover the corms with cool water** in a shallow tray.
2. **Optional: add a teaspoon of liquid seaweed** per litre to encourage early rooting.
3. **Soak for 3–4 hours**, never longer than 6.
4. **Lift them out** before they go visibly mushy or develop a sour smell.
5. **Pat dry briefly** and plant immediately.

Soaked corms double in size and feel firm, slightly springy. They start producing roots within days of planting, while unsoaked corms can take two weeks to wake up. Skipping the soak almost always reduces the eventual flower count.

## How deep and how far apart should the corms be planted?

The classic spacing for cut-flower production is the easiest to remember:

- **Depth:** 5 cm of soil over the top of the corm
- **Spacing:** 10–12 cm apart in all directions
- **Bed width:** 90–120 cm so you can reach the centre from each side

Slightly deeper planting (up to 7 cm) is acceptable in light sandy soils that warm fast and dry out quickly. Slightly shallower (4 cm) is better in heavy clay where excess moisture sits at the lower depth.

Avoid the temptation to plant in single dotted lines. Anemones look better in dense blocks because they support each other, the stems hold straighter, and the bed stays in flower for longer because flowering is staggered across many corms.

## When should I plant for the strongest flowering?

The right planting window depends on your climate, because *A. coronaria* prefers cool growing conditions and dislikes summer heat:

| Climate | Recommended planting | First flowers |
|---|---|---|
| Mild winter (Mediterranean, USDA 8–10) | Mid October to mid December | Late January through May |
| Cold winter (USDA 6–7) | Late August to mid October, with frost protection — or early spring outside | Mid spring |
| Cold winter (USDA 5 and below) | Early spring as soon as soil is workable | Late spring |
| Mild marine climates (UK, NW Europe) | September to November | March through June |

In hot summer climates, anemones simply stop flowering once daytime temperatures exceed about 24 °C consistently. The corms then go dormant. Lift them once the foliage yellows and store dry until autumn.

## What soil and aspect produce the best results?

Anemones reward thoughtful site selection more than most spring corms.

**Soil:** Fertile, free-draining loam with a near-neutral pH (6.5–7.0). Heavy clay rots the corms in winter; pure sand drys them out before they root. A bed enriched with composted manure 6–8 weeks before planting is ideal.

**Aspect:** Bright but not baking. A position with full morning sun and afternoon dappled shade gives the longest stems and best colour. Hot south-facing borders shorten flower life.

**Drainage:** Mandatory. If your soil pools after a heavy rain, raise the planting area on a 10–15 cm mound or grow in a deep raised bed.

**Mulch:** A 2–3 cm layer of leaf mould or fine compost after planting locks in moisture without smothering the emerging shoots.

## How do I cut and condition the flowers for the longest vase life?

Cut anemones at the right stage and they reward you with 7–10 days in the vase. Cut them too early or too late and they collapse in 48 hours.

The right stage is when the bud has lifted clear of the foliage, the bracts have separated, and the petals are showing colour but have not fully opened. Cut deep — into the foliage — to get the longest possible stem.

Conditioning:

1. Recut the stem under cool water at a sharp angle.
2. Strip leaves below the eventual water line.
3. Rest in deep cool water for 2–3 hours before arranging.
4. Use clean vase water with floral preservative; replace every 2 days.

For broader conditioning advice, see [How to condition cut flowers before arranging](/posts/condition-cut-flowers-before-arranging-en/).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I leave anemone corms in the ground year-round?**
In mild climates with dry summers, yes. The plants go dormant in summer and resprout from the same corm in autumn. In cold or wet-summer climates, lifting and dry-storing usually gives better year-on-year performance.

**Why are my anemones flowering on very short stems?**
The most common cause is heat. Daytime temperatures over about 22 °C while flower buds are forming push the stems up faster and shorter. Plant earlier in the season, or move to a cooler aspect, to extend stem length.

**My anemone corms have rotted in the soil. What went wrong?**
Almost always overwatering or poor drainage. Anemones like steady, moderate moisture, not saturated soil. If the position drains slowly, raise the bed or grow in deep pots filled with a free-draining mix.

**Are anemones safe around pets?**
*Anemone coronaria* contains protoanemonin, an irritant that causes drooling, vomiting, and skin redness in cats and dogs that chew the leaves or stems. Keep arrangements out of reach. For documented pet-safe cut-flower options, see [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).

**Can I plant anemone corms in pots instead of the ground?**
Yes — pots at least 25 cm deep work well. Use free-draining compost mixed with a third grit, plant 5 cm deep and 8 cm apart, water in, and keep the pot in a cool, bright position. Pots are an excellent option in heavy-soil gardens.
