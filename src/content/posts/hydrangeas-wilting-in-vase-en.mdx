## Quick Answer

Cut hydrangeas wilt fast because their woody stems leak a milky sap that **clogs the xylem within an hour of cutting**, blocking water from reaching the head. The fix is mechanical, not chemical: **recut the stem at a 45° angle under water**, then **dip the bottom 2–3 cm in boiling water for 30 seconds** (or alum powder) to clear the clog. For already-wilted heads, **submerge the entire flower head in cool water for 30–45 minutes** — hydrangeas drink through their petals, not just their stems, and almost always recover.

## Why do hydrangeas wilt so quickly compared with other flowers?

Hydrangeas are unusual among cut flowers in two ways, and both work against vase life.

First, the stems are **woody and full of latex-like sap**. The moment you cut the stem, the plant's natural defence kicks in: sap oozes out, dries, and **plugs the xylem vessels** — the tiny straws that carry water up to the bloom. Within an hour the head is effectively starving even though the stem is sitting in fresh water.

Second, hydrangea heads are **enormous water tanks**. A single mophead carries 100–250 individual florets, and the whole structure transpires water at a much higher rate than a rose or a lily. So you have a flower that needs *more* water than average being supplied through a stem that clogs *faster* than average. The result is the dramatic afternoon-wilt every florist knows: bouquet looks perfect at 9am, slumped over by 3pm.

Heat, low humidity, and direct sun all accelerate the collapse. A hydrangea in a sunny window above a radiator can wilt in under two hours; the same stem in a cool, shaded room may last several days.

## How do I prepare hydrangea stems so they actually drink water?

Three steps, in order, every time:

1. **Cut at a 45° angle, under running or standing water.** A diagonal cut exposes more xylem surface than a flat cut and prevents the stem from sitting flat against the bottom of the vase. Cutting under water blocks the air bubble that otherwise gets sucked into the stem (an "embolism") and causes its own form of wilt.
2. **Strip every leaf that would sit below the waterline.** Submerged leaves rot within 24 hours, fouling the water, feeding bacteria, and accelerating clog at the cut end.
3. **Clear the sap clog immediately.** Pick one of the three methods below — boiling water, alum, or a clean scoring cut — before the stem has a chance to sit dry.

Skipping step 3 is why most home arrangements fail. A fresh diagonal cut on its own is *not enough* for hydrangea; you need to actively unclog the stem.

## Which method works best — boiling water, alum, or scoring?

All three solve the same problem (sap clog) by different routes. Choose the one that fits the situation.

### The boiling-water dip (the florist's default)

- Boil a kettle.
- Pour 5 cm of just-boiled water into a heatproof mug.
- Dip the **bottom 2–3 cm** of the freshly recut stem into the water for **30 seconds**.
- Move the stem straight into a vase of cool, fresh water.

The hot water effectively melts the sap plug and lets cool water flow up the stem. Done correctly the heat damage is limited to the bottom centimetre — well below the parts that matter. This is the method most professional florists use on commercial hydrangea because it works on every stem in seconds.

### Alum powder (the supermarket trick)

- Buy potassium alum from the spice aisle (it is sold as a pickling spice).
- After the diagonal cut, dip the bottom 2 cm of the stem into the powder so a thin coat sticks to the cut surface.
- Place straight into the vase.

Alum is mildly astringent and prevents fresh sap from re-sealing the cut. It is gentler than boiling water and a good option if you are nervous about heat damage, but it can be slower to take effect.

### Scoring or splitting the stem

- After the diagonal cut, use a sharp knife to make a 2–3 cm vertical slit up the centre of the stem, or carefully shave off the outer bark on the bottom 3 cm.
- This exposes more xylem and reduces the surface area of any future sap plug.

Scoring is fine on its own for short displays (1–2 days) and works very well **combined** with either of the methods above for longer vase life.

## My hydrangea has already wilted — can I save it?

Almost always, yes. Hydrangeas have a superpower most cut flowers lack: **they absorb water through the bracts and petals as well as the stem**. This makes them one of the most reviveable cut flowers in the trade.

The full-submersion revival:

1. Fill a clean sink, basin, or bath with **cool tap water** — not cold, not warm.
2. Recut the stem at 45° under the water.
3. **Submerge the entire flower head**, plus 5–10 cm of stem, fully under the water.
4. Leave for **30–45 minutes** (longer for badly wilted stems — some florists go up to 90 minutes).
5. Lift out, shake gently to remove drops, and place in a vase of fresh cool water in a shaded room for the first 12 hours of recovery.

Eight times out of ten the head will return to full turgor. Stems that have been dry for more than 24 hours are harder to rescue but are still worth a try. If the bracts feel papery and stay papery after a soak, the cells have collapsed permanently and there is nothing further to do.

A faster shortcut for emergencies: **mist the head heavily with cool water**, wrap it in a damp paper towel, then a plastic bag, and leave for 20 minutes. Less effective than a full bath, but useful when a sink is not available.

## What should I put in the vase water — sugar, bleach, copper coins?

Lots of folk remedies, only a few that help hydrangea specifically:

- **A drop of bleach (about 1/4 tsp per litre)**: actually useful. It suppresses the bacteria that grow at the cut end and shorten vase life. It does *not* unclog sap; it only keeps fresh water clean.
- **Commercial flower food sachet**: helpful, mostly for the same antibacterial reason plus a small amount of sugar for the petals. Always use the full dose for the volume of water specified.
- **Sugar alone**: feeds bacteria more than the flower. Skip it unless paired with bleach.
- **Aspirin, copper pennies, vodka, lemonade**: no measurable benefit on hydrangea in side-by-side tests. Use the boiling-water dip instead.

The single biggest win is changing the water completely every 2 days, not chemistry. Fresh, cool water + a clean vase + a daily recut beats any additive.

## How long should fresh-cut hydrangeas last in a vase?

With proper handling — diagonal recut, sap clog cleared, cool water changed every 2 days, kept out of direct sun and away from heat — expect:

- **5–7 days** for florist-grade hydrangea (Hortensia, Annabelle, Limelight, etc.) bought in season.
- **3–5 days** for hydrangea cut from your own garden in mid- to late summer when the heads are fully developed and slightly papery.
- **1–2 days** if you cut very young, soft heads early in the season — these never reach full vase life regardless of treatment.

The single most reliable predictor of vase life is the maturity of the head at cutting. Florists call this the **paper test**: gently squeeze a couple of bracts between your fingers. If they feel slightly papery rather than soft and squeaky, the head is mature enough for cutting and will hold up. Soft, squeaky, juvenile bracts wilt within hours no matter what you do.

## What about dried hydrangeas — does any of this apply?

Almost none of it. Drying hydrangea is a separate craft (we cover it in [How to dry hydrangeas](/posts/how-to-dry-hydrangeas-en/)). The short version: cut heads when they have already started to feel slightly papery on the plant, stand the stems in a vase with **2–3 cm of water and let the water evaporate to dryness over a week**. The flowers air-dry while gradually losing turgor and end up holding their shape for months. You skip every fix described above precisely because you *want* the stem to slowly stop drinking.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why does my hydrangea wilt within an hour of arriving from the florist?

The stem was probably cut hours ago and the sap clog has fully formed. Recut at 45° at home, do the boiling-water dip, and submerge the head if it has already drooped. Most florist hydrangeas are still rescuable.

### Can I leave hydrangeas in a vase overnight without water and revive them in the morning?

Not reliably. Hydrangea bracts collapse permanently after about 4–6 hours dry. If you must transport stems dry, wrap the heads in damp paper towel and bag them — the moisture buys you a few extra hours.

### Does the vase water turning brown mean my hydrangeas are dying?

It means bacteria are growing on stem ends or submerged leaves. Strip below-water leaves, recut at 45°, wash the vase with hot soapy water, refill with cool water plus a quarter-teaspoon of bleach per litre. The flowers themselves are usually fine.

### Should hydrangea stems be cut short or long for vase life?

Length does not matter for vase life — the clog forms at the cut end regardless. Cut to whatever length suits the arrangement. What matters is recutting at 45° and clearing the sap before placing in water.

### Why did my hydrangea bloom shrivel and turn papery overnight?

Most likely the head was already starting to mature on the plant before cutting (the natural autumn drying-down). This is irreversible — the bracts are no longer absorbing water through the cells. Treat them as dried hydrangea instead and enjoy the antique colour change.

### Are mophead and lacecap hydrangeas equally hard to keep in a vase?

Mopheads (`Hydrangea macrophylla` round-headed cultivars) are the hardest because of their sheer water demand. Lacecaps and panicled types (`H. paniculata` such as Limelight) are noticeably more forgiving in the vase and often last 7+ days with the same treatment.
