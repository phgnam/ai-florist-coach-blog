## Quick Answer

Peonies brown in heat because of one of four causes: botrytis blight on a humid spring, sun-burn on developing buds when temperatures jump from cool to hot, drought stress during bud swell, or a sudden 30 °C heatwave that cooks the petals from the outside in. Identify which by checking the pattern — fuzzy grey on the bud points to botrytis; uniform tan on petal edges points to sun and heat.

## Why are peonies so sensitive to heat compared with other early-summer flowers?

Peonies evolved in cool temperate Asia, where their flowering window matches a cool, often damp May to early June. Their tissue is genuinely not built for the kind of heat-and-sun spike that has become more common in late spring across many gardens.

Three structural facts explain the sensitivity:

- **The petals are unusually thin** for the size of flower they produce. A 15 cm peony bloom might have 50+ petals, each with very little waxy cuticle for protection. They scorch on contact with sun in heat above about 28 °C.
- **The bud goes through a sticky-stage** where it exudes nectar and is wet to the touch. That wet surface is exactly where botrytis infects, and once infected the bud rarely opens.
- **The plant pulls water aggressively during bud swell.** A four-day dry spell in mid-May, when the buds are just sizing up, often produces brown, half-formed flowers two weeks later.

What you cannot fix: the underlying heat sensitivity. What you can fix: the conditions that turn modest heat into browning. Most peony browning is preventable with the right combination of watering, mulching, light shade in a heatwave, and disease control.

For care basics through the year, the [grow peonies](/posts/grow-peonies-en/) post covers planting depth, sun requirements, and division; this article focuses specifically on diagnosing brown buds and petals.

## How do you tell botrytis blight apart from heat damage?

Botrytis blight (*Botrytis paeoniae*) is the most common cause of peony bud browning in damp springs, and it is often misidentified as heat damage. The two look similar from a distance but have very different signatures up close.

**Botrytis bud blight**

- Buds turn brown and stop developing while still small (often 1–3 cm).
- Surface looks fuzzy, with grey or tan mould visible in damp weather.
- Affected buds eventually shrivel and dry on the stem.
- Lower leaves often show brown spots with pale halos at the same time.
- Most common in cool wet springs, less common in dry years.

**Heat / sun damage**

- Buds develop normally to nearly full size, then petals brown at the edges.
- No fuzz; the brown is dry and papery, not slimy.
- Damage progresses inward from petal tips, not outward from the centre.
- Often only the outer petals are affected; inner petals can still be intact.
- Most common in heatwaves above 28 °C with strong sun.

If you are not sure, check the bud surface. Botrytis leaves a powdery or fuzzy residue when you brush a damp bud lightly with a fingertip. Heat-damaged petals are dry and crisp like paper.

If botrytis is confirmed:

- Remove every affected bud and discard in the bin (not compost).
- Cut back any infected stems to ground level at the end of the season.
- Improve airflow by thinning crowded clumps.
- Spray with a copper-based or *Bacillus subtilis* fungicide weekly during humid spells.
- Mulch with fresh compost in autumn after removing all old peony foliage.

The same disease pattern affects other tubular-bud flowers; the [peony bud diseases](/posts/peony-buds-not-opening-en/) post (if you have one) goes deeper on diagnosis.

## Why does sun-burn happen so suddenly during a heatwave?

Peony petals develop their slight waxy cuticle gradually as the bud matures. Buds that develop through a typical cool May have time to harden before opening; buds that swell in a sudden heat spike do not, and the petals open with very little protection from UV and dry heat.

The signature of sun-burn on peony petals:

- **Tan or pale brown patches** on the side of the bud or open flower facing the strongest sun.
- **Curled or papery petal edges** even before the flower fully opens.
- **Asymmetric damage** — the side of the plant in afternoon sun is much worse than the side in morning sun.

Three fixes work, in increasing intensity:

**1. Mulch heavily before the heatwave.** A 5–8 cm layer of compost or bark over the root zone keeps soil moisture and root temperature stable. Plants with cool, evenly-moist roots cope with heat far better than plants with dry, sun-baked soil.

**2. Use temporary shade cloth in heatwaves.** A 30 % shade cloth on a frame over the plant for 3–4 days during the worst of a heatwave reduces petal burn by 50–80 %. Garden centres sell ready-made plant umbrellas; old white sheets work fine for a one-off.

**3. Cut buds and finish opening indoors.** If you have well-formed buds and a 30 °C heatwave is forecast, cut the buds at the marshmallow stage (soft and squishy when squeezed) and bring them inside. They open in cool indoor air just as cleanly as outdoors and avoid the heat damage entirely. The [keep cut flowers fresh](/posts/keep-cut-flowers-fresh-en/) post covers vase-life conditioning that applies directly to peonies.

## Could the plant simply be running out of water?

Yes, and this is the most under-diagnosed cause. Peonies have a deep, fibrous root system but they pull water aggressively during the two-week bud swell. A garden that gets enough water in March and April can run dry just as the buds are sizing up in May, and the result is half-formed brown flowers.

The diagnostic test is to dig a small probe down 15–20 cm next to the plant, not at the surface. If the soil is dry at root depth, the plant is water-stressed regardless of how moist the surface looks.

The fix is straightforward: water deeply at the base of the plant once a week in the bud-swell period (typically late April to mid-May, depending on climate). One generous slow soak with about 20 litres for a mature clump is far better than several light surface waterings.

Mulching dramatically reduces the watering frequency needed. A 5 cm layer of compost or composted bark over the root zone, applied in late March, holds moisture through most spring weather and only requires supplementary watering in a real dry spell.

## What about ants on peony buds — do they cause browning?

No. Ants on peony buds are a long-standing myth. They are attracted by the sugar-rich nectar that peony buds secrete during the sticky stage; they are doing no harm to the plant and have no role in the buds opening or not opening.

If you cut peonies for the vase, the only ant-related action is to dunk the cut stems in cool water for 30 seconds, then shake gently. The ants leave instantly. There is no need to spray, scrub, or treat the buds for ants — they are not the cause of any browning, opening problem, or disease.

That said, if your peonies are browning *and* covered in ants, the ants are circumstantial. The browning has another cause (almost certainly botrytis or heat) and the ants are simply enjoying the nectar that all healthy peony buds produce.

## How do you protect peonies from a forecast heatwave?

Three preparations done 24–48 hours before the heatwave reduce damage dramatically.

1. **Water deeply** the day before. Soak the root zone with 15–20 litres for an established clump. Do this in the early morning so the foliage is dry by midday.
2. **Mulch the surface** if not already mulched. Even a thin 3 cm layer of compost reduces soil temperature by 2–4 °C compared with bare soil.
3. **Set up shade for the worst hours.** A 30 % shade cloth on a frame, an old white sheet, or a parasol over the plant for the 11 a.m. to 4 p.m. window is enough to cut petal burn substantially.

During the heatwave itself:

- **Do not water during the day.** Water at 6 a.m. or after sunset, never on hot foliage in midday sun. Wet petals plus sun produces the same burn as a magnifying glass.
- **Cut any flowers worth saving** at the marshmallow stage and bring them indoors to finish. Buds at this stage open beautifully in a 18–22 °C indoor room, often producing larger and longer-lasting flowers than they would outside.
- **Tolerate some browning.** Mature peony plants survive even a bad heatwave without losing the underlying clump. Next year's flowers come from buds set in the underground crown, not from this year's stems.

After the heatwave, deadhead any ruined buds and water deeply once. The plant will recover and store energy for next year normally.

## How do you reduce browning long-term so the problem doesn't keep coming back?

Some simple changes to the plant's location and seasonal care reduce browning over multiple years.

| Change | Effect |
|---|---|
| Plant peonies where they get morning sun and afternoon part shade | Far less petal burn in heat spikes |
| Mulch heavily in late March | Stable root temperature and moisture into May |
| Water deeply once a week through bud swell (late April – mid May) | Eliminates drought-driven browning |
| Remove all dead foliage and stems in late autumn | Reduces botrytis spore load for next spring |
| Avoid overhead irrigation in May | Reduces botrytis pressure |
| Choose botrytis-resistant cultivars | 'Karl Rosenfield', 'Coral Charm', 'Sarah Bernhardt' tend to perform better than older varieties in damp springs |
| Stake mature clumps | Keeps flowers off wet ground and improves airflow |

The single biggest long-term improvement is the planting position. A peony in afternoon shade survives a 30 °C heatwave in flower far better than the same plant in full sun. If you have repeated browning year after year and the plant is mature enough to move, lift and replant in autumn — the [grow peonies](/posts/grow-peonies-en/) post explains the lifting and division procedure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My peony buds are pea-sized and brown. What happened?**
Almost certainly botrytis blight. Cut affected stems back to the ground, bin the trimmings, improve airflow, and treat next spring preventively with a copper or *Bacillus subtilis* spray.

**Why did the flowers open and brown within hours?**
A heat spike combined with sun on hydrated petals. The petals never had time to harden. Open buds at the marshmallow stage indoors next year if heat is forecast.

**Can heat damage from one year affect the plant the next year?**
Slightly. A severely heat-stressed plant may store less energy in the crown that summer, so next year's buds may be fewer or smaller. Two years of normal weather usually restores normal vigour.

**Should I cut off browned petals or leave them on the bud?**
Cut them off. They are not going to recover and they shed spores or hold moisture against the stem, both of which can spread other problems.

**Is it true that some peony cultivars handle heat better than others?**
Yes. Coral-toned and single-form peonies generally cope with heat better than the dense-double "Sarah Bernhardt" types because the lighter petal load needs less water during bud swell.
