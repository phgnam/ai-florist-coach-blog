## Quick Answer

Indoor cyclamen flower for **3–5 months** if kept **cool (12–18 °C)**, **watered from below** to keep the **corm dry**, given **bright filtered light**, **deadheaded by twisting old stems off** at the base, and fed a **weak balanced fertiliser every 2 weeks** while in flower. Move overheated plants to a cooler room overnight — most cyclamen failures are heat stress, not water issues.

## Why do most cyclamen stop flowering early?

Common reasons cyclamen quit after 3–4 weeks:

- **Too warm**: kept above 20 °C, especially overnight — corm signals end of season
- **Overwatered**: water sitting at the centre of the corm causes rot
- **Underwatered**: leaves wilt, plant goes dormant prematurely
- **Light starvation**: dark corner, no flower bud development
- **Old flowers left on plant**: signals to corm that breeding is "done"
- **Wrong feed**: high nitrogen produces leaves at flower expense
- **Brought in fully open**: shop-grown plants often have all open flowers; new buds may be limited

The single biggest factor is temperature. A cyclamen in a 22 °C centrally heated lounge often gives 3 weeks; the same plant in a 14 °C bedroom can flower for 4 months.

## What's the ideal temperature for cyclamen?

The sweet spot:

- **Day**: 14–18 °C
- **Night**: 10–14 °C
- **Critical maximum**: 20 °C — sustained higher = early dormancy
- **Minimum**: 5 °C — light frost kills indoor types
- **Sweet spot**: 12 °C constant

Best places in a typical home:

- **North-facing windowsill** with no radiator below
- **Cool entrance hall** with daylight
- **Unheated spare bedroom**
- **Conservatory** in autumn/spring (not summer)
- **Cool glassed-in porch**

Bad places:

- **Near radiators or fireplaces**
- **South-facing windows in winter sun** (leaf scorch)
- **Centrally heated open-plan rooms** (24+ °C)
- **On top of TV or kitchen warmth**

For overnight cooling: move the plant to a hallway or porch each evening, return to bright spot in the morning. This single trick can extend flowering by weeks.

## How should I water cyclamen correctly?

The "from below" method is essential:

1. **Stand the pot in a saucer of room-temperature water** for 15–20 minutes
2. **Tip out excess water** — never leave standing water
3. **Don't water again** until top 2 cm of compost feels dry to the touch
4. **Never water onto the corm** — water sitting in the corm crown causes rot

Watering frequency varies by season:

- **Active flowering (autumn–spring)**: every 5–7 days
- **Slow-down (early summer)**: every 10–14 days
- **Dormant (summer)**: minimal — every 3–4 weeks just to keep corm alive
- **Resuming (autumn)**: gradually increase

Test before watering: tilt the pot. If it feels light, water. If still heavy, wait. Heavy + dry topsoil = water from below; heavy + wet topsoil = wait.

## How important is light?

Critical for continued bud production:

- **Bright filtered light** is ideal — east window in winter, north window in summer
- **2–3 hours direct morning sun** acceptable (especially in winter)
- **Avoid harsh midday sun** — leaves scorch fast
- **Avoid deep shade** — buds fail to develop
- **Rotate the pot** every few days for even growth
- **Move closer to light** in dark winter weeks

A cyclamen with no flowering for 2 weeks often just needs more light. Move it 30 cm closer to a window and observe; new buds usually appear within 7 days.

## Why is deadheading critical?

Deadheading directs the plant's energy to **new buds** instead of seed production:

The technique:

- **Wait for the flower to fully fade** — not while still showing colour
- **Grasp the stem at the base** of the flower
- **Twist firmly while pulling gently** until the stem snaps off cleanly at the corm
- **Do not cut with scissors** — leaves a stub that rots

A clean twist-and-pull mimics how the plant naturally sheds spent stems. Once a week, remove all spent flowers and yellowing leaves the same way.

For a plant in full flower, expect to remove 3–5 spent stems per week.

## What feed works best?

For continued flowering, feed weakly and regularly:

- **Type**: balanced houseplant fertiliser (NPK 10-10-10) or specifically for African violets
- **Strength**: half the recommended dose — full strength burns roots
- **Frequency**: every 2 weeks during flowering season
- **Stop**: as soon as flowering slows in late spring

High-nitrogen feeds (lawn-style) make leaves at the expense of flowers. High-potash (tomato-style) can work but harder to dose correctly.

Diluted seaweed extract every 2 weeks is a gentle, foolproof alternative.

## How do I keep cyclamen flowering through to spring?

The full extension protocol:

- **Cool overnight** (move to porch or hallway): 12–14 °C
- **Bright filtered day light**: east-facing window
- **Bottom-water** every 5–7 days
- **Deadhead weekly** — clean twist removal
- **Feed half-strength** every 2 weeks
- **Refresh top of compost** in late autumn — replace top 1 cm with fresh compost
- **No leaf shine sprays** — clog stomata
- **Wipe dust off leaves** with damp cloth monthly

A plant treated this way commonly flowers from October to April — six months of continuous bloom.

## What about florist gift cyclamen vs hardy garden cyclamen?

Two different beasts:

**Indoor florist cyclamen** (*Cyclamen persicum*):

- The colourful potted gift
- Tender (cannot tolerate frost)
- Indoor only in cold climates
- Flowers October–April
- Various flower colours

**Hardy garden cyclamen** (*Cyclamen hederifolium*, *coum*):

- Outdoor border perennial
- Tolerates frost
- Plants outside in shade
- Flowers in different seasons by species
- Smaller blooms than indoor types

This guide is for **florist cyclamen (*C. persicum*)**. Hardy varieties have similar light/water needs but live outside year-round and don't need overnight cooling indoors.

## Can I keep a cyclamen for next year?

Yes — most florist cyclamen will rebloom if treated as perennial:

**Spring–early summer (after flowering)**:

- **Reduce watering** as leaves start to yellow
- **Stop feeding**
- **Let plant go dormant** naturally — leaves die back

**Summer (dormant phase)**:

- **Move pot to cool dry shaded place**
- **Water sparingly** — once every 3–4 weeks
- **Corm rests** for 2–3 months
- **Let compost almost dry between waterings**

**Late summer–autumn (revival)**:

- **Bring back to bright cool spot**
- **Refresh top compost**
- **Resume normal watering**
- **First leaves appear within 2 weeks**
- **Flower buds within 4–6 weeks**

A well-cared cyclamen can flower 5–10 years. Repotting every 2 years (in fresh compost, same pot size) keeps the corm healthy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are my cyclamen leaves yellowing while still flowering?

Most likely overwatering or too warm. Check for soggy compost (overwatering) or position near radiator (heat stress). Reduce watering, move to cooler spot, and yellow leaves should stop appearing within a week.

### Can I save a cyclamen that has all wilted?

Possibly. If the corm is firm (not soft or rotted), bottom-water once and move to a cool 14 °C spot. Within 24–48 hours, leaves should perk up. Soft mushy corm = beyond saving.

### How big a pot should a cyclamen be in?

Cyclamen prefer slight root constraint. Don't repot into a larger pot just because it's flowering well. Repot only when corm fills the pot (every 2 years), and use a pot 2 cm wider than the previous.

### Why are my cyclamen flowers shrivelling after one day?

Three causes: too dry (water from below), too hot (move to cooler spot), or low humidity (group plants together to raise humidity locally). Don't mist directly — water on leaves and corm causes rot.

### What about indoor flowers in winter generally?

Cyclamen pair well with other cool-loving winter bloomers like primula, hyacinths, and Christmas cactus. See the [indoor flowers on a kitchen counter in winter guide](/posts/which-indoor-flowers-grow-on-a-kitchen-counter-in-winter-en/) for more options.

### Do cyclamen need humidity to flower?

Moderate humidity helps but isn't essential. A pebble tray under the pot (don't let pot touch water) or grouping with other plants raises humidity 10–20% locally. Avoid direct misting — leaves and corm crown should stay dry.
