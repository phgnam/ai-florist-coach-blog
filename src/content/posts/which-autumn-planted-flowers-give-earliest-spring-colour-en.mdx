## Quick Answer

For the earliest spring colour, plant in **autumn (September–November)**: **snowdrops** and **winter aconites** (January), **crocus** and **Iris reticulata** (February), **hellebores** (December–March), **species tulips** and **Anemone blanda** (March), then **early daffodils** like 'February Gold' and 'Tête-à-Tête' (late February). A succession of these gives 12 weeks of colour from mid-winter through April with no replanting.

## Why does autumn planting matter?

Most early spring flowers are bulbs or tubers that need a cold dormancy of 12–14 weeks below 9 °C to trigger flowering. Plant them in spring and they sit dormant all year. Plant them in autumn and they:

- Develop roots through autumn and early winter
- Receive the cold trigger naturally
- Emerge promptly in late winter / early spring with stored energy ready

The planting window is **September to mid-November** in temperate climates. Tulips can wait until late November or December — the cold helps them. Snowdrops and aconites are best planted "in the green" (after flowering) but dry bulbs in early autumn are also workable.

## What flowers in January?

The earliest reliable bloomers, in rough order:

- **Snowdrops (*Galanthus nivalis* and *G. elwesii*)** — January through February
- **Winter aconite (*Eranthis hyemalis*)** — late December to February, brilliant yellow saucers
- **Hellebores (Christmas rose *H. niger*, then *H. orientalis*)** — December onwards
- **Witch hazel (*Hamamelis*)** (shrub, not bulb) — January, fragrant spider blooms
- **Cyclamen coum** — January–March, small magenta-pink flowers in shade

Plant snowdrops and aconites under deciduous trees where they get dappled spring light. Plant hellebores as established plants in autumn — they take 1–2 years to reach full flowering.

## What flowers in February?

The succession continues:

- **Crocus tommasinianus** — first crocus to open, early February
- **Iris reticulata** — small intense blue/purple iris, 10 cm tall
- **Early daffodils** — 'February Gold', 'Tête-à-Tête', 'Rijnveld's Early Sensation'
- **Cyclamen coum** still going
- **Bergenia** — pink panicles starting to push up

A pot or border with a mix of crocus, iris reticulata, and 'Tête-à-Tête' daffodils flowers continuously for 4 weeks.

## What flowers in March?

The main spring flush starts:

- **Species tulips** (*T. tarda*, *T. clusiana*, *T. saxatilis*) — earliest tulips, 10–25 cm tall
- **Anemone blanda** — daisy-like blue, pink, white
- **Pulmonaria** (lungwort) — pink/blue spotted-leaf perennial
- **Primroses** — wild type and named cultivars
- **Muscari (grape hyacinth)** — late March
- **Mid-season daffodils** — 'King Alfred', 'Carlton', 'Ice Follies'

A lawn naturalised with crocus, anemones, and species tulips gives 6 weeks of changing colour with minimal upkeep.

## Which combinations give the longest succession?

Plant these together in autumn for 12+ weeks of continuous colour:

**A small space (1 m² border or 30 cm pot)**:

- 25 snowdrop bulbs at the back
- 30 *Crocus tommasinianus* in front
- 10 *Iris reticulata* mixed through
- 6 'Tête-à-Tête' daffodils for late February
- Top-dress with leaf mould in October

**A larger drift**:

- Layer of 25 species tulips at the bottom (15 cm deep)
- Layer of 50 crocus above (8 cm deep)
- Naturalised aconites and snowdrops at the surface
- A few hellebore plants nearby for evergreen structure and December blooms

The "lasagne" planting principle works well — bulbs of different sizes layered in the same pot or hole emerge in succession.

## How deep should the bulbs go?

The general rule is **3 times the bulb's height**. Specifics:

- Snowdrops: 5–8 cm deep
- Winter aconites: 5 cm deep
- Crocus: 8–10 cm deep
- Iris reticulata: 8 cm deep
- Anemone blanda: 5 cm deep
- Species tulips: 10–15 cm deep
- Daffodils: 12–20 cm deep

In heavy clay soils, plant slightly shallower and add grit at the planting hole. In sandy soils, plant slightly deeper to prevent drying out.

## What about hellebores and other non-bulb early flowers?

Don't rely only on bulbs — perennials that flower very early give scale and structure:

- **Hellebores** (Christmas rose, lenten rose) — December–March
- **Bergenia 'Bressingham White'** — February–April
- **Pulmonaria** — March
- **Primulas** (cowslip, primrose, polyanthus) — March
- **Euphorbia characias** — March–May, lime green flower bracts

Plant hellebores and bergenia in autumn while the soil is warm enough for root establishment but cool enough to reduce transplant stress.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When is too late to plant spring bulbs in autumn?

Tulips can wait until late November or even December. Daffodils and crocus prefer September–October. After mid-November, look for any soft or sprouting bulbs at discount nurseries — you can plant them as a salvage; they may flower late but will recover for next year.

### Can I plant spring bulbs in pots?

Yes — plant in October in deep pots with drainage. Sink the pots in soil for winter protection or place against a sheltered wall. Water sparingly through winter, generously once growth starts.

### Do squirrels dig up the bulbs?

Tulips and crocus yes, daffodils and snowdrops no (they are bitter or toxic to rodents). Plant tulips deeper than recommended (15 cm) and cover with a layer of holly clippings or chicken wire to deter digging.

### How do you stop spring bulbs flowering thinly the second year?

Allow leaves to die back fully (6 weeks after flowering) before mowing or cutting. Feed once with a high-potash liquid feed after flowering. Lift and divide every 4–5 years if clumps become congested.

### Which spring flowers attract bees?

Crocus, snowdrops, *Iris reticulata*, hellebores, and pulmonaria are all major early-season nectar sources for emerging queen bees. A garden with these is doing serious pollinator work in February.

### Can I force bulbs for indoor January display?

Hyacinths and paperwhite narcissus yes — pot in October, keep cool 6 weeks, then bring indoors. Hellebores and snowdrops in pots can be brought into a cool porch for early display but don't force them in warm indoor conditions or they collapse.
