## Quick Answer

Bearded irises stop blooming when their rhizomes become overcrowded, competing for sunlight, water, and nutrients. The centre of the clump dies out while growth pushes outward, leaving old, exhausted rhizomes that produce leaves but no flowers. Dividing every three to four years in late summer — cutting healthy fans with fresh rhizome sections and replanting them at the surface — restores vigorous blooming.

## Why do bearded irises decline after a few years?

Bearded irises grow from thick, horizontal rhizomes that sit at or just below the soil surface. Each rhizome produces a fan of sword-shaped leaves and, in a good year, a tall flower stalk. After blooming, the rhizome pushes growth outward by producing side rhizomes, each of which may bloom the following year.

The problem develops within three to four years. The original centre rhizome exhausts itself and dies, leaving a ring of newer rhizomes around a dead, woody core. These outer rhizomes crowd each other, shade each other's growing points, and compete for water and soil nutrients. Many of them become too small or too shaded to initiate flower buds. The result is a large patch of healthy-looking leaves but very few flowers — or none at all.

This is not disease. It is the natural growth pattern of bearded irises, and the solution is simple: divide the clump, discard the spent centre, and replant the best outer rhizomes with fresh spacing.

## When is the best time to divide bearded irises?

The ideal window is four to six weeks after flowering finishes, typically late July through August in the Northern Hemisphere. At this point the plant has recharged its rhizome with energy from the leaves, and the summer warmth gives transplants time to establish new roots before winter.

Avoid dividing in spring (it disrupts the current year's bloom) or late autumn (new divisions may not root before cold weather arrives).

## How do you divide bearded iris rhizomes step by step?

1. **Lift the entire clump.** Use a garden fork to loosen soil around the clump, then lift it out. Shake or wash off excess soil so you can see the rhizome structure clearly.
2. **Identify healthy sections.** Look for firm, plump rhizomes with a fan of green leaves and white root nubs on the underside. Discard any that are soft, hollow, mushy, or show borer damage (tunnels or sawdust-like frass).
3. **Cut with a clean knife.** Separate the healthy outer rhizomes from the spent centre. Each division should have one strong fan of leaves and 5–10 cm of firm rhizome. Trim leaves to about 15 cm in a fan shape to reduce wind rock and water loss.
4. **Let cuts dry.** Leave the divisions in shade for a few hours or overnight so the cut surfaces callous. This reduces the risk of bacterial soft rot.
5. **Replant shallowly.** Dig a shallow hole with a small ridge in the centre. Set the rhizome on top of the ridge with roots spread down on either side. The top of the rhizome should sit at or just above soil level — burying it too deep is the single most common cause of non-blooming after division.
6. **Space generously.** Plant divisions 30–45 cm apart with the leaf fan facing outward so growth moves away from its neighbours.
7. **Water once, then ease off.** Water in to settle soil around the roots, then let the soil dry between waterings. Irises rot in constantly wet soil.

## What other problems cause bearded irises not to bloom?

**Too much shade.** Bearded irises need at least six hours of direct sun. Even light shade from nearby shrubs or perennials can suppress flower buds.

**Planting too deep.** The rhizome must bake in the sun. If mulch, soil, or neighbouring plants bury it, the growing point cannot receive enough warmth and light to initiate buds.

**Over-fertilising with nitrogen.** High-nitrogen fertiliser promotes lush foliage at the expense of flowers. Use a low-nitrogen, high-phosphorus feed in early spring and after bloom.

**Iris borers.** In North America, iris borer larvae tunnel into rhizomes, destroying them from inside. Watch for notched leaf edges and sawdust-like frass. Remove and destroy affected rhizomes.

**Late frost.** A hard frost after flower stalks have begun emerging can kill the developing buds without damaging the leaves.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should you divide bearded irises?**
Every three to four years is the standard recommendation. If your clump is still blooming well, you can wait longer. If blooming drops noticeably, divide sooner.

**Can you divide irises in spring?**
You can, but you will sacrifice that year's bloom and the divisions may establish more slowly. Late summer is far better.

**Do bearded irises need fertiliser?**
A light application of balanced or low-nitrogen fertiliser in early spring and again after flowering helps. Avoid heavy feeding, which promotes leaves over flowers.

**Why are my iris rhizomes soft and smelly?**
Soft, foul-smelling rhizomes have bacterial soft rot, often following borer damage or waterlogged soil. Cut away all affected tissue until you reach firm, white flesh, let the cut dry in sun, and replant in well-drained soil.

**Can I grow bearded irises in pots?**
Yes, but use a wide, shallow container with excellent drainage. The rhizome needs surface sun exposure. Repot and divide every two to three years because pots crowd faster than garden soil.
