## Quick Answer

Yes, freesia (*Freesia × hybrida*) grows well in pots indoors if you plant **5 corms per 20 cm pot, pointed end up, 5 cm deep** in free-draining compost in **autumn**. Keep at **8–13°C** for the first 8–10 weeks while roots and leaves develop, then move to a cool, bright spot at **12–18°C** for the flowering stage. Expect fragrant flowers **12–14 weeks** from planting. Slender stems usually need **light staking** before the buds open. After flowering, dry the corms off and store cool for replanting next year.

## What kind of plant is freesia, and why grow it indoors?

Freesia is a **South African corm** in the iris family with one of the most distinctive scents in horticulture — sweet, citrussy, and instantly recognisable. The flowers form on **one-sided arching spikes** with 6–10 trumpet-shaped flowers per stem.

Indoor pot growing is the easiest way to enjoy freesia outside the Mediterranean climates where it is hardy. Three reasons it works:

**It needs cool, bright winters.** Freesia evolved with cool wet winters and dry summers. A temperate-zone unheated porch, conservatory, or cool windowsill mimics that exactly.

**It will not survive a freeze.** Outside USDA zones 9–10, freesia is grown either as a summer annual (planted in spring, lifted in autumn) or as an indoor pot crop. The pot route gives the most reliable bloom.

**It is sensitive to summer heat once flowering.** Freesia flowers fall apart at 25°C+ and dislike warm dry air. Indoor pots in cool rooms outperform outdoor garden beds in most climates.

## When should you plant freesia corms indoors?

Timing is the most common point of failure. Plant too warm or too late and you get all leaves and no flowers.

**Autumn (September–November)** is the standard indoor schedule for **mid-winter to early spring bloom**. Plant in October for January–February flowers.

**Late winter (January–March)** also works for **spring-into-early-summer bloom** if your home stays cool. Plant in February for May flowers.

**Avoid summer planting indoors** — most homes are too warm for the cool root-development stage, and the corms will rot or sprout weak leaves with no flower spike.

The first 8–10 weeks after planting are the **critical cool stage**. The corm needs **8–13°C** during this period to set flower spikes correctly. If your home stays above 18°C throughout autumn and winter, freesia indoors will struggle. A bright cool porch, an unheated greenhouse, or a cool spare room is essential.

**Specially-prepared corms** (sometimes labelled "indoor freesias") have already had a controlled cold treatment from the supplier and are slightly more forgiving. Standard "garden" freesia corms benefit most from the cool start described above.

## How do you plant freesia corms in a pot correctly?

Freesia planting is straightforward but unforgiving of two specific mistakes (planting upside down and burying too deep).

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Choose a 20–25 cm pot, 20 cm deep**, with drainage holes. Plastic or terracotta both work; terracotta dries faster and is preferred in humid homes.
2. **Fill with free-draining compost.** A useful mix: 60% peat-free multipurpose, 30% horticultural grit or sand, 10% perlite. Avoid moisture-retaining bedding compost.
3. **Plant 5–7 corms per 20 cm pot**, pointed end up. The "point" is the narrow end with a slight projection; the flat or rough end goes down.
4. **Cover with 5 cm of compost.** Deeper than that and the shoot exhausts itself before reaching light; shallower than that and the corm dries out.
5. **Water lightly** — just enough to settle the compost, not soak it.
6. **Move to a cool, bright spot at 8–13°C**, ideally near a window for light but away from heat sources.

**Why corms fail to come up:**

- **Planted upside down** (a surprisingly common mistake; the flat end produces no shoot).
- **Planted too deep** (10 cm+ exhausts the corm).
- **Compost too wet, too cold** — freesia rots in waterlogged compost below 8°C.
- **Old, dried-out corms.** Buy from a reputable supplier; corms that have been stored warm and dry for years often fail to sprout.

You can plant **a second batch 3–4 weeks after the first** for a longer bloom window. Two staggered pots can give 6–8 weeks of continuous fragrance instead of 3 weeks all at once.

## What conditions does freesia want during growth?

Once leaves emerge, freesia needs three things: **steady cool temperatures**, **bright light**, and **sparing water until buds form**.

**Temperature:**

- **Weeks 0–8 (root and leaf development):** 8–13°C overnight, 13–17°C by day. Cool is non-negotiable here.
- **Weeks 8–14 (bud and flower):** 12–18°C is ideal. Above 22°C, buds drop or open distorted.
- **Avoid sudden temperature swings** of more than 5–6°C; the leaves yellow at the tips.

**Light:** the brightest light you have. A south or east-facing window, an unheated conservatory, or a cool greenhouse all work. **Without bright light, freesia leaves grow long, soft, and floppy and the flower spike often aborts**.

**Water:**

- **Weeks 0–4:** keep barely moist. Water only when the top 2 cm of compost is dry.
- **Weeks 4–14:** water more regularly as leaf growth accelerates. Water at the base, not over the foliage. Keep evenly moist but never soggy.
- **Post-flowering:** reduce water gradually as leaves yellow naturally; this triggers dormancy and lets the corm rest.

**Feeding:** light and infrequent. A **half-strength balanced flowering feed every 3 weeks** once leaves are 10 cm tall is plenty. Stop feeding as flowering ends.

## How do you keep the slender stems from flopping?

Freesia stems are notoriously thin (3–5 mm thick) and naturally arch sideways towards the light as the inflorescence develops. **Without support, the entire flower spike flops onto the soil under its own weight** the moment it opens.

Three options for staking:

**Option 1 — twiggy support.** Push 4–5 thin pea-sticks or birch twigs into the pot when leaves are 15 cm tall. The leaves and stems weave through the twigs naturally as they grow. Most invisible once the foliage fills in.

**Option 2 — bamboo cane and twine corral.** Push 4 thin bamboo canes (30 cm tall) around the pot edge and string twine between them in 2–3 horizontal layers as the stems grow. Slight visible structure but very secure.

**Option 3 — single-stem support.** For show-quality cut flower production, tie each stem to its own thin cane with soft string. Labour-intensive but produces ramrod-straight cut stems.

**Position the pot for stem strength.** Plants grown in **cool, bright, slightly breezy** conditions produce naturally stronger stems than those grown in warm, dim, still air. A small fan on low for a few hours a day stiffens stems noticeably.

**Rotate the pot 180°** every 2–3 days during growth. Without rotation, all stems lean uniformly toward the light and the spike becomes one-sided.

## What do you do with freesia corms after flowering?

Freesia is **technically perennial** but only flowers reliably from corms that have had a proper rest. Container growers usually choose between two paths.

**Path A — keep the corms for next year.**

1. After flowering finishes, **continue watering and feeding for 4–6 weeks** while the leaves are still green. This is when the corm rebuilds its reserves and forms next year's flower bud underground.
2. Once leaves yellow naturally, **stop watering** and let the soil dry completely.
3. **Tip the corms out**, brush off old soil, and air-dry them on a tray for 1–2 weeks in a cool dry spot.
4. **Store** in paper bags or open trays at **20–25°C** (yes, warm — they need a warm dry summer to mature) for **8–12 weeks**, then move to **8–13°C** for at least 4 weeks before replanting.
5. **Replant** following the original autumn schedule. Stored corms typically multiply into "cormlets" alongside the parent corm; the parent flowers next year, the cormlets need 1–2 more years.

**Path B — discard and replant fresh corms next year.**

If summer storage at warm temperatures is impractical, freesia corms make decent compost. Buy fresh nursery corms each autumn for the most reliable bloom. Many indoor growers go this route because the warm-summer / cool-winter storage cycle is awkward in homes with central heating.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do freesia bulbs take to flower indoors?

Typically **12–14 weeks** from planting to first open flower under good cool-growing conditions. Specially-prepared corms can flower in **10–12 weeks**. Warm conditions (above 18°C throughout) often produce all leaves and no flowers, so cool growing is the most important variable for hitting the schedule.

### Do freesia bulbs need a cold treatment before planting?

Standard "garden" freesia corms benefit from being kept **cool (8–13°C) for the first 8–10 weeks after planting** rather than a hard fridge cold-treatment beforehand. Specially-prepared "indoor" or "Christmas-flowering" freesia corms have already received a temperature treatment from the supplier and only need cool growing conditions.

### Why are my freesia leaves growing but no flowers appearing?

The two most common reasons are **temperature too warm during weeks 0–8** (above 16°C inhibits flower bud initiation), or **insufficient light** during early growth. Move to a cooler, brighter spot, and accept that this batch may flower late or skip flowering. Next autumn, plant earlier and keep cooler from the start.

### Can you grow freesia from seed instead of corms?

Yes, but it takes **2–3 years** from seed to first flower, and the resulting plants vary widely in colour and form. For predictable named-cultivar bloom, corms are far easier. Seed is mainly worthwhile for breeding projects or for specialist enthusiasts.

### Do freesias come back every year in pots?

With proper rest yes, but with caveats. Container freesias **need a warm dry summer dormancy (4 months at 20–25°C) followed by a cool autumn (8–13°C) before replanting**. In homes that stay 18–22°C year-round, replicating this cycle is awkward, and many growers find that buying fresh corms each autumn outperforms attempted storage.

### What pests bother indoor freesias?

Few. **Aphids** sometimes cluster on flower buds and can be rinsed off with water or a mild insecticidal soap. **Botrytis (grey mould)** appears on flower spikes in cold damp conditions; improve airflow with a small fan. **Thrips** can deform flowers if numbers build; sticky traps detect them early. Most indoor freesia pests are easier to manage than the same pests outdoors.
