## Quick Answer

Place a "prepared" hyacinth bulb on top of a tall narrow jar so the **base just touches water**, support with toothpicks, pebbles, or a clear sleeve. Keep in the **dark at 4–9 °C for 10 weeks** so roots grow before the shoot. When the shoot is **5 cm tall**, move to a **cool bright room** (15 °C). Flowers open about 3 weeks later. No special forcing vase required.

## Why are hyacinths well suited to water culture?

Hyacinth bulbs store enough energy in their fleshy scales to produce a full flower spike with no soil at all — the energy is already inside the bulb. Water gives the roots somewhere to grow, but the flower itself is fuelled by the bulb's stored sugars.

Three other species also work well in water:

- **Paperwhite narcissus** — easiest of all, no chilling needed
- **Crocus** — small but charming in mini glass cups
- **Amaryllis (*Hippeastrum*)** — large bulb, dramatic stem

Tulips and most daffodils need soil; their root pattern doesn't suit a jar.

## What's the difference between a "prepared" and a regular hyacinth?

For Christmas-flowering and reliable forcing, you need **prepared bulbs**:

- **Prepared (or "specially prepared") hyacinths** have already had a heat treatment by the supplier that triggers earlier flowering — they bloom in 12–14 weeks from planting
- **Regular hyacinths** need 14–16 weeks and flower at the natural spring time (March–April)
- Both work; prepared bulbs are recommended for indoor display from December

When buying, look for the word "prepared" on the label. Garden centres stock these from late August.

## What do I use instead of a forcing vase?

A purpose-made forcing vase has a narrow waist that holds the bulb above the water reservoir. You can replicate this with:

**Method 1 — Toothpicks**:

- Choose a glass or jar with a mouth slightly smaller than the bulb
- Push 3 toothpicks horizontally into the bulb's middle, equally spaced
- Set on the rim, toothpicks resting on the glass
- Add water until it just touches the bulb base

**Method 2 — Pebble support**:

- Use a wide bowl or vase
- Fill with clean pebbles or marbles to within 4 cm of the rim
- Nestle the bulb into the pebbles
- Add water to just below the bulb base

**Method 3 — Clear sleeve**:

- A short length of plastic pipe or the cut neck of a wine bottle
- Sit the bulb in the sleeve, sleeve resting in the jar with water below

The key in every method: **water touches the base of the bulb but does not wet the body**. A submerged bulb rots within days.

## How long does the cold dark phase need to be?

The cold dark phase is non-negotiable — it's what triggers root growth and convinces the bulb that winter has happened.

The recipe:

- **Temperature**: 4–9 °C (a fridge salad drawer or unheated garage works)
- **Duration**: 10 weeks for prepared bulbs, 12 weeks for regular
- **Light**: complete darkness or very low light
- **Top-up water** weekly to keep level just below bulb base

If keeping in the fridge, store away from ripening fruit (especially apples) — ethylene gas from fruit damages the developing flower bud.

After 10 weeks, lift one bulb gently to check root development. Roots should be 8–10 cm long and the shoot 4–6 cm tall.

## When do I move them out of the cold?

Bring out when the **shoot is 5 cm tall** and a yellow-green flower bud is visible at the centre. Move in stages:

- **First week**: a cool dim spot (12 °C, low light) — shoot greens up, becomes more vertical
- **Second week**: a cool bright window (15 °C, indirect light)
- **Third week**: bud pushes up out of bulb sheath, colour visible
- **Fourth week onwards**: full flowering — cool windowsill, indirect light, no draughts

Sudden movement from cold dark to warm bright produces leggy stems and small flowers. The gradual two-week transition gives strong sturdy spikes.

## How long do the flowers last?

Indoor hyacinths in cool 15–18 °C rooms flower for **2–3 weeks**. In warm rooms (22+ °C) flowers last 5–10 days only.

Tips for longer display:

- **Move into a cool bedroom at night** (a few degrees cooler extends bloom)
- **Avoid direct sun** at midday — it accelerates ageing
- **Keep away from radiators**
- **Top up water** below the bulb (no need to change water unless cloudy)
- **Stake** the heavy spike with a discreet wire if it leans

The scent fills a room for the whole flowering period — a single hyacinth in a hallway perfumes the entire ground floor.

## What do I do with the bulb after flowering?

The bulb has used most of its stored energy. To save it for next year:

- **Cut off the flower spike** but leave the leaves
- **Plant in soil outdoors** in early March — a sheltered border, 10 cm deep
- **Allow the leaves to feed the bulb** for 6–8 weeks before yellowing
- **Bulb may flower again in 2–3 years** in the garden, but with smaller spikes

Don't expect a forced bulb to give the same indoor display next year — they are mostly one-off displays. Buy fresh prepared bulbs annually for indoor use.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I use any glass jar?

Yes — jam jars, wine glasses, wide mouth bottles all work as long as the mouth supports the bulb above water. Decorative shapes are part of the appeal — clear glass shows the white root growth, which is itself a feature.

### Do I need to feed the bulb?

No. The bulb has its own stored food. Feeding can encourage algae in the water rather than helping the plant. Plain tap water (left to stand 24 hours so chlorine evaporates) is fine.

### How do I keep the water clean?

Use a small piece of charcoal in the bottom of the jar to keep water fresh. Replace water entirely if it turns cloudy or smelly. Keep direct sun off the jar to discourage algae.

### Why has my bulb rotted?

Either the water level was too high (touching the bulb body, not just the base) or the cold storage was too warm. Discard rotted bulbs and start over with new ones; the smell warns you that the bulb is gone.

### Can I do this with crocus or muscari?

Yes — both bulbs work in tiny shot glasses or test tubes filled with water. Use the same chill-then-bright sequence. Smaller flowers, shorter total time.

### What's the easiest forced bulb for first-timers?

**Paperwhite narcissus** — they need no cold treatment at all. Place in a bowl of pebbles with water at base level, keep in any cool bright spot, flowers in 4–6 weeks.
