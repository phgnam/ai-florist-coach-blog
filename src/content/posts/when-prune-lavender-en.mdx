## Quick Answer

Prune lavender **twice a year** for the longest-lived, tidiest plants: a **light tidy in early spring** once new green shoots show along the stems (March–April in temperate zones), and a **hard cutback right after flowering finishes** in late summer (August–September). The hard cutback is the more important of the two. **Cut back to 5–8 cm above the woody base, leaving 2–3 leafy nodes per stem** — never cut into bare brown wood, because lavender rarely regenerates from old wood.

## Why does timing matter for lavender pruning?

Lavender is a **Mediterranean sub-shrub**. It grows new green shoots from existing green or semi-green wood; it almost never regenerates from old brown bark-covered stems. **Pruning timing determines whether your plant lives 3 years or 15 years**, and whether it stays a tight mound or sprawls into an ugly woody hulk.

**The two purposes of pruning:**

1. **Tidiness and shape:** removing dead growth, keeping the plant compact and dome-shaped.
2. **Longevity:** preventing the plant from "going woody" — the natural decline where the woody base spreads and green growth concentrates only at the tips.

**The cost of skipping pruning:**

- After 1 year unpruned: plant looks slightly tousled but still good.
- After 2 years unpruned: visible bare stems below leafy tips.
- After 3 years unpruned: the centre of the plant is increasingly woody; flower stems are weaker and shorter.
- After 5 years unpruned: most of the plant is bare brown wood with green tufts only at the outer tips. **Recovery from this state is usually impossible** — the plant is past saving and replacement is the better option.

So timing isn't just about appearance — it's about whether the plant lasts a normal lavender lifespan (10–15 years) or dies prematurely at 3–4 years.

For complementary information, the existing [growing lavender from cuttings guide](/posts/grow-lavender-from-cuttings-en/) covers propagation, useful when an old plant has gone too woody to save and you need replacement plants.

## What does the spring tidy involve?

The spring pruning is gentle. It removes winter damage and shapes the plant for the coming growing season, but it should not be a hard cut.

**When:**

- **Early spring**, once frosts have passed and you can see fresh green shoots emerging along the lower stems.
- In temperate zones (USDA 7–9): March to early April.
- In milder zones (USDA 9–10): late February to early March.
- In colder zones (USDA 5–6): wait until late April once a sharp frost is no longer likely.

**Don't prune if:**

- New growth isn't yet showing.
- A late hard frost is forecast within 7 days.
- The plant is wet from rain — wait for a dry day.

**Technique:**

1. **Identify dead winter growth** — usually slightly browner and brittle stems.
2. **Snip dead tips back** to where you see green shoots emerging.
3. **Lightly shape the plant** — round off any uneven tufts.
4. **Remove no more than 25% of the plant**.
5. **Leave the major shape and bulk** for the post-flowering cut.

**Tools:**

- **Secateurs** (hand pruners) for individual stems.
- **Garden shears** (long-handled hedge shears) for shaping a large clump or hedge.
- **Sharp, clean blades** — wipe with rubbing alcohol between plants if removing diseased growth.

This spring pass takes 5–10 minutes per plant. The goal is **clean appearance + frame ready for new growth**, not radical reshaping.

## What's the post-flowering cutback technique?

This is the **most important cut of the year**. It restores the plant's compact shape and prevents woody decline.

**When:**

- **Right after flowering finishes**, when most flower spikes have faded.
- In temperate zones (UK, France, mid-USA): late August to early September.
- In hot dry zones (Mediterranean, US Southwest): July to mid-August.
- In cool zones (Scotland, Pacific Northwest): early September.
- **Don't wait** until October — late pruning produces tender new growth that gets damaged by frost.

**The 5 cm rule:**

1. **Identify the woody base** of each stem — the brown/grey bark-covered portion.
2. **Find the lowest point on the stem with healthy green leaves**.
3. **Cut 2–3 leaf nodes above that point**, typically 5–8 cm above the woody base.
4. **Repeat for every stem** — the goal is a tight rounded dome shape.
5. **Discard the cuttings** (or save soft tip cuttings for propagation).

**The fundamental rule:** **never cut into bare woody stems**. Cuts into brown wood without green growth below them rarely regenerate, leaving permanent dead patches in the plant. If you've inherited an already-woody plant, see the next section for damage control.

**The ideal end shape:**

- Rounded dome about **30–40% smaller** than the pre-cut plant.
- All cuts above green leafy growth.
- No bare brown wood exposed at the top.
- Plant looks tidy and compact, like a green/silver hedgehog.

**Volume removed:**

- For young plants (1–3 years old): **30–40% of total volume**.
- For mature plants (3–8 years old): **40–50% of total volume**.
- For older plants (8+ years): **lighter — 20–30%** to avoid stressing tired plants.

## Can you save an already-woody lavender?

Sometimes, but the success rate is low and depends on how far gone the plant is. Recovery requires patience and accepting partial success.

**Diagnostic stages:**

- **Stage 1 (mostly green, slight woodiness):** **fully recoverable** with normal post-flowering pruning. Cut back to leafy growth.
- **Stage 2 (woody base extends 5–10 cm up the stem):** **partially recoverable**. Hard cut back to lowest leafy growth on each stem. Some stems may die; replace with cuttings or new plants.
- **Stage 3 (most stems woody for 60%+ of length, green only at tips):** **mostly unrecoverable**. Replace the plant. Take soft-tip cuttings to propagate replacements.
- **Stage 4 (centre of plant is hollow, only outer ring has growth):** **definitely replace**. Don't waste a season trying to save it.

**Recovery technique for stage 2:**

1. **In early spring**, identify all stems with at least some green leaves below the tip.
2. **Cut back hard** to just above the lowest leafy growth on each stem — usually 8–15 cm from the base.
3. **Accept that 30–50% of stems may die** from the procedure.
4. **Wait through the season** without further pruning.
5. **In autumn**, evaluate. If the plant has recovered to compact green shape, treat it normally going forward. If it's still mostly woody, replace.

**A key insight:** lavender has a finite useful lifespan, and pruning history determines how long that is. **A well-pruned plant lasts 10–15 years; a poorly pruned plant lasts 3–5**. If you've inherited a 6-year-old neglected lavender that's gone woody, it may simply be at the end of its life.

For propagating replacements from cuttings, see [growing lavender from cuttings](/posts/grow-lavender-from-cuttings-en/) — soft-tip cuttings root easily in summer and produce flowering plants within 12–18 months.

## Are different lavender species pruned differently?

Yes — and pruning the wrong species at the wrong time produces poor results. The three common groups have slightly different rules.

**English lavender (*Lavandula angustifolia*) — most common in temperate gardens:**

- **Hardy** in USDA zones 5–9.
- **Strong response to hard cutback** — recovers well from the 5 cm rule.
- **Standard schedule:** spring tidy + post-flowering hard cut.
- **Examples:** "Hidcote", "Munstead", "Imperial Gem".

**French / Spanish lavender (*Lavandula stoechas*) — distinctive "rabbit-ear" flowers:**

- **Less hardy** (USDA zones 7–10).
- **Less tolerant of hard pruning** — never cut into woody stems.
- **Schedule:** light shaping after each flowering flush (multiple flushes per year), no hard autumn cut.
- **Particularly sensitive** to leaving woody stumps; replace plants every 4–6 years.

**Lavandin (*Lavandula × intermedia*) — hybrid, large plants:**

- **Hardy** in USDA zones 6–9.
- **Tolerates hard cutback** like English lavender.
- **Standard schedule:** spring tidy + hard post-flowering cut.
- **Examples:** "Grosso", "Provence", "Phenomenal".

**For all species:**

- **Never prune in autumn close to first frost** — fresh growth from late pruning won't harden in time and gets killed.
- **Never prune wet plants** — risk of disease entering cuts.
- **Always sterilise tools** between plants to prevent transmission of foliar diseases.

If you're not sure which species you have, **err on the side of light pruning** the first season. A light cut won't permanently damage any species; a heavy cut on the wrong species can.

## What about deadheading and harvesting during the season?

Cutting flower spikes for use (drying, infusion, fresh display) **counts as pruning** and contributes to plant health. Regular harvesting during peak bloom is good for the plant.

**When to harvest flowers:**

- **For drying:** when **half the florets on a spike are open and half are still buds**. Dried lavender from this stage retains the strongest scent.
- **For fresh display:** when 80% of florets are open — full colour, full fragrance.
- **For oil distillation:** when 70%+ florets are open.

**Technique:**

- **Cut whole stems** down to 5 cm above the woody base, just like the post-flowering cut.
- **Bundle and hang upside down** in a dry shaded space for 2–3 weeks to dry.
- **Cutting during bloom replaces some of the post-flowering cut** — if you've harvested heavily, the autumn cut may be lighter.

**Deadheading without harvest:**

- **Snip spent flower spikes** within a week of fading.
- **Shorten any leggy stems** to maintain shape.
- **Don't cut woody stems**; only spent flower spikes back to the leafy base.

Deadheading encourages a **second light flush** in some cultivars (often "Munstead" and "Hidcote") and keeps the plant looking tidy through to the autumn major cut.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will lavender regrow from old woody stems?

**Almost never.** Lavender lacks the dormant buds in old wood that allow regeneration like roses or hedging plants. **Cuts into brown bare wood produce no new growth** and leave permanent dead patches. The 5 cm rule (cutting only into stems with green leaves below the cut) is fundamental.

### Should I prune lavender in autumn or wait until spring?

**Right after flowering finishes** (late summer / early autumn) is the most important cut. **Don't wait until winter or spring** for the major cutback — late pruning produces tender new growth vulnerable to frost. The spring "tidy" is a separate, lighter operation, not a substitute.

### How much can I cut off a healthy lavender?

**40–50% of total volume** for a mature healthy plant in the post-flowering cut, **down to 5–8 cm above woody base** on each stem. Always leave 2–3 leafy nodes per stem. Cutting more than this puts the plant under stress; cutting less leaves it sprawling and woody-prone.

### What happens if I never prune lavender?

The plant goes through 4–5 years of progressive woodiness, becoming sprawling, ugly, and increasingly bloom-poor. By year 6–7 it's typically beyond saving and **dies prematurely** instead of lasting a normal 10–15 years. Even a single annual post-flowering cut prevents most of this decline.

### Can lavender be pruned into a hedge?

Yes — lavender hedges are a classic use, especially for *L. angustifolia* and *L. × intermedia* "Grosso" or "Phenomenal". **Use long-handled shears** to maintain a tight rounded profile. Apply the same 5 cm rule (never into bare wood). Trim 2–3 times per year: spring tidy, post-flowering cut, light autumn shaping if needed.

### Why is half my lavender plant dead after pruning?

**Almost certainly cut into bare wood.** Inspect the dead stems — if they have no leaves below the cut, that's the cause. Lavender's inability to regenerate from old wood means dead patches stay dead. **Remove the dead stems and accept the lopsided plant** while it grows in; or replace with new plants.
