## Quick Answer

For strawberry pots, plant **shallow-rooted small flowers in the side pockets** (sweet alyssum, trailing lobelia, viola, dwarf sedum, sempervivum, dianthus, creeping thyme) and a **taller upright at the top** (geranium, dwarf cosmos, basil, lavender). Water from the centre using a buried perforated tube to reach all pockets evenly. Side-pocket flowers must tolerate dry intervals because each pocket is a tiny soil reservoir.

## Why is a strawberry pot tricky for normal flowers?

A strawberry pot looks generous but each side pocket holds only **150–300 ml of compost** — enough for a single small plant with shallow roots. Most flowers prefer a deeper run and resent the pocket constraint:

- **Pockets dry out within 1–2 days in summer**
- **Roots circle and run out of space in 4–6 weeks**
- **Water poured at the top runs out of the lower pockets first**

The trick is choosing flowers that evolved on rocky shallow soils where these conditions are normal — and adopting a watering technique that gets moisture to all pockets, not just the top.

## Which flowers thrive in the side pockets?

The reliable shortlist:

**Annual flowers**:

- **Sweet alyssum** — fast-spreading, sweet honey scent, self-sows
- **Trailing lobelia 'Sapphire' / 'Cascade'** — small flowers cascading 30 cm
- **Viola** — non-stop blooming in cool seasons
- **Mexican fleabane (*Erigeron karvinskianus*)** — tiny daisies May–November
- **Bacopa** — small white or blue flowers, drought-resistant

**Perennial flowers**:

- **Sempervivum (houseleek)** — actually *evolved* for shallow rocky pockets
- **Dwarf sedum** ('Dragon's Blood', 'Cape Blanco') — drought-proof, tiny star flowers
- **Dwarf dianthus** — pink scented mat-forming
- **Creeping thyme** — flowers and herb in one
- **Aubrieta** — early spring trailer

**Edible flowers / herbs**:

- **Strawberries** (the original purpose) — the white flowers count
- **Chives** — purple pompoms in spring
- **Marjoram** — tiny pink flowers, pollinator-friendly
- **Nasturtium 'Tom Thumb'** — yellow/orange edible flowers

A mixed planting of three or four of these creates a balanced display that flowers continuously from May to October.

## What works in the top opening?

The top of a strawberry pot is the largest planting position and can hold a 1–1.5 litre rootball.

**Small upright choices**:

- **Compact geranium** (zonal pelargonium) — strong sun-tolerant flower
- **Dwarf cosmos 'Sonata' or 'Pixie'** — bushy 30–40 cm
- **Basil** — green or purple-leaved, flowering and culinary
- **Lavender 'Munstead'** — dwarf form to 30 cm
- **Marigold** — French or Tagetes 'Lemon Gem'

A taller centre plant gives the pot vertical interest and the side flowers create the trailing curtain. Avoid anything over 50 cm tall — taller plants tip the pot in wind.

## How do you water a strawberry pot evenly?

The classic problem: water poured at the top exits from the highest pocket, the lower pockets stay dry. Two reliable solutions:

**Buried watering tube method**:

- Cut a length of plastic pipe (15 mm diameter) the height of the pot
- Drill small holes along its length, one near each side pocket
- Cap the bottom or stand it on a small stone in the pot base
- Position the tube vertically through the centre when filling with compost
- Pour water into the top of the tube; it seeps out at every level

**Gravel column method**:

- Build a 5 cm wide column of gravel down the centre of the pot when filling
- Water from the top — the gravel column distributes water to every pocket
- Less efficient than the pipe method but easier to set up

For either method, water until **water drains from the bottom**, then wait until the top 2 cm of the top opening dries before watering again. In summer this is roughly every 1–2 days; in cool weather every 4–7 days.

## How do you plant a strawberry pot from scratch?

The order matters — plant from the bottom up while filling:

- Fill with compost to the level of the lowest pocket
- Slide the first plant's rootball gently through the pocket from inside
- Settle the leaves out and press compost firmly around the roots
- Add more compost up to the next pocket level, repeat
- Top with the upright centrepiece in the main opening
- Water from inside the buried tube (or top, if using gravel column)

Use **peat-free multipurpose compost mixed 50/50 with grit or perlite** — drainage is crucial in the small pockets.

## How often should I feed a strawberry pot?

The small soil volume runs out of nutrients quickly:

- **First 4–6 weeks** — no feed needed if the compost has slow-release fertiliser
- **From week 6** — half-strength balanced liquid feed every 10–14 days
- **From mid-summer** — switch to high-potash tomato food for stronger flowering
- **Stop feeding by end of September** as growth slows

Overfeeding pushes leafy growth that the small pockets can't support — moderate steady feeding works better than concentrated boosts.

## What goes wrong with strawberry pots?

The common failures:

- **Pockets staying dry while top is moist** — fix with the watering tube
- **Plants rotting in lower pockets** — over-watering, drainage too poor, soil too rich
- **One side flowers, the other doesn't** — uneven sun, rotate the pot quarterly
- **Pot cracks in winter freeze** — empty terracotta pots before frost or move to a sheltered spot
- **Top plant overshadows pockets** — choose smaller centrepieces or rotate annually

A strawberry pot works for 2–3 years before the compost is exhausted; refresh by replanting all positions with fresh compost in spring.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How big should the strawberry pot be?

A 35 cm tall pot with 6–8 pockets is the typical home-garden size and easy to manage. Larger 50+ cm pots with 12+ pockets are harder to plant and water evenly. Smaller pots (under 25 cm) are better treated as a single planter than as a pocket arrangement.

### Are sempervivums really happy in strawberry pots?

Yes — they are arguably the best match. The tiny pockets give exactly the kind of shallow well-drained pocket sempervivums evolved on. A single rosette per pocket multiplies into a clump within 2–3 years.

### Can I grow actual strawberries instead of flowers?

Yes — strawberry pots are named for the original purpose. Modern alpine strawberry varieties are best suited (smaller plants, fewer runners). Commercial strawberry varieties also work but produce fewer berries per plant than in open ground because root volume is limiting.

### Will strawberry pots survive winter outdoors?

Frost-resistant terracotta usually survives in milder climates if drainage is perfect. Cheaper terracotta cracks easily. Move pots to a sheltered spot or empty over winter and replant in spring. Sempervivums and most evergreen herbs survive in-pot in mild winters.

### Can I use a strawberry pot for herbs only?

Yes — chives, thyme, marjoram, oregano, sage, parsley, basil, and tarragon all work. Vary the heights for visual variety and keep aggressive spreaders like mint out (they take over).

### How do I stop the pot drying out in heatwaves?

Soak fully in the morning, mulch the top with fine gravel to slow evaporation, and consider clustering the pot with other containers so plants shade each other. In extreme heat, lift to a partly shaded position for the worst weeks.
