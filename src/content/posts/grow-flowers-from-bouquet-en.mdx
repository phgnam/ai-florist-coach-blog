## Quick Answer

Yes — but only some flowers, and the realistic success rate is **20–40% for chrysanthemums and alstroemeria**, **10–25% for roses**, and **near zero for tulips, lilies, peonies, and most other bulb or seed-grown flowers**. Take cuttings from semi-mature stems, dip in rooting hormone, and use a clear-bag humidity tent. The whole experiment costs almost nothing and works best in spring and early summer.

## What does "growing flowers from a supermarket bouquet" actually mean?

There are two distinct techniques people refer to when they ask this question:

**1. Stem cuttings (vegetative propagation).** Cutting a section of stem, treating it with rooting hormone, and growing roots from the stem to produce a genetic clone of the parent plant. This is what works for roses, chrysanthemums, geraniums, fuchsias, and similar woody or semi-woody flowers.

**2. Saving seeds or bulbs.** Most supermarket cut flowers don't have viable seeds (the flowers were cut before pollination) and don't come with bulbs (bulbs are sold separately). So this rarely works for tulips, lilies, peonies, alliums, or any bulb-grown flower from a bouquet.

Almost everything in this guide is about **technique 1 — stem cuttings**.

The realistic success rates from a typical supermarket bouquet:

**Good candidates (30–50% success rate)**
- **Chrysanthemum** — the easiest cut flower to propagate. Healthy stems strike roots within 10–14 days. The plant flowers the same year if cuttings are taken in spring.
- **Alstroemeria** — surprisingly easy. Mature stems can root in damp soil under a clear bag.
- **Carnation and dianthus** — semi-hard cuttings root reliably with rooting hormone.

**Moderate candidates (15–30% success rate)**
- **Rose** — works for many varieties, especially older garden roses. Some commercial hybrid roses are patented and propagation is technically prohibited (though enforcement is virtually never an issue at home scale). Take 15–20 cm cuttings of stems with at least 4 leaf nodes.
- **Hydrangea** — large-leaved hydrangeas (mophead, lacecap) root from cuttings reasonably well, though supermarket stems are often too soft. Better from a garden plant.
- **Geranium and pelargonium** — easy to root, but rarely sold in bouquets.

**Poor candidates (under 10% success)**
- **Lily** — bulbs are the propagation method; cut stems don't root. (Some lilies form bulbils on the stem axils, but these aren't usually present on cut stems.)
- **Tulip, daffodil, hyacinth** — bulb plants. Cut stems are completely unviable for propagation.
- **Peony** — propagated by root division, not stem cuttings. Cut peony stems will not root.
- **Sunflower** — annual, propagated by seed. The seed is in the head, but only after pollination — usually not present on cut stems.
- **Iris, allium, freesia** — bulb or rhizome plants. Cut stems don't root.
- **Eucalyptus and most foliage** — these are usually bred from seed and rarely root from cuttings.

For roses specifically, the success rate also depends on the variety. Hybrid teas are harder; floribundas and ground-cover roses are easier; old-fashioned shrub roses are easiest.

## How do I actually take a stem cutting from a supermarket flower?

Standard technique that works for roses, chrysanthemums, and most propagable flowers:

**1. Pick the right stem.** Choose a stem that is **fresh (not wilting), not too thin (pencil-width or thicker for roses)**, and **with at least 3–4 leaf nodes**. The stem should be semi-mature — not the soft new top growth, not the dried-out woody base. The middle 60% of the stem is usually best.

**2. Cut sections of 12–20 cm**, each containing at least 3 leaf nodes (the bumps where leaves emerge). Make the cut clean with a sharp knife or sterile pruners. The bottom cut should be just below a leaf node (where roots will emerge); the top cut should be 1–2 cm above the topmost node.

**3. Strip the lower leaves.** Remove all leaves from the bottom half of the cutting. This both reduces water loss through transpiration and clears the section that will go below soil level.

**4. Dip in rooting hormone.** Powdered rooting hormone (auxin) is widely available at garden centres for £4–7 a tub. Dip the bottom 2 cm of the cutting in water, then in the powder, and shake off the excess. This step roughly **doubles the success rate** for hardwood cuttings like roses.

**5. Plant in a propagating mix.** A 50/50 mix of perlite and seed-starting compost is ideal — light, free-draining, and warm. Stick the cutting 5–8 cm deep and firm gently.

**6. Cover with a clear bag or propagator lid.** A plastic sandwich bag over a small pot, held off the leaves with sticks or chopsticks, creates a mini-greenhouse. Humidity needs to stay close to 100% while roots form.

**7. Place in bright indirect light.** Direct sun cooks cuttings inside a humidity tent. East- or north-facing windowsills are ideal. Keep at room temperature (18–22°C is optimal).

**8. Check at 3–4 weeks.** Tug gently on the cutting. Resistance means roots have formed. New leaf growth at the top is also a strong signal.

**9. Pot on once rooted.** Move to a 9–13 cm pot of standard compost, water well, and gradually acclimatise to ambient humidity by opening the bag a little more each day for a week.

The total cost of the kit is around £10 for the rooting hormone and a few small pots. The cuttings themselves are free.

## When should I take cuttings for the highest success rate?

**Spring and early summer** are by far the best season for propagating cut flowers. The reasons:

- **Day length and warmth** drive the cell division that produces roots.
- **Cuttings root faster** in warm soil (typically 14 days in May vs. 28+ days in November).
- **The cuttings can be planted out the same year** — a rose cutting struck in May can be in the ground by July and flowering in year two.

**Autumn** can work but cuttings struck after September often won't root before winter and need to overwinter in a frost-free spot. Acceptable for hardier plants (chrysanthemum); risky for tender ones.

**Winter** is the worst time. Indoor heating dries out propagation tents, day length is too short for reliable root formation, and most cut flowers in supermarkets at that point have travelled long distances and lost vigour. Wait until February at the earliest.

## What's the realistic outcome — will I actually get a garden plant?

Be calibrated about expectations. Out of 10 attempted cuttings from a typical supermarket bouquet:

- **Realistic outcome**: 1–3 will root successfully and produce a garden plant.
- **Optimistic outcome** (with rooting hormone, ideal warmth, fresh material): 4–5 will succeed.
- **Pessimistic outcome** (winter, old stems, no rooting hormone): 0–1.

The 30–50% success rate is what experienced gardeners achieve. Beginners typically hit 10–20% on their first attempts. Like most propagation, success is an experience curve.

The financial calculation isn't really about the cost of plants — it's that growing your own from a bouquet is satisfying in a way that buying nursery plants isn't. A rose grown from a single stem cutting taken from a supermarket bouquet for a special occasion has more emotional weight than a rose bought from a garden centre for £15.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why won't my rose cuttings root?**
The four most common reasons: (1) the stem was too soft (use semi-mature stems, not the soft top growth), (2) the cutting dried out — humidity tent failure, (3) bottom cut wasn't at a leaf node — root formation is much weaker mid-internode, and (4) it's the wrong time of year (success rates drop hard outside spring/early summer).

**Can I propagate flowers in a glass of water instead of soil?**
Yes for some plants. Roses and chrysanthemums can root in water, though the roots that form are different (more brittle "water roots") and the plant has to readjust when transplanted. Soil propagation is more reliable, but water is fine for first attempts and lets you watch root formation.

**Are supermarket roses patented?**
Many modern hybrid varieties are patented or trademarked and technically protected from propagation. Enforcement at home-gardener scale is virtually nil, but commercial propagation of patented roses without licence is illegal. If you're propagating for personal use, the practical risk is zero; for commercial purposes, check the variety.

**Do I need a greenhouse to propagate cut flowers?**
No — a sunny windowsill, a clear plastic bag, and a few small pots are enough. A heated propagator (£20–40) speeds things up by maintaining 20–24°C soil temperature, but it's a nice-to-have, not a requirement.

**Will the rose I propagate look like the one in the bouquet?**
Yes — vegetative propagation produces a genetic clone of the parent plant. The colour, scent, and flower shape will be identical. The plant size and habit might be slightly different (some commercial roses are grafted onto a vigorous rootstock; cuttings grow on their own roots and may be smaller).

**What's the cheapest way to start propagating?**
Ask a neighbour with a garden for a few hardwood cuttings of a rose or hydrangea in autumn — much higher success than supermarket bouquet cuttings, because the parent material is healthier and freshly cut. Combined with bouquet experiments, this is the fastest way to build up a home cut-flower garden from £0.
