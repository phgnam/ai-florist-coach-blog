## Quick Answer

Cut tulips keep growing because their stems contain **active meristem tissue** that continues elongating for 5–10 days after harvest, often adding 5–10 cm. They also bend toward light (phototropism) and away from gravity (negative geotropism), which is why a tulip arrangement looks completely different in the morning than the night before. This is normal — and largely manageable.

## Why are tulips the only common cut flower that keeps growing?

Almost no other cut flower in the wholesale trade does this. A rose, a peony, a chrysanthemum, a lily — once you cut the stem, the stem is the length it will be. The bloom may open further, but the stem doesn't grow.

Tulips are different because of where their **growth tissue (meristem)** lives. In most flowers, growth happens at the very tip of the stem and in the bud — once the flower is cut, those tissues are removed or fixed. In tulips, an active meristem zone sits **just below the bloom and within the upper stem**, and it remains alive and dividing for days after the stem is cut.

Three things flow from this:

1. **The stem keeps elongating.** A tulip cut at 40 cm will commonly be 45–50 cm a week later.
2. **The stem keeps responding to light and gravity.** The cut stem moves through the vase day by day.
3. **The flower keeps opening and closing.** Tulip blooms open in warm light and close in cold dark — even after they're cut.

This is also why tulip arrangements are **never quite finished**. They are kinetic flowers: the same vase looks like a different arrangement every morning.

Typical cut-tulip growth observed in the vase:

- **Day 1–2 after cutting**: 1–2 cm of additional length, mostly invisible.
- **Day 3–5**: another 3–5 cm. The arrangement starts to look noticeably taller and looser; some heads tip over the rim of the vase.
- **Day 6–10**: another 2–3 cm. Stems begin to droop under the weight of the bloom; phototropic bending is now obvious.
- **Day 10+**: growth slows and stops. Bloom drops petals shortly after.

A 40 cm tulip stem at point of purchase is typically 45–50 cm by the end of vase life. The blooms also enlarge — often visibly bigger than they appeared on day one.

## Why do my tulips lean toward the window — and toward each other?

Two distinct movements happen simultaneously:

**Phototropism (bending toward light).** The cut tulip's growing meristem responds to light direction the same way the live plant in the field does. Tulips on a windowsill will tip their heads toward the window over 24–48 hours. Rotate the vase 180° each morning and you'll watch the heads track around to the new light.

**Negative geotropism (bending away from gravity).** Even in the dark, cut tulips will turn their heads upward over time. If a stem is bent or kinked, the flower will reorient the head to be vertical relative to gravity, even if that means the stem ends up in a curve.

When both forces act together, the result is the iconic **drooping, curving, individual-personality tulip arrangement** — every stem at a slightly different angle, every flower at a slightly different height.

This is the look that has made tulips a long-term favourite of florists who like organic, "wild" arrangement styles. It's also why **a tightly designed symmetrical tulip arrangement isn't really achievable for more than 12 hours**. Style your arrangement around the movement, not against it.

## How can I slow tulip movement and keep the arrangement neat?

If you want a tighter, less mobile arrangement (for a dinner party, a photoshoot, an event), several techniques slow tulip movement:

**Cold storage.** A tulip arrangement stored in a fridge or cool room overnight grows more slowly and bends less. The standard florist trick is to assemble the arrangement, refrigerate overnight, and pull it out 1 hour before the event.

**Pin the stems.** Inserting a pin (a sewing pin or sharp dressmaker's pin) through the upper stem just below the bloom, perpendicular to the stem, slows growth dramatically. The pin disrupts the meristem tissue and the stem stops elongating. This is a backstage florist trick rarely shown in the consumer-facing world.

**Constant low light.** A tulip arrangement in even, indirect light moves much less than one near a window. Move arrangements to interior tables for events; keep them near windows for the kinetic look.

**Put them in a tall glass vase.** A tall narrow vase holds tulips in place mechanically and prevents the heads from tipping outward. The growth is still happening, but the vase shape constrains it.

**Cold water and frequent water changes.** Cold water (3–5°C) slows metabolic activity, including stem elongation. Water changes every 1–2 days reduce bacterial activity that can weaken stem walls and accelerate drooping.

## Why do tulip blooms open during the day and close at night?

Tulip blooms have a behaviour called **nyctinasty** — they respond to temperature changes by opening and closing.

**Warm temperature (above ~20°C) plus light** triggers the inner cells of each petal to expand faster than the outer cells, and the bloom opens.

**Cool temperature (below ~15°C) plus darkness** reverses the differential expansion, and the bloom closes.

A tulip in a kitchen vase will open during the day, close at night, and open again the next morning — for about a week. The opening typically widens each day until the petals are fully reflexed, at which point the flower drops its petals 12–24 hours later.

This response is hard-wired into the cut flower; you can't turn it off. You can dampen it (constant temperature, constant low light) but the cycle continues until the bloom reaches end-of-life.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will my tulips eventually outgrow the vase?**
Yes — typically by day 5–8, several stems will tip over the vase rim, especially if the vase is tight. Cut 1–2 cm off the bottom of any over-tipping stems and re-place; this re-trims the stem to fit the vase and removes the section of stem that's been sitting in vase water (and may be starting to soften).

**Why do tulips wilt before other flowers in the same vase?**
Tulips are heavy-headed flowers on relatively soft stems. As they keep growing and the heads enlarge, the stem can't always support the weight. The "drooping tulip" often isn't dehydration — it's the flower outgrowing its own mechanical support. Cool storage and frequent recutting slow the effect.

**Can I stop tulips growing in the vase by adding sugar or other additives?**
Limited effect. Sugar in the vase water provides some carbohydrate for the flower but doesn't significantly slow stem elongation. Cold and pin-piercing the stem are far more effective. Don't bother with sugar-based "growth slowdown" recipes; they don't really work for tulips.

**Why do my tulips keep losing petals on the table?**
Tulip petals are loosely attached to the bloom and shed when the flower reaches end of vase life — usually day 7–10. Earlier petal drop usually signals heat stress (a sunny windowsill, a heated room, proximity to a radiator). Move to a cooler location.

**Should I refrigerate tulips overnight?**
Yes if you can — the trick that florists use to extend tulip arrangements for events. A standard fridge is fine (avoid storing tulips with apples, pears or other ethylene-producing fruit, which accelerates ageing). One night in cool storage typically buys 1–2 days of additional life.

**Do all tulips grow the same amount in the vase?**
No — French and double tulips elongate the most (often 7–10 cm), Triumph and Single Late tulips moderately (4–6 cm), and species tulips and Greigii tulips the least (2–4 cm). Parrot tulips fall somewhere in the middle and tend to bend more dramatically as they grow.
