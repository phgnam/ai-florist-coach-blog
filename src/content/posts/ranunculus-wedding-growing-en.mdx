## Quick Answer

Ranunculus are popular for weddings because their densely layered, tissue-thin petals create a romantic, peony-like look at a fraction of the price. They come in soft pastels ideal for bridal palettes, hold up well as cut flowers, and photograph beautifully. You can grow them at home by pre-soaking corms and planting in autumn (mild climates) or early spring (cold climates) in well-drained soil with full sun.

## What makes ranunculus perfect for wedding design?

Ranunculus (*Ranunculus asiaticus*) have become the florist's darling for bridal work, and the reasons go beyond beauty:

**Layered petals.** Each bloom contains dozens of paper-thin petals spiralling outward from a tight centre. The effect is lush and romantic without being heavy — exactly the aesthetic most modern brides want.

**Soft colour range.** Ranunculus come in blush, peach, cream, butter yellow, coral, burgundy, and pure white. These are the core colours of contemporary wedding palettes, and they blend naturally with roses, garden roses, and peonies.

**Compact form.** The rounded, globe-shaped blooms nestle together without gaps, making them ideal for hand-tied bouquets, boutonnieres, and centrepieces. They do not need wiring or heavy structural support.

**Good vase life.** Cut ranunculus last seven to ten days in water, which means arrangements prepared the day before a wedding still look fresh through the reception.

**Photographically forgiving.** The matte, tissue-like petal surface does not create harsh reflections under flash or studio lighting. This makes ranunculus one of the most photogenic flowers available.

**Cost.** Ranunculus are less expensive than peonies and garden roses, the flowers they most closely resemble. A bride can get the full, layered look she wants at significantly lower cost.

## How do you grow ranunculus at home?

Growing ranunculus is straightforward once you understand their corm-based lifecycle and temperature preferences.

**Start with corms.** Ranunculus grow from small, claw-shaped corms (not seeds). Buy them from a reputable bulb supplier in autumn. The "claws" should point downward when planted.

**Pre-soak the corms.** Soak corms in room-temperature water for three to four hours before planting. They will swell noticeably. Do not soak longer than six hours — they can rot.

**Plant at the right time.** In mild climates (USDA zones 8–10), plant in autumn for late winter and spring blooms. In cold climates (zones 3–7), plant in late winter or early spring as soon as the soil can be worked, or start in pots indoors and transplant after the last frost.

**Planting depth and spacing.** Set corms 5 cm deep and 10–15 cm apart, claws facing down. In heavy soil, plant shallower and amend with sand or perlite for drainage.

**Full sun, cool temperatures.** Ranunculus bloom best in full sun with cool nights (7–15°C). They thrive in the same conditions that suit sweet peas, poppies, and snapdragons — bright days, chilly nights.

**Water carefully.** Keep soil moist but never waterlogged. Once foliage appears, water regularly. After blooming finishes and leaves yellow, stop watering and let the corms go dormant.

## What problems should you watch for when growing ranunculus?

**Rot.** The most common killer. Over-soaking corms, planting too deep in heavy soil, or overwatering causes corms to rot before they sprout. Good drainage is non-negotiable.

**Heat.** Ranunculus stop blooming and die back when temperatures rise above 25°C consistently. In warm climates, they are strictly cool-season flowers. Plant early and harvest before summer heat arrives.

**Aphids.** Green aphids sometimes cluster on buds and young stems. A strong water spray or insecticidal soap handles them.

**Birds.** Sparrows and other birds occasionally peck at ranunculus buds. Lightweight bird netting over the bed prevents damage without blocking light.

**Short bloom season.** Each corm produces flowers for about four to six weeks. Succession planting two to three weeks apart extends the harvest period.

## Which ranunculus varieties are best for cutting?

**Italian ranunculus** (Elegance, Cloni series) have the largest, most layered blooms — up to 10 cm across — on strong stems. They are the standard for professional flower farming and wedding work.

**French ranunculus** produce slightly smaller blooms with more open centres. They have a charming, cottage-garden feel.

**Butterfly ranunculus** (Pon Pon series) have fringed, ruffled petals that give an unusual, whimsical look. They are increasingly popular for alternative bridal styles.

**Picotee varieties** have petals edged in a contrasting colour — blush tipped with rose, cream edged with burgundy. These are striking in arrangements and command premium prices at market.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do ranunculus last in a vase?**
Seven to ten days with fresh water and flower food. Re-cut stems every two to three days and keep the vase in a cool spot away from direct sun and fruit.

**Can you grow ranunculus in pots?**
Yes. Use a pot at least 20 cm deep with drainage holes, fill with a well-draining mix, and plant five to seven corms per 30 cm pot. Place in full sun in a cool location.

**Do ranunculus come back every year?**
In mild climates (zones 8–10), corms can perennialise if the soil stays dry during summer dormancy. In cold or wet climates, treat them as annuals or lift and store corms over winter.

**Are ranunculus poisonous?**
All *Ranunculus* species contain ranunculin, which breaks down into a mild irritant (protoanemonin) when crushed. Contact can cause skin rash, and ingestion causes mouth irritation. Keep them away from children and pets who might chew them.

**What is the difference between ranunculus and peonies?**
Ranunculus have thinner, more numerous petals, thinner stems, and smaller blooms than peonies. They bloom in spring while peonies bloom in late spring to early summer. Ranunculus are also significantly less expensive and easier to source year-round from cooler-climate growers.
