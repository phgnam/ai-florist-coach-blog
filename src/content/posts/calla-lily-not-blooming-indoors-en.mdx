## Quick Answer

Indoor calla lilies usually fail to bloom because they never received a proper dry rest, were over-fertilised with nitrogen, or never saw the bright light they need to set flower buds. Give the rhizome a 2–3 month dry dormancy in autumn, restart it in fresh soil with a phosphorus-leaning feed, and place it in 4–6 hours of bright direct light. With this cycle, a healthy Zantedeschia reliably reblooms once a year.

## Why does an indoor calla lily often produce only leaves?

A calla lily (*Zantedeschia*) flowers on energy stored in the rhizome from the previous growing season. If anything in the year before disrupts that storage, the plant rebuilds leaves first and skips flowers. The most common culprits are:

**No dormancy.** Calla lilies are not evergreen tropicals. They evolved to grow during a wet season and rest during a dry one. Indoors, they often stay damp and warm year-round, so the rhizome never gets the cue to mature flower buds.

**Too much nitrogen.** General houseplant fertiliser pushes leaf growth at the expense of bloom. Calla lilies want a low-nitrogen, higher-phosphorus formulation during the active growing months.

**Low light.** A calla lily on a north window or in a dim corner builds soft, floppy leaves and no flowers. They need at least 4–6 hours of bright, direct light.

**Wrong pot size.** A pot far larger than the rhizome holds soggy soil that rots the rhizome before it can flower.

**Young or exhausted rhizome.** Tiny offsets (under about 5 cm across) are usually too small to flower in their first year, and very old, depleted rhizomes flower less unless divided and refreshed.

## What does the calla lily growth cycle actually look like?

Reading the cycle correctly is the single biggest fix.

| Phase | Months (Northern Hemisphere) | What the plant looks like | Care |
|---|---|---|---|
| Active growth | Late winter to early summer | New leaves, then flower stalks | Bright light, regular water, balanced feed |
| Peak flowering | Late spring to mid summer | One to several spathes | Steady moisture, weekly low-nitrogen feed |
| Wind-down | Late summer | Leaves yellow naturally | Reduce water, stop feeding |
| Dormancy | Autumn (8–12 weeks) | Leaves dry off, rhizome rests | Dry, cool, dark — almost no water |
| Wake-up | Mid to late winter | First green shoot appears | Repot, restart watering and light |

Indoors, this rhythm is easy to derail. The most common mistake is treating the plant like a tropical houseplant during the wind-down phase and watering it through what should be the rest. The rhizome then never resets.

## How do I trigger a proper dormancy indoors?

Use the leaves as the signal, not the calendar. When the foliage starts yellowing in late summer or autumn:

1. **Stop fertilising immediately.**
2. **Cut watering by half** for 2–3 weeks.
3. **Stop watering completely** when most leaves have flopped or yellowed.
4. **Let the soil dry out fully** — this can take another 2–3 weeks.
5. **Cut all the foliage down to about 2 cm above the soil.**
6. **Move the pot somewhere cool and dark** (10–15 °C, e.g. an unheated room or cupboard).
7. **Leave it dry and untouched for 8–12 weeks.**

After 8–12 weeks, bring the pot back to a bright, warm room, top-dress with fresh mix, and water lightly. A green shoot should appear within 2–3 weeks. Do not start heavy watering or feeding until the leaves are 5–10 cm tall — that is when the rhizome is ready to draw water hard.

## What feed produces flowers instead of leaves?

Switch from a balanced houseplant fertiliser to a "tomato" or "bloom" formula during the active season. Look for an NPK where phosphorus (the middle number) is roughly equal to or higher than nitrogen — for example, 5-10-10 or 4-8-8. Apply at half the labelled strength every 1–2 weeks while the plant is actively growing.

Stop feeding the moment leaves begin yellowing in late summer. Continued feeding through the wind-down keeps the rhizome from maturing flower buds and is the single most common cause of "all leaves, no flowers next year".

If you are also growing other flowering plants on the same windowsill, the same low-nitrogen rule applies — see [Why are my peonies not blooming?](/posts/peonies-not-blooming-en/) for an outdoor-perennial parallel.

## How much light does an indoor calla lily really need?

More than most apartments offer without help. The minimum is:

- **South-facing window:** 1 metre back from the glass, behind a sheer curtain in midsummer
- **East- or west-facing window:** right at the glass
- **North-facing window:** not enough on its own — supplement with a 20–30 W full-spectrum LED grow light for 6 hours a day

Pale, drooping leaves and long stretched stems mean the plant is light-starved. Strong upright leaves with deep colour and visible white speckling (in spotted-leaf cultivars) mean it has the light it needs to flower.

For a deeper look at low-light flowering options that suit dim flats, see [Which flowering houseplants tolerate low light?](/posts/low-light-indoor-flowers-en/).

## What pot size, soil, and depth give the best results?

Match the pot to the rhizome, not the plant size:

- **Single rhizome (5–8 cm across):** 15–18 cm diameter pot
- **Cluster of rhizomes:** pot 2–3 cm wider than the cluster
- **Drainage:** mandatory — multiple holes, no decorative cover-pot that traps water

Plant the rhizome with growth points facing up, just under 5 cm of soil. Burying it deeper delays sprouting and rots the crown; planting too shallow exposes the rhizome to drying out in active growth.

Use a free-draining mix: two parts good-quality houseplant compost, one part perlite, plus a handful of coarse grit at the bottom. Calla lilies tolerate richer soil than many bulbs, but they hate sodden compost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do calla lilies bloom more than once a year indoors?**
Usually only once. With perfect light and feeding, a vigorous *Zantedeschia rehmannii* hybrid may push a second flush of one or two flowers, but a reliable repeat-bloom cycle is once per year.

**My calla lily rhizome is mushy after dormancy. Can I save it?**
Sometimes. Lift the rhizome and check for firm, white tissue. Cut away soft brown sections with a clean knife, dust the cuts with cinnamon or sulphur, and let the rhizome air-dry for a day before potting in fresh, dry mix. Skip watering for a week.

**Can I keep a calla lily as an evergreen houseplant without dormancy?**
You can keep it alive, but it will rarely flower well. Without the dry rest, the rhizome puts most of its energy into foliage. Even a short, drier 6–8 week rest is much better than none.

**Should I cut off spent calla lily flowers?**
Yes. Cut the stem at its base inside the leaf cluster as soon as the spathe starts curling and turning green. This redirects energy into the rhizome instead of seed production.

**Are indoor calla lilies safe around cats and dogs?**
No. All parts of *Zantedeschia* contain insoluble calcium oxalate crystals that cause severe mouth and throat irritation, drooling, and vomiting. Keep them out of reach of pets and small children. For pet-safe alternatives, see [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).
