## Quick Answer

The reliable flowers for paving cracks are **creeping thyme**, **sweet alyssum**, **Mexican fleabane (Erigeron karvinskianus)**, **Corsican mint**, **sea thrift**, **dwarf mossy saxifrage**, and **chamomile**. They handle hot, dry, low-soil conditions because they evolved on cliff faces or stony scree. Sow seed in autumn or spring directly into a brushed-out crack, water for the first 6 weeks, and they self-sow happily for years.

## Why is a paving crack a tough place to grow anything?

A 5–15 mm gap between slabs holds maybe a teaspoon of soil, drains in seconds, and bakes at midday because the surrounding stone is hot. Most ornamental flowers fail in those conditions. The plants that thrive evolved on Mediterranean cliffs, alpine scree, or coastal rocks — they prefer poor soil, full sun, and minimal water. Anything happier in a flowerbed is the wrong choice for a crack.

## Which flowers work in cracks reliably?

The shortlist, by where they perform:

- **Sun, hot paving** — creeping thyme (*Thymus serpyllum*), sweet alyssum, sea thrift (*Armeria maritima*), Mexican fleabane
- **Part shade, cooler joints** — Corsican mint (*Mentha requienii*), dwarf mossy saxifrage, ajuga, *Erigeron karvinskianus*
- **Damp shaded spots** — *Soleirolia* (mind-your-own-business — flowers tiny but plant lush), *Mazus reptans*

For a stepping-stone path through grass: choose creeping thyme or chamomile (*Chamaemelum nobile* 'Treneague'). Both tolerate light foot traffic and release scent when stepped on.

## How do you plant flowers in a paving crack?

Two methods work depending on whether you start from seed or plug:

**Seed sowing:**

- Brush out the crack with a stiff brush to remove moss and old soil
- Refill with a 50/50 mix of compost and grit
- Sprinkle seed lightly along the crack
- Cover with 2 mm of grit (helps seed sit in place)
- Water gently with a fine rose; keep moist for 2–3 weeks until germination

**Plug planting:**

- Use small (3 cm) plug plants — they fit a 10 mm gap with gentle root squeezing
- Excavate the crack to 5 cm deep
- Fill the bottom 2 cm with a free-draining compost/grit mix
- Set the plug so the crown sits flush with the paving surface
- Water thoroughly and again on day 3, then every 2–3 days for 4–6 weeks

After establishment, most paving-crack plants need no irrigation except in extended drought.

## Which flowers self-sow into cracks on their own?

Several species are notorious for colonising paving without help:

- **Mexican fleabane** — pretty white-and-pink daisies May–November, seeds blow into every crack within 50 m
- **Sweet alyssum** — soft white or purple, sweet-honey scent, self-sows freely
- **Corydalis lutea** — yellow tubular flowers, prefers shaded crevices
- **Centranthus ruber** (red valerian) — the flower you see on old stone walls in coastal villages

If you let one of these flower freely on your patio, you are likely growing it in cracks within 2–3 years even without effort.

## What flowers should you avoid in paving cracks?

The common mistakes:

- **Lavender** — too woody, sulks in restricted root run, looks scrappy
- **Pansies and violas** — tolerate cracks short-term but die in hot summer
- **Most herbs except thyme, oregano, and dwarf chamomile** — too much top growth for the available root volume
- **Anything aggressive like mint** — except low-growing Corsican mint, regular peppermint colonises everything

Match the plant's native habitat to the conditions of your paving. A plant from a cool moist meadow does not adapt to a south-facing patio crack, no matter how much you water.

## How do you keep paving-crack flowers from spreading too far?

The same self-seeding habit that makes these plants successful can also make them a nuisance:

- **Trim back after flowering** to reduce seed set — shears or a strimmer over thyme works fine
- **Pull seedlings while small** in unwanted spots
- **Choose sterile cultivars** where available — some Erigeron and alyssum cultivars produce less viable seed
- **Use plug plants in single chosen spots** rather than scattering seed everywhere

A bit of light pruning each autumn keeps a paving-crack mat looking deliberate rather than weedy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will paving-crack flowers damage the paving over time?

Surface plants like thyme and alyssum will not. Deep-rooted self-sown weeds (dandelion, woody perennials) can lift slabs over decades, but the flowers above are shallow-rooted by nature.

### Can I plant in cracks if my paving has weed-suppressing membrane underneath?

The membrane reduces what is possible. Surface plants in shallow grit pockets work; anything wanting deeper roots fails. Pull a small section of membrane back at the planting spot if possible.

### How long until the flowers fill the cracks?

From seed: visible flowers within 8–12 weeks, full coverage within one season for fast types like alyssum. From plugs: cosmetic fill within 6 weeks. Tighter-mat species like thyme need a full year to look mature.

### Do paving-crack flowers attract bees?

Yes — thyme, alyssum, and erigeron are bee magnets. A square metre of flowering thyme can have 20+ bees on a sunny morning. Pair with the [flowers for bees guide](/posts/flowers-for-bees-en/) for a coordinated planting.

### What about between block paving (small concrete blocks)?

The same plant list works. The narrower the gap, the more important plug planting becomes — seeds wash out of very tight joints. Mexican fleabane and Corsican mint specifically excel in narrow joints.

### Can I walk on a thyme path?

Light foot traffic is fine. A daily commuter path will wear thyme thin in 1–2 years; weekly use it lasts indefinitely. For genuinely high-traffic paths, *Chamaemelum nobile* 'Treneague' (a non-flowering chamomile) handles more wear.
