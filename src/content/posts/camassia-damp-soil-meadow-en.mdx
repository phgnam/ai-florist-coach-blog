## Quick Answer

Camassia (quamash) is one of very few bulbs that thrives in damp, heavy clay — the same soil that rots tulips and stunts daffodils. It evolved on seasonally flooded North American prairie meadows, so it tolerates wet winters and springs as long as the soil drains by midsummer. Plant the bulbs 10–15 cm deep in autumn, in clumps of 5–9, in full sun or light dappled shade. Once established it self-sows and slowly forms drifts. Choose *Camassia leichtlinii* for tall blue spikes, *C. quamash* for shorter naturalising drifts, or *C. cusickii* for early flowering.

## Why does camassia tolerate wet clay when most spring bulbs do not?

Most popular spring bulbs (tulips, hyacinths, many daffodils, dwarf iris) come from Mediterranean or central-Asian climates with hot dry summers and cool wet winters. Their bulbs survive winter wet by sitting at depth in coarse, free-draining soil; in heavy clay they rot.

*Camassia* is different. The genus is native to the western seaboard of North America — Oregon, Washington, British Columbia — where it grows on grasslands and prairie meadows that flood every winter and dry out in summer. Two adaptations make this possible:

**Tolerance of saturated soil during dormancy.** Where a tulip bulb in sodden clay quickly rots, a camassia bulb sits comfortably underwater for weeks at a time. The bulb tunic is dense and waxy and the inner scales tolerate anaerobic conditions far better.

**Active growth in the wet season.** Camassia leafs and flowers in spring, when soils are still wet. By the time soils dry in summer, the plant is already retreating into dormancy. There is no period of summer growth that needs irrigation — exactly the opposite of dry-summer hot-climate bulbs.

The practical implication: if your border drowns daffodils and turns tulips to mush, try camassia. It is one of the most reliable bulbs for difficult ground.

## Which camassia species is best for which conditions?

There are four widely available species and each has a distinct niche.

| Species | Height | Flower colour | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Camassia leichtlinii* | 90–120 cm | Pale blue, deep blue, white, cream | Tallest, latest; classic border statement |
| *C. leichtlinii* 'Caerulea' | 100 cm | Sky blue | The standard blue cultivar |
| *C. leichtlinii* 'Alba' / 'Semiplena' | 90 cm | White / cream double | White meadow plantings; semi-double form holds longer |
| *C. quamash* | 30–60 cm | Blue, sometimes white | Shorter, naturalising drifts in lawn or meadow |
| *C. cusickii* | 60–90 cm | Pale blue | Earlier flowering than the type; useful for extending the season |
| *C. scilloides* (wild hyacinth) | 30 cm | Pale blue | American native; small-scale meadow plantings |

For tallest impact in a perennial border, *C. leichtlinii* 'Caerulea' is the standard choice. For naturalising in long grass under fruit trees, *C. quamash* is best — its shorter stature copes with the surrounding grass and it self-seeds readily.

## How exactly should I plant camassia in heavy clay?

Camassia tolerates clay better than most bulbs, but a few details make the difference between a one-year flop and a long-lived planting.

### Step 1: Time the planting

**Autumn.** September to early November is ideal. Bulbs need a settling period before winter. Camassia is occasionally available "in the green" (potted in growth) in spring; that works too but autumn dormant bulbs are cheaper and more widely sold.

### Step 2: Choose the position

- **Full sun to light dappled shade.** Tolerates more shade than tulips but flowers best in sun.
- **Moisture-retentive ground.** Camassia is one of the few bulbs that prefers heavier, slower-draining soil. Even seasonally wet ground works.
- **Avoid waterlogged sumps.** Soil that is wet year-round is too much. Soil that is wet October–April but dry July–September is ideal.

### Step 3: Plant at the right depth

- **10–15 cm deep** to the base of the bulb, measured from the soil surface.
- **15–20 cm spacing** between bulbs in a clump.
- **Plant in groups of 5–9** for natural drift effect; avoid single bulbs scattered evenly.

### Step 4: Use a planting hole, not a grid

Dig a wider hole than for daffodils — about 30 cm across — and place 5–9 bulbs at the bottom, pointed-end up. Backfill with the original soil, mixing in a handful of leaf mould or coarse compost. Tamp and water once.

### Step 5: First-year establishment

Water sparingly through the first autumn if dry; usually no other care is needed. Camassia generally flowers in its first year from autumn-planted bulbs, but in heavy clay flowering may be delayed to year two.

## How do I combine camassia with other plants in a damp meadow or border?

Camassia rewards careful companions. The flower spike is so distinct that it works either as a strong vertical accent or as a complement to softer plants.

**For naturalising meadows:**

- *Camassia quamash* with cowslips (*Primula veris*) and lady's smock (*Cardamine pratensis*) creates a classic damp-meadow tapestry.
- Underplant with *Anemone nemorosa* (wood anemone) for a longer woodland-edge season.
- *Fritillaria meleagris* (snake's head fritillary) shares a love of damp soil and flowers slightly earlier.

**For perennial borders:**

- Plant tall *C. leichtlinii* behind clouds of *Geranium pratense* or *G. macrorrhizum*.
- Combine with the upright spires of *Iris sibirica* — both tolerate damp.
- Late-spring *Aquilegia vulgaris* fills the same vertical band before the summer perennials emerge.

**For damp-shade edges:**

- Camassia mixes well with *Hosta*, *Astilbe*, and *Rodgersia* — all of which appreciate moisture-retentive soil.
- *Polygonatum* (Solomon's seal) and *Dicentra* (now *Lamprocapnos*) flower at the same time and in the same conditions.

## How do I look after camassia after flowering, and through summer dormancy?

The post-flowering routine is simple but rigid.

**Immediately after flowering:** You can deadhead spent flower spikes if you want to prevent self-seeding. In meadow plantings, leave them — the seed dispersal is part of how drifts thicken naturally.

**During leaf life (4–6 weeks after flowering):** Do not cut leaves. They photosynthesise to refill the bulb. Resist the urge to tidy.

**As leaves yellow:** Pull or cut at the base. The bulb is now dormant.

**Through summer:** Avoid heavy watering of the area. Wet summer conditions are the only thing that will cause an established camassia clump to rot. Dry soil during dormancy is fine — the bulb is well-suited to it.

**Autumn:** Foliage may briefly emerge in mild autumns. This is a quirk of *C. quamash* in particular and does not damage the plant. Leave it alone; it will fade and the proper spring growth will follow.

## Are camassias safe for homes with pets and children?

Mixed answer — depends on which species and what part of the plant.

The North American native peoples ate the cooked bulbs of *C. quamash* and *C. leichtlinii* as a staple food (the name "quamash" is anglicised from a Native American word for the food bulb). However, *Camassia* bulbs can be confused with the bulbs of the deeply toxic *Zigadenus venenosus* (death camas), which grew in similar habitats — fatalities historically resulted from misidentification.

In a garden context:

- **Cooked traditional food does not mean raw is safe.** Raw bulbs are mildly toxic in quantity; cooking destroys most of the toxic compounds.
- **Discourage pets from digging up bulbs.** A determined dog that consumes several bulbs may experience digestive upset.
- **Do not let children eat any unknown garden bulb.** The risk of misidentification with toxic species is the historical hazard.
- **Wash hands after handling.** Mild skin irritation is possible.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My garden has heavy clay that drowns daffodils. Will camassia really thrive there?**
Yes — this is exactly the situation camassia evolved for. Plant 10–15 cm deep in autumn in groups of 5–9 bulbs. Expect some bulbs to skip flowering in their first year, but established clumps flower reliably from year two onward and slowly thicken.

**Can I grow camassia in pots?**
Less reliably than in the ground. The combination of small soil volume and dryer summer conditions of pots is the opposite of what camassia prefers. If you must, use deep pots (30+ cm), free-draining loam-based compost, and water generously through spring growth and bloom; let them dry out completely in summer.

**My camassia grew leaves but no flowers — what went wrong?**
Three common causes. (1) Bulbs planted too shallow (less than 8 cm). Replant deeper. (2) Bulbs planted in summer dryness — autumn-planted bulbs flower more reliably. (3) Heavy shade — camassia tolerates light dappled shade but flowers best in 6+ hours of direct sun.

**Should I lift and divide established camassia clumps?**
Only if flowering deteriorates noticeably, which usually takes 8–12 years. Lift in summer when foliage has died back, separate the bulb offsets, and replant immediately at the same depth. Most camassia clumps thrive on benign neglect — division is rarely needed.

**Does camassia work in lawns like crocus and snowdrops do?**
*C. quamash* does, especially in damp lawns that you mow late. Plant 10 cm deep in autumn in scattered drifts, and follow the same rule as for crocus: do not mow for at least 6 weeks after flowering finishes. *C. leichtlinii* is too tall for most lawns and should go in border or meadow positions instead.
