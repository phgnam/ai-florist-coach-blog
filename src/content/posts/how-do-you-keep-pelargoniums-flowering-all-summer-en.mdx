## Quick Answer

Pelargoniums flower from May to October when **deadheaded weekly**, **watered deeply but only when soil is dry**, **fed high-potash tomato food every 10–14 days**, **grown in 6+ hours of sun**, and **kept just-warm rather than hot**. Pinch any leggy stems by half in midsummer to trigger a second flush. The single biggest cause of stalling is irregular feeding — the plants flower on the buds they form 10–14 days earlier.

## Why does deadheading matter so much?

Pelargoniums put visible energy into setting seed once flowers are pollinated. Removing spent flower clusters before they form seed redirects that energy into the next bud spike, and the difference between a deadheaded and undeadheaded plant by late August is striking.

The technique:

- Wait until **most flowers in a head are spent** (a few fresh ones is fine to keep)
- Snap or cut the **whole flower stem** at the base where it joins the main stem
- Don't just remove dead petals — the stalk left behind still carries seed
- Compost the removed stems

A weekly 10-minute pass over a row of pelargoniums in midsummer roughly doubles the flower count for the rest of the season.

## How often should pelargoniums be watered?

The classic mistake is watering little and often. Pelargoniums prefer **deep but infrequent**:

- **Soak the pot until water drains from the bottom**
- **Wait until the top 3–5 cm of compost is dry** before watering again
- In summer this is roughly **every 3–5 days for terracotta pots**, **5–7 days for plastic**
- In peak heat (above 28 °C) container plants may need daily watering
- **Discard saucer water** — sitting water rots roots

Wilt is your friend with pelargoniums. A plant that wilts mildly between waterings sets more flower buds than one kept constantly moist. The same plant kept soggy is greener but blooms thin.

## What feed schedule keeps the flowers coming?

Pelargoniums are moderate feeders that respond strongly to potassium and modestly to nitrogen.

The schedule:

- **No feed in May** if planted in fresh compost (the compost has 4–6 weeks of nutrients)
- **High-potash tomato feed (5-2-7 type)** every 10–14 days from June through September
- **Half-strength balanced feed** if leaves yellow despite full sun (rare but possible)
- **Stop feeding in October** as growth slows and overwintering begins

Skip-feeding for 4 weeks in summer reduces flower count noticeably within 2–3 weeks. Most "why has my pelargonium stopped flowering" complaints are missed feeds.

## How much sun do pelargoniums actually need?

Six hours of direct sun is the practical minimum for heavy flowering.

- **Under 4 hours of sun** — leafy growth, few flowers
- **4–6 hours** — modest flowering
- **6–8 hours** — strong reliable flowering
- **8+ hours** — best flowering, but may need extra watering

In a mediterranean or hot continental climate, afternoon shade in midsummer (very high temperatures) protects flower colour from fading. In a UK garden, choose the sunniest spot you have — pelargoniums rarely get too much sun in temperate climates.

## Why does heat make pelargoniums stall?

A common surprise: pelargoniums actually slow flowering above 30 °C. They evolved on cool South African coastal slopes, not in tropical heat. The signs of heat stall:

- Flowers smaller than usual or fading fast
- Leaves slightly yellow at edges
- Buds aborting before opening
- Plant looks fine but flowering pauses

The remedies for hot summers:

- **Move pots to morning sun, afternoon shade**
- **Water in the early morning** so roots are cool through the day
- **Mulch the soil surface** with bark chippings to keep root temperatures stable
- **Wait** — they restart flowering as soon as nights cool below 22 °C

A peak-summer pause of 2–3 weeks is normal in hot regions and not a sign of plant trouble.

## Should you pinch back to refresh midsummer flowering?

Yes — most pelargonium varieties benefit from a midsummer cut-back to trigger a fresh flush.

The technique:

- **Late July or early August**, when first flush is fading
- Cut **all stems back by one-third to one-half**, just above a leaf node
- Remove any yellowing or congested lower leaves
- Water and feed immediately
- New flowering shoots emerge within 2 weeks

The first 2 weeks after cutting back look bare, but the August–September flush is often heavier than the first. A plant left uncut after midsummer continues to bloom but in a leggier, more sparse pattern.

## What about scented-leaf pelargoniums — same care?

Mostly yes, but with adjustments:

- **Less rich soil** — they prefer leaner compost, lower nitrogen
- **Slightly less water** — they tolerate drought better than zonal types
- **Same sun and feed** — high-potash food still benefits flowering
- **Cut more lightly** in midsummer — they have woody stems that take longer to regrow
- **Pruning preserves shape** of older specimens; an annual prune after flowering keeps them tidy for years

Lemon-, rose-, and peppermint-scented forms make excellent fragrant edges in a herb bed; the flowers are smaller than zonals but the leaf scent is the main feature.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are my pelargonium flowers dropping off before opening?

Bud drop usually indicates **water stress** (too dry or too wet), **low light**, or **sudden temperature changes**. Aim for steady soak-and-dry watering, full sun, and avoid moving pots from cold to hot positions overnight.

### Should I deadhead by pinching or cutting?

Either works. Pinching at the base of the flower stem is faster but bruises the stem; cutting with snips is cleaner. The result on flower count is similar.

### Can I take cuttings during the flowering season?

Yes — non-flowering side shoots root well in summer. A 10 cm cutting from a non-flowering stem, planted in damp gritty compost, roots in 2–3 weeks. This is the standard way to multiply favourite cultivars.

### Why are leaves turning red on the edges?

Cool nights (below 12 °C) cause anthocyanin pigments to develop in pelargonium leaves, producing red edges. This is cosmetic and not a problem. Sustained cold (below 4 °C) can damage the plant — bring inside before frost. See the [overwintering geraniums on a windowsill guide](/posts/how-do-you-overwinter-geraniums-on-a-windowsill-en/) for the cold-season management.

### Are zonal and ivy-leaved pelargoniums different to care for?

Slightly. Ivy-leaved (trailing) pelargoniums tolerate **less direct sun** (4–6 hours is fine), need **more frequent watering** in baskets, and benefit from **more frequent feeding** because basket soil volume is small. Otherwise the rules match.

### What's the best pot size?

For zonal pelargoniums, a 25–30 cm pot suits 1–2 plants. For ivy-leaved trailers in a hanging basket, a 30 cm basket holds 3–4 plants. Smaller pots dry too fast in midsummer; larger pots stay too moist for the deep-and-dry rhythm.
