Edible flowers are having a genuine culinary moment. Restaurant chefs have used them for decades, but they've become increasingly accessible to home cooks who grow their own or can find them at farmers' markets and specialist suppliers. Used well, edible flowers bring colour, fragrance, subtle flavour, and an undeniable sense of celebration to food.

But the single most important thing to know is this: **not all flowers are edible, and some are dangerously toxic**. The rule is absolute — never eat a flower unless you are completely certain of its identity and safety.

## The best edible flowers and how they taste

**Nasturtium** (*Tropaeolum majus*) is perhaps the most practical edible flower for home cooks. The flowers have a peppery, watercress-like flavour that works beautifully in salads and as a garnish for savoury dishes. The leaves are also edible and share the same peppery character. Easy to grow, prolific, and the flowers come in a wide range of colours from pale yellow to deep orange and red. Both the flowers and the seed pods (which taste like capers) are edible.

**Viola and pansy** (*Viola* species) are mild in flavour — slightly grassy, slightly sweet — and are primarily used for their visual impact. Their distinctive face-like markings make them one of the most visually striking garnishes available. Use them on cakes, desserts, cocktails, and cheese boards. They're also one of the easiest edible flowers to find as they're commonly grown as garden annuals.

**Rose petals** (*Rosa* species) have a delicate floral flavour that concentrates in darker, more fragrant varieties. The key to edible rose petals is sourcing — supermarket roses are treated with pesticides not approved for food use. You need roses either grown specifically for culinary use or your own unsprayed garden roses. Remove the white base of each petal (it's bitter). Use in jams, syrups, ice creams, cakes, and as garnishes.

**Lavender** (*Lavandula angustifolia*) is intensely fragrant and must be used sparingly — a little goes a long way, and too much makes food taste like soap. Culinary lavender is typically the species *Lavandula angustifolia*. Use dried or fresh flowers in shortbread, honey, lemonade, cocktails, and as part of *herbes de Provence* in savoury cooking with lamb or chicken.

**Borage** (*Borago officinalis*) produces bright blue star-shaped flowers with a flavour that is distinctly cucumber-like. Classic in the British drink Pimm's and in gin cocktails, borage flowers are also beautiful frozen into ice cubes. The flavour is subtle enough to use as a garnish without overpowering other elements.

**Courgette / zucchini flowers** (*Cucurbita pepo*) are a vegetable-adjacent edible flower that has been eaten around the Mediterranean for centuries. The large, delicate orange flowers are stuffed with ricotta and herbs, then battered and fried — a classic Italian preparation. They have a very short shelf life and are best used on the day of picking.

**Calendula** (*Calendula officinalis*) — often called pot marigold — has a slightly bitter, tangy flavour and vivid orange and yellow petals. Used to colour butter and cheese historically (before commercial food colouring), calendula petals work well in rice dishes, salads, and egg dishes. Note: calendula is different from *Tagetes* marigolds (see dangerous flowers below).

**Elder flower** (*Sambucus nigra*) has an unmistakable, intoxicatingly sweet fragrance. The flowers are used to make elderflower cordial (one of the great British summer drinks), elderflower champagne, and in fritters. The berries require cooking; raw elderberries are toxic. Only use the flowers of *Sambucus nigra* — other elderberry species may not be safe.

**Chamomile** (*Matricaria chamomilla*) flowers have the familiar apple-honey flavour of chamomile tea. Use fresh or dried in teas, baked goods, and desserts.

**Chive flowers** (*Allium schoenoprasum*) have a mild onion flavour and make a beautiful garnish for soups and salads. The flowers can be separated into individual florets and scattered.

## Flowers that are toxic — do not eat

**Foxglove** (*Digitalis*) — contains cardiac glycosides that can cause heart failure. This is a serious danger — foxgloves are commonly grown in gardens and are beautiful. Do not eat.

**Daffodil and narcissus** (*Narcissus*) — all parts are toxic, including the flowers. The toxins are present in the water used to hold cut daffodils — never mix daffodils in the same vase as flowers you intend to eat.

**Sweet pea** (*Lathyrus odoratus*) — the flowering ornamental sweet pea is toxic and should never be confused with edible garden peas.

**Lily of the valley** (*Convallaria majalis*) — highly toxic, capable of causing serious cardiac effects.

**Larkspur and delphinium** (*Delphinium* and *Consolida*) — toxic, can cause respiratory failure in large quantities.

**Wisteria** — all parts, including flowers, are toxic.

**Oleander** (*Nerium oleander*) — extremely toxic. Even smoke from burning oleander is hazardous.

## Critical sourcing rules

Even with safe, correctly identified flowers, sourcing matters enormously:

1. **Never use flowers from florists or supermarkets** — these are grown with pesticides not approved for food use
2. **Grow your own** — this is by far the safest and most economical approach
3. **Buy from specialist food-grade suppliers** who grow specifically for culinary use
4. **Avoid roadsides and public spaces** — contamination from exhaust fumes, dog urine, and general urban pollution
5. **Wash gently** just before use — edible flowers are fragile and will deteriorate if washed too far in advance

The world of edible flowers rewards the careful and curious cook. When used with a light touch and with proper sourcing, they bring something genuinely magical to food.
