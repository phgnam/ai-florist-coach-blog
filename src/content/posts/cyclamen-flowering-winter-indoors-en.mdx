## Quick Answer

Florist cyclamen (*Cyclamen persicum*) flower for **8–12 weeks indoors** if you keep them at **10–16°C**, give them **bright indirect light**, and water from **below into a saucer** rather than over the crown. Avoid heated rooms above 20°C, draughts from radiators, and watering the centre of the plant. After flowering, let the tuber dry out through summer, then restart watering in autumn for a second-year bloom.

## Why do supermarket cyclamen die so quickly?

A new cyclamen looks like a houseplant but actually behaves more like a **cool-season alpine bulb**. Most are killed within three weeks of being brought into a warm sitting room because the conditions are completely wrong for the plant.

Three pressures combine to crash a typical indoor cyclamen:

**Heat.** Florist cyclamen are bred from a Mediterranean species adapted to **cool damp winters and dry summers**. Above about **18°C** the leaves yellow, the flower stems flop, and the tuber starts moving towards summer dormancy months early.

**Dry air.** Central heating drops indoor humidity to 25–35%, well below the **50–60% humidity** the plant evolved with. Buds dry up before opening; petals brown at the edges within days.

**Tuber rot.** Watering the standard way — from above, over the crown — soaks the tuber in cold water and triggers fungal rot. The plant collapses suddenly with mushy stems and a bad smell at the base.

The plant is not difficult; it is just placed in the wrong room. Move it to a spot that mimics its native winter (cool, bright, slightly humid) and it will flower for months without fuss.

## Where should you put a cyclamen in the house?

The single most important call you make is **which room**. Get this right and almost everything else self-corrects.

**Aim for:**

- **10–16°C overnight, no more than 18°C by day.** Conservatories, unheated porches, cool bedrooms, and bright stairwells work well.
- **Bright indirect light.** A north-facing window with no direct sun, or an east-facing window with morning sun only, is ideal.
- **No direct heat sources.** Keep the pot at least 1.5 m from radiators, wood stoves, and heated floors. Even one warm afternoon next to a radiator can wilt the entire plant.
- **No cold draughts.** Hallway floors near doors, or shelves under leaky single-glazed windows, can get colder than the leaves tolerate at night.

**Avoid:**

- Living rooms heated above 20°C in the evening.
- Bathrooms (humidity is fine, but most are too warm and too dim).
- South-facing windowsills (direct sun cooks the leaves and tuber).
- Tops of TV cabinets, fridges, or other appliances that radiate warmth.

If your home has only warm rooms, you can still keep cyclamen alive by placing them in the **coolest available spot at night** and giving them a "winter trip" to a cool porch or balcony for several hours during the day.

## How should you water cyclamen so the tuber doesn't rot?

Cyclamen watering is unusual. Most houseplants are watered top-down; cyclamen prefer **bottom watering**, every time.

The bottom-watering routine:

1. Sit the plastic nursery pot in **3–5 cm of room-temperature water** in a saucer.
2. Leave for **15–30 minutes** until the soil surface looks evenly damp.
3. **Tip out any unused water.** Do not let the pot stand in water afterwards.
4. Repeat when the **top 2 cm of compost feels dry to the touch**, typically every 5–10 days indoors.

Why bottom watering matters:

- The tuber sits half-buried with the crown raised above the soil. Water poured over the crown collects between the petioles, soaks the tuber, and triggers grey mould (*Botrytis*) and bacterial soft rot.
- Bottom watering pulls moisture up through the rootball evenly, without ever wetting the crown.
- It also encourages roots to grow downward, which keeps the tuber stable in the pot.

Two watering mistakes that kill cyclamen indoors:

**Watering too often.** Indoor cyclamen prefer to dry slightly between waterings. A pot kept constantly moist rots in 2–4 weeks.

**Cold tap water shock.** Run cold water through the kettle to take the chill off, or fill a jug the night before. Shockingly cold water on warm roots stresses the plant.

## What humidity and feeding do cyclamen want?

Once temperature and watering are right, two smaller factors decide whether the plant simply survives or actively keeps producing new buds.

**Humidity.** Aim for **40–60% relative humidity** around the leaves. Easy ways to raise local humidity:

- Sit the pot on a saucer of pebbles with water just below the pebble surface (the pot itself stays out of the water).
- Group several plants together so they share transpired moisture.
- Run a small humidifier in winter, especially overnight.
- Avoid misting the leaves and flowers directly — this encourages grey mould on petals.

**Feeding.** Cyclamen are light feeders. Use a **half-strength balanced houseplant fertiliser**, or a tomato-style flowering feed at quarter strength, **every 2–3 weeks** while in active growth and flowering. Stop feeding once flowering slows and the leaves start to yellow naturally — this signals the plant is heading toward summer dormancy.

**Deadheading.** Remove faded flowers by **gripping the stem at the base of the tuber and pulling it cleanly out with a sharp twist**. Cutting the stem leaves a wet stub that rots and can carry infection back to the tuber. The same applies to yellowed leaves: pull, do not cut.

## What do you do when the cyclamen "stops" flowering?

There is a deflating moment 8–12 weeks in when buds slow, leaves yellow, and the plant looks tired. This is **not death**; it is the plant signalling the end of its winter cycle.

You have two reasonable paths.

**Path A — let it go fully dormant and re-flower next winter.**

1. As leaves yellow naturally, reduce watering to once every 2–3 weeks.
2. Stop feeding completely.
3. Once leaves are mostly gone, **stop watering** and move the pot to a cool, dry, dark spot (a cool cupboard, a frost-free shed, or under a workbench). The tuber rests there for **3–5 months** with no water.
4. In **late summer or early autumn**, bring the pot back into the light, top-dress with fresh compost so the top of the tuber is just exposed, and **start watering sparingly** from below. New leaves and flower buds usually appear within 4–8 weeks.

**Path B — treat as a single-season decoration.**

If summer storage is impractical (warm flat, no dark cool spot), accept the cyclamen as a 3-month winter decoration like cut flowers — composted and replaced next year. There is nothing wrong with this approach, especially in homes that struggle to provide cool dormancy conditions.

## How do you spot and fix cyclamen problems early?

Cyclamen telegraph their problems clearly if you read the leaves and stems.

| Symptom | Likely cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| All flowers and leaves flopping at once | Room too hot, or just watered into a hot rootball | Move to a cooler spot; cool but **do not** water more |
| Yellowing leaves, no flopping | End of natural cycle, or steady warmth | Reduce watering; consider summer dormancy plan |
| Soft brown tuber, bad smell | Crown rot from over-watering | Usually fatal; remove rotted plant, do not reuse soil |
| Grey fuzzy mould on flowers | Botrytis from misting / wet petals | Stop misting, increase airflow, remove affected blooms |
| Tiny bleached spots on leaves with mites | Cyclamen mite | Discard plant; mites spread fast and are hard to clear |
| Buds drying before opening | Hot dry air | Cooler spot, increase humidity, water more evenly |

The key signal is **how fast** problems develop. A slow yellowing over weeks usually means a cycle change or a creeping environment problem. A sudden collapse over 24–48 hours almost always means tuber rot from over-watering or a heat spike.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why are my cyclamen leaves turning yellow?

Cyclamen yellowing has two common causes. **Mild yellowing on a few outer leaves at the end of an 8–12 week bloom cycle** is normal — the plant is heading into summer dormancy. **Sudden widespread yellowing with floppy stems** usually means the room is too warm, or the rootball was watered while already wet.

### How long do indoor cyclamen flower for?

A healthy florist cyclamen in cool conditions flowers for **8–12 weeks**. In warm rooms (above 20°C) the same plant typically flowers for only **3–4 weeks** before going into early dormancy. Cool conditions are the single biggest lever for extending the display.

### Can you save a cyclamen for next year?

Yes, but it takes deliberate effort. As leaves yellow naturally, stop feeding, gradually reduce watering, and once the foliage dies back, **store the dry tuber in its pot in a cool dark spot for 3–5 months** with no water. Restart watering in autumn. Many indoor cyclamen survive 3–5 years with this routine.

### Should I mist a cyclamen?

No. Cyclamen leaves and flowers are prone to **grey mould (Botrytis)** when wet. Raise humidity around the plant using a pebble tray, a humidifier, or grouped plants, but never mist directly onto the leaves or flowers.

### Why is my cyclamen drooping but the soil is moist?

Almost always **heat or rotten roots**, not lack of water. If the pot feels warm, move it to a cooler spot and check whether the lower leaves and stem feel firm or mushy. If the base is firm, cooler temperatures usually revive the plant within hours. If the base is soft and smells, the tuber is rotting and the plant is likely lost.

### Are cyclamen safe for cats and dogs?

No. **All parts of cyclamen, especially the tuber, are toxic to cats and dogs.** Symptoms include drooling, vomiting, and (in larger ingestions) heart-rhythm problems. Place plants out of reach of pets, and choose [pet-safe indoor flowers](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/) instead if your animals are determined chewers.
