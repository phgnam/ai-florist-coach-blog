## Quick Answer

If you don't know which clematis group you have, follow the **safe rule**: in year one **don't prune at all** — let the plant establish and observe when it blooms. **Spring bloom (April–May) = Group 1**, prune only lightly after flowering. **Early summer bloom on previous year's wood (May–June) = Group 2**, prune lightly in February to remove dead wood, then again after first flush. **Late summer bloom on new wood (July–September) = Group 3**, prune hard back to 30 cm in late February. **The wrong cut at the wrong time** doesn't kill the plant but will eliminate that year's flowers entirely. Many gardeners successfully grow Group 2 as if it were Group 3 — they accept losing first-flush flowers in exchange for simpler care.

## How do you actually identify which clematis group you have?

Group identification is the foundation of clematis care. **Without knowing the group**, every pruning cut is a guess that risks losing the season's flowers.

**The three pruning groups (sometimes labelled A/B/C):**

| Group | Bloom timing | Bloom location | Prune timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** (or A) | Early spring (April–May) | On previous year's wood | After flowering only |
| **2** (or B) | Late spring + summer (May–September) | First flush on old wood, second on new | Light Feb prune, then after first flush |
| **3** (or C) | Late summer–autumn (July–October) | On current year's new wood | Hard cut back in Feb–March |

**Identification by bloom time:**

The most reliable identification method is **observing when the plant blooms**:

1. **Plant your clematis** and don't prune for a full year.
2. **Note when the first flowers appear** — this tells you the group.
3. **April–May bloom on woody framework** → Group 1.
4. **May–June bloom on side shoots from old wood** → Group 2 (often a second flush in August).
5. **July–September bloom on this year's growth** → Group 3.

**Identification by flower size and form:**

- **Group 1** flowers are typically **smaller, bell-shaped or open-faced** (e.g., *Clematis montana*, *C. armandii*, *C. alpina*).
- **Group 2** flowers are typically **large saucer-shaped flat blooms** in spring, often double or fancy varieties (*"Nelly Moser"*, *"Henryi"*, *"Vyvyan Pennell"*).
- **Group 3** flowers are typically **medium-large simple blooms** in late summer (*"Jackmanii"*, *"Étoile Violette"*, *C. tangutica*).

**Identification by named cultivar:**

If you know the cultivar name, the group is published:

- **Group 1:** *C. montana* "Rubens", *C. armandii*, *C. alpina*, *C. macropetala*.
- **Group 2:** "Nelly Moser", "The President", "Henryi", "Niobe", "Multi Blue", "Vyvyan Pennell".
- **Group 3:** "Jackmanii", "Étoile Violette", "Comtesse de Bouchaud", *C. tangutica*, *C. viticella* cultivars, *C. terniflora*.

**When you really can't identify:**

If you bought the plant unlabelled or inherited it:

- **Don't prune in year one.** Observe.
- **In year two, prune lightly** in February (just dead wood and broken stems).
- **Note the bloom timing** to confirm group.
- **In year three, prune properly** for the identified group.

## How do you prune a Group 1 clematis?

Group 1 includes the most vigorous spring-flowering species. **They flower on previous year's wood**, so any heavy pruning eliminates the next year's bloom.

**The Group 1 rule:**

- **Don't prune for the first 2–3 years** unless removing dead wood.
- **After flowering** (typically late May–early June), do light pruning if needed.
- **Never cut into old woody stems** unless the plant is severely overgrown.
- **Mature plants need very little pruning** — they bloom abundantly when left alone.

**When to prune Group 1:**

- **Immediately after flowering ends** (typically late May).
- **Only enough to control size** or remove damaged growth.
- **Maximum cut depth:** 1/3 of any stem.

**Step-by-step Group 1 pruning:**

1. **Wait until flowering is finished** — bloom usually ends late May.
2. **Identify any dead, damaged, or diseased stems** — remove these first.
3. **Cut back stems** that have grown beyond the support — to a healthy node.
4. **Tidy up tangled growth** — train stems back into the support.
5. **Clean up fallen petals** and old leaves.
6. **Apply mulch** at the base.

**Common Group 1 examples:**

- ***C. montana***: extremely vigorous; can cover entire walls. Pruning is often size control rather than flower control.
- ***C. armandii*** (evergreen clematis): early spring bloom, evergreen leaves. Prune sparingly to maintain shape.
- ***C. alpina* / *C. macropetala***: small bell-shaped early spring flowers; very low maintenance.

**Renovation pruning:**

Old overgrown Group 1 plants can be renovated:

1. **Cut back hard in early summer** (after flowering) — to about 60 cm of stem.
2. **Expect no bloom the following year** (and possibly the year after).
3. **Plant rebuilds framework** over 2 years.
4. **Bloom returns** in year 3 with renewed vigour.

**The "do nothing" approach:**

For most established Group 1 plants, the best strategy is to **leave them alone** except for occasional dead-wood removal. Plants thrive and bloom heavily without any pruning at all.

## How do you prune a Group 2 clematis?

Group 2 is the trickiest because they bloom **twice** — first on old wood (large early flowers), then on new wood (smaller late flowers). **Two pruning sessions** are required to manage both flushes.

**The Group 2 rule:**

- **Light February prune** — remove dead wood and shorten stems by 1/3.
- **After first flush** (June), prune lightly to encourage second flush.
- **Never hard prune** — you'd lose the early-season large flowers.

**February pruning steps:**

1. **Inspect the plant** — identify all stems.
2. **Remove dead or damaged wood** — cut back to first healthy bud.
3. **Shorten healthy stems by 1/3** — cuts encourage bushy growth.
4. **Remove crossing or rubbing stems** — for plant health.
5. **Tie remaining stems** to the support structure.
6. **Apply slow-release fertiliser** at the base.

**Post-first-flush pruning (June):**

1. **After the early-summer flowers fade** — typically late June.
2. **Cut spent flower stems** back to first pair of leaves.
3. **Light tidying** of any leggy growth.
4. **Apply liquid feed** to encourage second flush.

**Common Group 2 examples:**

- **"Nelly Moser":** classic pink-and-white striped flowers; large 15+ cm blooms.
- **"The President":** purple flowers; vigorous and reliable.
- **"Vyvyan Pennell":** double flowers in spring, single in late summer.
- **"Henryi":** large white flowers.
- **"Multi Blue":** double blue flowers.

**The "Group 2 as Group 3" trick:**

Many gardeners simplify Group 2 care by treating it as Group 3:

- **Hard prune in February** to 30 cm.
- **Skip the early flush** — plant only blooms in late summer on new wood.
- **Trade-off:** lose the large spring flowers; gain simpler maintenance.

This works for many cultivars but not all — some Group 2 plants struggle to recover from hard pruning. Test on one stem first.

**Year-one Group 2 special case:**

Newly planted Group 2 should be pruned hard in February of year 2 (down to 30 cm) regardless of the type, just to establish a strong basal framework. **From year 3 onward**, switch to standard Group 2 protocol with the lighter pruning.

## How do you prune a Group 3 clematis?

Group 3 is the easiest to prune because **all flowers come on new wood** — you cut everything back hard each February without losing any blooms.

**The Group 3 rule:**

- **Hard prune in late February** — cut all stems back to 30 cm above ground.
- **Single annual pruning** — no other pruning needed during the year.
- **Plant grows fresh stems each year** that produce flowers.

**Step-by-step Group 3 pruning:**

1. **Late February or early March** — before active growth begins.
2. **Cut all stems back to the lowest pair of healthy buds** — typically 30–60 cm above ground.
3. **Remove all dead, woody material** — cut to the ground if needed.
4. **Apply a thick layer of mulch** at the base — keeps roots cool and feeds the plant.
5. **Light fertilising** at the same time helps spring growth.

**What "hard prune" looks like:**

- **Year-end:** plant has grown 2–4 metres of stems with flowers and leaves.
- **February:** cut back to ankle height — 30–60 cm of bare stems remain.
- **Visual change is dramatic** but the plant will rebound rapidly.
- **By June:** plant has grown 2–3 metres of fresh growth.
- **By August:** flowers on the new growth.

**Common Group 3 examples:**

- **"Jackmanii":** the classic purple late-summer clematis; almost foolproof.
- **"Étoile Violette":** small dark-purple flowers in mass.
- **"Comtesse de Bouchaud":** pink late-summer flowers.
- ***C. viticella* cultivars:** smaller flowers, very prolific.
- ***C. tangutica*:** yellow lantern-shaped flowers.
- ***C. terniflora* (Sweet Autumn Clematis):** white masses in autumn.

**Why Group 3 is the easiest:**

- **No "wrong cut" risk** — you cut everything anyway.
- **No timing precision needed** — anytime in late winter works.
- **Fresh growth eliminates pest/disease accumulation** in old stems.
- **Reliable bloom each year** if pruned consistently.

**For new Group 3 plants:**

- **Year 1:** plant in spring; let it grow without pruning until late February of year 2.
- **Year 2 February:** hard prune to 30 cm.
- **Year 2 summer:** first significant bloom.
- **Year 3 onward:** standard hard February prune annually.

## What if you've already pruned wrong — can the plant recover?

A wrongly-timed prune doesn't kill the plant. It eliminates that year's bloom but the plant recovers fully the following year.

**Recovery scenarios:**

**Scenario 1: You hard-pruned a Group 1 in February**

- **Effect:** no bloom this spring.
- **Recovery:** plant grows new stems through the year; blooms next spring on new wood (which is now old wood).
- **One lost season.**

**Scenario 2: You hard-pruned a Group 2 in February**

- **Effect:** lost early-summer flush (large flowers); second flush still possible on new growth.
- **Recovery:** plant adapts to new growth pattern; some cultivars adjust well, others struggle.
- **Half-lost season.**

**Scenario 3: You forgot to prune a Group 3**

- **Effect:** plant produces fewer flowers higher up on stems; lower portions become twiggy and unproductive.
- **Recovery:** prune the following February; bloom returns to normal.
- **Mostly normal season** with somewhat sparse bloom.

**Scenario 4: You cut too much off a Group 1 mid-summer**

- **Effect:** plant uses energy regrowing instead of preparing flower buds.
- **Recovery:** bloom may be reduced for 1–2 years while plant rebuilds woody framework.
- **Reduced bloom for 1–2 years.**

**The "wait and see" approach:**

If you've made a pruning mistake:

1. **Don't panic** — clematis are tougher than they look.
2. **Don't make additional cuts** to "fix" the problem.
3. **Water and feed** as normal.
4. **Wait for next year** — bloom usually returns.
5. **Note what you did** for future reference.

**Permanent damage scenarios:**

Truly permanent damage is rare and usually involves:

- **Cutting all stems to ground level** in a Group 1 *C. montana* (rare full death from this).
- **Hard pruning during active growth** in summer (causes severe stress).
- **Pruning during severe drought** (plant can't recover).

In most cases, **clematis forgive pruning mistakes** within 1–2 seasons.

## How does pruning interact with planting depth and feeding?

Pruning works best when combined with proper planting and feeding. A well-fed, deeply-planted clematis bounces back from any pruning faster.

**Planting depth (the "deep planting" rule):**

- **Plant clematis 5–10 cm deeper than the original soil mark** on the stem.
- **Why:** buries 1–2 nodes underground, creating multiple shoot points.
- **Benefits:** if upper stems die back from disease or pruning, the plant resprouts from underground.
- **All groups:** deep planting helps every clematis type.

**Feeding schedule:**

- **Late February:** apply slow-release fertiliser at the base.
- **Late spring:** liquid feed every 2 weeks during active growth.
- **After flowering:** liquid feed continues if Group 2 (encourages second flush).
- **Stop feeding** by late August to allow plant to harden off for winter.

**Mulching:**

- **Thick mulch** (5–10 cm) at the base year-round.
- **Keeps roots cool** — clematis prefer "head in sun, feet in shade".
- **Retains moisture** during summer.
- **Insulates** in winter.
- **Refresh annually** in late winter.

**Watering:**

- **Deep weekly watering** during dry periods.
- **Established plants** are surprisingly drought-tolerant.
- **First-year plants** need consistent moisture.
- **Container-grown clematis** need much more frequent watering.

**Companion planting:**

- **Low-growing perennials at the base** shade the roots.
- **Hardy geraniums, hostas, ferns** are good companions.
- **Avoid aggressive ground covers** that compete for water.

The combined effect of deep planting + proper feeding + correct pruning produces dramatically better bloom than pruning alone.

## How do you train clematis on different supports?

Clematis climb by twining their leaf petioles around thin supports. Choosing the right support and training the stems early creates the best displays.

**Support compatibility by clematis type:**

**Group 1 (very vigorous):**

- **Walls and large fences** with trellis attached.
- **Established trees** (montana types are happy climbing trees).
- **Pergolas and arches** with wire mesh.
- **Avoid:** small obelisks (overwhelm them).

**Group 2 (medium vigour):**

- **Trellis on walls or fences**.
- **Free-standing obelisks** (1.5–2 m tall).
- **Through other shrubs** (e.g., growing through roses).
- **Small to medium pergolas**.

**Group 3 (annual restart):**

- **Any support** — they're cut back annually so size is manageable.
- **Excellent for obelisks and tripods**.
- **Mix with annuals** in containers.
- **Through other late-summer plants** for combined display.

**Support material:**

- **Wire mesh or netting** with 5–10 cm mesh — clematis tendrils grip easily.
- **Wooden trellis** with sufficient cross-pieces — leaves wrap around them.
- **Free-standing metal obelisks** — provide good twining points.
- **Avoid:** smooth surfaces (concrete walls without trellis); thick poles (tendrils can't grasp).

**Training technique:**

1. **Plant next to the support** — within 10–15 cm.
2. **Tie initial stems gently** to the support with garden twine.
3. **As stems grow**, redirect them across the support to fill gaps.
4. **Untie and re-tie** if stems are growing in unwanted directions.
5. **Don't force stems** into tight bends — they break.
6. **Allow natural twining** of leaf petioles around the support.

**Through other plants:**

A classic English-garden technique:

- **Plant Group 3 clematis at the base** of a rose or large shrub.
- **As the clematis grows**, it climbs through the host.
- **Combined display** in late summer when many shrubs are past peak.
- **Hard prune the clematis annually** without disturbing the host.

**Container clematis:**

- **Choose Group 3 or compact Group 2** cultivars for pots.
- **Large pot** (45+ cm diameter) — clematis hate small containers.
- **Free-draining potting mix**.
- **Annual repotting** in late winter; refresh top 5 cm of soil.
- **Daily watering** in summer.
- **Feed every 2 weeks** during growing season.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why is my clematis blooming only at the top with bare lower stems?

**This is normal for Group 2 clematis** that aren't pruned regularly — flowers form on old wood (top of plant) leaving lower stems bare. **For Group 3 clematis**, this means it wasn't hard-pruned in February — flowers formed only on the upper portions where the stems are still in active growth. **Solution:** identify the group, prune correctly, and the lower portion becomes productive again. **Permanent prevention:** Group 3 is hard-pruned annually so this never happens; Group 2 needs the February tidy-up to maintain dense lower growth.

### Can I move a mature clematis without killing it?

**Yes, but expect a setback of 1–2 years.** **Best timing:** early spring (February–March) before active growth begins, or autumn (October) after dormancy starts. **Dig wide** — clematis roots spread 60+ cm. **Prune back hard** at transplant time (regardless of group) to reduce stress. **Replant at the same depth or slightly deeper.** **Water generously** for the first year. Plants typically recover fully by year 3.

### Why does my clematis wilt suddenly even when watered?

**Clematis wilt** is a fungal disease (*Calophoma clematidina*) common in large-flowered hybrids (especially Group 2). **Symptoms:** stems suddenly collapse and turn black at the base. **Treatment:** cut affected stems back to ground level immediately; the plant typically resprouts from below soil level (one reason for deep planting). **Prevention:** good air circulation, deep planting, and avoiding overhead watering. Some cultivars are more susceptible than others.

### Are clematis toxic to pets?

**Yes — clematis contain protoanemonin** which is mildly toxic to dogs, cats, horses, and humans. **Symptoms** include mouth irritation, drooling, vomiting, and rarely diarrhoea. **Severity** is usually mild because the plant tastes bitter and pets typically don't eat much. **Plant in areas pets don't access** if your pets are prone to chewing plants. Severe poisoning is rare but contact your vet if a large amount is ingested.

### Can I grow clematis on a north-facing wall?

**Yes — many clematis tolerate north-facing positions.** **Best choices for north walls:** *C. montana*, *C. alpina*, *C. macropetala*, "Nelly Moser" (Group 2), "Comtesse de Bouchaud" (Group 3). **Avoid:** sun-loving large-flowered hybrids that need 6+ hours of sun. **Bloom may be delayed** by 2–3 weeks compared to south-facing positions, but flower quality is often excellent in cooler conditions.

### How long do clematis live?

**40–50+ years** with proper care. **Many heritage gardens** have clematis planted decades ago still blooming reliably. **Group 1 species** (especially *C. montana*) often outlive the gardener. **Group 2 large-flowered hybrids** are slightly shorter-lived (20–30 years) but still long-term plants. **Group 3 plants** benefit from annual hard pruning which keeps them youthful indefinitely.
