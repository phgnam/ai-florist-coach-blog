## Quick Answer

Daffodils return each year because they are true perennials that store energy in underground bulbs. They produce fewer flowers over time because the original bulb multiplies into a crowded clump where daughter bulbs compete for water, nutrients, and space. Lifting, dividing, and replanting every three to five years restores strong blooming.

## Why do daffodils multiply but bloom less?

Every daffodil bulb is a self-contained energy store. After flowering in spring, the leaves photosynthesise for six to eight weeks, sending sugars back into the bulb to fuel next year's bloom. But the bulb also produces offsets — small daughter bulbs that grow attached to the mother.

Over three to five years, one bulb can become a tight cluster of ten or more. The problem is competition: all those bulbs share the same pocket of soil, the same moisture, and the same nutrients. Each individual bulb gets less of everything, and many become too small to produce a flower. They still send up leaves — which is why you see plenty of foliage but few blooms. Gardeners call this going "blind."

The effect is gradual. Year two and three are often the best, because a few strong offsets bloom alongside the original. By year four or five, the clump is too dense and flowering drops noticeably.

## How do you divide overcrowded daffodil bulbs?

Dividing is simple but timing matters. The best window is after the foliage has yellowed and died back naturally — typically late spring to early summer — but before you lose track of where the clumps are.

1. **Wait for yellow foliage.** Never cut green daffodil leaves. They are recharging the bulb. Once they yellow and pull away easily, the bulb is dormant.
2. **Lift the clump.** Use a garden fork inserted well outside the clump to avoid slicing bulbs. Lever upward gently.
3. **Separate the offsets.** Twist or pull daughter bulbs away from the mother. Discard any that are soft, mouldy, or smaller than a grape — they are unlikely to bloom for several years.
4. **Dry briefly.** Let bulbs air-dry in shade for a day or two. Do not leave them in direct sun.
5. **Replant immediately or store.** Replant at three times the bulb's height in depth, spaced 10–15 cm apart. If replanting in autumn, store bulbs in a cool, dry, ventilated place over summer.

## What other reasons cause daffodils to stop flowering?

Overcrowding is the most common cause, but several other issues can suppress blooms:

**Premature foliage removal.** Mowing, braiding, or tying daffodil leaves before they yellow cuts off the energy supply. The bulb cannot recharge and may not flower the following year.

**Too much shade.** Daffodils tolerate light shade from deciduous trees (they bloom before full leaf-out), but heavy year-round shade from evergreens or buildings starves them of light.

**Shallow planting.** Bulbs planted too shallow heat up quickly, which can disrupt dormancy and flower formation. Replant at the correct depth if bulbs have risen to the surface.

**Nitrogen-heavy feeding.** High-nitrogen fertiliser promotes lush foliage at the expense of flower buds. Use a balanced bulb fertiliser (high potassium) in autumn or just after flowering.

**Waterlogged soil.** Daffodils need well-drained soil. Sitting in wet ground over winter causes bulb rot.

## Which daffodil varieties naturalise best without thinning?

Some varieties are bred to multiply freely and still bloom well for years with minimal intervention:

**'February Gold'** is a compact cyclamineus hybrid that naturalises beautifully in grass and borders. It is early, reliable, and rarely needs dividing for five or more years.

**'Tête-à-Tête'** is the most popular miniature daffodil. It multiplies rapidly and works well in pots, rockeries, and lawn edges.

**'Carlton'** is a classic large-cupped variety that naturalises in grass better than most. It is vigorous, fragrant, and very long-lived.

**'Thalia'** produces elegant white flowers with a sweet scent. It increases steadily without aggressive clumping.

**'Pheasant's Eye' (*Narcissus poeticus var. recurvus*)** is one of the latest-blooming daffodils and a strong naturaliser in meadows and wild gardens.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I just leave daffodils in the ground forever?**
Yes, but expect declining bloom after three to five years unless you divide them. Some vigorous varieties naturalise well for longer periods without intervention.

**Should I deadhead daffodils after they flower?**
Yes. Removing spent flowers prevents the plant from wasting energy on seed production, redirecting it into bulb recharging. But leave the foliage intact until it yellows.

**Can I move daffodil bulbs while they are still green?**
You can, but it stresses the bulb and often reduces flowering the following year. If you must move them, lift with as much root and soil as possible and water well after replanting.

**Why do some daffodils only produce leaves and no flowers?**
This is called going blind. It is almost always caused by overcrowding, premature foliage removal, or bulbs that are too small or too shallow. Dividing and replanting at the correct depth usually solves it within one to two seasons.

**How deep should I plant daffodil bulbs?**
Plant at three times the bulb's height. For a standard daffodil bulb about 5 cm tall, that means the base of the bulb sits about 15 cm below the soil surface.
