## Quick Answer

Fiddle leaf figs drop leaves from environmental stress — most commonly cold drafts, sudden moves, overwatering, underwatering, low light, or repotting shock. The location and colour reveal the cause: bottom yellow leaves usually mean watering issues, sudden mass drop usually means cold or movement, and brown-spotted leaves before drop usually mean root rot. Fix the cause first, recovery follows.

## What are the most common causes of leaf drop in a fiddle leaf fig?

Fiddle leaf figs (*Ficus lyrata*) are dramatic communicators — they drop leaves rather than gradually decline, and the *which leaves* and *how fast* tell you almost everything about why.

Six causes account for the overwhelming majority of leaf drop:

1. **Cold draft or sudden temperature swing.** A fiddle leaf fig sitting next to a leaky window, an exterior door that opens to freezing air, or an air-conditioning vent will drop leaves within 24–72 hours of the cold exposure. Threshold is roughly below 13 °C (55 °F).
2. **Sudden environmental change.** Moving the plant to a new room, rotating it dramatically, or relocating after purchase. Fiddle leaf figs reset their entire leaf orientation when they move and shed leaves they "decide" are no longer optimally positioned.
3. **Overwatering and root rot.** The single most common slow killer. Soil stays wet, roots suffocate and rot, the plant cannot uptake water — and paradoxically the leaves wilt and drop *as if* underwatered.
4. **Underwatering.** Less common but real, especially in dry winter heated rooms. Leaves go limp, the lowest leaves yellow first, then crisp brown edges before falling.
5. **Insufficient light.** Below ~150 foot-candles (typical of "bright but no direct sun" interior corners), the plant cannot photosynthesise enough to support its existing leaves and sheds the lowest, oldest ones over weeks.
6. **Repotting shock.** Loss of fine root hairs during repotting causes the plant to drop several leaves while it rebuilds the root system.

The diagnostic skill is matching the **pattern** of dropping leaves to the cause — which the next sections walk through in detail.

## How do you tell overwatering and underwatering apart on a fiddle leaf fig?

Both cause leaf drop, but the symptoms diverge if you look closely.

**Overwatering signature:**

- **Brown spots in the centre of leaves**, often surrounded by a yellow halo, before the leaf drops. This is the textbook *Ficus lyrata* root-rot signal.
- **Lower leaves drop first**, sometimes still partly green.
- **Soil stays wet 5+ days** after watering. Stick a finger 5 cm down — if it is still cool and damp at that depth a week later, the plant is overwatered or underpotted.
- **Yellowing is uniform**, not just on edges.
- **Stem near soil line softens** in advanced cases — squeeze gently with a finger; healthy stems are firm.

**Underwatering signature:**

- **Brown crispy edges and tips**, then the leaf goes brittle.
- **Leaves go limp before yellowing.** A thirsty fiddle leaf fig droops noticeably at the leaf level.
- **Soil pulls away from the pot edge.** A clear gap between dry soil and pot wall is a giveaway — water then runs straight down the gap and out the bottom without rehydrating the rootball.
- **Lower leaves drop**, but they are crispy rather than soft when they fall.

**Decision rule:** push a wooden chopstick or moisture meter to the bottom of the pot. If the chopstick comes out wet *and* you saw central-leaf brown spots, it is overwatering and probable root rot. If the chopstick comes out bone-dry *and* you saw crispy edges, it is underwatering and the soil has hydrophobed.

## Why does a fiddle leaf fig drop a lot of leaves at once after being moved?

This is the most-asked question in the fiddle leaf fig forums and the answer is almost always the same: light angle changed, and the plant is shedding leaves it can no longer support in their old orientation.

Fiddle leaf figs orient every leaf to the dominant light source. The leaves that face away from the light photosynthesise less efficiently. When the plant moves — to a new room, a new house, even rotated 180 ° — the back-facing leaves are now front-facing in the new light direction, but their *internal architecture* (chloroplast distribution, stomatal opening pattern) is still optimised for the old direction. The plant gives up on those leaves and grows new ones aligned to the new light angle.

What this means in practice:

- **Mass leaf drop within 1–2 weeks of a move** is normal and recoverable, *if* the new spot has adequate light.
- **Drop more than 50 % of leaves**, or drop accompanied by softening stems, points to a second problem — usually cold draft at the new location or overwatering during the "I'd better baby it" period after the move.
- **The recovery is to stop intervening.** Do not water more, do not fertilise, do not move it again. Once the plant settles into the new light direction (4–8 weeks), it begins growing new leaves in the correct orientation.

**Key prevention rule**: when you move a fiddle leaf fig, mark which side of the pot faces the dominant light and rotate the plant a quarter turn every two weeks rather than 180 ° at once. The plant adapts gradually instead of having to reset.

## What does root rot look like on a fiddle leaf fig, and can you save the plant?

Root rot is the most common terminal cause for fiddle leaf figs and is also the most salvageable *if you act early*.

**Diagnostic signs (in order of severity):**

1. Brown circular spots in the *centre* of older leaves, with a yellow halo.
2. Multiple lower leaves dropping over 1–2 weeks despite no other change.
3. Wet, heavy pot. Soil stays cool and damp 5+ days after a watering.
4. Sour, swampy smell when you sniff the drainage hole.
5. Black, mushy roots when you slip the plant out of the pot. Healthy fiddle leaf fig roots are off-white to tan and firm.

**Recovery procedure if you catch it before stem rot:**

1. Slip the plant out of the pot. Shake off most of the soil.
2. Inspect the roots. If most are still firm and pale, prune off the obviously rotted (black, mushy) roots with sterile scissors. If more than 60 % of roots are rotted, recovery is unlikely.
3. Rinse the remaining root mass briefly to wash off contaminated soil.
4. Repot in **fresh, well-draining mix** — typical recipe: 60 % indoor potting mix + 30 % orchid bark + 10 % perlite. The bark is critical; standard potting mix alone retains too much water for *Ficus lyrata*.
5. Use a pot only one size up from the salvaged root mass — overpotting after root rot recovery causes immediate re-rot because the soil holds water the smaller root system cannot uptake.
6. **Do not water for 7–10 days.** The damaged roots need to callus and start regenerating before being asked to absorb water.
7. Place in bright, indirect light. No fertiliser for 6–8 weeks.

Recovery, when it works, is visible within 4–6 weeks: new leaf at the top, no further drop, stem firm at the soil line.

## How do you tell if low light is causing leaf drop?

Low light leaf drop is the slowest of the common causes — leaves drop singly over weeks rather than in a sudden episode.

**Light-deficiency signature:**

- **Lowest, oldest leaves yellow uniformly** (no spots, no crispy edges) and drop one at a time.
- **Drop accelerates in winter** as available light declines.
- **Long bare gap below the canopy** — the plant gradually sheds the lowest leaves, leaving the foliage concentrated at the top of an otherwise bare stem.
- **New growth is small and pale** compared to existing leaves.
- **Plant leans toward the light source.**

How to confirm it is light, not water:

- Check the soil moisture cycle. If the plant dries out normally between waterings (5–10 days, depending on pot size and conditions), the issue is not water.
- Use a phone light meter app. Fiddle leaf figs need at least **150 foot-candles (1,600 lux)** for survival, and ideally 400 ft-candles (4,300 lux) for active growth. Many "bright corner" interior spots measure well below 100 ft-candles.

Fixes:

- Move closer to the brightest window in the home (south-facing in the Northern Hemisphere, north-facing in the Southern Hemisphere). A 50 cm move from a wall to right by the window typically doubles incident light.
- If no bright window is available, supplement with a full-spectrum LED grow light (white-light "houseplant" LEDs are fine) on a 12-hour timer at ~30 cm above the canopy.
- Be patient — once the light situation is corrected, the plant stops dropping leaves within 2–3 weeks, but the bare lower stem remains bare unless you propagate-and-restart (see FAQ).

## What is the recovery routine after a major leaf-drop event?

Once you have identified and corrected the cause, the recovery routine is the same regardless of the trigger.

**Week 1: stabilise.**

- Stop watering until the top 5 cm of soil dries — let the plant lose a little turgor rather than risk re-rotting the roots.
- Move to bright, *indirect* light if it isn't there already. Strong direct sun stresses a recovering plant further.
- Maintain stable temperature 18–24 °C; avoid placing near heat vents, AC vents, or doors that open to outdoor air.
- No fertiliser. No repotting. No moving. No leaf-cleaning sprays.

**Weeks 2–4: monitor.**

- Watch for new growth at the top. A single new leaf forming is the strongest signal that the plant is past the crisis.
- Keep watering on a *dry-down* schedule: water deeply only when the top 5 cm of soil is dry to the touch (typically 7–14 days between waterings indoors, longer in winter).
- Wipe dust off remaining leaves weekly so the surviving canopy can photosynthesise efficiently.

**Weeks 5–8: rebuild.**

- Resume light feeding only after one new leaf has fully unfurled — diluted balanced fertiliser (NPK ~3-1-2) at quarter-strength, monthly through spring and summer.
- Continue not repotting until the plant has produced 2–3 new healthy leaves, even if the pot looks too small.

**The lower bare trunk does not refoliate** once it has dropped its leaves — fiddle leaf figs only grow new leaves from the apical (topmost) growing point. If you want a fuller plant, see the FAQ on propagation. The recovery you are aiming for is healthy *new* leaves at the top, not regrowth on the bare stem.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it normal for a fiddle leaf fig to drop a few leaves now and then?**
Yes — losing one or two of the very oldest, lowest leaves over the course of a year is normal as the plant pushes new growth at the top. The warning sign is the *rate*: more than 2–3 leaves in a week, or any leaf above the lowest tier dropping, points to a real problem (cold, watering, or rot) rather than natural shedding.

**How long does it take for a fiddle leaf fig to recover from leaf drop?**
Visible new growth typically appears 4–8 weeks after the cause is corrected. Full canopy recovery — replacing the dropped leaves with new ones — takes 6–12 months in most home conditions, because fiddle leaf figs are slow growers indoors (one new leaf every 4–6 weeks during active spring/summer growth).

**Can a fiddle leaf fig regrow leaves on a bare lower trunk?**
No — not naturally. The plant only grows new leaves from the topmost (apical) bud. To get a fuller-looking plant, either notch the trunk just above a dormant lateral bud (which sometimes triggers a side branch) or air-layer / cut and propagate the top portion to start a new, fuller plant from the existing foliage.

**Should you fertilise a fiddle leaf fig that's dropping leaves?**
No — never feed a stressed plant. Fertiliser pulls water out of damaged roots through osmosis and worsens the drop. Wait until at least one new leaf has fully unfurled after the recovery, then resume feeding at quarter-strength monthly through the growing season only.

**Is it true that fiddle leaf figs hate being moved?**
"Hate" is anthropomorphic, but the underlying observation is correct — every move causes a partial leaf-drop episode while the plant re-orients to the new light. The practical rule: pick the brightest, draft-free, stable spot in your home *before* you bring the plant home, and move it as little as possible after that. Quarter-turn rotations every 2 weeks are fine; whole-room moves are not.
