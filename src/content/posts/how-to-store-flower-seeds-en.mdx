## Quick Answer

Flower seeds keep best in **cool, dark, dry conditions** — ideally in an **airtight container in the fridge** with a **silica gel pack** to absorb moisture. Stored this way, most flower seeds stay over 80% viable for **2–4 years**, against 1 season at room temperature. The biggest enemies are warmth, humidity, and light. The biggest mistake is putting seed packets in a kitchen drawer near the kettle or oven.

## Why do flower seeds lose viability?

A flower seed is a living embryo wrapped in a protective coat with a small food reserve. Three things kill that embryo over time:

**Warmth** speeds up the seed's metabolism so it burns through its food reserve while still sealed in the packet. The general rule: every 5°C rise above ideal halves storage life.

**Humidity** lets fungal spores grow on the seed coat, and worse, lets the seed start to "imbibe" (take up water) and metabolise without being able to actually germinate.

**Light** breaks down some pigments and natural inhibitors that protect embryos. UV is particularly damaging.

So the storage rule reduces to: **cold, dark, dry**.

## What is the ideal storage environment?

The gold standard is:

- **Temperature**: 4°C (a domestic fridge is perfect)
- **Humidity**: 20–30% relative humidity inside the container
- **Light**: zero (an opaque container or a drawer)
- **Container**: airtight glass jar or a sealed plastic Tupperware

Drop a small **silica gel sachet** (the kind that comes with new electronics) into the jar to keep humidity low. Replace it every 6 months, or recharge it by drying it for 2 hours in a 100°C oven.

Inside the jar, keep seeds in their original paper packets or in clearly labelled paper envelopes. Paper allows tiny amounts of moisture to equilibrate slowly, which is healthier than sealed plastic bags directly against the seeds.

## How long do flower seeds actually last?

This varies by species, but a useful rule of thumb at room temperature versus refrigerated storage:

| Flower | Room temperature | Cold + dry storage |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|
| Sweet pea | 1 year | 4–5 years |
| Cosmos | 2 years | 5–6 years |
| Marigold | 1–2 years | 3–4 years |
| Sunflower | 2 years | 5–7 years |
| Zinnia | 2 years | 4–5 years |
| Nasturtium | 3 years | 5+ years |
| Calendula | 1 year | 3 years |
| Poppy | 2 years | 5+ years (very long-lived) |
| Larkspur / delphinium | 6 months | 1–2 years (notoriously short) |
| Pansy / viola | 1 year | 2 years |

The big outliers worth memorising: **delphinium and pansy seed loses viability quickly** (use it within a year of harvest, even cold-stored), while **poppy seed is famously long-lived** — viable poppy seeds have germinated after decades in soil.

## How dry do seeds need to be before storage?

Seed moisture content matters more than most people realise. A seed at 5–7% moisture stores well; at 12% moisture, it starts to deteriorate fast.

Two practical tests:

**The bend test**: bend a seed between your fingers. A dry seed snaps. A moist seed bends or squishes. If your seeds bend, they are too wet to store.

**The hammer test** (for larger seeds like sunflower or sweet pea): tap a seed lightly with a hammer on a hard surface. A dry seed shatters. A moist seed flattens.

If your seeds fail these tests, dry them on a tray in a warm, ventilated room (not in direct sun, which heats them too much) for 1–2 weeks before sealing them away.

## Should I freeze flower seeds?

You can — but only if you have got the moisture content right first. Freezing seeds with too much moisture causes ice crystals to form inside cells and kills the embryo.

For seed banks (national gene banks like Kew's Millennium Seed Bank), the protocol is: dry seeds to 5% moisture, seal in metal-foil packets, store at -20°C. At home, this is usually overkill. A regular fridge at 4°C with silica gel gets you 90% of the longevity benefit without the risk of moisture damage from freezing.

The exception is if you are storing seeds for **decades** — then freeze them properly dried.

## Why label everything immediately?

This is not technically a storage tip, but it is the most-broken rule of seed saving. Seeds in unmarked envelopes might as well be confetti. Always label with:

- **Species and cultivar** (e.g. "Sweet pea, Cupani")
- **Year saved**
- **Source** (your garden, a swap, a packet from a specific supplier)
- **Special notes** ("cold-stratify", "sow direct", "needs light to germinate")

## How do I test if old seeds still germinate?

The **paper towel test**: place 10 seeds on a damp paper towel, fold it over, slide it into a labelled plastic bag, and keep at room temperature. Check daily. Most seeds that are going to germinate will do so within 7–14 days.

- 8 or more out of 10 germinate: sow normally
- 5–7 out of 10: sow at higher density to compensate
- Fewer than 5 out of 10: discard or sow only as a last resort

Doing this test in February before the spring sowing rush saves you from finding out your sweet pea seed is dead at the wrong moment.

## FAQs

### Can I store seeds from supermarket cut flowers?

Most supermarket cut flowers (lilies, roses, chrysanthemums) are F1 hybrids or sterile cultivars and either will not set viable seed or will not come true from seed. Save seeds from open-pollinated annuals from your own garden instead.

### Do silica gel packs really make a difference?

Yes — substantially. They drop the relative humidity inside the storage jar from typical room levels (40–60%) to under 30%, which roughly doubles the storage life of most seeds.

### Can I store seeds in the same fridge as food?

Yes, as long as they are in a sealed airtight container. Food fridges typically have higher humidity than ideal, which is exactly why the airtight jar plus silica gel matters. A separate container shelf or drawer works fine.

### What about seeds in the original paper packets the seed company sent?

Paper is a good seed-storage medium because it allows slow moisture equilibration. Keep the original packets, but put them inside an airtight jar with silica gel for long-term storage. Don't store paper packets loose in a kitchen drawer.

### How can I tell a seed is dead before sowing?

The most reliable check is a paper-towel germination test (10 seeds, damp paper towel, sealed bag, 7–14 days). Visual signs of dead seed include shrivelled or hollow seeds, mould on the seed coat, and a hollow sound when shaken.

### Is there a temperature too cold for seed storage?

Yes — but only at the extreme. Below -20°C with high seed moisture causes ice damage. A normal kitchen freezer at -18°C is fine for properly dried seeds, but offers little benefit over a regular fridge.
