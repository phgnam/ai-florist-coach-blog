## Quick Answer

Yes, gerbera daisies (*Gerbera jamesonii*) grow indoors year-round in the right spot. Place them in **4–6 hours of direct sun** (a south or east window), keep at **18–24°C by day with cooler nights**, and **water from below** to keep the crown dry. They flower in **6–8 week cycles** rather than continuously, with rest periods between flushes. Expect **2–3 bloom flushes per year** indoors, plus a partial winter rest. Pots usually last **2–3 years** before declining, at which point fresh plants outperform attempted recovery.

## Are gerbera daisies actually indoor plants?

Gerberas are sold as houseplants, garden bedding, and cut flowers — and they behave differently in each role. Setting realistic expectations matters for keeping them long-term indoors.

**Native habitat:** South African grassland with **bright sun, warm days, cool nights, and a dry winter rest**. Outside USDA zones 8–11 they aren't reliably hardy outdoors year-round, so indoor growing makes sense.

**Three common roles:**

- **As a flowering houseplant** (the topic of this article): plants live for 2–3 years, flower in cycles, take a partial winter rest. Realistic and rewarding.
- **As temporary "supermarket pot plants":** sold in flower, expected to be discarded after the bloom finishes. Often die quickly because the conditions for the second flush are very specific.
- **As cut flowers:** vase-life flowers from greenhouse-grown plants. Cut gerberas are a different story (see [why gerbera daisies bend at the neck](/posts/gerbera-daisies-bent-neck-en/) for the post-cutting issues).

The realistic indoor experience is **2–3 bloom flushes per year** with **6–10 week rest periods between**. That's not the constant bloom of a chrysanthemum, but it's still rewarding if you set up the conditions right.

## What conditions do indoor gerberas need?

The four variables that matter most are light, temperature, humidity, and airflow. Gerberas tolerate a fairly wide range, but each variable has a clear "this kills the plant" threshold to avoid.

**Light:**

- **Ideal:** 4–6 hours direct sun per day (south or east window).
- **Tolerable:** bright indirect light all day with 1–2 hours direct sun.
- **Too dark:** less than 4 hours of meaningful light produces leggy growth and few flowers.

In winter, even a south window provides less light than summer. **Supplement with a grow-light** on a 10–12 hour timer for reliable winter bloom, or accept that the plant rests through the darkest months.

**Temperature:**

- **Day:** 18–24°C is ideal.
- **Night:** 13–18°C — the plant flowers more reliably with a 5–8°C day-to-night temperature drop.
- **Avoid:** above 27°C (flowers fade and stop forming) and below 8°C (cold damage to leaves).
- **Avoid:** sudden swings of more than 8°C between day and night, especially in winter.

**Humidity:**

- **Comfortable:** 40–55%.
- **Too dry:** below 30% causes leaf-edge browning and slower flower formation.
- **Too humid:** above 70% increases botrytis risk on flowers and the crown.

**Airflow:**

- **Essential.** A small clip-on fan running on low for a few hours a day prevents botrytis on the crown — the single most common cause of indoor gerbera death.
- **Avoid:** stagnant warm humid air with leaves sitting against a window in condensation.

## How do you pot and water gerberas correctly?

Watering technique matters more than watering frequency for indoor gerberas. The crown (the central knot where leaves emerge from the soil surface) must stay **dry**, but the rootball below must stay **evenly moist**.

**Potting:**

- **Pot size:** start with a **15 cm pot** for a young plant, increasing to **20–25 cm** as the plant matures. Pots larger than 25 cm hold too much wet soil and increase rot risk.
- **Drainage:** absolutely essential. Holes in the bottom, no exception.
- **Soil mix:** **70% peat-free multipurpose, 20% horticultural grit, 10% perlite**. Avoid moisture-retaining bedding compost or rich manure-amended mix.
- **Plant depth:** the **crown sits at or just slightly above the soil surface**, never buried. Burial under 1 cm of soil rots the crown within a few weeks.

**Watering technique:**

1. **Water from below** by sitting the pot in a tray with 2–3 cm of room-temperature water for **15–20 minutes**.
2. **Drain thoroughly** before returning to the saucer.
3. **Frequency:** check the top 2 cm of compost; water when dry. In summer this is typically **once a week**; in winter, every 10–14 days.
4. **Never water into the crown.** Top-watering is the most common cause of indoor gerbera failure.

**Signs of correct watering:**

- Leaves stay firm and glossy.
- New leaves emerge regularly from the centre.
- Crown stays dry-looking and tan-coloured.
- Flower buds form and open at full size.

**Signs of too-wet roots:**

- Yellow lower leaves.
- Soft brown patches at the crown (early rot).
- Mushroom-like smell from the soil.

If the plant has waterlogged soil, **remove from pot, trim mushy roots, repot in fresh dry mix, and withhold water for 5–7 days** before resuming.

## How do you feed gerberas for steady flower cycles?

Gerberas are **moderate feeders**. Too little food and the plant produces only leaves; too much pushes leafy growth at the expense of flowers, exactly like over-feeding [pansies](/posts/pansies-leggy-stretched-en/) or [marigolds](/posts/marigold-buds-dropping-en/).

**Feeding schedule:**

- **Active growth (spring–autumn):** half-strength balanced liquid feed every 2 weeks. A "tomato" feed (5-10-10 or 4-10-10) works well.
- **Active flowering:** continue the same feed; do not increase strength.
- **Winter rest:** stop feeding entirely from late November to early February.
- **Resume gradually** in late winter as new leaves appear.

**Signs of correct feeding:**

- Compact dense rosette of leaves.
- Steady appearance of new flower buds during active periods.
- Bright leaf colour without unnatural deep green.

**Signs of over-feeding:**

- Tall lush dark-green leaves with few buds.
- White salt crust on soil surface or pot rim.
- Soft floppy growth that wilts despite watering.

**Signs of under-feeding:**

- Pale lower leaves.
- Small flower count, normal-sized but few.
- Slow growth.

**Flush salt buildup quarterly** by watering the pot through with twice its volume of plain water (let the excess drain out the bottom). This single step extends the productive life of an indoor gerbera by 6–12 months.

## How do you encourage repeat flowering through the year?

The 6–8 week bloom cycle is normal but often misinterpreted as "the plant is dying". Knowing what to expect prevents the wrong intervention.

**The natural cycle:**

1. **Active bud formation:** 2–3 weeks. New buds emerge from the centre; expect 3–6 stems per cycle.
2. **Open bloom:** 2–4 weeks. Each flower lasts 10–14 days; fresh ones replace older ones.
3. **Rest:** 4–8 weeks. No new buds; only foliage. Plant looks "boring" but is rebuilding reserves.
4. **New cycle starts.** The plant breaks rest and begins forming buds again.

**To shorten rests and increase total bloom:**

- **Remove spent flowers immediately.** Snip stems all the way back to the base; don't leave stubs.
- **Remove yellow or damaged leaves** to redirect energy.
- **Keep light high.** Plants in marginal light skip cycles or rest longer.
- **Provide cool nights.** A 5–8°C drop between day and night is the trigger for new bud formation.
- **Repot every 18–24 months** in fresh compost. Tired soil reduces flower count noticeably.

**Winter:** in low-light periods (December–February in temperate zones), accept a longer rest. The plant returns to flowering in March if you've kept it healthy through winter.

**Force a flush** for a special occasion by moving the plant to your sunniest cool window for 3 weeks before the desired bloom date. Bud formation takes about 3 weeks from "ready" to "open".

## What goes wrong with indoor gerberas, and how do you fix it?

The same handful of problems comes up repeatedly. Identifying which one you have makes the fix straightforward.

**Crown rot.** Soft brown spot at the centre where leaves emerge. Caused by water sitting in the crown, soil too wet, or burial of the crown during repotting. **Remove the plant**, cut away rotten tissue, dust with cinnamon (mild antifungal), repot in fresh dry mix with the crown clearly above soil level, and withhold water for 5–7 days.

**Powdery mildew.** White dusty coating on leaves. Caused by stagnant air and poor ventilation. **Improve airflow** with a fan, **water from below**, **remove worst leaves**, and the plant usually clears in 2–3 weeks.

**Aphids.** Small clusters on leaf undersides and bud stems. **Rinse off** with a strong water spray, treat with **insecticidal soap** every 4 days for 2 weeks.

**Whiteflies.** Tiny white insects fly up when foliage is disturbed. Tougher to control than aphids; **yellow sticky traps** plus insecticidal soap, repeated weekly for a month.

**Bent flower necks** (mainly on cut stems but sometimes on potted flowers). Usually a hydration issue. See [why gerbera daisies get bent necks](/posts/gerbera-daisies-bent-neck-en/) for the detailed cut-flower discussion; for potted plants, ensure the soil is evenly moist and humidity is at least 40%.

**No flowers, only leaves.** Either **insufficient light** (most common), **excess nitrogen** (second most common), or **the plant is in a natural rest period** (just wait 4–8 weeks). Diagnose by checking light and feed history before assuming the plant is "broken".

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do indoor gerbera daisies live?

Typically **2–3 years** as productive indoor plants. Performance peaks in years 1–2, then flower count gradually drops as the rootstock ages. **Repot annually** with fresh compost and divide every 18–24 months to extend life. After 3 years, replacement plants usually outperform attempted recovery of declining originals.

### Do gerberas need direct sunlight indoors?

Yes — at least **4–6 hours of direct sun** per day for reliable bloom. Bright indirect light alone produces leaves but few flowers. A south or east window is ideal; west windows work but may overheat the plant in summer. North windows are too dim without supplementary grow-lights.

### Why is my indoor gerbera not flowering?

The four most common reasons are: **light too low** (move to a sunnier spot), **excess nitrogen** (switch to a low-N flowering feed), **plant in natural rest period** (wait 4–8 weeks), or **rootbound in old soil** (repot in fresh compost). Fix the most likely cause first and wait 3 weeks before changing other variables.

### Can you grow gerberas from seed indoors?

Yes, but it takes **18–22 weeks from seed to first flower**. Sow on the surface of warm (20–22°C) seed compost, do not cover, keep moist with bottom-watering. Germination is 14–21 days. Cheaper than buying named cultivar plants but the offspring vary in colour and form. For known colour varieties, buying potted plants is more reliable.

### Do gerbera daisies clean indoor air?

NASA's 1989 indoor-plant air quality study included gerbera daisies as one of the better volatile-organic-compound (VOC) absorbers, but the practical effect at typical home plant densities is small. Treat air-cleaning claims as a minor benefit, not a primary reason to keep them indoors. Their value is decorative.

### Can gerberas be moved outdoors in summer?

Yes — and they often bloom more reliably outdoors during warm months. **Harden off** over a 7–10 day window before moving to a sheltered spot with morning sun and afternoon shade. Bring back indoors before night temperatures drop below 8°C in autumn. The plants benefit from natural temperature swings, fresh air, and rainfall during summer.
