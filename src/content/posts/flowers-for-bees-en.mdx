Pollinators — bees, butterflies, hoverflies, moths, and beetles — are responsible for one third of the food we eat. Their populations have declined dramatically over the past fifty years due to habitat loss, pesticide use, and climate change. A garden planted with the right flowers can be a genuinely meaningful contribution to reversing that decline, as well as being one of the most rewarding things a gardener can create.

## Understanding what pollinators actually need

Before choosing plants, it helps to understand what pollinators are looking for: **nectar** (for energy) and **pollen** (for protein, especially for bee larvae). Different pollinators have different physical characteristics — a bumblebee with its long tongue can reach deep tubular flowers that a small solitary bee cannot. Butterflies, with their proboscis, prefer flowers with a flat landing platform where they can feed while stationary.

The other critical factor is **continuity of bloom**. A garden with abundant flowers in July but nothing in April or October leaves pollinators without support during crucial periods. The goal should be a succession of flowers from early spring through to late autumn.

## Early spring flowers (March–April)

Early pollinators — particularly queen bumblebees emerging from hibernation — are desperately vulnerable in early spring, when there may be little available. These plants provide essential early support:

**Crocus** (*Crocus tommasinianus* in particular) is one of the first flowers to open, often in late February. Even on cold days, bees will emerge to visit crocuses when temperatures briefly rise. Plant in drifts for maximum impact.

**Lungwort** (*Pulmonaria*) flowers in early spring with tubular flowers in pink, purple, and blue. Long-tongued bumblebees are particularly attracted to these.

**Hellebores** (*Helleborus*) are underrated pollinator plants. Their downward-facing blooms are excellent early sources of pollen for bumblebees.

**Willows** (*Salix*) — including pussy willows and ornamental species — produce enormous quantities of pollen and nectar in early spring. A large willow can support thousands of bees on a single warm day.

## Late spring and summer flowers (May–August)

This is the peak season for most pollinators, and the choice of plants is enormous:

**Lavender** (*Lavandula*) is perhaps the single most popular bee plant in temperate gardens. On a warm summer day, a lavender hedge hums with activity from multiple bee species simultaneously. The long flowering period (June–September for most varieties) adds to its value.

**Borage** (*Borago officinalis*) produces striking blue star-shaped flowers that are exceptionally rich in nectar. Bees visit constantly throughout the day. It self-seeds prolifically once established.

**Echinacea** (*Echinacea purpurea*, the coneflower) provides nectar-rich flowers in mid-summer and then, as the flower heads dry into seed heads in autumn, provides food for goldfinches. A genuinely multi-purpose wildlife plant.

**Foxglove** (*Digitalis purpurea*) with its deep tubular flowers is perfectly designed for the long tongue of the bumblebee. Watch a large bumblebee disappear entirely inside a foxglove floret. The flowers also attract hummingbird hawk-moths in southern Europe.

**Phacelia** (*Phacelia tanacetifolia*) — sometimes called the bee's friend — is a fast-growing annual with vivid purple-blue flowers that bees go absolutely mad for. Often planted by farmers on field margins as a "bee pasture." Easy to grow from seed.

**Verbena bonariensis** is a tall, airy perennial that flowers for months and is beloved by butterflies. The flat flower clusters provide a perfect landing platform for peacock butterflies, red admirals, and painted ladies.

**Comfrey** (*Symphytum*) is often grown primarily as a fertiliser plant, but its tubular flowers are exceptional for bumblebees. A patch of comfrey near a vegetable garden benefits both wildlife and the gardener.

**Alliums** — ornamental onions — are spectacular pollinator plants. The large globe-shaped flower heads of varieties like *Allium hollandicum* and *Allium cristophii* are visited by hundreds of bees simultaneously.

## Autumn flowers (September–November)

Autumn is often a hungry time for pollinators preparing for winter, and late-flowering plants are more valuable than many gardeners realise:

**Aster** (*Symphyotrichum*) — particularly the North American species — are late-flowering daisy relatives that provide nectar well into October. They're particularly valuable for butterflies seeking energy for hibernation or migration.

**Ivy** (*Hedera helix*) in flower is one of the most important late-season nectar sources available. Ivy blooms in October and November, when almost nothing else is flowering — and it attracts an extraordinary variety of insects. Resist the urge to cut ivy before it's finished flowering.

**Sedum** (*Hylotelephium*) — particularly the 'Autumn Joy' variety — produces flat-topped flower heads in shades of pink and rust that butterflies cover in September and October.

**Mahonia** flowers in November and December with yellow flowers that bumblebees regularly visit on mild winter days.

## What to avoid

**Double flowers** — the highly bred, showy, double-petalled varieties of many garden flowers — are often nearly useless to pollinators. The extra petals block access to nectar and pollen, and many double varieties have been bred to produce little or none of either. A single-flowered rose or dahlia is far better for wildlife than a fully double variety.

**Peat-based planting** is associated with habitat loss for bog plants and associated wildlife. Choose peat-free compost.

**Pesticides** — particularly systemic insecticides like neonicotinoids — are devastating to pollinators. If you want a pollinator-friendly garden, the trade-off is accepting some plant damage from aphids and caterpillars. The wildlife that results is more than worth it.

A garden that hums with bees is a sign of ecological health. It is also, genuinely, one of the most pleasurable environments a person can spend time in.
