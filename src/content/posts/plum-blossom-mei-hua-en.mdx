## Quick Answer

The plum blossom (*Prunus mume*, called *mei hua* in Chinese) is one of the most culturally significant flowers in China — it blooms in winter snow before any other tree, and that single fact makes it the symbol of perseverance and resilience. It is one of the "Four Gentlemen" of Chinese painting and the national flower of the Republic of China (Taiwan). Bloom is January–March, weeks before cherry blossom.

## What is the plum blossom and how is it different from cherry blossom?

The plum blossom is the flower of *Prunus mume* — sometimes called Chinese plum or Japanese apricot, though it is botanically neither a true plum (*Prunus domestica*) nor an apricot (*Prunus armeniaca*). The species is native to southern China and was introduced to Korea (where it is *maehwa*) and Japan (where it is *ume*) more than 1,500 years ago.

Plum blossom is the flower most often confused with cherry blossom in search and conversation, but the differences are clear once you know what to look for:

| Feature | Plum blossom (*ume*/*mei hua*) | Cherry blossom (*sakura*) |
|---|---|---|
| **Bloom timing** | Mid-January – early March | Late March – early May |
| **Petal shape** | Round, no notch | Has a small split (notch) at the petal tip |
| **Stem length** | Very short — flowers grow directly from the branch | Long stems, flowers in clusters |
| **Bark** | Dark, knotty, gnarled | Smoother, with characteristic horizontal lines |
| **Fragrance** | Strong, sweet | Subtle to none |
| **Blooming with leaves?** | No — flowers on bare branches in snow | No — flowers before leaves emerge |

The single fastest tell: if the flower is blooming through snow with no leaves on the tree, it's plum. If the air is mild, the leaves are starting to push, and the petals have a notched tip, it's cherry. See [When and where can you see cherry blossoms in Japan?](/posts/cherry-blossom-japan-en/) for the cherry-blossom timing detail.

## What does the plum blossom symbolise in Chinese culture?

Three layered meanings, all rooted in the same observation that *mei hua* blooms in winter:

**Perseverance against hardship.** The poet Wang Anshi (11th century) wrote of plum blossom blooming "alone in the cold wind" — and the flower has been the standard literary metaphor for moral integrity in adverse circumstances ever since. It is the flower most often invoked in Chinese descriptions of soldiers, scholars, and political dissidents.

**The promise of spring.** Because plum blossom is the *first* flower of the year, it announces that winter will end. In Chinese New Year decoration the plum branch is the equivalent of the Christmas tree — a domestic symbol that the cycle has turned.

**The Five Blessings.** Each of the five petals on a classic *mei hua* flower stands for one of the *wǔ fú* — long life, prosperity, health, virtue, and a peaceful death — making the flower a stylised wish for a complete good life.

The plum blossom is also one of the **Four Gentlemen** (*sì jūnzǐ*) of classical Chinese painting, alongside the orchid, bamboo, and chrysanthemum — together they represent the four seasons and four virtues a cultivated person should embody. For the chrysanthemum companion-piece, see [What does the chrysanthemum symbolise in Japan and China?](/posts/chrysanthemum-asia-en/).

## When and where does the plum blossom bloom?

Bloom timing varies by latitude and elevation:

- **Southern China** (Guangzhou, Hong Kong) — late December to mid-January.
- **Yangtze region** (Shanghai, Hangzhou, Nanjing) — late January to late February.
- **Northern China** (Beijing) — mid-February to mid-March.
- **Taiwan** (mountains) — January to February.
- **Japan** — *ume* blooms January (Kyushu) to March (Tohoku), 4–8 weeks ahead of the cherry-blossom front.
- **Korea** — late February to March.

Famous viewing sites include **West Lake (Hangzhou)**, where Lin Bu's tomb is surrounded by a thousand plum trees and the flower has been written about for over a thousand years; **Nanjing's Plum Blossom Hill (Meihua Shan)**; and **Mito's Kairakuen** in Japan, one of the country's three great gardens, with 3,000 *ume* trees.

## How is the plum blossom used in Asian art and poetry?

Plum blossom is arguably the single most painted flower in Chinese ink-wash tradition. The 13th-century Song-dynasty handbook *Mei Hua Xi Shen Pu* ("Plum Blossom Manual") is one of the earliest illustrated painting guides anywhere and standardised the brush conventions still used today: the bare twisted branch in heavy ink, the five-petal flower in light wash, no leaves, no background.

In poetry, plum blossom is the conventional setup for any poem about endurance, lonely virtue, or the recluse-scholar. The 11th-century poet Lin Bu famously "married" a plum tree and "fathered" the cranes around his hermit's hut at West Lake — the image entered the cultural shorthand for a life chosen above worldly ambition.

For an adjacent treatment of how Asian painters use flower motifs, see [Flowers in art history](/posts/flowers-in-art-history-en/).

## Can you grow plum blossom outside China?

Yes — *Prunus mume* grows well anywhere in USDA zones 6–9. It needs full sun, well-drained soil, and a defined winter chill (around 800 hours below 7 °C) to flower well. Mature trees reach 4–10 metres and live 100+ years; the gnarled trunk a good *mei hua* tree develops over time is half the visual point.

If you can't accommodate a tree, *Prunus mume* makes excellent cut branches for the home: a bare branch with closed buds will open over 7–10 days in a warm room and is one of the few flowers that opens better indoors than out (the warmth speeds the bud break). For care of woody-stem cut flowers in general, see [How long do cut flowers last in a vase?](/posts/cut-flower-vase-life-en/).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What does the plum blossom symbolise?**
Perseverance, resilience, and the promise that hardship will end. The symbolism comes from the flower's habit of blooming in winter snow before any other tree. In Chinese tradition the five petals also represent the *wǔ fú* — long life, prosperity, health, virtue, and a peaceful death.

**Is plum blossom the same as cherry blossom?**
No. They are different species (*Prunus mume* vs. *Prunus serrulata*) that bloom 4–8 weeks apart. Plum blossom has rounded petals on very short stems and blooms in winter snow on bare branches; cherry blossom has notched petals on longer stems and blooms in spring as leaves emerge.

**Why is plum blossom the national flower of Taiwan?**
The Republic of China designated *mei hua* as its national flower in 1964, citing the same perseverance symbolism that has carried the flower through Chinese poetry. The five petals also map onto the five branches of the ROC government and the *wǔ fú* blessings.

**Can you eat plum blossom?**
The flowers themselves are not commonly eaten, but the fruit of *Prunus mume* is widely used: Chinese pickled plums (*suānméi*), Japanese *umeboshi*, Korean *maesil*-syrup, and the salty preserved-plum drinks of Hong Kong and Taiwan all derive from the same tree.

**How long does plum blossom last as a cut branch?**
A bare branch with mostly closed buds will open progressively over 7–10 days in a vase of warm water and continue to flower for another week. Total useful display from a fresh-cut branch is around 14–18 days — far longer than cherry blossom's 4–6 days as a cut.
