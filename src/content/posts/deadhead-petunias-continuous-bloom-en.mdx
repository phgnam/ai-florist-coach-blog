## Quick Answer

To keep petunias flowering all summer, deadhead by pinching off the whole flower base (the green seedpod and stalk behind the petals), not just the wilted petals. Do this every 3–4 days. By mid-July, cut the entire plant back by one-third, water deeply, and feed with a high-potassium tomato feed. New flowers appear within 10–14 days.

## Why do petunias stop flowering by mid-summer?

Petunias are botanically annuals — their entire life mission is to set seed and die. Every time a flower fades and is allowed to form a seedpod, the plant gets the chemical signal "you have done your job; stop investing in new flowers."

That signal is real. The seedpod produces ethylene and abscisic acid, which suppress the development of the next round of flower buds further down the stem. Leave seedpods on for 2 weeks and the plant noticeably slows down; leave them on for 4 weeks and the plant goes essentially flowerless and starts looking leggy and tired.

Most "self-cleaning" varieties marketed today (Wave, Supertunia, Surfinia) drop their old petals on their own — but they still form seedpods if you don't physically remove them. The petals fall off; the seedpod stays. Self-cleaning is a marketing term about appearance, not about reproductive cessation.

The fix is almost embarrassingly simple: remove the seedpods before they mature.

## Where do you actually pinch — petals or pod?

This is where most gardeners go wrong. You see a wilted petunia flower, you grab the brown petals, and you pull them off. The petals come away in your hand. The plant looks tidier. But the seedpod is still there — a small green swollen base where the flower joined the stem — and it carries on developing seeds.

To deadhead correctly:

1. Look behind the wilted petals. You will see a small green stub (the calyx) and behind it a slightly larger swelling (the developing seedpod and ovary).
2. Pinch the whole stub off with thumb and forefinger, or use floral snips. Take off everything from the seedpod outwards.
3. Follow the stem back to the next pair of leaves. If the dead flower's stalk is more than 2 cm long, snip it back to the leaf node — that triggers a new flowering shoot from the leaf axil.

Done correctly, you remove a green pellet about the size of a grain of rice, plus the dead petals. Done incorrectly, you only remove the petals and the seedpod stays behind, defeating the purpose.

If you have a hanging basket of trailing petunias and 30+ flowers, use floral snips and run a quick sweep every 3–4 days. Two minutes per basket, twice a week. Compare to no deadheading: the plant peaks in late June and is essentially over by mid-July.

## How often should you deadhead petunias for continuous bloom?

Frequency depends on flower count and weather:

- **Single plant in a 30 cm pot:** check every 3–4 days. Remove anything that has finished or is wilted.
- **Hanging basket with 30–60 flowers:** twice a week, more often after rain (rain speeds up bud development and flower turnover).
- **Bedding planting of 20+ petunias:** weekly sweep is enough; the goal is preventing seedpod maturation, not perfect tidiness.
- **After a thunderstorm:** the rain will batter petals; do a deadheading pass within 24 hours to prevent rot in the wet calyces.

The rhythm matters more than the perfection. Three minutes per pot every 3–4 days beats a 30-minute "perfect" deadheading session once a month.

## When and how do you cut petunias back hard?

Even with perfect deadheading, petunias get leggy by mid-July. The lower stems go bare, the new flowers cluster only at the tips, and the plant looks straggly.

The fix is the **mid-summer hard cut-back**:

1. Around 10 July (Northern Hemisphere) or about 6 weeks after planting out, take sharp scissors and cut the entire plant back by one-third. For a hanging basket, that means shortening the trailing stems back to about half their length.
2. Yes, you will be cutting off open flowers and buds. Do it anyway.
3. Water the plant deeply, drench-style.
4. Feed with a high-potassium tomato feed (the same one you'd use for tomatoes; potassium drives flowering, not foliage).
5. Move the plant briefly out of the hottest afternoon sun for 3–4 days while it recovers.

For 7–10 days the plant looks half-dead and you regret cutting it. Around day 10–14, new flowering shoots emerge along the entire length of every stem and the plant explodes into bloom — usually with more flowers than it had before the cut-back.

This is the same logic as the [snapdragons keep flowering all summer](/posts/snapdragons-keep-flowering-summer-en/) cut-back, applied to petunias.

## What feed and watering routine sustains the bloom?

Petunias are heavy feeders, especially in containers. The feeding rule is simple but uncompromising: **feed weekly, every week, until late September**.

- Use a high-potassium liquid feed (any tomato feed: 4-2-7 or similar NPK ratio).
- Dilute as the bottle directs and apply weekly to wet (not dry) compost.
- Stop feeding when night temperatures fall below 10 °C in autumn.

For watering: petunias in pots dry out very fast in mid-summer. Check daily, and water when the top 2 cm of compost is dry. In a heatwave, water once in the morning and again at dusk if the pot is small.

A common error is overhead watering — splashing the flowers themselves. Wet petunia petals collect dirt, develop mould (Botrytis), and look ugly. Water at the base, not the foliage. Drip irrigation or a long-spouted can directed at the soil works best.

For more on container watering rhythms, see [how to grow ranunculus in pots](/posts/grow-ranunculus-in-pots-en/) for the same drench-and-drain approach.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do you need to deadhead "self-cleaning" Wave or Supertunia petunias?

Yes, despite the marketing. Self-cleaning means the petals drop on their own without sticking to neighbouring foliage — but the seedpod still forms behind. For maximum bloom you still want to pinch off the seedpods every 5–7 days. The bonus is that the plant looks tidy between deadheadings.

### Can you deadhead petunias by simply running your hand through the basket?

A rough sweep with your hand removes wilted petals but rarely pulls out the seedpods, which sit behind the calyx and have a tougher attachment. It's better than nothing, but seedpods will still form. For real continuous bloom, snip or pinch each spent flower at its base.

### Why are my deadheaded petunias still not flowering?

Three common causes: (1) you're removing only petals, not the seedpod; (2) underfeeding — petunias are gross feeders and starve in container compost by July without weekly feed; (3) too much shade — petunias need 6+ hours of direct sun for continuous bloom; in less than 4 hours of sun, no amount of deadheading will keep them flowering.

### When should you stop deadheading petunias in autumn?

Stop deadheading and feeding 2–3 weeks before your first expected frost, or when night temperatures drop below 10 °C consistently. The plants will slow naturally, and any final flowers can be enjoyed without intervention. Once frost hits, pull the plants and compost.

### How do you save petunia seeds for next year?

Most modern petunias are F1 hybrids and the seeds will not come true; you'll get a mix of inferior offspring. If you still want to try, leave a few flowers on the plant in late summer, let the seedpods turn brown and dry, then collect them and store somewhere cool and dark. Sow indoors in February. Expect variable results.
