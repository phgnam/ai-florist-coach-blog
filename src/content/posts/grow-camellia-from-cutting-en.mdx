## Quick Answer

Take 8–12 cm semi-ripe camellia cuttings from the current year's growth in late July to mid-August, when the bottom of the stem is woody but the tip is still flexible. Wound the lower stem, dip in rooting hormone with 0.4 % IBA, insert into a 50:50 perlite-and-acidic-compost mix, and keep at 18–22 °C with 90 % humidity for 8–12 weeks. Expect 50–70 % rooting success and 3–4 years to first flower.

## Why are camellias slow to root and what does "semi-ripe" mean?

Camellias are evergreen shrubs from the *Theaceae* family with thick, glossy leaves and woody stems. Their tissue is naturally resistant to rooting compared with soft-stemmed plants like pelargoniums or fuchsias — the same waxy cuticle that protects the leaves from water loss also blocks the stem from generating root primordia easily.

The single biggest factor in camellia rooting success is the maturity of the cutting wood. Three categories of cutting wood exist:

- **Softwood:** the very tip of new spring growth, light green and bendy. Easy to root in many plants but on camellias it withers and rots before rooting.
- **Semi-ripe:** mid-summer growth where the bottom 2–3 cm has hardened to a brown-green colour but the tip is still pliable. The right choice for camellia.
- **Hardwood:** fully woody dormant stems from autumn or winter. Roots slowly and unreliably for camellia.

Semi-ripe cuttings root because they have the right balance of stored energy (in the woody base) and active growth hormones (in the still-soft tip). Take them in late July to mid-August in temperate climates — earlier in cooler climates, later in warmer ones. The exact window is when you can bend the tip of a stem easily but the base snaps cleanly.

For other propagation methods across different plants, the [propagate roses from cuttings](/posts/propagate-roses-cuttings-en/) post covers a similar semi-ripe approach on roses.

## How do you select and prepare the cutting?

Cutting selection is where most of the success is decided. A 30-second pause to inspect the parent plant pays off across the next 12 weeks.

**Choose the parent plant carefully.** Avoid plants with virus symptoms (mottled leaves, irregular streaks), insect damage, or fungal blotch. Take cuttings only from healthy, vigorous shrubs.

**Choose the right stems.** Look for current-year stems with:

- 3–4 mature, glossy leaves
- A semi-flexible tip and a woody base
- No flower buds (which divert energy from root formation)
- Length of 12–15 cm before trimming

**Time of day matters.** Cut in the early morning when stems are fully turgid, before the day's transpiration. Place cuttings immediately into a plastic bag with a damp paper towel to prevent wilting on the way to the propagation bench.

**Trim the cutting:**

1. **Cut to 8–12 cm length** with a sharp knife, just below a leaf node.
2. **Remove the lower 2 leaves** to expose the section that will be in compost.
3. **Halve the remaining 2 leaves** by cutting off their tips. This reduces water loss without removing photosynthetic surface entirely.
4. **Wound the lower stem** by scraping a 1 cm strip of bark off one side with a clean knife. This exposes the cambium where root primordia form.

Wounding is the step many propagators skip and that doubles success rates. Camellia bark is thick enough that root primordia struggle to push through; the wound creates a direct path.

## What rooting hormone and compost mix do you use?

For camellia, rooting hormone makes a significant difference. Without hormone, success rates are 20–40 %; with the right hormone, 50–70 %.

**The right hormone:**

- **Active ingredient:** indole-3-butyric acid (IBA) at 0.3–0.4 % concentration.
- **Form:** powder is easiest for hobbyists; liquid is more precise for batches over 50 cuttings.
- **Brand:** any garden-centre rooting powder with "0.3 % IBA" or "hardwood/semi-ripe" on the label.

**Application:** dip the lower 2 cm of the cutting (where the wound is) into the powder, tap off excess. Avoid dipping the leaves.

**Compost mix:**

- 50 % horticultural perlite (drainage and air)
- 50 % ericaceous (acidic) compost (camellias are calcifuges and need pH 5.0–6.0)

Avoid: standard multipurpose compost (too alkaline), pure peat (too dense and acidic when wet), or sand-only mixes (no nutrients for the slow rooting period).

**Pot setup:** use small individual 7 cm pots rather than communal trays. One cutting per pot reduces transplant shock when you pot on the rooted plants. Drainage is critical; standing water causes rot before rooting.

The compost surface is best topped with a 5 mm layer of fine grit. The grit reflects light slightly, reducing surface algae, and improves drainage at the stem base where rot most often starts.

## How do you set up the propagation environment?

The cutting needs warm soil, cool air, and very high humidity for 8–12 weeks. The traditional setup is a heated propagator with a transparent lid; a homemade alternative is a plastic-bag-and-pot greenhouse.

**Commercial heated propagator:**

- Bottom heat at 20 °C (camellia roots best at 18–22 °C base temperature).
- Air temperature 16–20 °C — slightly cooler than the soil.
- Humidity inside the lid 80–95 %.
- Indirect bright light, no direct sun.
- Position in a cool greenhouse or unheated north-facing room.

**Homemade alternative:**

1. Place the pots on a windowsill with morning sun only.
2. Cover each pot with a clear plastic bag, supported by 4 short canes pushed into the compost so the bag doesn't touch the leaves.
3. Place the whole arrangement on a heat mat (commercial seed-warming mat) or near a low-heat radiator.
4. Open the bag for 5 minutes daily for air exchange.
5. Mist the cuttings lightly when opening for air.

**Watering during rooting:**

- Compost should stay just damp, never wet.
- Water from below by sitting the pot in a tray of rain or filtered water for 5 minutes.
- Mist the leaves daily for the first 4 weeks; reduce to alternate days from week 5.

The single biggest cause of failure is fungal rot from too-wet compost. The single biggest cause of slow rooting is too-cool soil temperature. Soil temperature matters more than air temperature for camellia.

For camellias suffering bud-drop on a mature plant rather than rooting issues, the [camellia bud drop](/posts/camellia-bud-drop-en/) post covers the related diagnostic.

## When and how do you check for rooting?

The first 4 weeks, do not disturb the cuttings beyond opening for air exchange. Tugging at cuttings to test rooting damages the fragile primary roots that are still forming.

From week 5, start visual checks:

- **New shoot growth at the tip** is the most reliable rooting sign. The cutting is putting energy into top growth because the roots are now feeding it.
- **Leaves become slightly more turgid and glossy** compared with neighbouring un-rooted cuttings.
- **The cutting resists a very gentle tug** when lifted.

By week 8–10, well-rooted cuttings can be carefully tipped out for inspection. A rooted cutting has 3–10 white root tips emerging from the base of the stem. Replace immediately if not ready.

By week 12, most cuttings are either rooted or rotted. Remove rotted cuttings (soft, dark stems) immediately; they spread mould to the rest of the batch.

**Once rooted:**

1. Pot up into individual 12 cm pots in fresh ericaceous compost.
2. Water in well; place in a cool shady spot for 2 weeks while roots develop.
3. Mist daily for the first week to reduce stress.
4. Apply a half-strength ericaceous liquid feed every 14 days from week 4 in the new pot.

The newly potted plants spend a year in the 12 cm pot, then move up to a 20 cm pot for the second year. By year 3, the plant is large enough to plant out. First flowers appear in years 3–4 in temperate gardens.

## What goes wrong, and how do you avoid it?

Five common failures and the cause/fix for each.

**1. Cutting wilts within a week.**
Cause: insufficient humidity or stem damage. Fix: check the bag is sealing, ensure the wound was clean (not crushed), and re-cut and re-pot.

**2. Cutting is firm but no new growth at week 12.**
Cause: rooted but slow. Fix: leave another 4 weeks before potting on. Camellias can take 14–16 weeks rather than 8–10.

**3. Stem turns black at the base.**
Cause: fungal rot from too-wet compost. Fix: discard the cutting; reduce watering on the rest of the batch.

**4. Mould on the compost surface.**
Cause: high humidity + organic compost. Fix: increase air exchange (open bag for 30 minutes daily), top with grit, accept some surface mould as normal.

**5. Mature cuttings root but seedling-like cuttings wither.**
Cause: cuttings taken too late in the season (October–November) when wood is fully ripe. Fix: take next batch in July–August window.

For unsuccessful rooting batches, the parent material is worth re-examining. Some camellia cultivars are genuinely difficult to root from cuttings — *C. japonica* cultivars are reliable, *C. reticulata* hybrids less so. For difficult cultivars, layering or grafting is more reliable than cuttings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are camellia cuttings true to the parent?**
Yes. Cuttings are clonal — the new plant is genetically identical to the parent and produces the same flower colour, form, and habit.

**Can I take camellia cuttings in winter?**
Hardwood winter cuttings work for some cultivars but rooting takes 4–6 months and success rates are lower than semi-ripe summer cuttings.

**Why are flowers smaller on a cutting-grown camellia than the parent?**
Young plants take 3–4 years to flower at full size. The first 1–2 seasons of flowering produce smaller blooms while the plant matures.

**Can I propagate a camellia by air layering instead?**
Yes. Air layering on a low branch produces a rooted plant in 6–9 months and has a higher success rate than cuttings for difficult cultivars.

**Will my rooted cutting survive in the garden?**
With a sheltered position out of cold winter winds and acidic soil (pH 5.0–6.0), yes. Camellias are hardy to about –10 °C; younger plants need the first winter under cover.
