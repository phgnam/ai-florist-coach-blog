## Quick Answer

Calla lilies (*Zantedeschia aethiopica*) are mildly toxic to cats and dogs but they are not the deadly "true lily" that causes kidney failure in cats. The whole plant contains insoluble calcium oxalate crystals, which cause mouth pain, drooling, vomiting, and difficulty swallowing if a pet chews a leaf or stem. Call your vet if symptoms last more than a few hours.

## Are calla lilies the same as the lilies that kill cats?

No, and this is the most important point. Calla lilies are not true lilies. They belong to the genus *Zantedeschia* in the Araceae family — the same plant family as peace lilies, philodendrons, and dieffenbachia. The "true lilies" that cause acute kidney failure in cats belong to the genera *Lilium* and *Hemerocallis* (Easter lily, tiger lily, daylily, Asiatic and Oriental hybrids).

The danger profile is therefore very different. A cat that bites a true lily can go into renal failure within 72 hours and die without aggressive treatment, even from licking pollen off its fur. A cat that bites a calla lily will be in pain and may vomit, but the toxic mechanism is mechanical — sharp microscopic crystals — rather than systemic kidney damage.

If you are not sure which lily you have, the [pet-safe flower checker](/tools/pet-safe-checker/) on this site cross-references species against the ASPCA toxicity database for cats, dogs, and horses. Always confirm species before assuming a "lily" is one or the other.

## Why are calla lilies toxic in the first place?

The plant defends itself with a mechanical irritant rather than a chemical poison. Inside the cells of the leaves, stems, and spathe are bundles of needle-shaped insoluble calcium oxalate crystals called raphides. When a pet bites the tissue, the crystals are ejected and embed themselves in the tongue, gums, lips, and the soft lining of the mouth and throat.

The result is immediate and very uncomfortable. Most pets stop chewing within seconds because it is so unpleasant. That self-limiting reaction is one reason calla lily poisoning is rarely fatal: the animal usually does not eat enough to do serious damage.

The same family of toxic crystals appears in many common houseplants — peace lily (*Spathiphyllum*), philodendron, monstera, pothos, dieffenbachia, anthurium. If a pet reacts the same way to several of your houseplants, calcium oxalate is almost always the common factor.

## What are the symptoms after a pet bites a calla lily?

Symptoms appear within seconds to minutes and usually peak within an hour or two. Watch for:

- **Drooling** — often heavy and foamy, the most obvious sign.
- **Pawing at the mouth** — cats especially will paw repeatedly at their face.
- **Vocalising or hiding** — pain response.
- **Refusing food or water** for several hours.
- **Vomiting** — common, especially if any plant material was swallowed.
- **Swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat** in more severe cases.
- **Difficulty swallowing or breathing** — rare, but a 24/7 emergency.

Most pets recover within 12 to 24 hours without treatment, because the crystals are eventually broken down or expelled. The main risks are dehydration if vomiting continues, and airway swelling in small pets where the throat is anatomically narrow.

If your pet stops drooling within an hour, eats normally that evening, and has no further vomiting, observation at home is usually appropriate. If any of those signs persist past a few hours, or if you see swelling, breathing changes, or repeated vomiting, call your vet or an animal poison control line.

## What should you do immediately after a pet bites a calla lily?

Move calmly. The faster you respond, the easier it is to limit how much the pet ingests.

1. **Take the plant material away.** Remove any leaves, petals, or stem fragments from the pet's mouth and from the area.
2. **Rinse the mouth gently.** If the pet will tolerate it, wipe the gums and tongue with a clean, damp cloth. Do not force water down the throat.
3. **Offer cold water or a small amount of milk.** Cold liquid soothes the irritated tissues. A teaspoon of milk binds some of the calcium oxalate. Do not force-feed.
4. **Do not induce vomiting.** Vomiting brings the crystals back through the same irritated tissues and makes things worse, not better.
5. **Photograph the plant.** Identify it by genus and species before you call the vet. *Zantedeschia* and *Lilium* require very different responses.
6. **Call a vet or poison line if symptoms persist.** In the US, the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center is (888) 426-4435; in the UK, the Animal PoisonLine is 01202 509000.

If the bite was minor — a quick chew on a leaf edge, normal behaviour resuming within an hour — most pets are fine with home observation. The seriousness scales with how much was actually swallowed and the size of the animal.

## How do you keep cats and dogs away from calla lilies?

Prevention is much easier than dealing with a bitten plant. A few practical changes work better than constant vigilance.

| Method | What to do | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| Move the plant up | Place the pot on a high shelf, mantel, or a wall hook | Cats can still jump, but it removes casual access |
| Use a closed room | Keep calla lilies in a study, bedroom, or bathroom the pet does not enter | Removes opportunity entirely |
| Bitter spray | Apply a pet-safe bitter apple spray to leaves | Strong taste deters most cats and dogs |
| Provide cat grass | Offer wheatgrass or oat grass in a separate pot | Gives chewers a safe alternative |
| Swap to a non-toxic plant | Replace with African violet, *Phalaenopsis* orchid, or spider plant | Removes the risk altogether |

If the household includes both pets and small children, swapping to a non-toxic flower is usually the cleanest answer. The [pet-safe flowers guide](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/) lists alternatives that look similar enough to satisfy a calla lily aesthetic — peace alternatives include moth orchids, anthurium hybrids bred for low oxalate, or fresh-cut snapdragons. Note that anthurium is also in Araceae and contains oxalate, so it is not a "swap" — read the guide for confirmed-safe options.

For cut bouquets brought into a pet household, place the vase in a room the pet does not enter and pick up any fallen petals or stem fragments at the end of the day. Most pet-related ingestions are opportunistic, not deliberate, and removing the opportunity removes most of the risk.

## Are calla lily flowers more or less toxic than the leaves?

Both are toxic, but the concentration of crystals varies. The leaves and stems carry the highest density of raphides; the spathe (the showy white "petal") and the spadix (the yellow centre) contain fewer crystals but are still irritating. Pollen, by contrast, is not the problem here that it is in true lilies — *Zantedeschia* pollen is not nephrotoxic.

In practice, this means:

- **A leaf bite** is the most common scenario and the most painful for the pet.
- **A spathe bite** still triggers drooling and pawing but with less swelling.
- **A pollen lick** from a calla lily is essentially harmless (very different from licking pollen off a true *Lilium*, which is a veterinary emergency for cats).
- **Eating cut stem water** is mildly irritant, mostly because of the dissolved oxalate, but rarely causes more than transient drool.

If you grow callas as cut flowers indoors, treat the stem trimmings as the most concentrated waste. Bag them and put them in a pet-inaccessible bin, not the open compost where a curious dog can dig them up.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are calla lilies safe for rabbits?**
No. Rabbits are also susceptible to calcium oxalate irritation and should not have access to calla lily plants or cut stems.

**My cat licked the calla lily water in the vase — should I worry?**
Probably not. Watch for drooling and offer fresh water. The dissolved oxalate is a low dose compared with biting the leaf itself. Call your vet if drooling lasts more than an hour.

**Are peace lilies the same as calla lilies for pets?**
The chemistry is the same — both contain insoluble calcium oxalate raphides — but they are different species in the same family. Both are mildly toxic to cats and dogs and cause similar mouth-irritation symptoms.

**Can a calla lily kill a small dog?**
Extremely rarely, and only if a small dog manages to eat a large amount and develops severe airway swelling. The vast majority of calla lily incidents in dogs resolve without veterinary intervention.

**Is it safe to grow calla lilies on a balcony if my cat goes out there?**
Better to choose a different plant. Outdoor pots make it easier for a cat to chew leaves unsupervised. The [north-facing balcony flowers](/posts/north-facing-balcony-flowers-en/) guide includes pet-friendlier alternatives that tolerate similar light.
