## Quick Answer

Snowdrops (*Galanthus*) are divided "in the green" — meaning while the leaves are still green and active, just after flowering — rather than as dormant dry bulbs. Lift the whole clump with a fork in mid-to-late spring, gently tease apart into smaller groups of 3–6 bulbs, and replant immediately at the same depth at which they grew, about 8–10 cm spacing. Water in once and leave alone. Snowdrops divided in green re-establish far more reliably than dry stored bulbs, which is why specialist nurseries sell them this way.

## What is "in the green" and why is it the best time to divide snowdrops?

"In the green" is gardener shorthand for handling snowdrops while they are in active growth — leaves green, sometimes flowering, never dry and dormant. It contrasts with "dry bulbs", which is how most spring bulbs (tulips, daffodils, crocuses) are sold.

Several biological reasons make snowdrops different:

**Thin, papery tunic.** Daffodils and tulips have thick protective bulb tunics that survive months of dry storage. Snowdrops have thin tunics; they desiccate quickly out of soil.

**Active root system.** Snowdrops keep some root activity even in summer. Lifting and replanting in active green growth lets the roots reseat into damp soil before they have a chance to fully dry out.

**Reliable take.** Garden surveys consistently show snowdrops planted in the green establish at near 100%, while dry-stored bulbs often fail at 30–50% — they look normal in the bag but never flower.

**Easier to find.** Snowdrops disappear underground from May to January. Trying to find dormant bulbs in summer often results in damage from the spade. In green, the clumps are obvious.

## When exactly should I divide snowdrops in the green?

The window is short but forgiving. Aim for the period after the last flower has faded but while the leaves are still healthy and green.

**Ideal:** Mid-March to late April in temperate climates. The flowers are gone, the leaves still photosynthesising, the soil moist.

**Late but possible:** Up to early May if the leaves have not yet started to yellow. Past this point you are dividing tired plants that are about to go dormant; success drops noticeably.

**Too early:** Before flowering finishes, the bulbs are still actively expending energy on producing flower stalks. Disturbance during this stage often causes the current year's flowers to abort or fail to set seed.

**Too late:** Once leaves have yellowed and the plant is starting to go dormant, the roots stop functioning and replanted clumps fail.

A useful rule: divide as soon as you can identify the clump after flowering, but before the leaves begin to yellow.

## Why have my snowdrops stopped flowering, even though leaves come up every year?

This is the most common snowdrop complaint, and it is almost always one of three problems.

**Overcrowding.** Established clumps slowly outgrow themselves over 5–10 years. The bulbs become so densely packed that none has enough soil resources to flower well. Symptoms: lush leaves, few flowers, larger clumps year after year.

**Too dry in summer.** Snowdrops need moist conditions year-round, even in summer dormancy. Clumps planted under thirsty trees or in baking sun often persist as leaves but stop flowering. Symptoms: leaves come up but few or no flowers, often near tree roots.

**Too rich, too disturbed.** Heavy feeding (especially of high-nitrogen fertilisers) produces lush leaves at the expense of flowers. Repeated cultivation disturbs the bulbs.

The fix for the first problem (overcrowding) is to divide. The fix for the second is to relocate to a moister, more shaded position. The fix for the third is to leave them alone — snowdrops thrive on benign neglect.

## How exactly do I lift, divide, and replant snowdrops in green?

A simple sequence works for any clump size. Allow about 30 minutes per clump for a careful job.

### Step 1: Prepare the new positions

Decide where the new clumps will go before you lift. Dig the holes ahead of time so the bulbs are out of the soil for as little time as possible.

- **Spacing:** New clumps 30–40 cm apart for a natural drift; 8–10 cm apart for individual bulbs within a clump.
- **Depth:** Same depth as the original — usually 8–10 cm to the base of the bulb. Look at the bulb itself: the line between green leaf and white bulb is the soil line.
- **Soil:** Snowdrops thrive in moist humus-rich soil. Add a handful of leaf mould or well-rotted compost to each hole.

### Step 2: Lift carefully

Use a garden fork, not a spade. Insert the fork well outside the visible clump (15 cm out is safe) and lever the whole rootball up. Slap off excess soil to expose the bulbs.

### Step 3: Tease apart

With clean hands, gently separate the clump into smaller groups of 3–6 bulbs. Do not pull single bulbs apart — keep small natural groupings. Damaged bulbs (squashed, cut) heal poorly; discard or compost them.

### Step 4: Replant immediately

Plant new groups into the prepared holes. Backfill with the original soil mixed with leaf mould. Tamp gently — do not compact heavily.

### Step 5: Water in

A single thorough watering settles the soil around the bulbs. Mulch with a 2 cm layer of leaf mould.

### Step 6: Leave alone

Do not feed, do not cut leaves, do not weed too aggressively for the rest of the year. The leaves continue photosynthesising for another 4–6 weeks; do not cut them off until they have yellowed naturally.

## Which snowdrop varieties are easiest for beginners to divide and increase?

The genus *Galanthus* has perhaps 20 species and over 1,500 named cultivars. For beginners, vigour matters more than rarity.

| Variety | Vigour | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| *G. nivalis* | Very high | The common snowdrop; bulks up fast; cheap to buy in green. |
| *G. elwesii* | High | Larger flowers, broad grey leaves; thrives in drier shade than *G. nivalis*. |
| *G. nivalis* 'Flore Pleno' | Moderate | Double-flowered classic; reliable but slower to bulk up. |
| *G. plicatus* | High | Folded-leaf species; vigorous; good for drier conditions. |
| 'S. Arnott' | High | Tall, fragrant, large flowers; the connoisseur's choice that is still easy. |
| 'Magnet' | Very high | Long arching pedicels; bulks up fast. |
| 'Atkinsii' | High | Early flowering, large; reliable garden plant. |
| 'Tiny Tim' / 'Wendy's Gold' | Low to moderate | Specialist cultivars; expensive; less suitable for division as a beginner. |

For larger drifts at lower cost, stick with *G. nivalis* or 'Magnet' for the first few years, then add specialist cultivars once you have a feel for the technique.

## Are snowdrops safe for homes with pets and children?

No — snowdrops are mildly toxic and worth respecting. *Galanthus* bulbs and leaves contain galantamine and related alkaloids. Galantamine is actually used pharmaceutically to treat early Alzheimer's, but in unmonitored doses it causes nausea, vomiting, drowsiness, and gastrointestinal upset.

Practical precautions:

- **Do not let pets dig and chew the bulbs.** Cats and dogs occasionally find buried bulbs and chew them; the bulbs taste unpleasant but a determined animal will eat enough to feel ill.
- **Plant in beds, not in pots accessible to pets or small children.** Pot-grown snowdrops are more easily reached.
- **Wash hands after handling bulbs.** The sap can irritate sensitive skin.
- **Discard damaged bulbs responsibly.** Compost them in a sealed bin, not where livestock graze.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My snowdrops have stopped flowering but the leaves are huge and lush. What's wrong?**
Usually overcrowding. Lift the clump in green, divide into groups of 3–6 bulbs, and replant 30–40 cm apart. Flowers normally return the following spring. If overcrowding is not the cause, suspect summer drought (relocate to moister shade) or heavy feeding (stop feeding).

**Can I divide snowdrops in autumn from dry bulbs?**
Yes, but expect lower success rates. Buy from a specialist who has stored bulbs correctly (cool, slightly damp), plant immediately on receipt, and water well. Some cultivars (especially common *G. nivalis*) tolerate autumn dry-bulb planting better than others.

**Should I cut back the leaves after dividing snowdrops?**
No — leave the leaves entirely alone. They photosynthesise for another 4–6 weeks and feed the bulbs for next year's flowers. Cutting back accelerates the decline of newly disturbed plants.

**My divided snowdrops did not flower the following spring. Have I killed them?**
Probably not. Newly divided bulbs sometimes skip a year of flowering while they re-establish. If leaves come up the next spring, the plant is alive and will return to normal flowering the year after.

**Can I grow snowdrops from seed?**
Yes — snowdrops self-seed in suitable conditions, which is how natural drifts spread. Collect ripe seeds in early summer, sow immediately in pots of leaf-mould-based compost, leave outside through winter. Germination is in spring; first flowering takes 4–7 years. For most gardeners, division is faster.
