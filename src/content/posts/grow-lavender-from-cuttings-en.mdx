## Quick Answer

Take **8–10 cm softwood cuttings** from non-flowering side shoots in **late spring or early summer**, strip the lower two-thirds of leaves, and dip the base in rooting hormone. Push them into a free-draining mix of **50% perlite + 50% gritty compost**, keep just barely moist in **bright indirect light**, and roots form in **4–6 weeks**. Pot up individually once roots are 2–3 cm long, then plant out the following spring.

## When is the best time to take lavender cuttings?

Timing decides whether your cuttings root or rot.

**Softwood cuttings (May–June)** are the easiest and fastest method. The current season's growth is still pliable, the plant is in active growth mode, and roots typically appear in 4–6 weeks. This is the option to choose if you only want to do this once.

**Semi-hardwood cuttings (August–September)** root more slowly (8–12 weeks) but are sturdier. They handle heatwaves better than softwood and are useful if you missed the spring window. Look for stems that bend without snapping but feel firm at the base.

**Hardwood cuttings (October–November)** are taken from fully woody stems and rooted directly in a sheltered nursery bed over winter. Success rates are lower (around 30–40%) but you get a free supply of larger plants by spring.

Avoid taking cuttings while the parent plant is in full bloom — the stem's energy is going into flowers, not roots. If your only options are flowering shoots, **pinch off every flower bud** before sticking the cutting.

## How do you select and prepare the cutting?

The cutting itself matters more than any fancy technique.

**Step 1 — Pick the right shoot.** Look for **non-flowering side shoots** that are 8–10 cm long, pencil-thin, and pale green. Skip shoots with a flower spike on top — they are reproducing, not growing. Skip thick woody bases too; they root poorly.

**Step 2 — Take the cutting in the morning.** Stems are turgid (full of water) at dawn and rapidly lose moisture as the day heats up. A morning cutting wilts much less in the rooting tray.

**Step 3 — Make a clean cut.** Use a sharp, clean knife or secateurs and cut **just below a leaf node** at the bottom. Aim the cut at a slight angle — this exposes more cambium tissue (the layer where roots form) and stops the base from sitting flat on the substrate.

**Step 4 — Strip the lower two-thirds.** Run your finger and thumb down the stem to remove all leaves from the bottom 5–6 cm. Leave only **4–6 small leaves at the tip**. Each leaf you keep loses water; each one you remove is one less stress point.

**Step 5 — Wound the base (optional but worth it).** Lightly scrape **two thin vertical strips of bark** off opposite sides of the bottom 1 cm. The wounded tissue produces more callus, which produces more roots. This step alone can lift success rates from 60% to 85% on tricky cultivars.

**Step 6 — Apply rooting hormone.** Dip the bottom 1 cm in fresh **powdered rooting hormone (IBA — indole-3-butyric acid)** and **tap off the excess**. A dusty coating is enough; a clump of hormone actually inhibits rooting.

## What is the best rooting medium for lavender?

Lavender roots **hate** wet feet. The single most common failure mode is a too-rich, too-wet seed-starting mix that turns into a black sponge.

The mix you want is **50% horticultural perlite + 50% gritty seed-and-cutting compost**, or alternatively **two parts sharp sand to one part peat-free compost**. Both drain fast, hold just enough moisture, and stay aerated.

Fill a 9 cm pot or a half-tray, water thoroughly until it drains freely, and let the surface dry slightly before sticking cuttings.

**Use a pencil to make a 4–5 cm hole**, drop the cutting in (hormone-coated end down — pushing it in directly scrapes the hormone off), and **firm the mix** gently around it. Each cutting should stand on its own without leaning.

Space cuttings at least 4 cm apart so leaves don't touch — touching leaves stay damp and rot.

## Do lavender cuttings need a humidity dome?

Yes — but a **lazy** one.

Lavender hates the saturated air inside a closed propagator just as much as it hates wet soil. What it needs is **mild humidity plus free airflow**.

Practical setups that work:

- A **clear plastic bag** loosely tented over the pot, with the open end folded under the rim (not sealed). Lift it off for 10 minutes every other day.
- A **clear plastic bottle** with the bottom cut off, the cap **left off**, placed over the pot like a cloche. The open neck vents constantly.
- A **propagator with the vents fully open**. Closed vents will rot Mediterranean herbs almost every time.
- An **unheated cold frame** in dappled shade — almost ideal in mild climates from May onwards.

Place the whole setup in **bright indirect light**, never direct midday sun, ideally at 18–22°C. A north- or east-facing windowsill is perfect; a south-facing one cooks them.

Water from below — stand the pot in a shallow tray of water for 5 minutes once the surface is dry, then drain. Top-watering compacts the mix and washes the hormone off.

## How long do lavender cuttings take to root?

For softwood cuttings in late spring at 18–22°C: **4–6 weeks** to first roots, **8 weeks** to a transplant-ready root ball.

**Signs you have roots without disturbing the cutting:**

- New leaf growth at the tip — almost always means roots have formed.
- Resistance when you tug very gently — if the cutting holds firm, roots are anchoring it.
- White root tips visible at the bottom drainage hole.

**Avoid the temptation to dig them up to check.** Disturbed roots break and the cutting often dies. If 8 weeks have passed with no new growth and a gentle tug pulls the cutting straight out, that one has failed — bin it and move on.

Once you can see white roots through the drainage hole, **pot each cutting individually** into a 9 cm pot of free-draining peat-free compost mixed with 25% grit. Keep them in a sheltered spot for another 4–6 weeks before planting out, and **never feed in the first two months** — tender roots burn on fertiliser.

## How do you care for rooted cuttings before planting out?

Rooted cuttings are baby plants, not adult lavender. Rush this stage and you will lose the lot to the first cold snap or hot week.

1. **Pot up** into 9 cm pots once roots fill the cell. Use a gritty, peat-free mix; never garden soil straight from the bed.
2. **Pinch the tip** out at 8 cm tall to force two side shoots. Repeat once more at 12 cm. This is what turns a weedy stick into a bushy lavender.
3. **Water sparingly** — let the surface dry between waterings. Lavender prefers slightly thirsty over slightly wet.
4. **Harden off** for 1–2 weeks before planting out: an hour in dappled outdoor shade on day one, then build up to a full day in sun by day ten.
5. **Plant out the following spring**, after the last frost, into the sunniest, sharpest-drainage spot you have. See [best soil for lavender](/posts/best-soil-for-lavender-en/) for the exact mix that keeps roots alive long-term.
6. **First flowers come in year two.** Pinch off any year-one flower buds — you want roots and bushy growth, not a tired stem trying to set seed.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can you root lavender cuttings in water?

You can, but the success rate is lower than in a gritty mix and the roots that form are **water roots**, which are thin, brittle, and adapted to low oxygen. They struggle to convert to soil roots and many cuttings collapse a week after potting on. If you want to try, change the water every 2–3 days, keep it in bright indirect light, and pot up the moment roots reach 2–3 cm — do not wait for a full fluffy root ball.

### Which lavender varieties root most reliably from cuttings?

**English lavender** (*Lavandula angustifolia* — 'Munstead', 'Hidcote', 'Folgate') roots easily, often at 80%+ from softwood cuttings. **Lavandins** (*L. × intermedia* — 'Grosso', 'Provence') are nearly as easy. **French lavender** (*L. dentata*) and **Spanish lavender** (*L. stoechas*) root well in spring but are frost-tender and need overwintering indoors in cool climates.

### Do I have to use rooting hormone on lavender?

No, but it lifts your success rate from roughly 50–60% to 80–90% on softwood cuttings, and it speeds up rooting by about a week. A small tub of IBA powder lasts years if kept dry and dark. Honey is **not** a substitute — it has antimicrobial effects but does not contain auxins, the actual rooting compound.

### Can I take lavender cuttings from a supermarket lavender plant?

Yes, and this is one of the cheapest ways to build a hedge. Pick a healthy plant, water it well for two days at home, then take softwood cuttings from non-flowering side shoots. Be aware that supermarket plants are sometimes treated with growth regulators, which can delay rooting by a few weeks but rarely prevent it.

### How many cuttings do I need to make a lavender hedge?

For a 3 m hedge spaced at 30 cm apart, you need 10 plants — but plan to take **at least 20 cuttings**. Even with good technique, expect 70–80% to take, and you want spares to replace any that struggle in their first year. The same approach works when bulking up for [a cottage garden border](/posts/best-cottage-garden-flowers-en/) or [a pollinator strip for bees](/posts/flowers-for-bees-en/).

### Can I propagate lavender by layering instead?

Yes — and it is even easier than cuttings if the parent plant has flexible lower stems. Bend a low side branch to the ground in spring, scrape the bark on the underside where it touches the soil, pin it down with a tent peg, and cover the wounded section with 3 cm of gritty mix. By autumn it has rooted, and you can cut it free from the parent. Useful when you only need 2–3 new plants and don't fancy the propagator setup.
