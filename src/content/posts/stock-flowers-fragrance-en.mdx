## Quick Answer

For the strongest stock-flower fragrance, sow *Matthiola incana* indoors 8 weeks before the last frost, expose seedlings to a week of cool weather (5–10 °C) before transplanting, plant 25 cm apart in full sun and rich soil, and harvest spikes when the bottom third of flowers are open. Choose double-flowered cultivars from a "selectable" seed strain — the lighter green-leaved seedlings produce the doubles, which are nearly twice as fragrant as singles.

## Why are stock flowers so heavily fragrant compared with other spring flowers?

*Matthiola incana* — common stock or gillyflower — produces one of the most distinctive scents in temperate gardens: a clove and spice fragrance with a hint of vanilla that fills a small room from a single bunch. The scent is strongest at dusk and after rain, which is why old garden manuals call stocks an "evening flower" alongside nicotiana and night-scented stocks (*M. longipetala*).

Three factors make the fragrance so concentrated:

- **High essential-oil content.** Stock petals contain glycosylated benzaldehydes and other clove-related volatiles at higher concentrations than most spring annuals.
- **Released continuously through the day**, not in pulses. Unlike jasmine or honeysuckle (which release scent in evening surges), stock petals emit their volatiles at a steady rate from morning through to late evening.
- **Petal density**. A single double-flowered stock bloom has 30–40 packed petals, each contributing scent, instead of the 4–6 petals on most cruciferous flowers.

The key practical fact is that double-flowered stocks are roughly twice as fragrant as single-flowered stocks. Single-flowered plants set seed and produce 4-petalled flowers; double-flowered plants are sterile, produce no seed, and instead produce dense rosette-form flowers with vastly more fragrance per stem. Most modern stock seed is sold from "selectable" strains that let you tell which seedlings will produce doubles before you plant them out.

## How do you select the double-flowering seedlings?

This is the single skill that distinguishes mediocre stocks from spectacular ones. Selectable seed strains include 'Cinderella', 'Cheerful', 'Vintage', and 'Katz' — the seed packet should explicitly say "selectable" or "double-selectable" on the label.

Sow seed at the normal density. After the second true leaf forms (7–10 days from germination at 18 °C), examine each seedling carefully. Two leaf colours are visible:

- **Light yellow-green seedlings:** these are the double-flowered plants. Keep them.
- **Dark green seedlings:** these are the single-flowered plants. Discard them.

The colour difference is genuine and reliable. Light-yellow-green seedlings carry the homozygous mutation that produces the sterile double-flowering form, and they show the colour from very early development.

If you're growing from non-selectable seed, you'll get roughly 50 % singles and 50 % doubles. The display still works but the bed will lack the consistent intensity of a fully-double planting. For a small bed where every plant matters, paying for selectable seed and rogueing out the singles is well worth the effort.

## What's the right sowing window for the longest fragrance season?

Stocks grow best in cool weather and finish quickly when temperatures climb above 22 °C. Timing is therefore everything: sow too late and the plants flower briefly in heat, with thinner scent and a 2-week display. Sow at the right time and you get a 6-week scented display in cool spring weather.

The rule of thumb: sow indoors 8–10 weeks before your last expected frost.

| Climate | Sow indoors | Transplant outside | Flowering window |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild winter (UK, mild Mediterranean) | Late January | Mid-March | April – early June |
| Temperate (continental Europe) | Mid-February | Mid-April | May – mid-June |
| Cool winter (Northern Europe) | Early March | Early May | June – mid-July |
| Warm climate (subtropical) | November | January | February – early April |

Stock seeds germinate best at 18 °C and tolerate cooler temperatures during early growth. Sow into modules at this temperature, then once seedlings have two true leaves, move them to a cooler 10–15 °C location (a bright porch, an unheated greenhouse, or a north-facing windowsill). The cool growing-on period is what produces stocky, well-branched plants — too warm and they go leggy.

Two weeks before transplanting outdoors, expose the seedlings to a week of 5–10 °C nights. This "cold treatment" or vernalisation triggers the flowering hormone earlier and produces denser flower spikes. Skipping the cold period gives leafier, less productive plants with a shorter flowering window.

## What soil and light conditions does the plant need?

Stocks want full sun and rich, well-drained, neutral-to-slightly-alkaline soil (pH 6.5–7.5). Acid or peaty soils produce thin growth with weak fragrance.

Soil preparation:

- Dig in 5–8 cm of well-rotted manure or composted bark in late autumn.
- Add a handful of bone meal per square metre.
- Lightly lime if the soil pH is below 6.5.

Avoid freshly added high-nitrogen feeds (lawn fertiliser, fresh manure). Stocks fed with too much nitrogen produce all leaf and weak short flower spikes.

Spacing matters more than people expect. Stocks crowded at 15 cm apart produce shorter, thinner spikes; stocks at 25–30 cm apart produce the tall, dense spikes the cultivars are bred for. A bed for cutting wants 25 cm spacing minimum; a bed purely for fragrance can go to 30 cm.

For pots, use a 25 cm pot per 3 plants, in standard multipurpose compost mixed with 20 % grit. Pots dry quickly and you'll need to water every 2–3 days in spring.

## How do you maximise the fragrance once they're flowering?

Three things during the flowering period materially increase the scent intensity.

**1. Water deeply once a week, not lightly daily.** A deep watering forces roots down and produces stockier plants with more concentrated essential oils. Light surface watering keeps roots shallow and dilutes the scent.

**2. Avoid feeding during flowering.** The slow-release fertiliser at planting carries them through. Adding more feed pushes leaf growth at the expense of flowers.

**3. Cut spikes in the cool of the morning** for maximum vase fragrance. Stems harvested at midday have already lost some of their volatile compounds to the day's warmth; morning-harvested stems hold the scent for longer indoors.

The fragrance peaks when the bottom third of flowers on a spike are open, with the middle still in bud. At this stage the spike has the most balance between open-flower scent (strong, intense) and bud-released scent (sweet, subtler). Spikes harvested after most flowers have opened are still scented but past peak.

For evening fragrance specifically, plant a small group near a window or sitting area where you can enjoy the scent at dusk. ## Do stocks come back year after year?

Most modern stock cultivars are biennials grown as annuals. They flower once, set seed (or fail to, in the doubles), and decline. A tiny number of seedlings overwinter in mild climates and flower the following spring, but treating them as repeat performers is not reliable.

Two ways to keep the display going year-round:

**1. Successional sowing.** Sow a small batch every 2–3 weeks from January through March. Each batch flowers a couple of weeks after the previous one, extending the scent season from 4 weeks to 8–10 weeks.

**2. Combine with night-scented stock (*M. longipetala*).** Sow night-scented stock outdoors in March directly where you want it to flower. It is much smaller and less showy than *M. incana*, but the evening fragrance from August onward is among the heaviest in the garden.

For autumn-into-spring fragrance in mild climates, you can sow a final batch in August, overwinter the seedlings under cloches, and they flower in early spring with the strongest fragrance of the year. This is the technique commercial cut-flower growers use for early-spring stock crops.

## What goes wrong, and how do you fix it?

Five common problems and the practical fix for each.

**1. Lots of leaves, no flowers.**
Too much nitrogen or too warm during growing-on. Stop feeding, reduce growing-on temperature to 12–15 °C, and let the plant catch up.

**2. Spikes are short and scrawny.**
Crowded spacing or insufficient light. Thin to 25 cm apart and ensure 6+ hours direct sun.

**3. Yellow lower leaves.**
Drought stress or root rot, depending on whether soil is dry or wet. Check soil moisture; correct the underlying cause.

**4. Plants flower briefly and decline in heat.**
Sown too late. Note the date and sow earlier next year. Once heat hits, accept the season is ending and sow autumn-flowering replacement annuals.

**5. Flowers form but barely scent.**
Single-flowered seedlings rather than doubles. Next year, use a selectable strain and rogue out the dark-green single seedlings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are stock flowers the same as wallflowers?**
No. Wallflowers are *Erysimum cheiri*, a related Brassicaceae genus with similar growing requirements but different flower form and a different fragrance profile.

**Can I grow stocks in winter on a windowsill for fragrance?**
Not reliably. Stocks need cool nights to develop properly. They can be brought indoors briefly when in flower for fragrance, but they don't grow well as a permanent indoor plant.

**Why didn't my stock seeds germinate?**
Stock seeds need light and moisture but not deep burial. Sow on the surface of damp compost and press lightly; cover too deeply and germination drops sharply.

**Are stocks deer-resistant?**
Generally yes. Deer rarely browse Brassicaceae unless very hungry. Slugs are a bigger threat to young plants.

**How long do stock flowers last in a vase?**
About 5–8 days with daily water changes. The fragrance fades roughly 24 hours before the petals start to crumple.
