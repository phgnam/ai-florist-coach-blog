## Quick Answer

Limp, drooping African violet leaves are almost always overwatering, an exhausted compacted rootball, or a sudden cold draft from a winter window. Lift the pot first — if it is heavy and the soil sodden, you are watering too much; if the leaves are limp despite dry soil, the roots have died back and the plant cannot drink. Repot into fresh African-violet mix, water from below only, keep in stable warmth, and the plant rebounds within 2–3 weeks.

## Why are my African violet leaves drooping?

Drooping in *Saintpaulia ionantha* (the African violet) is essentially a hydraulics failure: water is not reaching the leaf cells in the right amount. Either too much water has rotted the feeder roots and the plant cannot draw any up, or too little has reached the roots, or the temperature has dropped low enough that root water uptake has stalled. Three causes account for almost every drooping case:

1. **Overwatering and root rot.** Soggy compost suffocates feeder roots, which then rot. The plant looks limp, but the soil is wet — a classic, counter-intuitive symptom. Pulling the plant out of its pot reveals dark, mushy roots and a sour smell.
2. **Severe underwatering / hydrophobic rootball.** Compacted old African-violet compost can repel water and force it down the side of the pot without wetting the rootball. Water "comes out the bottom" but the rootball is bone dry. Leaves droop just like with overwatering.
3. **Cold-draft shock.** A sudden drop below 13 °C — for example, a winter night with the heating off, or leaves touching cold window glass — temporarily paralyses water uptake. Leaves droop overnight and recover slowly once warmth returns.

A fourth, slower-developing cause is a **pot-bound, exhausted rootball.** African violets actually flower best in slightly small pots, but after 18–24 months in the same pot the rootball becomes one solid mass with depleted compost and circling roots, and the plant cannot draw enough water through the dense mat. Symptoms: chronic mild drooping, sparse flowers, generally lacklustre look. Repotting into fresh mix transforms the plant.

A fifth cause to consider is **overhead watering damage.** Cold tap water spilled onto leaves causes ring-shaped chlorotic patches and can permanently damage the petiole (leaf stalk), which then can no longer support the leaf — that single leaf droops while the rest are fine. Always water from below.

## How do you tell which kind of drooping you actually have?

A 2-minute diagnostic that pinpoints the cause:

**Step 1 — lift the pot.**

- Heavy and waterlogged → overwatering and probable root rot. Skip to the rescue routine in the next section.
- Surprisingly light → either underwatering or a long-pot-bound plant. Continue to step 2.

**Step 2 — push your finger 2 cm into the compost.**

- Bone dry, hard, and pulling away from the pot wall → hydrophobic rootball (the soil has lost its ability to absorb water).
- Slightly damp throughout → pot-bound exhausted compost.
- Dry on top but moist 2 cm down → not a watering problem; check temperature and drafts.

**Step 3 — slip the rootball out and examine.**

- Solid mat of yellow-tan roots filling the pot from edge to edge → pot-bound. Repot.
- Black or brown mushy roots, foul smell → root rot. Hard prune all rotted material, repot in fresh dry mix.
- Healthy white roots, plenty of compost, plant still droopy → cold-draft or sudden temperature shock. Move to a warmer steady spot.

**Step 4 — check the location.**

- Touching cold window glass on a winter night → cold damage.
- Within 1 m of a forced-air heating vent → desiccation droop. Move further from the vent.
- Recently moved from a propagation tray to a sunnier windowsill → light-shock droop, usually self-corrects in 5–7 days.

This diagnostic flow is similar to the diagnostic logic for [hibiscus bud drop](/posts/hibiscus-buds-dropping-en/) and other house-plant collapse symptoms — environmental cause first, then water mechanics, then disease.

## How do you rescue an African violet that is overwatered and root-rotting?

This is the most common cause and, if caught within a week of the first droop, has a 70–80 % survival rate.

**Step 1 — stop watering immediately.** Do not water again until you have unpotted and inspected the plant. Adding more water makes things worse fast.

**Step 2 — unpot the plant.**

1. Tilt the pot, support the leaf rosette in one hand, and gently squeeze the pot to release the rootball.
2. Brush off as much of the wet rotted compost as possible without breaking healthy white roots.
3. Inspect the roots carefully. Healthy roots are firm, white-to-tan, and slightly fibrous. Rotted roots are dark brown, mushy, slimy, and may smell sour.

**Step 3 — surgical root cleanup.**

1. Using a clean blade or scissors, cut every rotted root section off back to firm white tissue.
2. If the central stem (the "neck" beneath the leaf rosette) is also dark and soft for more than 1 cm, you have advanced rot — the rescue is harder. See the leaf-cutting propagation note below.
3. Dust all cut surfaces with cinnamon powder (a mild natural fungicide).

**Step 4 — repot in a smaller pot.**

- A plant that has lost half its roots needs a *smaller* pot than it had before — perhaps 9 cm rather than 12 cm. The rootball must fit snugly with about 1–2 cm of fresh compost around the edge.
- Use a dedicated African-violet compost or make your own (peat-free multi-purpose compost mixed 2:2:1 with perlite and coarse vermiculite).
- Plant so the leaf rosette sits just above the compost surface — burying the crown causes new rot.

**Step 5 — first watering.**

- Wait 48 hours after repotting. The cut surfaces need to seal first.
- Then bottom-water gently: stand the pot in a saucer of room-temperature water for 15 minutes, then drain.
- Wait another 5–7 days before the next watering. Bottom-water from now on, never from above.

**Step 6 — convalescent care.**

- Bright but indirect light. A north- or east-facing windowsill is ideal in cool-temperate climates.
- Steady warmth, 18–24 °C day and night. No drafts, no cold glass contact.
- No feeding for 4 weeks while the plant rebuilds roots.
- After 4 weeks, restart half-strength African-violet feed every 14 days.

**Step 7 — leaf-cutting insurance.**

If the central neck is severely rotted, the parent plant may not survive. Take 2–3 healthy outer leaves with petioles intact and root them as cuttings: insert each petiole 1 cm into a small pot of damp African-violet compost (or a glass of water, replacing it weekly). Roots form in 4–6 weeks, baby plantlets in 8–12 weeks. This kind of insurance propagation is similar in principle to taking [stem cuttings to overwinter geraniums](/posts/overwinter-geraniums-indoors-en/), and is a safety net every African-violet owner should know.

## What ongoing routine prevents the leaves from drooping again?

A drooping African violet recovers fully only if the underlying watering and environmental routine changes. The plant cannot read your good intentions; it just responds to inputs. Here is the steady-state routine that keeps a *Saintpaulia* turgid, flowering, and never drooping:

**Watering.**

- **Always bottom-water.** Stand the pot in 1–2 cm of room-temperature water in a saucer for 15–20 minutes, then drain. Never spill water onto the leaves or the central crown.
- **Frequency.** Every 5–10 days in summer, every 10–14 days in winter, depending on pot size and room humidity. Always check the top 1 cm of compost first — water only when it is dry to the touch.
- **Water quality.** Hard tap water deposits calcium on leaves and slowly raises soil pH. Use rainwater or filtered water if your tap is hard. Cold water shocks roots — always at room temperature.

**Light.**

- Bright but indirect. North- and east-facing windowsills are best in cool-temperate climates; south- and west-facing windowsills require sheer curtains in summer.
- 12–14 hours of light daily produces the most reliable flowering. In winter, a small LED grow light on a timer transforms a struggling violet into a steady bloomer.

**Temperature.**

- 18–24 °C day, 16–20 °C night.
- Avoid cold drafts and avoid contact with winter window glass.

**Feed.**

- A specialist African-violet feed (lower nitrogen, higher phosphorus and potassium) at half-strength every 14 days during active growth.
- No feed in the dimmest weeks of midwinter (mid-December to mid-January in cool-temperate climates).
- A general houseplant feed is acceptable but will produce more leaves and fewer flowers — switch to violet-specific feed if your plant is healthy but rarely flowers.

**Pot and compost.**

- Repot every 12–18 months into fresh African-violet compost. Same-size or one-size-larger pot.
- Avoid burying the crown.
- Do not use a deep pot — *Saintpaulia* roots are shallow and a 7–9 cm deep pot is plenty.

**Grooming.**

- Remove yellowing or damaged outer leaves at the base. Twist gently rather than cut to avoid leaving petiole stubs that rot.
- Deadhead spent flowers by twisting off at the base of the flower stalk.
- Once a year, clean dust off the leaves with a soft dry brush — never with water.

For more on combining cool-room care with steady winter flowering across multiple species, the same principles apply to the [overwintering routine for cyclamen](/posts/cyclamen-after-blooming-en/) and other shade-loving winter bloomers, and a similar clean-foliage approach helps with general indoor-flower garden hygiene.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will a drooping African violet recover, or should I just take cuttings and start over?**
If the central crown is still firm, the plant almost always recovers within 2–3 weeks of correct repotting and bottom-watering. Take 2–3 leaf cuttings as insurance regardless — they cost nothing and a single rooted leaf cutting becomes a flowering plant in 4–6 months. If the central crown is mushy, propagate from leaves and treat the parent plant as a write-off.

**Why do my African violet leaves droop only on one side of the rosette?**
Almost always uneven light. The leaves on the side facing the window get light, transpire, and stay turgid; the leaves on the dark side don't and gradually droop. Rotate the pot 90° every 7 days to balance the rosette. Also check that the dark-side leaves are not pressed against a cold wall.

**Are yellow leaves on an African violet the same problem as drooping leaves?**
Related but not identical. Persistent yellowing on lower leaves is often natural ageing or low-light shortfall; widespread yellowing across the whole rosette suggests overwatering and crown stress (the early stage of root rot, before drooping). Pale yellow new leaves with green veining is iron deficiency from hard tap water — switch to rainwater for a few weeks.

**Can I save an African violet that has lost most of its leaves to drooping and rot?**
Yes, if the central crown still has at least 3–4 healthy small leaves and the neck below the rosette is firm. Repot in a small pot of fresh compost, place under a clear plastic bag (without it touching the leaves) on a north-facing windowsill, and water sparingly from below. New leaf growth resumes within 4 weeks if the crown is alive.

**Should I mist African violets to fix drooping?**
No. Misting wets the velvet leaves and causes the same ring-shaped damage as overhead watering. African violets prefer 50–60 % room humidity, achieved with a pebble tray under (not touching) the pot, or a small humidifier in winter rooms.
