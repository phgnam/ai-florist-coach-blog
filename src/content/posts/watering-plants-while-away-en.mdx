## Quick Answer

For trips up to **3 days**, a heavy pre-trip soak and moving plants out of direct sun is usually enough. For **1 week**, capillary mats (a damp towel with one end in water) work for indoor plants. For **2 weeks**, wine-bottle drippers or sealed plastic-bag "humidity tents" cover most cases. For **a month or more**, you need an automated drip system, a self-watering reservoir pot, or a friend on a watering rota — there is no DIY substitute that lasts that long for thirsty plants.

## How long can plants really go without water?

A useful rule of thumb at room temperature in normal indoor light:

- **Succulents, cacti, snake plant, ZZ plant**: 4–6 weeks without intervention
- **Most houseplants (peace lily, pothos, ferns)**: 5–10 days from a thoroughly soaked starting point
- **Outdoor pots in shade**: 5–7 days
- **Outdoor pots in full sun**: 1–3 days
- **Cut flowers in a vase**: 4–7 days if conditioned and out of sun
- **Vegetable seedlings or freshly potted bedding**: 1–2 days

The numbers double in cool weather and halve in heatwaves. Plant size, pot size, soil type, and light all matter — but the order of magnitude tells you which holiday-watering strategy to use.

## What should I do the day before I leave?

The single most important step, and the one most people skip:

1. **Water every plant thoroughly until water runs out the bottom of the pot**
2. **Move all plants away from south-facing windows** to a darker, cooler interior spot
3. **Lower indoor air temperature** by drawing curtains and turning off heating
4. **Group plants together** to raise local humidity
5. **Trim any flowers, fruit, or buds you can spare** to reduce water demand

This single sequence often doubles the survival time of a houseplant collection during a 1-week trip. A south-window ficus that would dry out in 4 days lasts 8 days in a curtained interior corner.

## What works for 1 week away?

For indoor houseplants, the best DIY method is the **capillary mat**:

1. Place a folded old towel, microfibre cloth, or capillary mat on a kitchen draining board
2. Drape one end into a bowl, basin, or bathtub of water
3. Set plants on the towel (in their normal pots, drainage holes intact)
4. Water draws up through the towel into the pot bases by capillary action

Done correctly, this keeps plants moist for 5–10 days. The water level in the basin needs to last that long — a deep washing-up bowl filled to the brim works for most setups.

For outdoor pots:

- **Move pots into the shade** of a wall or tree
- **Group pots together** to shade each other
- **Mulch heavily** (5 cm of compost or bark on the soil surface)
- **Soak each pot until water runs out**

A grouped, mulched, shaded outdoor pot collection survives a week in average summer weather without any further intervention.

## What works for 2 weeks?

Two methods scale well to two weeks:

**Wine-bottle drippers** (also sold as "Aqua Globes"): a glass globe or upside-down wine bottle filled with water, neck inserted into the soil. Water releases as the soil dries. One 750 ml bottle releases roughly 100–200 ml per day depending on soil dryness. Match bottle size to pot size.

The trick to make wine-bottle drippers work reliably:

- Use bottles, not stems with narrow necks (stems clog with soil)
- Fill the bottle, place a finger over the neck, invert into pre-watered moist soil, then release
- Test the system 3–4 days before you leave to see how fast the bottle empties
- Use multiple bottles for any pot bigger than 30 cm diameter

**Sealed plastic-bag humidity tents**: water the plant, slide a clear plastic bag over the whole plant, and seal it loosely with a rubber band around the pot. The plant transpires water that condenses on the bag and drips back. Works brilliantly for ferns, peace lilies, and small leafy plants. Doesn't work for cacti or fuzzy-leaved plants (which rot in trapped humidity).

For outdoor pots, a basic **drip irrigation kit on a battery-powered timer** is well worth the investment for any garden you take 2-week holidays from. Kits start cheap, last several years, and free you from holiday anxiety.

## What works for 1 month or more?

For trips of a month or longer, DIY is unreliable. The two solid options:

**Automated drip irrigation**: a network of thin tubes with drippers at each pot, fed from a tap or large reservoir, controlled by a battery or mains timer. Set it up months ahead so you have time to dial in the dripper rates. Cost: about the same as a single nice flower delivery.

**A friend or neighbour with clear written instructions**: a once-a-week visit with a checklist is hard to beat. Provide:

- A labelled watering can
- A list of which plants need water and how much
- A photo of each plant in its normal location
- A spare key plus your contact details
- A small thank-you gift on return

Neither is glamorous. Both are far more reliable than any DIY contraption for trips longer than 2 weeks.

## What about cut flowers in a vase while I am away?

Most cut flowers in a vase last 5–7 days, so a 2-week trip is not the right time for fresh flowers. If you must:

- Choose long-lasting types (chrysanthemum, alstroemeria, carnation)
- Place out of direct sun
- Top up the vase fully before leaving
- Use a fresh sachet of flower food at full strength
- Accept that the flowers may be on the way out by the time you return

A better holiday-friendly choice is a flowering houseplant (peace lily, anthurium, moth orchid) which actually benefits from the pre-trip routine of a single thorough watering and a cool corner.

## What about garden borders, not just pots?

Established garden plants in the ground rarely need holiday irrigation in temperate climates. Their roots reach far enough that 2–3 weeks of average summer weather is no problem. Exceptions:

- **Newly planted shrubs and perennials** (under 2 years old) — water deeply before leaving
- **Vegetable gardens in full production** — set up a soaker hose on a timer
- **Container plants on patios** — treat as outdoor pots above

## FAQs

### How effective is a wine-bottle dripper, really?

For pots in the 15–25 cm range, a 750 ml bottle typically lasts 5–10 days depending on soil and weather. For larger pots, you need either a bigger reservoir or multiple bottles. Test 3–4 days before leaving to see how your specific setup performs.

### Will my plants survive if I just water them once before a 2-week trip?

Some will, most won't. Succulents, snake plant, ZZ plant, and pothos can manage 2 weeks from a thorough soak in cool conditions. Anything thirstier (ferns, calathea, peace lily on a sunny day, most flowering plants) will be wilted or worse by day 10.

### Can I put plants in the bathtub with a few inches of water?

Yes, but only briefly. For trips up to 5 days this works for ferns and peace lilies. Beyond that, plants sitting in standing water rot at the roots. Use the capillary-mat method instead — it gives water access without submerging the pot.

### What about smart plant sensors and automatic devices?

Quality automatic drip systems work brilliantly. Cheap "smart" sensors that just monitor moisture without dispensing water are useless when you are away — they tell you about a problem you can't fix from another country.

### Will my houseplants forgive me for one bad holiday?

Usually yes. Most houseplants drop a few leaves after a stressful watering gap and recover within 4–6 weeks of normal care. Catastrophic failure (whole plant dies) usually means more than 3 weeks without water, or sustained heat stress on top of dryness.

### Is it worth installing a fully automatic system if I only travel once a year?

Probably not for a once-a-year holiday. The break-even is roughly 2–3 holidays a year, or any home with significant outdoor pot collections, or older gardeners who travel for extended periods.
