## Quick Answer

Begonia leaves turn red when the plant gets stressed — usually **too much direct sun**, a **cold draft**, **sudden temperature swings**, or **dry root stress**. The red pigment is anthocyanin, a sunscreen the plant produces when its chlorophyll is breaking down. Move the begonia to bright indirect light, away from cold windows or AC vents, water on a steady rhythm, and the new leaves grow back green within 3–4 weeks.

## What does a healthy begonia leaf actually look like?

Most popular indoor begonias — Rex, Angel-wing, Tuberous, Wax, and Dragon-wing types — have leaves that range from deep green through bronze and silver, sometimes with a red **back** to the leaf as a normal feature. The clue that something is wrong is when previously green leaves shift to red **on the upper surface**, the new growth comes in red instead of green, or the red is patchy and centred on the parts of the plant exposed to a window.

Compare a leaf in the affected area to one in the centre of the plant, where light is filtered. If the centre leaves are green and the outer leaves are red, that is almost always a light or temperature problem rather than disease.

## Is too much sun the most common cause?

Yes. Begonias evolved as understory plants in tropical forests — their natural light is bright but filtered through a canopy. Direct sun on the upper surface of the leaf damages chlorophyll faster than the plant can rebuild it, so the leaf produces anthocyanin (a red-purple pigment) as a sunscreen.

Symptoms that point to sunburn:

- Red colour appears mostly on the side of the plant facing the window
- The reddest leaves are on top of the plant, not in the shade of other leaves
- Some leaves are also showing **bleached** patches (cream or white) before they turn red — these are the badly burnt spots
- The colour shift happened within 1–2 weeks of moving the plant or after a season change

The fix:

- Pull the plant **back from the window by 60–100 cm**, or hang a sheer curtain
- For south- or west-facing windows in summer, indirect light is enough; the plant does not need direct beams
- Rotate the pot a quarter-turn each week so growth stays even
- New leaves that emerge after the move come in green; do **not** rip off red leaves — they still photosynthesise

## Could a cold draft or AC vent be causing it?

Yes — and this one catches people in summer just as often as in winter. Begonias are tropical and dislike sudden cold. A persistent cool draft from a single-glazed window in winter, an air-conditioning vent blowing onto the plant in summer, or a refrigerator that opens and closes near the pot all stress the leaves. Anthocyanin is also produced as a cold-protection pigment.

Symptoms that point to cold stress rather than sun:

- Red leaves are on the side of the plant facing the AC, fridge, or cold window
- The colour deepens overnight or after a particularly cold night
- Some leaves curl downwards or feel slightly limp before they turn red
- The room sits below 16 °C (60 °F) at any point in 24 hours

The fix is mechanical: move the plant 1–2 metres away from the cold source, or redirect the AC airflow. Most begonias are happy at 18–24 °C with no draft.

## Why does watering go wrong with begonias?

Begonias have small, fibrous root systems that hate two extremes:

- **Bone dry**: the leaves go limp, then redden as the plant pulls chlorophyll out of older leaves to save energy
- **Soggy**: roots rot, the plant cannot take up water, and leaves redden as if drought-stressed even though the soil is wet

Diagnose by sticking a finger 2 cm into the soil. If it is dry, water until liquid drains from the bottom and discard the saucer water. If it is wet but the leaves look stressed, wait at least a week before watering again and check the drainage.

Most indoor begonias do best with the **soak-and-dry** rhythm: water thoroughly, let the top 2 cm dry out, water again. The frequency depends on the pot, the season, and the room — but the rhythm stays the same.

## Are nutrient or pH problems behind it?

Sometimes — but they are a slower cause and rarely show up first. A begonia in old, exhausted potting mix with no recent feed can produce small, reddish leaves because phosphorus uptake is reduced. The fix is a half-strength balanced liquid fertiliser every 2–3 weeks during the growing season, or a fresh repot into peat-free houseplant compost.

A pH that is too far from slightly acidic (pH 5.5–6.5) also locks out micronutrients like iron and magnesium, which can shift leaf colour. Tap water in hard-water areas can creep the pH upwards over time. Switching to filtered or rainwater for a month is a cheap way to test whether water chemistry is the issue.

## Is it ever just normal colouring?

Yes. Several begonia varieties are genetically programmed to flush red:

- **Rex begonias** in cultivars like 'Escargot', 'Fireworks', or 'Red Robin' have permanent silver, pink, or red zones
- **Tuberous begonias** can throw red leaves naturally during flowering
- **Bronze-leafed wax begonias** (Begonia semperflorens 'Cocktail' series) are red-bronze on top by design

If the colour pattern matches images of a named cultivar and the plant is otherwise growing well, no fix is needed. The clue is consistency: a Rex begonia's red zones appear on every new leaf and stay put, while a stressed plant's redness shifts over weeks as the plant moves through different conditions.

## What about pests and disease?

Far less common, but worth a 30-second check:

- **Spider mites** cause stippled bronzing that can look reddish from a distance — flip a leaf and look for fine webbing or salt-and-pepper specks
- **Begonia mildew** causes white powdery patches that later brown — not red, but easy to confuse if the plant is stressed by both at once
- **Botrytis** on dying flowers can spread to leaves; remove spent blooms regularly

If a hand lens shows mites, rinse the leaves with lukewarm water and wipe with a damp cloth, then keep humidity higher (a pebble tray or grouping plants together helps).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I cut off the red leaves?

No. Red leaves still photosynthesise, and removing them stresses the plant further. Once you fix the cause, leave the existing red leaves alone and let new green growth come in. Only remove a leaf if it is fully dried, mushy, or covered in disease.

### How long until the new growth comes back green?

If the cause is light or draft and you fix it, green new growth usually appears in 3–4 weeks during the growing season, longer in winter. The plant grows from the centre, so the freshest leaves emerge first and tell you the trend.

### Can I just move my begonia outside in summer?

A begonia can move outside to a shaded patio above 18 °C, but acclimatise it over 7–10 days, starting in deep shade and gradually adding morning light. Skipping acclimatisation is the single most common cause of red, scorched leaves on outdoor begonias.

### Why are only the outer leaves red and not the centre?

Because the outer leaves are the ones receiving the most light or draft. The centre leaves are shaded by the rest of the plant. This pattern is one of the strongest tells for "environmental stress" rather than "disease".

### Does too much fertiliser cause red leaves?

Over-fertilising mainly causes **leaf-tip burn** (brown crispy edges), not red leaves. But salt buildup in the soil from heavy feeding can stress roots, and stressed roots lead to redder foliage. If you suspect this, flush the pot with plain water until 2–3 pot volumes have drained through, then resume feeding at half strength.

### My Rex begonia has always had red on the leaves — should I worry?

No. Rex begonias are bred for dramatic colour and many cultivars are red, pink, or silver by design. The check is whether the colour is consistent with the cultivar's catalogue photo and whether the plant is putting out healthy new leaves at the centre.
