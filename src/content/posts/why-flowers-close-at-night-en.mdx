Flowers that close at night exhibit **nyctinasty** — a reversible movement driven by changes in light and temperature. The petals open and close because cells on the inner and outer surfaces of the petal base grow or swell at different rates, bending the petal inward (closing) when light fades and outward (opening) when it returns.

## What causes flowers to open and close daily?

The mechanism is elegant. At the base of each petal sits a region called the **pulvinus** (or a functionally similar zone in petals). Cells on the inner face and outer face of this zone respond differently to light and temperature:

- **During the day**: Light and warmth cause cells on the **inner surface** to grow or absorb water faster than outer cells. This differential pushes petals outward — the flower opens.
- **At night**: Darkness and cooling reverse the process. Outer cells expand relative to inner cells, pulling petals inward — the flower closes.

This isn't muscle movement. It's differential growth and turgor pressure changes — essentially hydraulics powered by water moving in and out of cells in response to environmental cues.

The trigger is primarily **light intensity**, with temperature as a secondary driver. You can demonstrate this by placing a tulip in a dark room during the day — it will close. Bring it back into bright light and it reopens within 30–60 minutes.

## Which flowers close at night?

Many common garden and wild flowers show pronounced nyctinasty:

- **Tulips** — among the most dramatic closers. A fully closed tulip looks like a completely different flower than an open one.
- **Daisies** (*Bellis perennis*) — close tightly each evening, which is actually how they got their name: "day's eye" → daisy
- **California poppies** — close on cloudy days as well as at night
- **Dandelions** — close in the evening and in rain
- **Crocus** — temperature-sensitive; close when it cools down
- **Morning glories** — open at dawn and close by midday (not a night response, but a time-of-day cycle)
- **Gazania** — only fully open in bright direct sunlight; close in shade, cloud cover, and at night
- **Lotus** — close at night and reopen at dawn, famously described in ancient Egyptian and Hindu texts

## Why is closing at night an evolutionary advantage?

Several hypotheses explain why this behaviour evolved:

**Pollen protection**: Open flowers expose pollen and nectar to dew, rain, and nighttime humidity. Wet pollen doesn't transfer well. Closing protects reproductive structures and keeps pollen dry for morning pollinators.

**Pollinator targeting**: Most nyctinastic flowers are pollinated by daytime insects (bees, butterflies). Closing at night prevents nocturnal visitors (beetles, moths) that might consume pollen without effectively pollinating, saving resources for productive pollinators.

**Temperature regulation**: Closed petals create a microclimate that insulates reproductive organs from cold night temperatures. This is especially important for early-spring flowers (crocus, tulips) that bloom when nights are still frosty.

**Predation defence**: Open flowers attract herbivores and pollen thieves. Closing reduces visibility and accessibility to nighttime foragers.

## Are there flowers that do the opposite — open at night?

Yes, and they're pollinated by **nocturnal visitors** — moths, bats, and night-flying beetles:

- **Moonflower** (*Ipomoea alba*) — large white blooms that unfurl at dusk and close by mid-morning
- **Night-blooming jasmine** (*Cestrum nocturnum*) — intensely fragrant at night, nearly scentless during the day
- **Evening primrose** (*Oenothera*) — yellow flowers that open in late afternoon
- **Queen of the night** (*Epiphyllum oxypetalum*) — cactus flower that blooms for one night only, producing an extraordinary fragrance
- **Night-blooming cereus** — another cactus with dramatic once-a-year nocturnal blooms

These night-bloomers are typically **white or pale-coloured** (visible in moonlight) and **heavily scented** (moths navigate by smell more than sight).

## Can you prevent cut flowers from closing at night?

Partially. Since the response is driven by light and temperature:

- **Keep cut flowers in a lit room** — ambient room lighting may be enough to keep some flowers partially open
- **Maintain warmth** — cooler temperatures accelerate closing. A warm room slows the response
- **Choose varieties that close less** — double tulips and fully-double ranunculus close less dramatically than single forms because the dense petal layers resist movement

However, you can't completely override nyctinasty in cut flowers. It's a fundamental cellular response, not something you can disable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do all flowers close at night?**
No. Many flowers stay open 24/7 — roses, carnations, lilies, orchids, and most tropical flowers don't close at night. Nyctinasty is common but far from universal.

**Why do my tulips open so wide they flatten out?**
Warmth causes tulip petals to open progressively wider over their vase life. This is the same nyctinastic mechanism in overdrive — indoor heating keeps them in "open mode." To slow it down, keep tulips in a cooler room (15–18°C).

**Do flowers close because they're dying?**
Not necessarily. Nyctinastic closing is a daily rhythm — the flower reopens each morning. Permanent closing (petals folding inward and not reopening) signals the end of the flower's life, which is a different process driven by ethylene gas and senescence.
