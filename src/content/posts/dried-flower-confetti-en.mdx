## Quick Answer

**Real dried flower confetti** — undyed dried delphinium, rose, or hydrangea petals — is fully biodegradable, breaks down in 2–6 weeks outdoors, and is allowed at most wedding venues including National Trust sites and most churches. **"Eco confetti" with synthetic dyes, glitter, or shaped paper bases is often not** truly compostable. Always confirm with the venue first; many ban anything that isn't 100% real petals.

## Why is dried flower confetti the dominant wedding confetti now?

Three things drove the shift away from paper confetti and rice/grain confetti over the last 15 years:

**Venue rules.** The National Trust, English Heritage, most cathedrals, and a growing number of country-house wedding venues banned paper confetti during the 2000s and 2010s because clean-up was difficult, paper got into ponds and lakes, and dyed paper stained stone steps. Many venues now permit **only natural petal confetti** — and some are stricter and require it to be undyed.

**Photography.** Real petal confetti is photographed beautifully — the petals fall slowly, catch the light, and look painterly in motion. Paper confetti looks like litter in photographs and is rarely flattering.

**Sustainability culture.** Plastic confetti, biodegradable plastic confetti (which often isn't actually biodegradable in cool damp conditions), glitter, and dyed paper all became culturally unacceptable for many couples by around 2018–2020. Real flower petals are the cleanest sustainability story.

The reliable petals for wedding confetti, ranked by visual impact and biodegradability:

**Top tier**
- **Delphinium petals** — small, light, slow-falling; the standout confetti petal. Available in vivid blues, lilac, white, and pink. Most "designer petal confetti" mixes are dominated by delphinium.
- **Rose petals** — luxurious-looking, fall fast; mix with lighter petals for the right falling speed.
- **Cornflower petals** — bright blue, very small and light, photograph as little dots of intense colour.
- **Hydrangea petals** — soft pink, blue, or cream; large enough to read at a distance.

**Acceptable but with trade-offs**
- **Lavender** — fragrant, beautiful, but very small; visible only up close.
- **Statice** — papery and long-lasting, but small and not as graceful.
- **Marigold petals** — strong yellow and orange; biodegradable but often too heavy.

**Avoid for confetti**
- **Heavy waxy petals** (peony, magnolia) — too heavy, fall fast and look like clumps.
- **Coated or treated florist petals** — often have anti-fade or longevity treatments that aren't biodegradable.
- **Whole small flowers** rather than petals — fall fast and look chunky.

A good wedding-confetti mix is roughly **60% delphinium petals, 25% rose petals, 15% accent (cornflower, hydrangea, marigold)**. Buy in bulk from a dried-flower wedding supplier; expect to use **roughly 1 small handful per guest** for a satisfying confetti shower.

## Is "eco confetti" actually compostable?

This is the key question, and the answer is: **read the label very carefully.**

Truly biodegradable / compostable dried flower confetti is:

1. **Real flower petals** — not paper or rice paper or any synthetic substitute.
2. **Undyed or naturally dyed** — vivid pinks, deep purples, and saturated colours sometimes indicate synthetic dyes that linger in the soil.
3. **No additives** — no glitter, no metallic flake, no scent-coating, no preservative spray.

Many products sold as "eco confetti" or "biodegradable confetti" are:

- **Paper confetti shaped like flowers**, marketed alongside real petals — looks similar, isn't real flowers.
- **Coloured rice paper hearts and shapes** — biodegrades but slowly, and often dyed with non-organic colour.
- **Real petals dyed with synthetic dyes** for vivid colour — biodegradable as petals, but the dye may not be.
- **Petals coated with preservative** — adds shelf life but reduces compostability.

The cleanest test: a confetti that is **100% labeled as a single flower species, undyed, with no other ingredients** (e.g. "100% dried delphinium petals, naturally air-dried, no additives") is genuinely compostable. Anything else needs the supplier asked.

## Are real dried flower petals allowed at all wedding venues?

Most venues allow real petal confetti, but rules vary widely. Three common venue policies:

**1. Real petals allowed without restriction.** Most country-house venues, garden venues, beach venues, and modern wedding venues. The standard policy.

**2. Real petals allowed in designated areas only.** Many churches, cathedrals, and historic-building venues. The confetti shower happens at a specific spot (often the church door or steps outside) so clean-up is contained.

**3. No confetti of any kind.** Some indoor museum venues, conservation buildings, and venues with delicate stone or plaster floors. Increasingly rare for newer venues.

**Always ask in writing.** Even venues that allow real petals often ban dyed petals, shaped paper confetti, and anything with glitter or metallic content. A venue that allows "biodegradable confetti" may interpret that more strictly than you do — confirm what they specifically permit.

## How do I make dried flower confetti at home?

Three reliable home methods:

**Garden petal collection (slow but free).** Collect rose, delphinium, hydrangea, and cornflower petals from your garden as flowers go over each summer. Spread on a tray in a warm dry room, turn daily. After 1–2 weeks they will be papery dry. Store in airtight glass jars in a dark cupboard. **You'll need to start collecting 3–6 months before the wedding** to gather enough.

**Microwave drying (fast, smaller volume).** Lay petals between paper towels and microwave at 50% power in 60-second bursts, checking between each burst. Total drying time: 2–4 minutes per batch. Less colour preservation than air drying but works for fresh-cut flowers a few weeks before the wedding.

**Bought in bulk (easiest).** Wedding-confetti suppliers sell pre-mixed dried petal confetti in bulk packs (typically £3–6 per generous handful at retail; cheaper in 1–5 kg boxes for weddings). Suppliers in the UK, EU, and US specialise in this and ship to wedding parties. The standard order is **1 handful per guest plus 20–30% extra**.

For a 100-guest wedding, expect to spend **£100–250** on real petal confetti — comparable to one wedding centrepiece. A DIY garden-collected confetti can be done at near-zero cost if you have a flower garden and 6 months' notice.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will real dried petal confetti stain stone or fabric?**
Undyed dried petals don't stain stone, brick, gravel, or grass at all. Synthetic-dyed dried petals can leave faint marks if they get wet on light fabrics or pale stone. Test before throwing — drop a few wet petals onto a similar surface and check after an hour.

**How long do dried petals stay confetti-throwable in storage?**
Properly air-dried petals stored in airtight glass jars in a cool dark cupboard remain throwable for 12–18 months. After that they become brittle and dust-like. Do a smell test before the wedding — petals that smell musty have rehydrated and need to be re-dried for 24 hours in a warm dry room.

**Will the petals decompose in the same colours as fresh petals?**
The dried colour is the colour they will appear when thrown. Once they hit grass and absorb moisture, dried petals revert to a more muted appearance and break down within 2–6 weeks (faster in damp conditions, slower in dry).

**Is rose-petal confetti safer than mixed petal confetti for guests?**
Most wedding-confetti petals are non-toxic to humans and pets. The few exceptions: avoid foxglove, lily, and other genuinely toxic flowers in confetti mixes. Reputable confetti suppliers exclude these by default.

**Can guests bring their own confetti?**
Yes, but the venue's rules apply. If the venue restricts confetti, guests' homemade confetti may also be restricted. The cleanest practice: provide single-flower-petal confetti yourself in small paper cones at the ceremony, so all guests use compliant confetti.

**Are there alternatives to petal confetti for indoor venues?**
Yes — bubble wands, sparklers (in suitable venues), and dried lavender on its own (less visually dramatic but venue-friendly) are common alternatives. Some couples skip confetti entirely and have a "first-look photo line" of guests applauding instead.
