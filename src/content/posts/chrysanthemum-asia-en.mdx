## Quick Answer

The chrysanthemum, originally cultivated in China around the 15th century BCE, is the imperial flower of Japan — the imperial seal is a 16-petalled yellow chrysanthemum — and one of the "Four Gentlemen" of classical Chinese painting. It symbolises long life, nobility, and autumn in East Asia. In some European cultures it is associated with funerals; in East Asia it is a symbol of joy and longevity.

## What is the chrysanthemum and where did it originate?

The chrysanthemum (*Chrysanthemum* genus, family Asteraceae) is one of the most heavily cultivated flowers on earth — there are over 20,000 named cultivars and the genus has been domesticated longer than almost any other ornamental flower.

The plant is native to East Asia and northeastern Europe. Documented Chinese cultivation begins around the 15th century BCE, and by the 8th century CE the chrysanthemum had reached Japan, where it became immediately associated with the imperial court. From Japan it spread to Korea (where it is *gukhwa*), then to Europe via Dutch traders in the 17th century.

The flower most consumers picture as "chrysanthemum" — the dense, ball-shaped autumn supermarket bloom — is one specific cultivar group. The genus actually contains daisies, spider mums, pompons, spoon mums, anemone-form mums, and quill-petalled mums, all distinct in shape.

## Why is the chrysanthemum Japan's imperial flower?

The chrysanthemum reached Japan in the Nara period (710–794 CE) and was immediately adopted by the imperial court — reportedly because the 9-day Chrysanthemum Festival (*Chōyō no Sekku*, the ninth day of the ninth month) was already established in Tang-dynasty Chinese court calendars and the Japanese emperor's court adopted the same observance.

The **Imperial Seal of Japan** (*Kikumon*, "chrysanthemum crest") is a stylised 16-petalled yellow chrysanthemum and has been the official emblem of the Japanese imperial family since the 12th century. It appears on Japanese passports, embassies, and the Throne (formally called the Chrysanthemum Throne, *Kōi*). Members of the imperial family are not allowed to use any other flower as a personal seal.

Outside the imperial context, the chrysanthemum still carries connotations of:

- **Long life.** Drinking chrysanthemum-petal-infused sake on *Chōyō no Sekku* was — and in some households still is — a wish for longevity.
- **Nobility.** The flower's late-autumn bloom, after most other flowers have finished, codes as "lonely virtue", a much-favoured Confucian theme.
- **Joy.** In modern Japanese flower-giving, white chrysanthemums are restricted to funerals; pink, yellow, and red chrysanthemums are entirely positive gifts associated with longevity and joy.

## What does the chrysanthemum mean in China?

In China, the chrysanthemum (*júhuā*) is one of the **Four Gentlemen** (*sì jūnzǐ*) of classical Chinese painting, alongside the orchid, bamboo, and plum blossom. Together they represent the four seasons (the chrysanthemum is autumn) and the four virtues a cultivated person should embody — the chrysanthemum specifically representing the scholar's withdrawal from worldly affairs.

The cultural prototype for this association is the 4th-century poet Tao Yuanming, who famously resigned from a government post to grow chrysanthemums at his rural retreat. His couplet *"采菊东篱下，悠然见南山"* — "Picking chrysanthemums by the eastern fence, idly I see the southern mountains" — is one of the most quoted lines in classical Chinese poetry.

The chrysanthemum also appears in the **Double Ninth Festival** (*Chongyang*, the same date as Japan's *Chōyō*), when families climb high places, drink chrysanthemum wine, and visit the graves of ancestors. The flower's autumn association ties cleanly to the festival's theme of remembrance.

## What are the main chrysanthemum varieties florists use?

Chrysanthemums are usually grouped by flower form. A florist working in 2026 will encounter most often:

| Form | Characteristic |
|---|---|
| **Pompon** | Small, dense, ball-shaped — the supermarket "mum". |
| **Spider** | Long, fine, curling petals; dramatic and architectural. |
| **Decorative** | Large flat flower with overlapping flat petals; the classic "show mum". |
| **Spoon** | Petals tipped with a small concave "spoon" shape. |
| **Anemone** | Single ring of petals around a raised central cushion. |
| **Daisy** | Single ring of petals around a flat yellow disc — looks like a true daisy. |

Spider mums are the form most strongly associated with traditional Japanese arrangement (especially ikebana — see the existing [ikebana guide](/posts/ikebana-guide-en/)) because their long curling lines work beautifully in linear compositions.

## How do you care for chrysanthemums in a vase?

Chrysanthemum is one of the longest-lasting cut flowers on earth — a properly handled stem will give 14–21 days in a vase, comparable to carnation and roughly double the life of a rose. The full vase-life table is in [How long do cut flowers last in a vase?](/posts/cut-flower-vase-life-en/).

Five small habits maximise vase life:

1. **Strip every leaf below the waterline.** Submerged chrysanthemum foliage rots fast and accelerates bacterial growth in the water.
2. **Smash or split the woody base of the stem.** Chrysanthemum stems are slightly woody — a clean diagonal cut plus a 1–2 cm vertical split increases water uptake.
3. **Use cool water and the flower-food sachet at the recommended dose.** Chrysanthemums are heavy drinkers; an under-dosed sachet feeds bacteria without killing them.
4. **Keep them away from ripening fruit.** Chrysanthemums are mildly ethylene-sensitive and will close up faster near apples, bananas, or pears.
5. **Refresh the water every 2–3 days.** The single most-effective vase-life intervention for any cut flower.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why is the chrysanthemum the imperial flower of Japan?**
The chrysanthemum was adopted by the Japanese imperial court in the Nara period (710–794 CE), following the Chinese Tang-dynasty practice of celebrating the 9-day Chrysanthemum Festival. The Imperial Seal of Japan, a 16-petalled yellow chrysanthemum, has been the official emblem of the imperial family since the 12th century.

**Is it bad luck to give chrysanthemums?**
It depends on the country and the colour. In Japan, white chrysanthemums are restricted to funerals; pink, yellow, and red chrysanthemums are positive gifts. In France, Italy, Belgium, and parts of Eastern Europe, all chrysanthemums are associated with funerals and graves. In the US, UK, China, and Korea, chrysanthemums are entirely positive flowers with no funereal association.

**What is the difference between a chrysanthemum and a daisy?**
Both belong to the Asteraceae family, but a chrysanthemum is specifically a member of the *Chrysanthemum* genus and typically has many overlapping rows of ray florets that make a dense ball or layered flower. A "daisy" — usually *Bellis perennis* — has a single ring of ray florets around a clearly visible yellow disc. Daisy-form chrysanthemums exist and blur the line.

**How long do chrysanthemums last in a vase?**
14–21 days with basic care — among the longest-lasting cut flowers commonly available. Carnations are the only common florist's flower that reliably matches them.

**Are chrysanthemums safe for pets?**
**No.** Chrysanthemums are mildly toxic to cats, dogs, and horses (vomiting, drooling, ataxia, dermatitis are typical signs). The flowers contain pyrethrins — the same compounds used in flea treatments and pet-safe insecticides. Most pets recover from a small ingestion but the flowers should be kept out of paw and mouth range. See the full reference in [Which flowers are safe for cats and dogs?](/posts/pet-safe-flowers-en/).
