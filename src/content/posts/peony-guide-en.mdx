The peony occupies a singular position in the world of flowers. No bloom is more anticipated, more instantly recognisable, or more photographed. In May and June, when the first fat buds crack open into impossible fullness, florists report their highest sales of the year. Brides plan their wedding dates around peony season. And then, almost as quickly as it came, it's over — and the wait for next year begins.

This scarcity is part of the peony's power. But the flower itself — its form, fragrance, history, and cultural weight — would make it extraordinary even if it bloomed all year.

## Types of peonies

There are three main types of peony that gardeners and florists work with:

**Herbaceous peonies** (*Paeonia lactiflora*) are the classic garden peony — they die back to the ground each autumn and re-emerge in spring. They're the most widely grown type, producing the large, globe-shaped blooms in white, pink, red, and cream that dominate florist markets in May and June. They can live for 50–100 years in the right conditions. Once established, they're among the most reliable and long-lived of all perennials.

**Tree peonies** (*Paeonia suffruticosa*) are woody shrubs that retain their structure year-round. They bloom slightly earlier than herbaceous peonies, often in April, and their flowers — which can reach 30cm across — are even more spectacular. They're slower to establish and more expensive, but a mature tree peony in full bloom is one of the most dramatic sights in any garden.

**Intersectional (Itoh) peonies** are a hybrid between tree and herbaceous types, combining the large blooms and extended colour range of tree peonies with the reliable die-back and hardiness of herbaceous peonies. Developed by Japanese breeder Toichi Itoh in 1948, they're increasingly popular in modern gardens.

## Flower forms

Within these types, peonies come in several distinct flower forms:

**Single** — one or two rows of petals around a prominent boss of golden stamens. Elegant and simple; best for pollinators.

**Anemone** — cup-shaped outer petals surrounding a dense cluster of narrow, petal-like stamens (staminodes). 

**Japanese** — similar to anemone form with large outer petals and a central mass of narrow staminodes, often in a contrasting colour.

**Semi-double** — multiple rows of outer petals with visible stamens between. A lovely middle ground.

**Bomb double** — a globe of petals, the outer ones large and cupped, the inner ones smaller and tightly packed. The classic "full" peony of florist arrangements.

**Full double** — every stamen has converted to a petal, creating a flower that is pure, dense, layered petals with no visible centre. Spectacular but not useful to pollinators.

## Growing peonies

Peonies are not difficult to grow, but they have a few firm requirements:

**Full sun**: Peonies need a minimum of 6 hours of direct sun daily. In shade they grow but rarely flower well.

**Excellent drainage**: The most common cause of peony failure is poorly drained, waterlogged soil. Peonies tolerate drought far better than wet feet. In heavy clay soils, raise the bed or add significant grit and organic matter.

**The planting depth rule**: This is the single most important rule in peony growing, and failure to follow it explains why many new plants never flower. When planting a bare-root peony, the **"eyes" (red growing tips) must be positioned no more than 2–5cm below the soil surface**. Planted deeper, the plant will produce beautiful lush foliage and never bloom. In warmer climates, plant even shallower.

**Patience**: Do not expect significant flowering in the first 1–2 years after planting. Peonies direct their energy into establishing a root system before investing in bloom. The reward comes from year 3 onwards, and a well-planted peony will outlive you.

**The ants myth**: Many new peony growers panic when they see ants crawling over the buds. The ants are after the sugary nectar secreted by the buds and do no harm whatsoever to the flower. They do not need to be present for the buds to open.

## Cut flower care

Peonies as cut flowers have a quirk that experienced florists use to their advantage: **buds cut at the right stage can be held refrigerated for weeks and opened on demand**.

For immediate use, cut when the bud is at the "soft marshmallow" stage — the bud has swollen, feels soft when gently squeezed, and shows colour, but hasn't begun to open. These will open within 24–48 hours at room temperature.

For storing and shipping, peonies are cut at the "hard bud" stage — the buds are firm, show colour, but have not softened. Wrapped in paper, they can be refrigerated (not frozen) at 1–4°C for up to 6 weeks, then brought to room temperature to open on demand.

**Care in the vase**:
- Remove all foliage below the waterline (leaves rot and produce bacteria)
- Re-cut stems at a 45-degree angle under water
- Use a clean vase with fresh, cool water
- Change water every 2 days, re-cutting stems each time
- Keep away from heat, direct sun, and ethylene-producing fruit
- A vase life of 7–10 days is typical for well-conditioned peonies

## Symbolism and cultural significance

The peony holds profound cultural importance across multiple traditions.

In **Chinese culture**, the peony is the national flower and has been cultivated for over 1,500 years. It symbolises prosperity, good fortune, beauty, and feminine grace. Called *mǔdān* (牡丹), it was the imperial flower of the Tang Dynasty and features in silk paintings, porcelain, and embroidery throughout Chinese art history. The city of Luoyang in Henan Province holds an annual Peony Cultural Festival, drawing millions of visitors to see the blooms.

In **Japanese culture**, the peony (*botan*) is associated with bravery, honour, and good fortune. It appears frequently in traditional tattoo art and woodblock prints.

In **Western tradition**, peonies have long been associated with romance, bashfulness (because of their tendency to blush from bud to bloom), and healing. The genus is named after Paeon, the physician to the Greek gods.

In the **language of flowers**, peonies mean bashfulness, happy marriage, and good fortune. Given at weddings, they are one of the most auspicious flowers in multiple cultural traditions simultaneously.

The peony repays everything asked of it — patience, good soil, and a careful eye — with blooms of impossible beauty. It is genuinely the queen of flowers.
